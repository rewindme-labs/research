# Cognitive Mapping
<!-- @field: description -->
A cognitive map is the internal mental representation of spatial relationships between locations - where things are relative to each other, how far apart they are, what routes connect them, and what is found at each location. This is not a conscious, deliberate construction; it is built continuously from experience, observation, and movement through an environment, updated each time new information is acquired.

For early hominins ranging over territories of 50-200 km², maintaining an accurate cognitive map was a core survival competence. The map encoded: water sources and their seasonal reliability; fruiting tree locations and their phenological timing; predator territories and predictable ambush locations; raw material outcrops; sleeping site options; and the routes between all of these. An individual with a rich, accurate cognitive map could navigate efficiently to the right resource at the right time; an individual with a poor map wasted energy and time searching where nothing was found.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
Neurological evidence for the deep evolutionary roots of spatial mapping comes from hippocampal studies. The hippocampus — the brain region primarily responsible for spatial memory and cognitive mapping — is present in all mammals and shows remarkable size increases in species with larger home ranges or more complex foraging strategies. Work by John O'Keefe (Nobel Prize 2014) demonstrated that hippocampal "place cells" create a continuous spatial map of experienced environments in rats, updated in real time during movement. The same cells are present in humans and are proportionally larger in individuals with extensive navigation experience (London taxi drivers show measurably larger hippocampal volume after years of complex route learning, per Maguire et al. 2000, *PNAS*).

Comparative primate data from field studies shows that chimpanzees at Bossou, Guinea (documented by Christophe Boesch and colleagues) navigate directly between widely separated food trees without visible landmarks, moving as if guided by an internal map rather than following trails. They time their routes to arrive at trees during peak fruiting — suggesting not just spatial memory but temporal tagging of location-based resource information.

Isotope and lithic provenance data from early *Homo* sites shows transport of specific raw materials from sources 10-90 km distant, implying that individuals maintained multi-day navigational memories and could plan routes across complex terrain.

## Building and Using a Cognitive Map
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Active map building — what to register at each location:**

| Location Type | Information to Record | Update Frequency |
|---------------|----------------------|-----------------|
| Water source | Size, reliability in dry season, predator presence | Every visit |
| Fruiting tree | Species, location relative to landmarks, fruiting month | Annually |
| Raw material outcrop | Rock type, quality, exact location | Once (rarely changes) |
| Sleeping site | Safety, access to trees, water distance | Each use |
| Kill/carcass site | Location, predator patterns nearby | Each occurrence |
| Predator sighting | Species, location, direction of movement | Each occurrence |

**Encoding new locations:**

1. On arrival at a new location, pause and look back the way you came — this encodes the return route view
2. Identify 2-3 distinctive landmarks visible from this location (ridge line, distinctive tree, rock formation)
3. Estimate compass direction and approximate distance from the last known location
4. Verbally or mentally describe the location in terms of these landmarks
5. On the return journey, pay explicit attention to the landscape from the new perspective

**Route learning:**

1. Walk new routes slowly and attentively the first time — stop at junctions and high points to look around
2. Memorize the sequence of landmarks in order: "cross the dry stream, then the double-topped hill is on your left, then the grove of fever trees, then the waterhole is below the rocky escarpment"
3. Travel the same route 3-5 times before treating it as reliably mapped
4. Add shortcuts as you discover them — cognitive maps improve with use

**Cross-checking your map:**

1. When you reach a location you expected to be in a specific direction, verify with sun position or star alignment
2. If your map and the landscape disagree, trust the landscape — update the map
3. Share map information across the group — one person's observation updates everyone's map
4. Verbally rehearse routes and locations that have not been visited recently — memory fades without refreshing

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Efficient foraging:** A group that moves directly from known water to known food patches rather than searching covers the same caloric territory in a fraction of the time, conserving energy for other activities.
- **Emergency resource access:** When the primary food source fails, immediate knowledge of backup patches is the difference between adaptation and starvation. The cognitive map is the database of alternatives.
- **Safe movement at night:** Familiarity with terrain — knowing where the safe clearings and danger zones are — allows limited movement at night or in low visibility that would be impossible without a precise internal map.
- **Seasonal planning:** Knowledge of where resources appear at what time of year allows departure from one patch before depletion, arriving at the next patch at peak productivity. This is impossible without temporal tagging in the cognitive map.

## Day 0 — Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0 -->
You are in new territory. Everything is unfamiliar. You need to build a map of this place before you can use it efficiently.

When you arrive somewhere important — a water source, a fruiting tree, a safe sleeping rock — stop and look back the way you came. That view is the one you need to recognize on the return. It looks different from how you approached.

Find two or three things you can see from this spot that you can also see from a distance: a double-peaked hill, a rock formation, a dead tree taller than the others. These are your anchors.

When you move on, count roughly — not steps, but time. "Half a morning's walk" is enough precision.

That night, tell your group what you found. Where it is. What you passed on the way. Their map and your map become one map.

In three weeks, you will know this territory. You will move through it directly, without searching. You will arrive at the water before you are thirsty and at the food before you are hungry.

The map in your head is the most valuable tool you carry. It weighs nothing and cannot be lost.
