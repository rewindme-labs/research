# Seasonal Migration Routes
<!-- @field: description -->
Seasonal migration - the cyclical movement between different resource zones following predictable annual patterns - is the landscape-scale survival strategy that allowed early hominins to sustain themselves year-round in environments where no single location provides adequate food, water, and shelter through all seasons. Rather than exploiting a fixed home range until it is depleted and starving through the lean season, groups that understood seasonal resource availability could time movements to always be in the right place when critical resources peaked.

This is not wandering. It is structured, knowledge-intensive movement along established corridors between specific known resource patches, timed to the predictable rhythms of rainfall, plant phenology, and animal migrations. The knowledge of these routes - when to be where, what will be available, where the water is in the dry season - is the most valuable information a group can possess, typically accumulated over generations and transmitted from older to younger members.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
Archaeological site distributions across East Africa provide strong evidence for hominin seasonal mobility patterns. Analysis of hominin fossil and archaeological site concentrations in the Turkana Basin (Kenya/Ethiopia) by Anna Kay Behrensmeyer and colleagues has shown that early hominin material clusters in lowland locations during dry seasons and disperses across higher-altitude areas during wet seasons - exactly the pattern predicted by a strategy of following water and food resources.

Isotope analysis of carbon and oxygen in early hominin tooth enamel provides even more direct evidence. A 2015 study by Thure Cerling et al. (*Nature*) analyzing *Australopithecus* and early *Homo* tooth enamel from the Turkana Basin showed oxygen isotope signatures consistent with habitual use of multiple distinct environmental zones with different rainfall histories - incompatible with a fixed territorial lifestyle but consistent with seasonal range shifts of 50-200 km.

Modern hunter-gatherers provide ethnographic models. The Hadza of Tanzania move between distinct dry-season and wet-season camp areas on a predictable annual cycle. The San of the Kalahari similarly follow seasonal migration routes to permanent water during dry season and disperse into temporarily flooded areas during wet season. Richard Lee's (1979) detailed mapping of !Kung San territory showed that groups returned to the same series of seasonal camps year after year, with routes and timing transmitted explicitly from parents to children.

## Route Planning and Knowledge
<!-- @field: procedure -->
Seasonal migration operates at two timescales: the within-year cycle (wet season base to dry season base and back) and the multi-year calibration of when exactly resources peak.

**Landscape reading for route planning:**

| Feature | Information | Use |
|---------|------------|-----|
| River flow direction and gradient | Where water drains during dry season | Follow drainage toward lowland pools |
| Vegetation color change | Timing of plant food peaks | Green flush = tuber growth, fruiting = move to fruiting zone |
| Animal herd movements | Large ungulate migrations = temporarily rich hunting ground | Follow wildebeest, zebra herds |
| Bird aggregations | Water sources, fish concentrations, insect emergences | Weaver bird colonies = permanent water nearby |
| Mountain orography | Rain shadow vs. windward face | Windward = wetter, more reliable vegetation |

**Standard seasonal movement pattern (East African savanna):**

| Season | Location | Primary Resources |
|--------|----------|------------------|
| Long rains (Mar-May) | Dispersed across high ground | Fruit, green tubers, water everywhere |
| Dry season 1 (Jun-Aug) | Mid-altitude, near seasonal rivers | Concentrated near water sources |
| Short rains (Oct-Nov) | Dispersed again | Fruit again, new tubers |
| Long dry season (Dec-Feb) | Lowland, permanent waterholes | Dependent on cached knowledge of water location |

**Route waypoints to memorize:**

1. Permanent water sources (wells, springs, rock pools) - these are the skeleton of the route
2. Reliable fruiting trees at known times (fig trees fruit semi-continuously; marula trees fruit December-January)
3. High animal traffic corridors - river crossings where herds concentrate
4. Elevated lookout points for landscape surveillance before committing to descent
5. Shelter locations (rock overhangs, dense bush patches) spaced no more than one day's walk apart

**Navigation during movement:**

1. Move primarily in early morning (cooler, better visibility, predators less active)
2. Rest during midday heat (reduce water loss from exertion)
3. Move secondary section in late afternoon, arriving at water or sleeping site before dark
4. Use landmarks - distinctive hills, rock formations, river confluences - not just directional sense

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Year-round caloric adequacy:** No single patch in the East African savanna provides adequate food year-round. Seasonal migration is what converts a marginal habitat into a viable homeland.
- **Conflict avoidance:** Migration routes are selected partly to avoid other groups' core territories, using boundary zones and seasonal overlap areas where resources temporarily accommodate multiple groups.
- **Knowledge preservation:** The existence of detailed multi-generational route knowledge creates strong selection pressure for social learning and language - the means by which this information is transmitted.
- **Disaster recovery:** When drought, fire, or disease depletes resources in one area, knowledge of alternative patches 50-150 km away is the difference between group survival and extinction.

## Day 0 — Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0 -->
It has not rained in 40 days. The waterhole near your camp is down to mud. The fruit is gone. You have 2 days of food left.

Do not wait for it to get worse.

Think about what you know. Where does the river come from? Water moves downhill. The valley 3 days walk east - is there permanent water there? What do you remember from the last dry season?

If you know a better place, move toward it now. Moving costs energy, but running out of water and food in your current position costs everything.

Plan the walk in sections. How far to the next water? Can you reach it in one day? If not, what do you carry now?

Move in the early morning. Rest at midday. Reach water before dark.

The route between your wet season and dry season ranges is the most important piece of knowledge your group possesses. Walking it now, even in difficulty, teaches it to the ones who will lead the group in 20 years.
