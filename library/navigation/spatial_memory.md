# Spatial Memory

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

Spatial memory is the detailed episodic recall of specific resource locations, their seasonal availability, the routes connecting them, and the temporal patterns of their productivity. It is the internal database that answers: where are the tuber patches, which fruiting trees are productive now, where is water during the dry season, where is the flint outcrop, which areas had game last week, and which sleep sites are safe? This is not abstract geographic knowledge. It is time-stamped, resource-specific, experientially encoded information that guides daily foraging decisions. Research on the Tsimane of Bolivia (Trumble, 2015) shows that experienced foragers carry mental maps of 200-300 resource locations within their home range, and navigate to specific trees, patches, and water sources across 50+ km2 without trails or tools. Clark's nutcrackers cache 33,000 seeds in 5,000 locations each autumn and retrieve them months later with 90% accuracy, demonstrating the extreme potential of evolved spatial memory systems.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

At Olduvai Gorge (1.8 Ma), the transport of stone raw materials from specific outcrops to butchery sites across distances of 2-13 km provides direct evidence of spatial memory in early Homo. These transport distances cannot be explained by random encounters with stone: the knappers knew where specific stone types occurred and planned trips specifically to collect them (Toth, 1985). At Koobi Fora (Kenya), the association of stone from multiple geographically distinct sources at single processing sites indicates that individuals maintained a mental catalogue of multiple source locations and selected from them based on current need. Among the Australian Aboriginals, spatial memory is encoded in songlines that map hundreds of water sources, food patches, and route landmarks across 1,600+ km of terrain, maintained orally across thousands of generations.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->

**Building a spatial resource database:**

| Resource type | What to encode | How to remember |
|---------------|---------------|-----------------|
| Water sources | Location, flow rate, seasonal reliability | Visit each weekly, note changes |
| Fruiting trees | Location, species, fruiting month, yield | Mark with cairn or blaze |
| Tuber patches | Location, soil type, seasonal availability | Note proximity to water and terrain |
| Stone sources | Location, stone type, quality, remaining quantity | Visit monthly, stockpile at camp |
| Game trails | Route, direction of travel, timing, species | Walk trails regularly, note tracks |
| Shelter sites | Location, quality rating, water distance | Catalogue for emergency use |
| Danger zones | Predator lairs, flood zones, unstable terrain | Mark mentally, warn group |

**Techniques for spatial encoding:**

1. Walk, do not be carried. Active locomotion through a landscape creates stronger spatial memory than passive observation. Walk every route you need to remember.
2. Count landmarks. Between camp and the water source, note: dry tree stump, then termite mound, then split boulder, then water. Four landmarks in sequence create an address system.
3. Associate resources with terrain features. "The tuber patch is on the south slope below the quartzite ridge." Anchoring resources to permanent features prevents confusion as vegetation changes seasonally.
4. Create a daily reporting habit. Each evening, foragers report findings to the group with directional and distance references. "Found a fruiting tree, two ridge lines east, near the dry stream bed." This distributes spatial knowledge.
5. Return to important locations by different routes. This builds a network of connections between resource points rather than a single trail.
6. Update seasonal changes. The water hole that was full in March may be dry in June. Seasonal reliability data is as important as location data.

**Group spatial memory management:**

1. No single person should hold all spatial knowledge. If the elder who knows every water source dies, the group is vulnerable.
2. Pair experienced foragers with learners. The experienced person narrates the route: "We are heading east toward the obsidian outcrop. Past the baobab, turn south at the red soil patch."
3. Draw maps in sand during evening sessions. Simple circles for water, triangles for shelter, lines for trails. These ephemeral maps transfer spatial relationships between minds.

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Foraging efficiency:** A forager with spatial memory of 200+ resource locations spends energy travelling directly to known productive sites instead of searching. Search time can be reduced by 60-80% compared to naive exploration.
- **Seasonal planning:** Knowing which resources are available in which season at which location enables pre-emptive migration and stockpiling before shortages occur.
- **Emergency resource access:** When the primary water source dries, spatial memory of 3-5 alternative sources means the difference between rapid relocation and panicked wandering.
- **Stone tool logistics:** Knowing exactly where high-quality flint occurs 8 km away enables planned procurement trips during low-activity periods rather than frantic searches when tools break.
- **Social advantage:** Groups with richer spatial knowledge exploit larger territories more efficiently than competing groups with less. This is a direct competitive advantage for survival and reproduction.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

Your brain is a map. Start filling it in right now.

Every time you walk somewhere today, pay attention. Not to your thoughts or your conversation but to the terrain under your feet and the landmarks around you. Count features between here and there. Dry tree, then red rock, then ant mound, then water. Four landmarks in sequence is an address you can follow in darkness, in rain, in panic.

When you find something valuable, a fruiting tree, a tuber patch, water, a good stone outcrop, stop and look around. What permanent feature is nearby? Not a bush that will be gone next season. A rock formation, a distinctive tree, a terrain feature that will be here in five years. Anchor the resource to the permanent feature in your mind.

Walk back to camp by a different route than you came. Now you know two paths to the same resource. If one is blocked by a predator, flooded, or impassable, you have an alternative without thinking.

Tonight at the fire, tell the group what you found and where. Point in the direction. Estimate the distance in walking time. Describe the landmarks along the way. If three people know where the water is, the group survives losing any one of them. If only you know, your injury is the group's emergency.

Draw in the dirt. A circle for camp, a line for the ridge, an X for the water hole. These crude maps transfer spatial relationships between brains in seconds. Tomorrow someone else walks a different direction and adds to the map.

Do this every single day. Within two weeks you will have a mental database of 30-50 locations. Within a month, 100. An experienced forager carries 200-300 locations and their seasonal patterns. That database is the most valuable survival asset your group possesses. Build it relentlessly.
