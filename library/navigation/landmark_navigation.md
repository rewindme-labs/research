# Landmark Navigation & Mental Mapping

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

Landmark navigation is the use of visible terrain features - mountains, rivers, rock formations, distinctive trees, coastlines - to establish position, direction, and route. It is the oldest and most intuitive wayfinding method, requiring no instruments and no astronomical knowledge. The core skill is building a mental map: a spatial model of the environment that links landmarks into routes, routes into networks, and networks into territories. A skilled navigator can retrace a path walked once, estimate distance by terrain features, identify water sources from vegetation patterns, and find a base camp from any direction. Chimpanzees maintain mental maps of 20+ fruit trees across home ranges of 10-30 km2. Human hunter-gatherers routinely navigate territories of 500-5000 km2 using purely landmark-based wayfinding.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

Spatial navigation is neurologically ancient - the hippocampal place cells that underpin mental mapping exist in all mammals, with homologous structures in reptiles and birds. The San Bushmen of the Kalahari navigate featureless semi-desert by reading micro-topography, vegetation transitions, and sand dune orientation that are invisible to untrained observers, as documented by Elizabeth Marshall Thomas (1959). Australian Aboriginal songlines encode navigation routes spanning thousands of kilometres as oral narratives linking sacred landmarks in sequence - each verse corresponds to a geographic feature, creating memorizable itineraries that can cross an entire continent. Polynesian navigators combined landmark knowledge (island profiles from different approach angles) with wave patterns and cloud formations to navigate across 3000+ km of open ocean. The Marshall Islanders constructed stick-charts - physical models of ocean swell patterns around island chains - representing the earliest known cartographic aids, dated to at least 500 AD.

## Method / Construction
<!-- @field: method -->

**Building a mental map (progressive protocol):**

**Day 1 - Anchor points:**

1. From your camp, identify the 4-5 most visible landmarks in each cardinal direction.
2. Assign each a memorable name based on shape: "forked tree ridge", "flat-top hill", "red cliff".
3. Note which side of each landmark the sun rises from and sets behind.
4. These are your primary anchor points - they should be visible from 5+ km.

**Day 2-3 - Route mapping:**

1. Walk to your water source, food areas, and raw material sites.
2. On each route, identify 3-5 intermediate landmarks visible at decision points (trail forks, river crossings, ridge crests).
3. Look back frequently - landmarks look different from opposite directions. Memorize the "return view" at each waypoint.
4. Estimate segment distances by walking pace count (average stride = 0.7-0.8 m, 1250-1400 steps per km).

**Day 4-7 - Network building:**

1. Walk connecting routes between known areas to create a navigation network.
2. Test your mental map by navigating to a known point from a new starting direction.
3. Identify terrain corridors: ridgelines for long-distance travel with visibility, valleys for water access, game trails for path of least resistance.

**Landmark categories:**

| Category | Examples | Reliability |
|----------|----------|-------------|
| Permanent (geological) | Mountains, cliffs, rock formations, river confluences | Highest - unchanged over lifetimes |
| Semi-permanent (biological) | Large distinctive trees, forest edges, termite mounds | High - decades to centuries |
| Seasonal (variable) | Snow patches, flood levels, green/dry vegetation bands | Moderate - requires seasonal knowledge |
| Transient (temporary) | Smoke columns, animal herds, temporary camps | Low - useful for hours to days only |

**Terrain reading (distance and direction without instruments):**

| Technique | Method |
|-----------|--------|
| Distance by sound | Thunder: 3 sec per km. Gunshot: audible to 5-10 km. Waterfall: 1-3 km by sound. |
| Distance by visibility | Human figure visible to 2-3 km. Campfire smoke to 15-30 km. Mountain peak 50-100 km in clear air. |
| Direction by water flow | Rivers flow downhill toward the sea or endorheic basins. Following downstream usually leads to settlements and larger water. |
| Direction by vegetation | In Northern Hemisphere, moss often grows thicker on north side of trees (less reliable than commonly stated). Lichen growth heavier on the windward side. |
| Direction by terrain | Mountain ranges create rain shadows: wet side has denser vegetation than dry side. |
| Elevation by vegetation | Tree species change with altitude. Tree line (no trees above) indicates specific elevation depending on latitude. |

**Trail marking (for return navigation):**

| Method | Materials | Visibility |
|--------|-----------|------------|
| Broken branches | Point broken end in travel direction | 3-5 m, subtle |
| Stone cairns | Stacked rocks (3-5 stones) | 20-50 m, persistent |
| Bark blazes | Strip bark patch from tree at eye level | 10-20 m, lasts weeks-months |
| Ground arrows | Arranged sticks or stones on path | 5-10 m, fragile |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Daily foraging:** Navigate reliably between camp, water, food sources, and material sites across a home range of 10-50 km2.
- **Seasonal migration:** Follow memorized multi-day routes between seasonal resource areas, water sources, and sheltered wintering sites.
- **Group coordination:** Communicate routes and locations to other group members using shared landmark names.
- **Emergency return:** Navigate back to camp from any point in the territory, including under poor visibility (rain, fog, dusk).
- **Territory expansion:** Systematically explore outward from known territory, linking new areas to the existing mental map.
- **Resource mapping:** Track productive foraging sites, water sources, stone outcrops, clay deposits, and seasonal fruit trees across the territory.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

You need to find your way back. Every time you leave camp, you need to return. Start building your mental map now.

Stand at your camp and turn slowly in a full circle. Find four or five things you can see that are permanent and distinctive. A hill with a notch in the ridgeline. A tall tree that stands above the canopy. A cliff face with a vertical stripe of different-coloured rock. A river bend. Name them. "Notch Hill", "Tall Pine", "Stripe Cliff". These are your anchor points.

When you walk to water, stop every 200 paces and look back. The route looks completely different from the other direction. Memorize what your camp direction looks like from each stop. Find intermediate landmarks at every decision point - a forked tree at the trail split, a boulder where you turn left, a stream crossing with a fallen log. Three to five waypoints per route is enough.

Count your paces. One thousand three hundred steps is roughly one kilometre on flat ground. If your water source is 2600 paces east and slightly downhill from camp, you know your position relative to water at all times.

Mark your trails. The simplest method: break a branch at head height, leaving the broken end pointing in the direction you are travelling. Anyone following your trail sees the markers. At major decision points, stack three stones. These persist through rain and wind.

After three days of walking your key routes (camp to water, camp to food, camp to stone source), you will have a functional mental map. Test it: approach from a direction you have not used before and navigate to camp using only landmarks. If you can do this, your map works.

Expand outward. Each day, walk further along a new bearing. Link new territory back to known landmarks. Within two weeks you will know your home range well enough to navigate in poor light, in rain, or after being displaced by a predator encounter. This knowledge is as essential as water - without it, every journey risks becoming permanent.
