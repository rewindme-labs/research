# Terrain Knowledge

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

Terrain knowledge is the accumulated understanding of the physical landscape within a group's home range: ridge lines, valleys, water courses, rock outcrops, tree clusters, soil types, seasonal flood zones, predator territories, and resource concentrations. It is the mental map that allows an individual to navigate without landmarks, predict where water will appear after rain, know which slope face catches morning sun, and choose the safest route between two points. All surviving hunter-gatherer groups possess extraordinary terrain literacy. Australian Aboriginal songlines encode topographic knowledge across hundreds of kilometres in oral tradition. San Bushmen navigate the featureless Kalahari by reading soil colour, vegetation patches, and subtle elevation changes invisible to outsiders. Terrain knowledge is not innate. It is learned by walking, observing, remembering, and sharing.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

Homo erectus expanded from Africa into the Caucasus (Dmanisi, Georgia, 1.8 Ma) and Southeast Asia (Java, 1.6 Ma), requiring terrain knowledge across radically different landscapes: volcanic highlands, river deltas, coastal plains, and dense forest. This colonisation implies sophisticated landscape reading, since wrong decisions about river crossings, mountain passes, or arid stretches would have been fatal. At Olduvai Gorge (Tanzania), the distribution of stone tools across different terrain zones (lakeshore, river channel, upland plateau) shows that Homo habilis groups at 1.8 Ma regularly moved between distinct ecological patches within a single day, requiring detailed terrain awareness (Blumenschine and Peters, 1998). Among the Hadza of Tanzania, individuals can navigate to water sources, tuber patches, and berry groves across 1,500 km2 of savanna-woodland by reading terrain alone, with no trails or markers.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->

**Building a mental terrain map:**

| Feature | What to observe | Why it matters |
|---------|----------------|----------------|
| High ground | Ridges, hilltops, rock outcrops | Orientation points, defensive positions, wind exposure |
| Water drainage | Valley bottoms, channels, dark vegetation lines | Water sources follow terrain; low ground floods first |
| Sun exposure | South-facing slopes (northern hemisphere) get more sun | Warmer camps, earlier snowmelt, different plant growth |
| Soil colour | Dark = organic/wet, red = iron/dry, grey = clay | Ground trafficability, water table depth, plant types |
| Vegetation bands | Dense trees = water, sparse grass = dry, reeds = wetland | Resource prediction without walking there |
| Rock type | Granite = hard ground, limestone = caves/sinkholes, sandstone = shelters | Shelter potential, knapping material, water storage |

**Active terrain survey (first 48 hours in a new area):**

1. Climb the highest accessible point. Spend 30 minutes scanning 360 degrees. Note: water (reflective surfaces, dark vegetation lines), shelter (rock faces, dense canopy), open ground (savanna, clearings), and barriers (cliffs, deep rivers, dense swamp).
2. Walk toward the nearest water indicator. Count paces. Note terrain changes: where soil gets softer, where vegetation gets greener, where animal tracks converge.
3. Return to your high point by a different route. You now know two paths and the terrain between them.
4. Next day, explore in the opposite direction. Build your map quadrant by quadrant.
5. Each evening, describe your route to the group. Draw in sand or dirt if needed. Shared terrain knowledge multiplies survival options.

**Reading terrain for danger:**

| Terrain feature | Danger indicated |
|-----------------|-----------------|
| Narrow canyon with smooth walls | Flash flood channel. Never camp here. |
| Tall grass near water with heavy tracks | Predator ambush zone. Approach water from open side. |
| Steep unstable slope with loose scree | Landslide risk. Traverse above, not below. |
| Flat open ground with no cover for 500+ m | Exposure to predators, weather, and hostile groups. Cross quickly. |
| Dense single-species vegetation | Possible bog or swamp beneath. Probe with stick before walking. |

| Soil samples | Ground surface | Compare colours to predict water/drainage |
| Sun position | Sky observation | Cardinal direction, slope aspect |
| Water flow | Any stream or runoff | Drainage direction reveals topography |
| Animal trails | Ground tracks | Converging trails indicate water or shelter |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Route planning:** Choosing the path between camp and water that avoids predator zones, unstable ground, and unnecessary elevation change. A 500 m longer route that stays on solid ground beats a direct route through bog.
- **Camp site selection:** Terrain knowledge locates the ideal camp: elevated, windbreak-backed, water-proximate, with clear sightlines and multiple escape routes.
- **Seasonal prediction:** Knowing which valleys flood in wet season, which slopes dry out first, and which ridges catch first rain enables pre-emptive relocation.
- **Resource mapping:** Each terrain type supports different resources. Rocky outcrops provide knapping stone. Valley floors grow tubers. Forest edges concentrate small game. Mapping terrain maps resources.
- **Emergency navigation:** Lost individuals use terrain reading to recover direction: follow water downhill, use ridge lines as travel corridors, identify landmarks from elevation.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

Get high. Right now, before you do anything else today, climb the tallest thing near you. A hill, a rock outcrop, a large tree. Get above the canopy and look in every direction.

What you see from up here determines every decision you make for the next week. Find water first. Look for dark lines of vegetation cutting across dry ground. Look for reflective surfaces. Look for animal trails converging. That convergence point is water.

Now find shelter. Look for rock faces, dense tree clusters, anything that blocks wind from one direction and provides overhead protection. Note where it is relative to your water source.

Identify danger. Where is the open ground with no cover? Mark those kill zones mentally and plan routes that avoid them. Where are the narrow channels that could flash flood? Where is the tall thick grass where predators wait?

Come back down. Walk to the water you spotted. Count your steps. Note every change underfoot: where the ground goes soft, where the soil colour changes, where animal tracks appear. These are data points your life depends on.

Walk back by a different route. Now you know two ways to water. Tomorrow, explore the opposite direction from your camp. Day by day, you build a map in your head that covers more terrain.

Every evening, tell someone else what you saw. Trace routes in the dirt. Point at features along the horizon. If you get injured tomorrow, the person you told can find water, can find shelter, can navigate the group to safety. Terrain knowledge that lives in only one head dies when that head goes down. Share it like food. It is just as critical.
