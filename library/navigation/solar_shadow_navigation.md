# Solar & Shadow Navigation

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->
Solar and shadow navigation is the determination of cardinal directions and approximate time using the position and movement of the sun, without any instruments beyond a straight stick. It is the oldest and most universally available form of orientation on Earth: it works in any location outside the polar circles, in any season, at any point in history, with no training beyond the techniques described here.

The key principle is that the sun rises approximately in the east and sets approximately in the west, and moves in a predictable arc across the sky that tilts toward the equator. In the Northern Hemisphere, the midday sun is due south; in the Southern Hemisphere, due north. Shadow tips trace an arc opposite to the sun's movement, providing a stable reference system that can be used to determine cardinal directions to within 10-15 degrees of accuracy with basic methods, and within 5 degrees with the refined shadow-tip method.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
The oldest known solar instrument is the gnomon (a vertical stick used to cast a shadow), with the earliest formal records from Egypt around 3500 BCE. However, the behavior of using shadow position to determine direction is far older - ethnographic records of hunter-gatherer groups in Australia, the Amazon, and the Kalahari all describe solar orientation as a fundamental navigational tool requiring no artifact or intentional construction.

The shadow clock (a graduated stick measuring shadow length for time of day) appears in Egyptian records by 1500 BCE. The Romans used the gnomon for urban planning, aligning the layout of military camps with cardinal directions determined by noon shadow. Julius Caesar's legions could establish a standardized camp layout within 2 hours of arrival at any new site, in any previously unknown territory, using only solar orientation.

The Polynesian navigational tradition (perfecting Pacific voyaging by 3000 BCE) combined solar orientation with star paths, wave patterns, and bird behavior. The sun's rising and setting positions on the horizon (which shift seasonally but predictably) defined the seasonal "star compass" used to navigate between island groups separated by thousands of kilometers, with accuracy sufficient to make intentional landfalls consistently. No physical compass was used.

In medieval Europe, the church canonically determined prayer times and the orientation of church buildings by solar observation. All churches built before the magnetic compass became widespread (~1100-1200 CE) are oriented by solar east, obtained by marking the shadow-tip of a gnomon at dawn and aligning the building accordingly.

## Method / Construction
<!-- @field: method -->
**Method 1: The Shadow-Tip Method (most accurate)**

This method works anywhere on Earth except within the polar circles and gives accuracy of 10-15 degrees.

1. Find a straight stick 60-90 cm long. Push it vertically into flat, level ground in an open area where it casts a clear shadow. Mark the exact tip of the shadow with a stone, twig, or scratch in the soil. Call this Point A.

2. Wait 15-30 minutes without moving the stick. Mark the new shadow tip position. Call this Point B.

3. Draw or scratch a straight line from Point A to Point B.

4. Stand with your left foot on Point A and your right foot on Point B (or vice versa if in the Southern Hemisphere). You are now facing approximately north (Northern Hemisphere) or south (Southern Hemisphere).

| Position | Northern Hemisphere | Southern Hemisphere |
|----------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Left foot (A - first mark) | West | East |
| Right foot (B - second mark) | East | West |
| Facing direction | North | South |

The first shadow tip is always in the west (the sun rises in the east, casting shadows westward). The second tip is always east of the first. The line runs roughly west to east.

**Method 2: The Noon Shadow Method**

The shortest shadow cast by any vertical object during the day points directly toward the nearest pole. In the Northern Hemisphere, the noon shadow points due north. In the Southern Hemisphere, it points due south.

1. Set up a vertical stick. Mark shadows every 30 minutes starting from mid-morning (approximately 9-10 AM local solar time).
2. The shadow will shorten throughout the morning, reach a minimum length at local solar noon, then begin lengthening in the afternoon.
3. The minimum shadow (lunar noon shadow) points directly poleward. Mark this direction - this is your north/south line.

**Note:** Local solar noon is NOT the same as 12:00 on a clock. It can vary by up to 2 hours depending on longitude within a time zone. Use the shortest shadow method to find true solar noon, not the clock.

**Method 3: Star orientation at night**

*Northern Hemisphere - Polaris (North Star):*
1. Find Ursa Major (the Big Dipper). The two stars forming the front edge of the dipper cup (Dubhe and Merak) point directly to Polaris.
2. The distance from Dubhe to Polaris is approximately 5x the distance between Dubhe and Merak. Follow this line and the bright star at the end is Polaris.
3. Polaris sits within 1 degree of true north and does not move in the sky. The entire sky rotates around it.

*Southern Hemisphere - Southern Cross (Crux):*
1. Find the Southern Cross (4 main stars in a compact cross pattern). The long axis of the cross points toward the south celestial pole.
2. Extend the long axis 4.5x its own length in the direction the short end points. This is the south celestial pole (no bright star marks it).
3. Drop a perpendicular from this point to the horizon. This is south.

**Method 4: Watch orientation method (if in possession of an analog watch)**

In the Northern Hemisphere:
1. Hold the watch flat and horizontal.
2. Point the hour hand toward the sun.
3. The true south direction is halfway between the hour hand and 12 o'clock.

In the Southern Hemisphere:
1. Point 12 o'clock toward the sun.
2. The bisection line between 12 and the hour hand points north.

**Accuracy note:** This method loses accuracy near equinoxes and near the equator. Shadow-tip method is preferred.

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Day 2 orientation:** Determine which direction leads to features seen from elevation (valley, coast, river) relative to your camp. Establish a consistent bearing and maintain it through terrain.
- **Camp layout:** Orient your camp so the prevailing wind and morning sun are predictable. Place the fire downwind; sleep facing the entry.
- **Resource mapping:** Mark the cardinal direction of resource locations from your base camp. "Water source is 200 paces due east" is a navigational record that survives memory failure.
- **Return navigation:** Before leaving camp, mark the direction you are traveling. The return bearing is exactly opposite (N becomes S, NE becomes SW).
- **Seasonal resource prediction:** Sun rises north of due east in summer, south of due east in winter (Northern Hemisphere). Tracking this shift tells you season and thus resource availability.

## Technical Data

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Shadow-tip method accuracy | 10-15 degrees (within 1.5 compass points) |
| Noon shadow method accuracy | 3-5 degrees under clear conditions |
| Polaris angular distance from true north | Under 1 degree (currently 0.7 degrees; varies with precession) |
| Southern Cross long-axis overshoot multiplier | 4.5x the cross length |
| Solar variation from true east/west (equinox) | 0 degrees at equinoxes; up to 23.5 degrees at solstices |
| Shadow-tip wait time | 15 minutes minimum; 30 minutes for better accuracy |
| Gnomon optimal length | 60-100 cm; longer = more shadow movement per unit time |
| Local solar noon vs. clock noon | Can differ by up to 120 minutes depending on longitude within time zone |
| Accuracy of watch method near equator | Unreliable; use shadow-tip method instead |
| Polar circles (method fails) | Above 66.5°N or below 66.5°S in midsummer (sun circumpolar) |

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->
You are on Day 2 and you need to move toward water, toward a valley, or toward a coastline you saw from high ground yesterday. You have no map, no compass, no phone. You need to walk in a consistent direction for several hours without looping in circles (which untrained humans do inevitably in terrain without visual cues).

**Do this now, before you start walking:**

1. Push a straight stick into level ground. Mark the shadow tip with a stone. Wait 20 minutes. Mark the shadow tip again. The line from the first stone to the second stone runs west to east (first stone = west, second stone = east). Stand perpendicular to this line facing the second stone. You are now facing north (if in the Northern Hemisphere).

2. Face the direction you need to travel. Identify the angle between your direction and north (north = 0, east = 90, south = 180, west = 270). Remember this number or write it in soil.

3. To maintain your bearing while walking: choose a tree, rock, or hill exactly in your travel direction far ahead. Walk to it. Then choose another landmark in the same direction. Keep the sun on a consistent side of your body - if you are walking east at 9 AM, the sun should be roughly behind your right shoulder. If it moves to your front, you have drifted north.

4. Every 30-45 minutes, check your bearing by re-casting a brief shadow. A 5-minute shadow-tip check corrects any cumulative drift before it becomes significant.

**At night:** Face Polaris (Northern Hemisphere) or the extended Southern Cross axis (Southern Hemisphere). Your compass heading is now calibrated to within 2-3 degrees. Identify the star that rises exactly in the direction you need to travel and use it as your travel star until it becomes too high in the sky (it will shift left if moving right across the sky, and so on).

You will not get lost if you do the shadow check every 45 minutes. The check takes 5 minutes maximum. The cost of drifting 20 degrees for 4 hours of walking is ending up 3 km off your intended destination. Do the check.
