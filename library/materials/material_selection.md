# Material Selection

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

Material selection is the evaluation and choice of natural materials based on their physical properties: hardness, weight, density, grain structure, flexibility, fracture behaviour, durability, and suitability for a specific task. Choosing a hard, fine-grained cobble for knapping instead of a porous sandstone. Selecting a dense hardwood branch for a digging stick instead of a soft, pithy one. Testing a vine for tensile strength before using it to bind. This is applied materials science at its most fundamental. Chimpanzees at Bossou (Guinea) select granite over laterite for nut-cracking hammerstones, demonstrating that material evaluation predates humans (Matsuzawa, 1994). In the Oldowan record, preferential selection of basalt, chert, and quartzite over locally available but inferior materials, sometimes transported 10+ km, proves that early Homo deliberately sought optimal materials at significant energy cost.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

Nicholas Toth's refitting studies at Koobi Fora (Kenya, 1.5 Ma) showed that specific cobble types (phonolite, basalt) were carried up to 13 km from their geological source to butchery sites, bypassing closer but inferior stone types. This transport implies evaluation, comparison, and forward planning. At Olduvai Gorge, Mary Leakey documented preferential use of Naibor Soit quartzite for large cutting tools, requiring 4 km transport from the source outcrop, while local lava was used only for expedient flakes. Among the Hadza, wood selection for bow making involves testing 5-10 candidate branches by flexing, checking grain direction, and assessing elasticity before committing to any single piece, a process taking up to 2 hours. In Aboriginal Australian lithic traditions, certain quarry sites were traded across 800+ km because their stone possessed uniquely fine flaking properties unavailable locally.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->

**Material evaluation tests (field-applicable):**

| Test | Method | What it reveals |
|------|--------|-----------------|
| Tap test | Strike two stones together, listen | Ringing tone = homogeneous, knappable. Dull thud = flawed |
| Scratch test | Scratch surface with a known hard stone | Harder materials resist scratching. Soft materials groove |
| Flex test (wood) | Bend the branch slowly | Returns to straight = resilient. Stays bent or snaps = reject |
| Weight test | Hold in palm, compare to similar size objects | Unusually heavy = dense material, good for hammerstones |
| Grain test | Break a small piece, examine fracture surface | Smooth conchoidal = fine-grained (knapping). Rough/granular = coarse |
| Water test | Submerge or wet the surface | Reveals hidden cracks, grain direction, and internal structure |
| Burn test (wood) | Hold near flame | Dense hardwood chars slowly. Softwood ignites rapidly |

**Material selection by task:**

| Task | Ideal material | Key property | Where to find |
|------|---------------|--------------|---------------|
| Knapping (flake production) | Chert, flint, obsidian, fine basalt | Conchoidal fracture, fine grain | Riverbeds, limestone beds, volcanic areas |
| Hammerstone | Dense rounded granite or quartzite | Weight, hardness, durability | Riverbeds (water-rounded) |
| Digging stick | Dense hardwood (acacia, olive, ironwood) | Hardness, straight grain | Woodland edges, river margins |
| Cordage | Inner bark fibre, sinew, grass | Tensile strength, flexibility | Dead trees (bark), game carcass (sinew) |
| Fire drill | Dry softwood (hearth), dry hardwood (spindle) | Friction coefficient, dryness | Dead standing trees |
| Shelter frame | Straight branches, 3-5 cm diameter | Rigidity, lightweight | Forest floor deadfall |
| Container | Bark, large leaves, stomach | Flexibility without cracking, waterproof | Dead trees, tropical plants, game |

**Quality indicators for stone:**

1. Colour uniformity: consistent colour throughout indicates homogeneous grain. Bands or spots indicate mixed composition and unpredictable fracture.
2. Texture: smooth surface suggests fine grain. Sugary or sparkly surface (visible crystals) means coarse grain.
3. Shape: naturally angular cobbles with existing flat surfaces provide ready-made striking platforms for knapping.
4. Cortex condition: weathered outer rind should be thin. Thick cortex means more wasted material before reaching usable interior.

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Tool quality:** A flake from fine-grained chert cuts 3-5x more efficiently than one from coarse quartzite. Material selection determines tool performance before a single strike is made.
- **Energy efficiency:** Walking 5 km to a known quartzite outcrop saves the frustration and caloric waste of attempting to knap unsuitable local stone. Knowing where good material lives saves time every subsequent trip.
- **Durability planning:** A digging stick made from dense ironwood lasts weeks. One from softwood lasts hours. Material choice determines replacement frequency and therefore total foraging time spent on maintenance.
- **Multi-property matching:** A fire drill requires two different materials: a soft hearth board (low ignition point) and a hard spindle (friction resistance). Matching complementary properties is more complex than single-material selection.
- **Trade foundation:** Knowledge of where specific high-quality materials occur creates the basis for exchange networks. Groups with access to obsidian sources traded with groups hundreds of kilometres away.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

Not all rocks are equal. Not all sticks are equal. The difference between the right material and the wrong one is the difference between a tool that works and a waste of an hour.

Go to the nearest riverbed. Pick up stones. Tap them against each other and listen. The one that rings like a bell is the one you want. That clear tone means the interior is uniform, solid, and will fracture predictably when you knap it. The one that thuds is full of invisible cracks and grain changes. Throw it back.

Now pick up sticks. Bend each one slowly. The one that bends and springs back is live, resilient wood with straight grain. The one that stays bent or snaps is dead, dry, or has twisted grain. For a digging stick, you want the heaviest, hardest branch you can find that is still straight. Try to dent it with your thumbnail. If you cannot, it is hardwood.

Learn to read stone by colour. Dark, uniform colour usually means fine grain. Banded or spotted stone has mixed composition and will fracture unpredictably. Glassy surfaces mean very fine grain, potentially obsidian or high-quality chert. These are the premium materials and they are worth walking extra distance to collect.

Start building a mental map of where good materials are located. That riverbed with the dark basalt cobbles, remember it. That outcrop with the glassy chert fragments, mark it with a cairn so you can find it again. Good material sources are as valuable as water sources and food patches. They are permanent features of your territory and every trip back to them saves the time you would spend testing inferior local stones.

When you find an excellent stone or branch, carry it home even if you do not need it today. A cache of pre-selected materials at camp means you can produce tools immediately when need arises rather than spending an hour sourcing materials before you can even begin.
