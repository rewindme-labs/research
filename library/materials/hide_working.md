# Hide Working
<!-- @field: description -->
Raw animal hide — the skin removed from a butchered carcass — is one of the most versatile raw materials available to early hominins. Processed hide becomes flexible, durable material for clothing, sleeping blankets, water containers, carrying bags, binding material, and shelter components. Unprocessed, a raw hide dries into a stiff, brittle board that is not useful for most purposes. The transformation from raw skin to usable hide requires specific processing steps that preserve flexibility and durability.

Hide working is documented archaeologically at least to the Middle Paleolithic (~100,000-40,000 ya), with scraper tools showing wear patterns consistent with sustained hide-working, and perforated shells and ivory that suggest hide garments with fastenings. The cognitive and material demands of hide working are substantial — it requires multiple steps spread over days, appropriate tools at each stage, and environmental knowledge (use of brain, fat, smoke, or bark tannins). Nevertheless, the payoff in material utility is enormous: a single large bovid hide provides 3-5 m² of working material.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
The earliest indirect evidence of hide working comes from stone scraper tools with use-wear patterns. Microscopic analysis of edge damage on Middle Stone Age and Middle Paleolithic scrapers from sites including Pinnacle Point (South Africa, ~100 ka) and sites across Europe reveals characteristic "hide polish" — a smooth, bright micro-wear pattern produced by lateral scraping of skin against a stone edge, distinct from the wear produced by wood or bone working.

Neanderthal hide use is well documented: lithic assemblages from Mousterian sites (250-40 ka) include high proportions of side scrapers showing hide-working wear, and isotope analysis of Neanderthal remains shows stable nitrogen ratios consistent with high animal protein consumption, including the fat-rich organs that contain the brain used in brain tanning.

Ethnographic documentation of hide working is extensive. The Plains peoples of North America perfected the brain-tanning process: after scraping, the hide is treated with an emulsion of brain tissue (every animal has just enough brain to tan its own hide) which prevents collagen cross-linking and maintains suppleness. This method produces the softest possible hide and was the standard process before commercial tanning.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Step 1 - Green hide removal and immediate handling:**

Remove the hide from the carcass as soon as possible after death — delays allow bacterial decomposition to weaken the skin's fiber structure, making the hide prone to tearing during processing.

1. Make a cut along the belly midline from neck to tail
2. Cut down the inside of each leg to meet the belly cut
3. Work the skin away from the carcass using a combination of cutting (sharp flake) and fisting (pushing your fist between hide and fascia to separate them without cutting)
4. Remove any attached fat or connective tissue immediately while the hide is warm — this is much easier now than after cooling
5. Lay flat, flesh side up, in shade to prevent rapid drying

**Step 2 - Fleshing (removing fat and membrane):**

This is the most labor-intensive stage.

1. Drape the hide over a smooth log or rounded rock (fleshing beam), flesh side up
2. Work the flake scraper (see: *Flake Scraper*) across the flesh side at 45 degrees, using firm even strokes away from your body
3. Remove all fat, membrane (periosteum-like connective tissue), and muscle remnants — any remaining organic material will rot and weaken the hide
4. Work systematically in sections — complete each section fully before moving to the next
5. The hide should be uniformly pale white/cream color when fully fleshed

**Step 3 - Drying (if immediate use is not needed):**

1. Stretch the hide by driving stakes or pegs through the edges into the ground, pulling taut
2. Allow to dry in sun and wind for 1-2 days
3. A dried hide can be stored indefinitely and worked later

**Step 4 - Brain tanning (for soft, flexible leather):**

1. Take the brain of the animal (removed from skull — see: *Bone Marrow Extraction*). Mash into a paste.
2. Mix with a small amount of water and the rendered fat from the carcass.
3. Work the brain-fat emulsion into both sides of the still-damp hide, rubbing firmly with hands and a smooth stone
4. Fold and knead the hide repeatedly over several hours to work the emulsion into all fibers
5. Allow to rest overnight, then stretch and work again as it dries
6. During drying, continuously stretch and work the hide — pulling, folding, twisting — to prevent stiffening

**Step 5 - Smoking (extends longevity and water resistance):**

1. Construct a simple smoke tent over a smoldering fire (green wood, rotted wood — produces smoke with minimal flame)
2. Hang the tanned hide inside the tent 30-50 cm above the smoke source
3. Smoke for 1-2 hours per side — the hide turns light brown and acquires a distinctive smell
4. Smoked hide remains soft even when wet — unsmoked brain-tanned hide stiffens if re-wetted

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Cold climate clothing:** A single tanned deer or bovid hide provides enough material for a full-body garment — critical for cold season survival in temperate or high-altitude environments.
- **Sleeping insulation:** A single large hide laid under the body during sleep reduces conductive heat loss from ground contact — equivalent in thermal benefit to several centimeters of plant-fiber bedding.
- **Water container:** The stomach, bladder, or a folded and sewn section of hide holds 3-8 liters of water — transforming liquid collection and transport capacity.
- **Binding and cordage:** Strips of hide (rawhide) are extremely strong when dry and can be used for hafting, binding tools, and constructing frames. They shrink on drying, tightening joints permanently.

## Day 0 — Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0 -->
You have a fresh hide. It is still warm. You have 2 hours before it starts to stiffen.

Start now. Drape it flesh side up over a log. Take your scraper. Work it across the membrane — push forward, angle at 45 degrees, firm pressure. Curls of fat and membrane will come off. Keep going. Every section must be uniformly white. Grey or yellow patches will rot.

Stretch it between stakes when you have fleshed it. Pull it taut in all directions and peg it to the ground. Let it dry in the sun.

The brain is in the skull. Crack the skull at the back. Scoop the brain into a cup of bark. Mash it into paste. Add a little fat from the carcass. Work this paste into both sides of the hide while it is still a little damp — not completely dry. Rub firmly. Fold and knead for an hour. Repeat tomorrow.

Each time it starts to stiffen during drying, pull and stretch it. Work it between your hands.

When it dries soft and uniform, smoke it for an hour over green wood.

That hide will outlast you if you store it dry.
