# Ochre Use
<!-- @field: description -->
Ochre is iron oxide (Fe₂O₃) occurring naturally as a red, yellow, or brown mineral in many geological settings worldwide. It is one of the first non-food materials demonstrably selected, transported, and processed by hominins for deliberate use. Red ochre in particular — hematite — was processed into powder by grinding on stone and used for a range of functional and, eventually, symbolic purposes from at least 300,000 years ago and possibly as far back as 1.5 million years ago.

The functional uses of ochre are well-documented: powdered ochre inhibits bacterial growth on freshly scraped hides, preventing putrefaction; ochre mixed with fat creates a durable adhesive for hafting; ochre powder on skin provides UV protection and discourages insect biting; and ochre applied to cordage slows degradation. The later symbolic uses — body marking, burial, cave art — represent an elaboration of an already-established material relationship.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
The oldest known ochre processing sites are at Wonderwerk Cave, South Africa (~1 Ma, Berna et al. 2012) which produced ochre-stained bone fragments, and Twin Rivers, Zambia (~400,000 ya, Barham 2002), where a large collection of deliberately selected ochre pieces shows clear grinding wear. Pinnacle Point Cave 13B in South Africa (Marean et al. 2007, *Nature*) produced evidence of consistent ochre processing from 164,000 years ago, associated with shellfish consumption and blade tool use.

The Blombos Cave site in South Africa (Henshilwood et al. 2002, 2009) yielded ochre "toolkits" — abalone shells containing a mixture of ochre powder, fat, charcoal, and bone fragments — dated to 100,000 ya. These are interpreted as the earliest known prepared pigment compounds with probable body-decorating function.

Ian Watts (2009) proposed the "Red Ochre Hypothesis," arguing that female hominids used red ochre to signal and extend the perception of sexual receptivity — an early form of symbolic communication. This remains debated but highlights the difficulty of separating functional from symbolic use in the archaeological record.

Regardless of symbolic interpretation, the functional utility of ochre — as hide preservative, adhesive component, and UV/insect barrier — provides a straightforward adaptive explanation for its systematic collection and processing well before behavioral modernity.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Step 1 — Sourcing:**

| Ochre type | Color | Best for | Where to find |
|------------|-------|----------|---------------|
| Hematite (red) | Bright red-orange | Pigment, hide preservation | Red-streaked rock faces, iron-rich soil layers |
| Limonite (yellow) | Yellow-ochre | Pigment, adhesive | Stream sediments, weathered iron deposits |
| Goethite (brown) | Dark brown-orange | Adhesive mix | Same as limonite |

Select pieces with rich, saturated color — higher iron concentration = more effective pigment. Test by scratching against a white or grey stone: vivid streak = good quality.

**Step 2 — Processing (grinding to powder):**

1. Select a flat sandstone or quartzite grinding stone
2. Place the ochre nodule on the grinding surface
3. Grind in circular strokes, using a pestle-shaped stone
4. Collect the powder in a hollowed stone, shell, or bark container
5. Grade of powder: coarser for hide work, finer for skin application and adhesives

**Step 3 — Applications:**

*Hide preservation:*
- Rub powdered ochre into freshly scraped hide immediately after scraping, before any drying
- Ochre inhibits bacterial colonization in the wet hide, extending the window for working it before it putrefies
- Reapply after each wet processing step

*Hafting adhesive:*
- Mix ochre powder with plant resin (ratio ~1:2 powder to resin) on a flat stone
- Warm the mixture slightly above warm coals (not flame) to improve mixing
- Apply as adhesive to join stone tool to wooden haft (see: *Hafting*)
- The ochre acts as a filler and binding agent in the resin, improving adhesion and impact resistance

*Skin application:*
- Mix powder with animal fat (ratio ~1:1) to create a paste
- Apply to exposed skin: provides weak UV protection (SPF ~2-4), moderate protection against biting insects, and visible social signaling
- Fat base prevents the powder from washing off in light rain or sweat

*Cordage preservation:*
- Rub dry powder directly into twisted plant fiber cordage
- Ochre's iron compounds inhibit fungal and bacterial degradation
- Treated cordage lasts 2-3x longer than untreated in humid conditions

## Materials
<!-- @field: materials -->

| Item | Purpose | Notes |
|------|---------|-------|
| Ochre nodule (hematite or limonite) | Raw pigment | Must be high-iron, vivid color |
| Flat sandstone grinding stone | Reduction to powder | Flat surface for even grinding |
| Pestle stone | Grinding | Fits the hand comfortably |
| Shell, hollow stone, or bark | Container for powder | Avoid porous materials |
| Animal fat | Mixing medium for skin paste | Any fat, rendered or raw |
| Plant resin | Adhesive mixing | Heated for best incorporation |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Hide preservation:** Ochre on freshly scraped hide prevents bacterial putrefaction, extending the working window from hours to days.
- **Hafting adhesive:** Ochre-resin composite adhesives are documented at Sibudu Cave (~70,000 ya) and show superior adhesion to resin alone, enabling more durable and reliable hafted tools.
- **UV and insect protection:** Ochre-fat paste on skin offers measurable (if modest) UV protection and significant insect deterrence — both relevant to daily life on the open savanna.
- **Social signaling:** Even if the earliest ochre use was purely functional, the visible redness created on skin and hides by ochre use provides a basis for the later, unambiguously symbolic uses in burial, cave painting, and body decoration.

## Day 0 — Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0 -->
You have just scraped the hide. It is wet, warm, and will start to rot within 6 hours.

Find the red rock — you know where the ochre vein is, 400 meters from camp. Bring a nodule back. Grind it against the flat stone until you have a small heap of red powder.

Rub the powder into the wet hide with your palms, working it into the surface across every scraped centimeter.

The hide will turn red. The bacteria will slow down. You now have 2 more days to work with this hide instead of 6 hours.

If you also need adhesive for that hafted tool — take the leftover powder, press it into a ball of pine resin, warm it over the coals, mix it with a stick until it flows. Apply while hot to the binding and let it cool.

The red mineral does both jobs. It has been doing both jobs for a million years.
