# Ochre Use

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->
Ochre is a family of naturally occurring iron oxide minerals, principally hematite (Fe2O3, red-brown) and goethite (FeOOH, yellow-brown), mixed with clay and sand in varying proportions. Rub a piece of hematite across a rough stone surface and it leaves a vivid red streak. Mix the resulting powder with animal fat and you have a paste that adheres to skin, wood, or hide, resists washing, and provides UV-A/UV-B protection measured at SPF 4-15 depending on the iron oxide concentration and layer thickness.

This is not face paint. The iron oxide in processed ochre provides three survival-relevant functions simultaneously. First: sun protection. Sustained equatorial UV exposure destroys folate (vitamin B9) in the blood, causing anemia, immune suppression, and neural tube defects in developing embryos. Dark skin provides baseline UV defense, but exposed hairless surfaces (lips, eyelids, scarred areas, newborn scalp) benefit measurably from applied mineral sunscreen. Second: antimicrobial action. Iron oxide inhibits the growth of Staphylococcus, Streptococcus, and other wound-infecting bacteria through direct oxidative damage to cell membranes. Ochre paste applied to cuts reduces infection rates in pre-antibiotic contexts. Third: adhesive enhancement. Powdered ochre mixed with plant gum, beeswax, or tree resin increases the tensile strength of the resulting mastic by 30-40%, probably through particle bridging that creates a composite matrix. This is critical for hafting stone tools to wooden handles.

Ochre is the oldest known processed mineral on Earth. Its collection and use precede art, language, agriculture, and metallurgy by hundreds of thousands of years.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
The oldest deliberate ochre collection comes from the Twin Rivers site, Zambia (~400,000-270,000 ya, Larry Barham 2002, *Journal of Human Evolution*). Hundreds of ochre fragments show grinding facets inconsistent with natural abrasion, indicating systematic processing. The knappers living at Twin Rivers selected specific color grades (deep red preferred over yellow or brown), which implies aesthetic or functional discrimination between iron oxide varieties.

Blombos Cave, South Africa, has produced the most spectacular early ochre evidence. Christopher Henshilwood and colleagues reported two ochre pieces from the 77,000-year-old M1 phase with abstract cross-hatch engravings on their flat surfaces (2002, *Science*). The 100,000-year-old M3 phase yielded an ochre-processing toolkit: two abalone shells containing a mixture of ochre powder, charcoal, bone, and crushed quartz sealed with a quartzite rubstone (2011, *Science*). This is not incidental contact. Someone measured and mixed components, combined them in a shell container, and ground them to a consistent paste. It is the oldest confirmed example of a multi-component chemical recipe.

At Pinnacle Point, South Africa (164,000 ya), Curtis Marean's team documented systematic ochre processing alongside the earliest known use of marine shellfish and microlithic blades (2007, *Nature*). The association suggests that ochre was embedded in a behavioral package of advanced resource exploitation and symbolic marking that characterizes modern human cognition.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Step 1 - Sourcing raw ochre:**

| Source | Color | Iron oxide content | How to identify |
|--------|-------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Hematite nodules | Deep red to purple | High (60-90% Fe2O3) | Heavy, metallic lustre, red streak on rough stone |
| Goethite nodules | Yellow to brown | Moderate (40-60% FeOOH) | Earthy, lighter than hematite, yellow streak |
| Laterite clay | Red-orange | Variable (10-40%) | Widespread in tropical soils, brick-red color |
| River gravel (iron-stained) | Rust-red | Low (5-15%) | Reddish cobbles in stream beds, soft enough to crumble |

**The streak test:** Rub the candidate piece firmly across an unglazed rough stone. The color of the powder streak - not the surface color - is the true pigment color. A rich, vivid red streak means high-quality hematite. Pale or brownish streak means low iron content. Best material leaves a streak you cannot easily wipe off with your thumb.

**Step 2 - Processing to powder:**

1. Break the raw piece into fragments 2-3 cm across by striking with a hammerstone on an anvil
2. Grind fragments on a rough sandstone slab using a smooth grinding stone. Circular motion. Apply steady, firm pressure
3. Continue grinding until the powder is fine enough to feel smooth between your fingertips when pinched. Gritty particles indicate coarse quartz inclusions that reduce pigment quality
4. Scrape the accumulated powder into a shell, bark trough, or cupped stone
5. Yield: approximately 5-10 grams of fine powder per 15 minutes of grinding from good material

**Step 3 - Mixing for application:**

| Formulation | Components | Ratio | Application |
|-------------|-----------|-------|-------------|
| Body/skin paste | Ochre powder + animal fat | 3:1 by volume | Apply directly to skin. Smooth, adhesive, waterproof |
| Wound antiseptic | Ochre powder + water | Thick paste | Pack directly into wound. Replace daily |
| Hafting mastic | Ochre powder + tree resin + beeswax | 2:1:1 | Heat resin to liquid, mix in ochre and wax, apply to joint hot |
| Surface paint (rock, hide) | Ochre powder + water or saliva | Thin paste | Apply with fingertip, chewed stick brush, or blown from mouth |

**Notes on fat sources:** Any animal fat works. Marrow fat is best: it remains semi-liquid at body temperature and does not go rancid as quickly as subcutaneous fat. Fat from around the kidneys (suet) solidifies too firmly in cool weather and must be warmed before mixing. In the absence of animal fat, plant oils from crushed seeds (marula, mongongo) are adequate but less adhesive.

| Item | Purpose | Alternatives |

| Hematite or goethite nodule | Raw pigment source | Laterite clay, iron-stained rock, red river sediment |
| Sandstone grinding slab | Processing surface | Any rough, flat stone surface |
| Smooth grinding stone (pestle) | Crushing and grinding | Dense river cobble, rounded to fit the palm |
| Animal fat or plant oil | Binder for skin paste | Marrow fat preferred. Seed oil acceptable |
| Shell or bark container | Mixing and storage vessel | Any concave, non-porous surface |
| Tree resin (acacia, pine) | Adhesive base for mastic | Beeswax can substitute partially |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **UV protection:** Applied to exposed skin, ochre paste functions as mineral sunscreen at SPF 4-15. In equatorial environments with UV index 10-12, this reduces folate photodegradation and sunburn severity measurably. Applied to infant scalps (thin hair, high UV sensitivity) and to wound margins (new skin lacks melanin), it provides real protection
- **Wound treatment:** The iron oxide in ochre paste has demonstrated antimicrobial effects against common wound pathogens in vitro. Packed into cuts, abrasions, or animal bites, it creates a physical barrier while simultaneously suppressing bacterial growth. Not equivalent to antibiotics, but significantly better than nothing
- **Adhesive reinforcement:** Ochre powder added to tree resin or beeswax increases tensile strength 30-40% and reduces brittleness. This improvement is critical for hafted tools - composite spearheads, stone-tipped arrows, and adze blades - where the stone-to-wood joint must withstand repeated impact without shattering
- **Hide treatment:** Rubbing ochre paste into a freshly scraped hide acts as a tanning agent, preserving the skin against bacterial decomposition and improving flexibility. The iron oxide reacts with collagen proteins in a mechanism similar to mineral tanning
- **Social signaling:** Red ochre applied to the body communicates group identity, status, mourning, aggression, and reproductive availability across documented ethnographic contexts. The signal function is secondary to the material functions but becomes increasingly important after 100,000 ya

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->
You cut your forearm on a sharp branch yesterday. The wound is 8 cm long, shallow, and it hurts. You cleaned it with water but the edges are pink and warm this morning. In 48 hours it will be visibly infected - red, swollen, weeping. In 5 days the infection tracks up the lymphatic vessels toward the elbow. In 10 days it reaches systemic circulation and you are in serious trouble with no antibiotics.

Find a piece of heavy red rock. It does not need to be large - a fist-sized nodule is enough. Test it: rub it across a rough stone surface. If it leaves a vivid red streak that does not brush off, it is hematite. If the streak is faint or yellow, keep looking.

Grind the hematite on a flat, rough rock. Press hard and grind in circles. Work until you have a tablespoon of fine, smooth red powder. Pinch it between your fingers - it should feel like flour, not sand.

Mix the powder with a small amount of animal fat. Marrow fat is ideal - crack a long bone, scrape the inside, and use the soft yellow fat. Mix until you have a thick paste, dark red, with the consistency of wet clay.

Clean the wound with water. Press the edges together. Pack the ochre paste into and over the wound, covering it entirely in a layer 2-3 mm thick. Press it firm. The iron oxide in the ochre will suppress the bacteria trying to colonize the wound tissue. The fat seals the surface against dirt, insects, and reinfection. It is not perfect. It is vastly better than leaving a wound open in a tropical environment.

Replace the dressing daily. Wash the old paste off with clean water, inspect the wound (is it less red? less warm? less swollen?), and apply fresh paste. If the wound is improving, continue for 5-7 days. If it is getting worse despite treatment, you have a deeper problem that ochre alone cannot solve.

Save the leftover powder. Mix it with fat to make sunscreen for your scalp and the back of your neck. Your skin protects itself from UV radiation, but the top of your head receives direct overhead sun for 6 hours a day. Ochre paste reduces UV penetration and prevents the headaches, peeling, and folate destruction that follow sustained sun exposure in equatorial latitudes.

The grinding stone with the red stain on it is your pharmacy, your sunscreen, and your adhesive factory. Keep it at camp.
