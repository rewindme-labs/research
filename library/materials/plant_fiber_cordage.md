# Plant Fiber Cordage
<!-- @field: description -->
Cordage — rope, twine, lashing material — is required for almost every complex survival task: hafting stone tools, constructing shelters, setting traps, binding materials into composite structures, and carrying loads. Plant fiber cordage, produced by twisting or braiding plant fibers without any tools beyond hands, is the simplest and most universally available cordage production method. Virtually every terrestrial ecosystem contains at least one suitable plant species.

The reverse-wrap twist is the fundamental technique: two bundles of fiber are twisted individually in one direction, then the two bundles are wrapped around each other in the opposite direction. The opposing tensions lock each other in place, creating a structure that tightens under tension rather than unwinding. This is the same mechanical principle used in all modern rope.

Cordage is so fundamental that its absence from the toolkit makes many other techniques impossible. A group that can produce cord from local plants can haft tools, build shelters, set traps, carry loads, and bind injuries. A group that cannot is limited to what hands alone can hold and carry.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
The oldest confirmed cordage fragment dates to ~90,000 years ago, from Sibudu Cave, South Africa (Wadley et al. 2020, *PLOS ONE*) — twisted plant fiber strands preserved in waterlogged sediment. However, the production of cordage almost certainly predates this by hundreds of thousands of years; the preservation conditions required for organic fiber survival are extremely rare, creating a large gap between likely origins and confirmed evidence.

Indirect evidence for early cordage includes: hafted tools (which require lashing to attach stone to handle), composite traps (documented from ~77,000 ya at Sibudu), and the transport of heavy loads over long distances (which benefits from carrying harnesses). All of these imply cordage production capability.

The oldest direct evidence of fiber production technology is the twisted flax fiber found at Dzudzuana Cave, Georgia (~30,000 ya), but plant fiber use in Africa is inferred much earlier based on the functional requirements of documented tool use.

Modern experimental studies by Dietrich Stout and colleagues have confirmed that reverse-wrap cordage can be produced from a wide variety of plant species with zero tools, at rates of 30-50 cm per minute once the technique is learned — making it a rapid, high-return material production technique.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Step 1 - Select fiber source:**

| Plant Type | Fiber Location | Preparation |
|-----------|----------------|-------------|
| Stinging nettles (*Urtica* spp.) | Inner stem (bast fiber) | Crush stem, peel outer layer, strip bast |
| Agave / sisal type | Leaf | Strip outer layer, scrape to expose fibers |
| Bark (willow, lime, cedar) | Inner bark (bast) | Peel outer bark, strip inner layers, separate |
| Dry grasses (long-stemmed varieties) | Entire stem | Soak briefly to increase flexibility |
| Sedge (*Cyperus* spp.) | Leaves | Split into fine strips, use immediately |
| Dogbane (*Apocynum* spp.) | Inner stem | Same as nettle |
| Palm leaf | Frond strips | Split lengthwise |

**Step 2 - Fiber preparation:**

1. If using fresh plant: pound the plant stem/leaf against a rock to separate fibers, then peel away the non-fibrous outer layers
2. Long fibers (30+ cm) produce the strongest cord; sort and align them
3. Dampen dry fibers briefly in water to increase flexibility and reduce breakage during twisting
4. Divide into two roughly equal bundles, each 3-6 mm in diameter (for 3-4 mm finished cord)

**Step 3 - Reverse wrap twist:**

1. Hold both bundles at one end between thumb and index finger
2. With your other hand, twist the near bundle (Bundle A) clockwise (away from you, if held horizontally)
3. While maintaining tension on Bundle A, bring Bundle B forward over Bundle A (counterclockwise relative to the cord)
4. Now Bundle B is near you — twist it clockwise, then bring Bundle A forward over it
5. Continue this rhythm: twist the near bundle clockwise, wrap it counterclockwise over the far bundle, repeat
6. Maintain consistent, firm tension — loose sections weaken the cord
7. As bundles thin out, overlap new fibers into the bundle 5-10 cm from the bundle end, staggering joins so they do not all occur at the same point

**Quality assessment:**

| Test | Method | Pass Standard |
|------|--------|--------------|
| Tensile strength | Pull straight on a 30 cm length with both hands | Should not break with full hand-pull force |
| Untwisting | Release tension and observe | Should not spring open or unravel |
| Junction strength | Pull at splice points | Same strength as main cord length |
| Diameter consistency | Visual | Even diameter throughout |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Hafting:** Lashing stone tools to wooden handles requires 50-100 cm of 3-4 mm cord per haft — enough to do 10-20 wraps plus tie-off.
- **Shelter construction:** Binding frame members together in a debris hut or lean-to requires 3-10 meters of cord of various thicknesses for a single shelter.
- **Trap construction:** A spring snare, deadfall trigger, or loop snare requires 50-150 cm of fine, strong cord per trap.
- **Load carrying:** A tumpline (head strap for carrying heavy loads) requires 1.5-2 meters of strong cord, 15-20 mm wide. Carried loads of 15-25 kg can be sustained all day with an appropriate tumpline.
- **Medical binding:** Wound closure (see: *Primitive Wound Care*) and splint binding require lengths of fine cord.

## Day 0 — Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0 -->
You need to attach a stone to a stick. You have no cord.

Find a nettle plant — the stems are full of strong fiber. Grab it with confident hands, not tentative ones. Pound the stem against a rock until it is crushed and soft. Peel away the outer green layer. Inside, you will find pale, silky fibers running the length of the stem. Strip them out. You need two bundles.

Hold both bundles at one end between your fingers. Twist the front bundle away from you — clockwise. Then wrap it over the back bundle. Now the old back bundle is in front. Twist it away from you, wrap it over. Repeat.

What you are making tightens under tension. The harder you pull, the stronger the hold.

After 30 cm, work new fibers in as each bundle thins, overlapping 5 cm. Keep the twist even.

In 10 minutes you have 1 meter of cord. That is enough to haft a stone, set a trap, or bind a broken pole.

Everything you want to build with more than two pieces requires this.
