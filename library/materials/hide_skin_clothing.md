# Hide & Skin Clothing

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

Hide clothing is the first manufactured body covering - a processed animal skin worn for thermal protection, abrasion resistance, and environmental shielding. Raw animal hide rots within days. Processing transforms it into a durable, flexible material that lasts months to years. The key is removing the epidermis (outer skin with hair follicles) or leaving it for warmth, scraping away the subcutaneous fat and membrane, and then stabilizing the collagen matrix through tanning. Brain tanning uses emulsified animal brain (every animal has enough brain to tan its own hide, roughly), which penetrates the collagen fibers and prevents them from bonding rigidly as they dry. Smoke-curing follows, depositing formaldehyde and phenol compounds that make the hide waterproof and rot-resistant. The result is soft, supple buckskin or fur garments that provide insulation (R-value 1.0-2.5 per layer) and wind protection equivalent to modern fleece.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

The earliest indirect evidence for clothing comes from genetic studies of body lice (Pediculus humanus corporis), which diverged from head lice roughly 170,000 years ago - indicating a new habitat (clothing) became available. Stone hide-scraping tools appear at sites associated with Homo erectus (500,000+ BP), though they could serve other purposes. Bone awls (for punching stitch holes in hide) appear at Blombos Cave (South Africa, 75,000 BP) and Sibudu Cave (60,000 BP). The oldest surviving leather fragment is from Areni-1 Cave (Armenia, 5,500 BP) - a complete moccasin shoe of cow leather stuffed with grass for insulation. Eyed bone needles, essential for tailored fitted clothing, appear around 40,000 BP in Europe and China, coinciding with the spread of Homo sapiens into cold northern environments. Otzi the Iceman (3300 BC, Alps) wore a complex multi-hide outfit: goat-skin leggings, sheep-skin loincloth, deer-skin coat, bear-fur cap, and cattle-hide shoes with grass insulation.

## Production Process
<!-- @field: production -->

**Overview of stages:**

| Stage | Time | Purpose |
|-------|------|---------|
| 1. Fleshing | 1-2 hours | Remove fat, meat, membrane from inner surface |
| 2. De-hairing (optional) | 1-3 days | Remove hair for smooth leather (skip for fur garments) |
| 3. Braining | 2-4 hours | Work emulsified brain into hide to soften fibers |
| 4. Wringing & stretching | 3-8 hours | Mechanical softening while drying |
| 5. Smoking | 2-4 hours | Waterproofing and rot prevention |

**Stage 1 - Fleshing:**

1. Drape the fresh hide over a smooth log (beam), inner side up.
2. Using a blunt stone scraper (round edge, not sharp), push firmly downward to remove all fat, membrane, and meat scraps.
3. Work systematically from the centre outward.
4. The hide should be uniformly thin and white when done.
5. Do not puncture the hide - use a dull tool and controlled pressure.

**Stage 2 - De-hairing (for smooth leather only):**

- **Ash soak method:** Submerge hide in water with hardwood ash (creates a weak lye solution, pH 10-12). Soak 2-3 days, checking daily. When hair pulls out easily by hand, remove from soak.
- **Scrape method:** Drape over beam, scrape hair off with a bone or stone edge. Tedious but works without chemicals.
- **For fur garments:** Skip this stage entirely. Leave hair intact.

**Stage 3 - Braining:**

1. Extract the brain from the animal skull (break open with hammerstone). One brain tans one hide of equivalent animal size.
2. Warm the brain in a small amount of water, mash to a smooth paste.
3. If no brain available: substitute 3-4 egg yolks, or liver mashed with fat, or rendered bone marrow mixed with water.
4. Work the brain paste into both sides of the damp hide, rubbing vigorously.
5. Fold hide into a bundle and let it soak for 4-12 hours.
6. Re-apply brain paste and repeat soaking once more.

**Stage 4 - Wringing and stretching:**

1. Wring the hide around a tree branch or stout stick to squeeze out excess moisture.
2. While still damp, pull and stretch the hide in all directions.
3. Work every square centimetre - pull, stretch, rub over a rope or stake edge.
4. The hide must not be allowed to dry without stretching, or it will become stiff board-like rawhide.
5. Continue for 3-8 hours until the hide is fully dry and uniformly soft.
6. This is the most labour-intensive step. It cannot be skipped.

**Stage 5 - Smoking:**

1. Build a small smouldering fire of punky (rotten) wood - birch, alder, or any hardwood that produces dense cool smoke, not flames.
2. Sew or pin the hide edges together into a bag shape, open at the bottom.
3. Suspend the bag above the smoke source, trapping smoke inside.
4. Smoke for 1-2 hours per side until the hide turns from white to golden-amber.
5. The smoke deposits formaldehyde and phenol compounds that cross-link collagen and make the hide water-resistant.
6. Smoked hide will remain soft even after getting wet and drying - unsmoked brain-tan will become stiff.

**Garment assembly (no needles):**

| Method | Tools | Strength |
|--------|-------|----------|
| Awl-and-sinew stitch | Bone awl, dried sinew thread | Strong, traditional |
| Leather lacing | Thin-cut hide strips through punched holes | Quick, repairable |
| Tied wrapping | Cordage at shoulders and waist | Fastest, least fitted |

## Properties
<!-- @field: properties -->

| Property | Brain-tanned buckskin | Rawhide (untanned) | Fur (hair-on) |
|----------|----------------------|--------------------|----|
| Flexibility | Extremely supple | Stiff, board-like | Moderate |
| Insulation (R-value per layer) | 1.0-1.5 | 0.3-0.5 | 2.0-2.5 |
| Water resistance (smoked) | Good - dries soft | Poor - warps | Moderate |
| Durability | 6-24 months heavy use | 2-5 years if dry | 6-18 months |
| Rot resistance (smoked) | Good | Poor | Moderate |
| Breathability | Excellent | Poor | Moderate |
| Weight (shirt-sized piece) | 300-500 g | 400-700 g | 500-1000 g |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Cold protection:** A fur-lined hide garment provides insulation equivalent to a modern fleece jacket. Critical for survival below 10C.
- **Wind protection:** Even uninsulated hide blocks wind completely, reducing wind-chill exposure.
- **Abrasion protection:** Protects legs and torso during bush travel, crawling, and work.
- **Foot protection:** Moccasins protect feet from sharp rocks, thorns, and parasitic infections through soil contact.
- **Containers:** Rawhide folded into parfleche containers, water bags, or quivers.
- **Cordage:** Thin-cut rawhide strips (babiche) make extremely strong lashing and binding.
- **Shelter:** Multiple hides sewn together create tent covers (tipi, yurt coverings).

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

You killed or scavenged an animal. You ate the meat. Now use the skin. Raw hide rots in 2-3 days in warm weather, so process it immediately.

Drape the hide over a smooth log, inner side facing up. Take a blunt river cobble or the edge of a large bone and scrape all the fat, meat, and translucent membrane off the inner surface. Work from the centre outward. Press firmly but do not puncture. This takes 1-2 hours for a deer-sized hide. When done, the inner surface should be uniformly white and slightly rough.

If you want leather (no hair), soak the hide in water mixed with wood ash for 2-3 days. This creates a weak alkaline solution that loosens the hair. Pull it out by hand or scrape it off. If you want fur for warmth, skip this step.

Now the critical step: braining. Crack open the animal's skull and scoop out the brain. Warm it in a little water and mash it into a smooth paste. Rub this paste into every part of both sides of the damp hide. Fold it up and leave it for several hours, then brain it again. The oils and phospholipids in the brain penetrate the collagen fibers and keep them from locking together.

After braining, wring the hide out using a stick for leverage - wrap the hide around the stick and twist hard. Now stretch. Pull in every direction. Rub the hide over a taut rope or a stake edge. Do not stop until the hide is completely dry and feels like cloth in your hands. This is exhausting work - 3 to 8 hours of continuous pulling and rubbing. If you let it dry without stretching, you get stiff rawhide, not soft leather. You cannot undo this.

Finally, smoke it. Build a small smouldering fire of rotten wood. Sew or tie the hide edges into a bag shape and hang it above the smoke on a tripod. Let cool smoke fill the bag for 1-2 hours per side. The hide should turn golden-amber. This step makes it waterproof - a brain-tanned hide that gets wet and dries without smoking will stiffen permanently.

One deer hide gives you enough material for a vest or a pair of leggings. Four to six hides give you a complete outfit. Every successful hunt should yield both calories and clothing material. Waste nothing.
