# Raw Material Sourcing

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

Raw material sourcing is the identification, evaluation, and transport of suitable stone, wood, bone, fibre, and other natural materials from geological or biological source locations to processing or habitation sites. It transforms material selection from an opportunistic activity (using whatever is at hand) into a logistical operation (knowing where the best materials are, planning procurement trips, and maintaining supply). At Koobi Fora (Kenya, 1.5 Ma), Nicholas Toth's refitting studies proved that specific lava cobbles were transported up to 13 km from identified source outcrops to butchery stations, a one-way trip requiring 2-3 hours of walking. This transportation represents a significant energy investment justified only by the superior quality of the distant material over locally available alternatives. Raw material sourcing requires geological knowledge (which rock types produce good flakes), spatial memory (where the sources are), and logistical planning (when to make procurement trips).

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

At Olduvai Gorge (Tanzania, 1.8 Ma), Mary Leakey identified preferential use of Naibor Soit quartzite for large cutting tools. The Naibor Soit inselberg lies 4 km from the main archaeological sites, and its quartzite produces larger, sharper flakes than the locally available lavas. This selective long-distance procurement demonstrates material evaluation and transport planning. By the Acheulean period (1.7 Ma onward), stone transport distances increased to 20-40 km at some sites. At Boxgrove (England, 500,000 BP), Mark Roberts documented the import of high-quality flint nodules from chalk outcrops 10 km from the site, and the refitting evidence shows that initial rough-shaping was done at the quarry to reduce transport weight, an efficiency strategy identical to modern raw material logistics. In Aboriginal Australia, Mt Isa silcrete quarries were traded across 800+ km networks for over 10,000 years.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->

**Identifying stone source types:**

| Source type | What you find | How to identify |
|------------|---------------|-----------------|
| Riverbed | Water-rounded cobbles of mixed geology | Walk streambeds, test cobbles by tapping |
| Volcanic outcrop | Dense basalt, obsidian, phonolite | Dark, heavy, glassy-surfaced rock exposures |
| Limestone bed | Chert/flint nodules within chalk/limestone | Look for dark nodules in light-coloured rock faces |
| Quartz vein | White crystalline quartz in host rock | Visible white veins cutting through bedrock |
| Surface scatter | Weathered-out cobbles from ancient deposits | Open ground with loose stone concentrations |
| Erosion gully | Mixed exposed geology | Cut-bank faces revealing embedded stone |

**Procurement trip planning:**

1. Locate the source during regular foraging or scouting. Note exact location using terrain landmarks.
2. Assess the quality on-site: tap test (ring = good), fracture test (break a small piece, check grain), and abundance (enough to justify repeat trips).
3. Plan the trip for a low-activity day when foraging parties are not needed at full strength.
4. Send 2-3 people with carrying capacity (bark trays, hide bags). Solo procurement trips to distant sites are unsafe.
5. Rough-shape materials at the quarry site. Remove cortex and shape rough blanks before transport. This reduces weight by 30-60% while preserving usable material.
6. Carry prepared blanks back to camp for final processing.
7. Stockpile raw material at camp. Maintain a cache of 10-20 cobbles or blanks to avoid emergency procurement trips when tools break.

**Evaluating a new stone source:**

| Test | Method | Pass criteria |
|------|--------|---------------|
| Tap test | Strike two pieces together | Clear ringing tone |
| Hardness | Scratch with known hard stone | Resists scratching |
| Grain | Break a piece, examine fracture | Smooth, fine-grained, conchoidal |
| Flaking | Strike a test flake | Clean detachment, sharp edge |
| Abundance | Visual survey of exposed material | Enough for 10+ procurement trips |
| Accessibility | Evaluate extraction difficulty | Surface or easily pried loose |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Tool quality:** Sourcing fine-grained stone from a distant quarry yields tools 3-5x sharper and more durable than expedient local flakes. The transport cost is recovered in superior cutting performance.
- **Supply security:** A stockpile of raw material at camp ensures tool production continues regardless of daily foraging results. No hunting day is wasted because tools broke with no replacement material.
- **Trade potential:** Groups with access to high-quality or rare stone sources (obsidian, high-grade chert) possess a trade commodity valuable to distant groups. Sourcing enables exchange networks.
- **Territorial planning:** Knowledge of raw material sources within and adjacent to your territory informs range defence priorities. Losing access to your primary stone source is a critical strategic loss.
- **Efficiency through pre-processing:** Rough-shaping blanks at the quarry (removing 30-60% of waste material) reduces transport weight, saving 100-300 kcal per procurement trip.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

Your tools break. Your flakes dull. Your hammerstone eventually cracks. Where your replacement material comes from determines whether you spend 30 minutes restocking or 3 hours desperately searching while work stops.

Find your nearest stone source. The best bet is the closest riverbed. Rivers concentrate cobbles from every geological formation upstream. Walk the riverbed and tap stones together. Collect every one that rings clearly, feels dense, and shows fine grain when you break a piece off. Fill a bark tray. Carry them back to camp.

If there is no riverbed, look for rock outcrops, erosion gullies, or exposed cliff faces. Dark, dense stone in volcanic regions. Nodules of dark material in light-coloured limestone.

Once you find a good source, remember exactly where it is. Landmark it. This location is as important as your water source and your camp because it is the supply chain for every tool you will ever make.

Do not carry whole cobbles if the source is far. Shape them at the source. Knock off the outer rind (cortex) and rough-shape the blanks. You just reduced the weight you carry by a third to half while keeping all the usable material. This is logistics, not laziness.

Build a stockpile at camp. Ten to twenty cobbles or rough blanks piled near your knapping station. When a tool breaks during butchering, you walk to the stockpile and make a replacement in 2 minutes instead of mounting a half-day procurement expedition. Every toolmaker in the world maintains a raw material cache. Do the same.

Go on procurement trips during low-demand days. Send two or three people with carrying capacity. Never one person alone to a distant quarry site. One person injured 8 km from camp with no partner is one person who does not come back.
