# Natural Adhesive

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->
A stone point tied into a split stick with plant fiber works. It works until the first hard thrust, when the impact drives the stone backward through the binding, the split widens, and the point falls out. Cordage alone cannot absorb the shear forces generated when a spear hits bone or a scraper catches on a knot. You need something that fills the gap between stone and wood, hardens into a rigid matrix, bonds to both surfaces, and distributes impact force across the entire joint rather than concentrating it at the binding wraps.

Natural adhesives do this. Tree resins, beeswax, birch bark tar, and bitumen are the raw materials. Heated, mixed with mineral powder (ochre, charcoal, sand), and applied to a joint while hot, they cool into a rigid composite mastic that holds stone to wood, bone to wood, or shell to shaft with enough strength to survive repeated use. The addition of ochre or charcoal powder to liquid resin is not decorative - it increases tensile strength by 30-40% through particle bridging, creating a composite analogous to fiberglass or reinforced concrete at a molecular level.

This is the technology that makes composite tools possible. Without adhesive, every tool is either a handheld stone or a plain wooden shaft. With adhesive, you can combine materials to produce tools that neither material can make alone: a spear with a stone tip and a wooden handle, a scraper with a bone blade and a resin grip, an arrow with a microlith edge glued into a slot.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
The oldest confirmed adhesive use is birch bark tar at the Italian Neanderthal site of Campitello, dating to ~200,000 ya (Mazza et al. 2006, *Journal of Archaeological Science*). Birch bark tar requires a multi-stage process: bark must be heated to 340-400 degrees C in a reduced-oxygen environment (condensation, not combustion) to drive off volatile terpenes and produce a black, sticky residue. The cognitive and procedural demands of this process are significant - it requires temperature control, oxygen exclusion, and an understanding of material transformation that goes beyond simple heating.

At Sibudu Cave, South Africa (~70,000 ya), Lyn Wadley and colleagues (2009, *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*) identified compound adhesives on stone tool hafts consisting of plant gum mixed with ochre and fat. Wadley's experimental replication showed that the ochre-loaded adhesive was significantly stronger than plant gum alone, and that the recipe required precise ratios: too much ochre made the mastic brittle, too little left it weak. The conclusion: Middle Stone Age toolmakers were operating as applied chemists, adjusting formulations to performance requirements.

At Umbu Cave, Klasies River, and other South African MSA sites, adhesive residues on backed tools show that compound mastics were standard technology by 70,000-60,000 ya. The geographic spread and consistency of the recipes suggest cultural transmission of adhesive knowledge across populations.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Raw adhesive materials:**

| Material | Source | Working temperature | Strength (alone) | Availability |
|----------|--------|--------------------|--------------------|-------------|
| Pine/conifer resin | Sap bleeding from bark wounds | 60-80 degrees C (softens) | Moderate - flexible but weak in tension | Widespread in temperate/montane forests |
| Acacia gum | Dried sap exudate on bark | Dissolves in water, dries hard | Low-moderate - water-soluble, brittle when dry | Savanna, semi-arid woodlands |
| Beeswax | Wild bee hives | 62-64 degrees C (melts) | Low alone - too flexible | Anywhere wild bees exist |
| Birch bark tar | Condensation distillation of bark | 340-400 degrees C production | High - strongest natural adhesive | Birch forests (temperate zones) |
| Bitumen (natural asphalt) | Surface seeps, tar pits | 50-100 degrees C (softens) | High - rigid and waterproof | Geologically specific locations |

**Making simple resin mastic (most universal method):**

1. Collect pine, spruce, or acacia resin. Look for amber-colored lumps on tree bark where branches have broken or insects have bored. Scrape with a stone flake into a shell or bark tray
2. Build a small fire. Place a flat stone at the edge of the coal bed, positioned so it receives heat but is not in the flame
3. Place the resin on the heated stone. It will soften and become liquid at 60-80 degrees C. Stir with a stick to remove bark fragments and debris
4. Add filler material while the resin is liquid:

| Filler | Ratio (filler:resin) | Effect |
|--------|---------------------|--------|
| Powdered ochre (red iron oxide) | 1:3 to 1:2 | Increases tensile strength 30-40%. Reduces brittleness |
| Charcoal powder | 1:4 to 1:3 | Increases rigidity. Makes mastic more workable when warm |
| Fine sand | 1:4 | Increases hardness. Reduces flexibility |
| Beeswax | 1:3 to 1:2 | Increases flexibility and water resistance. Lowers melting point |

5. Stir until the filler is uniformly distributed. The mixture should be the consistency of thick honey when warm. If too liquid, add more filler. If too thick, add a small amount of melted fat
6. Apply immediately to the prepared joint while still warm and workable. It sets hard within 5-10 minutes as it cools

**Applying mastic to a hafted tool:**

1. Split the working end of the shaft 5-8 cm deep using a stone flake driven by a hammerstone
2. Insert the base of the stone tool into the split, seating it firmly
3. Apply the warm mastic around the entire junction: fill the gap between stone and wood, cover the split, and build up a collar of adhesive extending 1-2 cm onto both the stone and the wood surfaces
4. While the mastic is still warm, bind the joint with wet sinew or plant fiber cordage. Wrap tightly, 8-10 turns. The cordage reinforces the mastic and the mastic fills the gaps the cordage cannot reach
5. Let the assembly cool and cure for 30-60 minutes before use. The mastic hardens as it cools. Wet sinew shrinks as it dries, tightening the binding further

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Tool hafting:** The primary application. Adhesive fills the irregular gap between a stone tool and its wooden handle, creating a rigid bond that distributes impact force. A hafted spear with a mastic-bonded stone point survives 10-20 thrusting impacts before the joint needs repair. A tied-only joint survives 2-3
- **Waterproofing:** Resin and beeswax mixtures seal bark container seams, making them watertight for carrying and storing water. Applied to the stitched seams of a hide bag or bark canoe, they prevent leakage
- **Fletching and microlith mounting:** In later periods, adhesive bonds feathers to arrow shafts and glues small stone blades into grooved bone or wood points. These applications require precise, small-scale adhesive work that cordage alone cannot achieve
- **Repair:** A cracked tool handle, a split container wall, or a failing binding can be reinforced with a smear of warm mastic. Adhesive extends tool lifespan and reduces the need for complete replacement
- **Wound closure:** Tree resin applied to a clean wound edge acts as a natural liquid bandage - antimicrobial (terpene compounds), waterproof, and mechanically sealing. Documented ethnographically in multiple cultures

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->
Your hafted spear broke. The stone point shifted backward in the split on the third thrust and fell out. The binding is intact but the gap between the stone and the wood is too wide for cordage alone to bridge. You need something to fill that gap and lock the point in place.

Find a conifer tree or an acacia with visible resin on the bark. Look for amber or reddish lumps where branches have broken or bark has been damaged. Scrape off a lump the size of your thumb using a stone flake. Collect it on a flat piece of bark.

Build a small fire. Place a flat stone the size of your palm at the edge of the coals - hot enough to melt the resin, not hot enough to burn it. Put the resin on the stone. It will soften in 30 seconds and become liquid in 2 minutes. Stir with a stick.

If you have ochre powder (from grinding red rock), add a pinch - roughly one part ochre to three parts liquid resin. Stir until uniform. This is your mastic. It looks like dark honey.

Reset the stone point in the split shaft. Seat it firmly. Using the stirring stick, drip and smear the warm mastic around the entire junction. Fill every gap between the stone and wood. Build up a collar of adhesive that covers the split and extends onto both surfaces.

While the mastic is still warm and sticky, wrap wet sinew or bark cord over it. 8-10 tight turns. The cord presses into the soft mastic and embeds itself. As the mastic cools, it locks the cord in place. As the sinew dries, it shrinks and tightens.

Wait 30 minutes. Test by striking the butt of the spear against the ground - the point should not shift. Press your thumb against the stone and try to rotate it - it should be rigid.

The joint is now a composite: stone for cutting, wood for reach, resin for bonding, cord for tension, ochre for strength. Five materials working together. That is what makes it a tool instead of a rock on a stick.
