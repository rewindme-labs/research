# Fire Starting
<!-- @field: description -->
The ability to reliably start fire from friction — rather than maintaining a fire started by lightning or natural sources — represents one of the most important capability thresholds in human prehistory. Consistent fire production eliminates the need to transport fire, eliminates the catastrophic risk of fire loss, and enables settlement of colder environments. It changes the relationship between hominins and fire from opportunistic exploitation to reliable tool use.

Two friction fire methods have the widest documented use across cultures: the hand drill (a straight spindle rotated rapidly between the palms) and the bow drill (the same spindle driven faster and with more force by a bow string). Both methods work by generating frictional heat in a wooden hearth board until a small coal (ember) forms, which is then transferred to a tinder bundle and blown into flame. The hand drill is simpler but requires more physical conditioning; the bow drill is mechanically more powerful and more accessible to beginners.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
The earliest confirmed hominin fire use dates to ~1.0-1.5 Ma at sites including Wonderwerk Cave, South Africa (Berna et al. 2012, *PNAS*) and Gesher Benot Ya'aqov, Israel (~780 ka). However, these early fires may represent exploitation of natural fires rather than deliberate production. Consistent evidence for controlled fire use is widespread by ~400,000 ya.

The question of when hominins could reliably *start* fire (rather than maintain it) is distinct and harder to resolve archaeologically. Anthropologists speculate that fire transportation — carrying an ember or slow-burning material between locations — was practiced long before reliable ignition from friction, and that the friction fire technique may have emerged as recently as 100,000-200,000 years ago.

Ethnographic documentation confirms friction fire production across virtually every indigenous culture: Hadza use the hand drill with *Cordia* wood; Aboriginal Australians use hand drill with *Acacia* spindle on *Xanthorrhoea* board; many North American cultures use bow drill with locally appropriate wood pairs. The technique is universal because it works with available materials everywhere.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Critical wood pair selection:**

| Criterion | Requirement | Test |
|-----------|-------------|------|
| Spindle wood | Dry, straight-grained, medium-hard | Thumbnail press: slight give, not soft |
| Hearth board | Same or slightly softer than spindle | Same hardness or just softer |
| Both pieces | Bone dry | Snap test — must snap cleanly, not bend |
| Grain | Straight | No spiraling grain (causes spindle to skip) |

**Proven friction fire wood pairs (East Africa):**
- Spindle: *Commiphora* spp. (myrrh trees), hearth: same species
- Spindle: *Cordia* spp., hearth: *Grewia* spp.
- Spindle: dry *Acacia* branch, hearth: *Acacia* root section

**Bow drill method:**

*Equipment preparation:*
1. **Spindle:** 30-40 cm long, 1.5-2 cm diameter. Straight-grained dry wood. Sharpen one end to a blunt point (the friction end), round the other (the top end)
2. **Hearth board:** 2-3 cm thick, flat on bottom, 30+ cm long. Cut a depression 1 cm from the edge, 5 mm deep
3. **Notch:** Cut a V-shaped notch from the edge to the center of the depression. This is where the ember forms.
4. **Bark tray or leaf:** Placed under the notch to catch the ember
5. **Bow:** A slightly curved branch 50-60 cm long, with a cord (plant fiber, rawhide) tied loosely between the ends — slack enough to wrap once around the spindle
6. **Hand stone:** A smooth, hard stone with a small depression for the spindle top, to act as a bearing

*Bow drill operation:*
1. Kneel with one knee on the hearth board, left foot near the depression (right-handed)
2. Hold the hand stone firmly against the spindle top with the left hand, wrist locked against the left shin for stability
3. Wrap the bow cord once around the spindle
4. Begin drawing the bow back and forth in long, even strokes — the spindle rotates rapidly in the hearth depression
5. Maintain consistent downward pressure through the hand stone
6. After 30-60 seconds, black powder will accumulate in the notch and begin to smoke
7. When smoke becomes sustained and thick even after stopping, an ember has formed in the notch
8. Tap the hearth board gently to drop the ember onto the bark tray. Remove the board.
9. Transfer the ember to the tinder bundle

*Tinder bundle ignition:*
1. Hold the tinder bundle cupped in both hands around the ember
2. Blow steadily, directly at the ember — not too hard (scatters tinder), not too soft (insufficient oxygen)
3. The bundle will glow, then smoke intensely, then burst into flame in 20-60 seconds of sustained blowing
4. Place immediately on prepared kindling

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Cold protection:** Fire enables survival in environments and seasons otherwise uninhabitable. The thermal advantage of sleeping near fire versus sleeping in the open at 10°C is equivalent to several layers of insulation.
- **Carnivore deterrence:** Fire maintained through the night transforms the camp from a vulnerable open sleeping site into a defended position that large carnivores avoid.
- **Cooking:** Fire dramatically expands the range of edible foods by denaturing toxic compounds, killing parasites, and improving caloric availability from starchy foods.
- **Toolmaking:** Fire-hardening wooden spear tips, heat-treating stone to improve knapping quality, and processing materials (rendering fat, producing charcoal) all require reliable fire production.

## Day 0 — Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0 -->
It is getting cold. You have no fire. You have not needed to start one from scratch before.

You need two pieces of dry wood of the same type — dead branch material, not green wood. It must snap cleanly. If it bends, find different wood.

Carve a depression in the flat piece, 1 cm from one edge. Cut a V-notch from that edge to the center of the depression. Place a piece of bark underneath the notch to catch the coal.

Make a bow from a curved branch with a fiber cord tied between the ends — not too tight. Wrap the cord once around your straight spindle. Find a smooth stone with a small pit to press the top of the spindle.

Kneel on the flat board. Press the spindle top with the stone in your left hand, wrist against your shin. Draw the bow back and forth — long, even strokes. Maintain downward pressure.

Smoke will come. When it is thick and continuous even after you stop, the coal is in the notch.

Tap the board. The coal falls on the bark. Pick it up. Nestle it into your dry grass bundle. Cup your hands around it. Blow — steady, long, direct.

The bundle will flame in your hands. Place it on your kindling.

This took 5 minutes. Tonight, you are warm.
