# Fire Transport

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->
Starting fire from scratch is hard. A bow drill takes 10-30 minutes of intense physical effort in dry conditions. In wet conditions it can take an hour, or it can fail entirely. A hand drill is worse. Flint-and-steel percussion requires specific materials. Every method demands dry tinder at the point of ignition - and tinder is the first thing destroyed by rain, dew, or humidity.

Carrying fire eliminates the problem. A live coal wrapped in slow-burning material can be transported for 6-12 hours across any terrain, in any weather, and reignited at the destination in 60 seconds with a handful of dry grass. The technology is nothing more than a smoldering bundle of punk wood, dry bark, or fungus bracket, maintained by occasional blowing and protected from wind and rain by wrapping.

The cost of fire transport is one person's hands occupied for the duration of travel. The cost of losing fire and restarting from scratch is 30-120 minutes of the entire group's time, plus the risk of total failure in adverse conditions. In groups where fire is not yet reliably startable on demand - which describes most of the early Pleistocene - fire transport is not optional. It is the difference between having fire and not having fire.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
Rhys Jones (1969, *The Geographical Magazine*) documented fire-carrying practices among Australian Aboriginal peoples of Arnhem Land. Designated carriers (typically older women or adolescents) transported smoldering bark bundles during group moves between campsites. Jones noted that fire was treated as communal property of extreme value - its loss imposed a social cost on the carrier, and groups without fire would negotiate with neighboring groups for a live coal rather than attempt friction restart.

Beth Gott (2002, *Australian Aboriginal Studies*) recorded that multiple Aboriginal groups used the bark of *Melaleuca* (paperbark) and *Eucalyptus* species as fire-transport material. The bark smolders at a rate slow enough to maintain a coal for 8-12 hours when tightly rolled, and fast enough to be blown into flame at any stop.

The behavior is inferred for much earlier periods. The Gesher Benot Ya'aqov site, Israel (~790,000 ya, Alperson-Afil and Goren-Inbar 2010, *Science*), shows evidence of fire use in specific activity zones, but no evidence of fire-making tools (no pyrite, no drill components). The most parsimonious explanation: *Homo erectus* populations at this site were maintaining and transporting fire rather than creating it de novo. The same inference applies to Wonderwerk Cave, South Africa (~1.0 Ma, Berna et al. 2012), where controlled fire is evident but fire-making evidence is absent.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Selecting transport material:**

| Material | Smolder rate | Duration | Availability |
|----------|-------------|----------|-------------|
| Punk wood (dry, spongy, rotting heartwood) | Very slow | 8-12 hours per fist-sized piece | Forest floor, dead standing trees |
| Tightly rolled bark (eucalyptus, birch, paperbark) | Slow | 6-10 hours per bundle | Living or dead trees |
| Bracket fungus (Fomes, Ganoderma) | Very slow | 10-24 hours per piece | Dead hardwood trees, stumps |
| Dried herbivore dung | Moderate | 2-4 hours per piece | Grasslands, near water sources |
| Tightly bound grass rope | Fast | 1-2 hours per section | Anywhere grass grows |

**Preparing the fire carrier:**

1. **Punk wood method:** Find a section of dead standing tree with soft, spongy heartwood. It should be dry, crumbly when pinched, and brown or reddish in color - not black (too decayed) and not hard (too fresh). Break off a fist-sized chunk. Press a live coal into the center. Blow gently until the punk catches and begins to glow. The smolder will spread slowly outward through the material
2. **Bark roll method:** Peel a strip of bark 30-50 cm long, 15-20 cm wide. Place a coal on one end. Roll the bark tightly around it, leaving the ends open for airflow. Tie the roll with a strip of vine or bark fiber at two points to prevent unrolling. The coal smolders inside the tube, consuming bark at 1-2 cm per hour
3. **Fungus bracket method:** Dry bracket fungus from the genus *Fomes* or *Ganoderma* is the longest-burning natural material. It has a dense, fibrous interior that smolders at an extraordinarily slow rate. Place a coal against the underside (the pore surface), blow until it catches, and carry. A palm-sized bracket can smolder for 12-24 hours

**Maintaining the ember during transit:**

1. Every 30-60 minutes, check the carrier by looking for a visible glow or feeling heat through the wrapping. If neither is detectable, blow gently on the exposed end. If the glow returns, the fire is alive
2. If the coal is dying (no glow after blowing), stop. Unwrap carefully. Add a pinch of fine, dry tinder directly against the fading ember and blow. Re-wrap once the new material catches
3. In rain, shield the carrier under your body, inside a folded leaf, or inside a bark tube with one end plugged. Water on the ember kills it instantly
4. In strong wind, the coal burns too fast. Shield with your body. Turn the open end downwind. Wrap tighter to restrict airflow

**Reigniting camp fire from transported ember:**

1. Prepare a tinder bundle: dry grass, shredded bark fiber, cattail fluff - a ball the size of two fists, loose and airy in the center
2. Unwrap the carrier. The coal should be glowing red or at minimum warm and smoking
3. Transfer the coal into the center of the tinder bundle. Cup the bundle in both hands
4. Blow steadily through the bottom of the bundle. Long, even breaths aimed at the coal. The tinder will begin to smoke, then glow, then burst into flame
5. Place the flaming bundle under prepared kindling. Build fire normally from there

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Camp-to-camp continuity:** A group that carries fire never needs to restart from scratch. The evening fire at the new camp is lit from the morning fire at the old camp, which was lit from the fire before that. Fire becomes a continuous thread stretching back indefinitely to the original lightning strike or friction start
- **Wet-weather insurance:** Rain extinguishes open fires and saturates tinder. A coal carried inside a bark tube or under the body stays dry when everything else is soaked. It is the backup that prevents total fire loss
- **Travel efficiency:** Groups moving across territory can light fires at rest stops, cook, warm up, and move on - all without spending 30 minutes at each stop attempting friction ignition. The coal is always ready
- **Fire distribution between groups:** A neighboring group that has lost fire can receive a live coal from a carrier rather than waiting for lightning or spending hours on friction methods. Fire-sharing is documented as a fundamental social exchange in Aboriginal Australian, San, and other forager cultures
- **Controlled burning:** Landscape management through fire (driving game, clearing brush, encouraging new growth) requires a portable ignition source. A carried ember can be applied to dry grass at selected points across a wide area

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->
It is morning. The camp fire is burning down to coals. You are leaving. The next camp is 12 km north. You will arrive by late afternoon. If you leave this fire behind, you will spend the first hour at the new camp crouching over a bow drill trying to produce friction heat while the sun sets and the temperature drops. If it rained during the walk, you will not succeed at all, and your group sleeps cold tonight.

Take the fire with you.

Find a piece of punk wood. Look on the forest floor for a dead log with soft, spongy insides - the kind that crumbles when you press your thumb into it. Brown, dry, lightweight. Break off a piece the size of your fist.

Take a coal from the fire. Not a flaming stick - a coal. A chunk of glowing hardwood ember the size of a walnut. Pick it up with two sticks or roll it out of the fire onto a flat stone.

Press the coal into the center of the punk wood. Blow gently until the punk catches - you will see a ring of orange glow spreading outward from the coal into the spongy wood. When 2-3 cm of the punk is visibly smoldering, wrap it loosely in a strip of bark. Leave the ends open. Tie with a vine in the middle.

Carry it in your non-dominant hand. Check it every 30 minutes - feel for warmth, look for a thread of smoke from the open end. If you see neither, stop and blow on the exposed end. The glow comes back.

If it rains, tuck the bundle under your arm, against your body, sheltered from the water. Water on the coal kills it. Your dry armpit saves it.

When you reach the new camp, build a tinder nest. Dry grass, shredded bark, anything fine and dry. Open the bark wrapping. The punk wood should still be glowing. Drop it into the tinder nest. Cup the nest in both hands and blow. Steady, long breaths. In 30-60 seconds, the tinder ignites. Place it under kindling. Your fire is rebuilt.

Total effort: 5 minutes of preparation this morning, 30 seconds of attention every half hour, 90 seconds of blowing tonight. Compare that to 30 minutes of aggressive bow-drilling. The math is not close.

Never leave fire behind if you can carry it. Fire is the hardest thing to make and the easiest thing to keep.
