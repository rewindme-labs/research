# Camp Fire Management
<!-- @field: description -->
Starting a fire is a discrete skill event. Managing a fire over hours, days, and across camp relocations is a continuous practice discipline. A well-managed camp fire provides warmth, light, predator deterrence, smoke for insect control, cooking heat, and ember preservation for the next fire-starting cycle — all simultaneously. A poorly managed fire causes burns, destroys camp materials, exhausts fuel reserves faster than necessary, and creates smoke hazards inside enclosed shelters.

Camp fire management includes knowing how much fuel to add and when, how to bank coals for overnight preservation, how to create a safe fire perimeter, how to control smoke direction, and how to transport live coals when moving camp. These are the skills that turn a successfully started fire into a reliable daily infrastructure asset.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
The controlled use of fire at Wonderwerk Cave, South Africa (~1 Ma, Berna et al. 2012) and Gesher Benot Ya'aqov, Israel (~790,000 ya, Alperson-Afil et al. 2007) shows fire being maintained in enclosed or semi-enclosed spaces — implying management rather than opportunistic use of natural fire. Both sites show consistent fire locations within the same habitation area, confirming repeated use of a fire site rather than incidental encounters.

Richard Wrangham's *Catching Fire: How Cooking Made Us Human* (2009) argues that regular cooking fire use drove significant changes in digestive anatomy in early *Homo* — reduced gut, reduced jaw, increased brain — beginning with *Homo ergaster* (~1.8 Ma). If correct, consistent fire management at a camp level must have been established very early in the *Homo* lineage.

Ethnographic data on fire management across forager societies shows consistent practices: banking coals overnight under ash (universal), using fire location as a social center (universal), smoke direction awareness (universal), and carrying coals or live fire brands when moving camp (documented in the Hadza, San, Aboriginal Australians, and many other groups).

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Daily fire cycle:**

| Phase | Time | Action |
|-------|------|--------|
| Morning ignition | Dawn | Uncover banked coals, add fine tinder and breath |
| Cooking fire | Morning | Moderate flames, hardwood fuel, 30-45 min sustained burn |
| Work fire | Midday | Low bed of coals — enough for tool heating and smoking but minimal flame |
| Evening fire | Dusk | Build up for warmth, predator deterrence, social gathering |
| Banking | Night | Cover with ash, no new fuel, preserve coals until morning |

**Fuel management:**

| Fuel type | Use | Notes |
|-----------|-----|-------|
| Fine tinder (dry grass, bark fiber) | Starting / rekindling | Keep dry in a protected location |
| Small dry twigs (pencil-thumb diameter) | Building up new flame | Essential transition fuel |
| Medium hardwood (wrist diameter) | Sustained cooking fire | Long burn, good coals |
| Large hardwood logs | Overnight banking | Burns down to long-lasting coals |
| Green wood | Avoid for cooking | Creates smoke, poor heat. Use only for smoke as insect deterrent |

**Coal banking (overnight preservation):**

1. Two hours before sleep, stop adding new large fuel
2. Push remaining fuel into a compact pile — smaller fire volume
3. When the flames die to coals, cover with a thick layer of dry ash (10+ cm)
4. The ash insulates the coals from air — they smolder without burning out
5. In the morning, expose the coals, add tinder directly, and blow steadily

**Fire safety:**

1. Clear a 1-meter perimeter of dry combustible material around the fire site
2. Never leave a fire unattended in high-wind conditions
3. Never build a fire directly under an overhanging branch
4. Always have a camp fire area designation — do not move the fire location casually
5. Position the fire between the shelter entrance and the open approach direction for carnivore deterrence

**Smoke management:**

1. Fire produces smoke based on fuel: wet wood = heavy smoke, dry hardwood = minimal smoke
2. Position yourself and shelter upwind of the fire — smoke follows wind
3. For insect control specifically: add damp green grass or leaves to create dense cool smoke
4. Never rely on smoke inside an enclosed shelter — carbon monoxide risk in still conditions

**Transporting fire when moving camp:**

1. Select several large live coals — palm-sized, glowing orange
2. Place in a bark or clay vessel with a layer of dry ash
3. The ash insulates and slows combustion — coals can survive 2-3 hours in transit
4. Alternatively, wrap live coals in a bundle of fresh green bark and carry
5. At new camp location, place coals on prepared tinder bed and rekindling proceeds as normal

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Reliable daily cooking:** A camp fire that is managed correctly — banked overnight, rekindled from coals each morning — means cooking heat is available within minutes every day without needing to restart from scratch via friction. The difference between having coals and not having coals in the morning is 10 minutes vs. 30-60 minutes of effort.
- **Predator deterrence:** A well-maintained overnight fire — sufficient coals to maintain visible glow and occasional flame — is effective against the approach of lions, hyenas, and leopards. Ethnographic evidence from multiple Kalahari groups confirms that camp fire presence is a primary deterrent.
- **Insect and parasite control:** Smoke from a managed fire suppresses mosquito, fly, and tick activity in the immediate area. Groups that maintain fire continuously at camp sites have lower ectoparasite loads than those without.
- **Social center:** The fire management role creates a daily structure around which other activities are organized. The person tending the fire is simultaneously the one processing food, drying materials, performing fine tool work in firelight, and maintaining awareness of the camp environment.

## Day 0 — Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0 -->
It is almost dark. The fire has been burning since morning.

Push the remaining wood into the center. Let it burn down to coals for 30 minutes — you want heat, not flame. Add one large log to the edge; it will take all night to burn slowly.

When you can no longer see flame, just deep red coals: scrape the ash from the edge of the fire pit over the top. Cover completely. 10 cm of ash. The coals will stay alive until dawn.

In the morning, pull the ash back from one edge with a stick. The coals underneath will still glow orange. Hold a pinch of dry grass above them and blow — steady, not hard. The grass will smoke, then flame. Add small twigs immediately.

Your fire from yesterday is your fire from today.

If you are moving camp: pick up two fist-sized coals with a bark strip, place them in your bark bowl with an inch of ash underneath. Carry carefully. Do not cover the top — coals need air. When you arrive, set them on prepared tinder and blow.

You carry your fire the way you carry your tools: it is yours, it belongs with you.
