# Camp Fire Management

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->
Starting a fire is a skill. Keeping one alive for days, weeks, and months is a discipline. In the forager context, fire is not an event - it is permanent infrastructure. The camp fire provides cooking heat (which doubles the caloric bioavailability of tubers and meat), water purification, predator deterrence through the night, warmth during cold periods, smoke for insect repulsion, light for after-dark tool repair and social bonding, and the psychological anchor that defines "camp" as distinct from "exposed ground."

Losing the fire is a crisis. Before friction fire-starting methods are mastered, reignition depends on finding a natural fire source (lightning strike, volcanic vent) or obtaining fire from another group - both unreliable. Even after friction fire techniques are established, starting a fire from scratch in wet conditions can take 30-60 minutes of sustained effort. A well-maintained coal bed, by contrast, can be revived from apparent death to active flame in 90 seconds with proper technique. The difference between a group that maintains fire and a group that repeatedly loses and restarts it is measured in hours of labor diverted from foraging, hunting, and tool production every week.

The core skill is not flame management. It is coal management. Flames are transient. Coals persist. A dense hardwood coal bed at 400-600 degrees C, buried under 5 cm of ash, will remain viable for 8-12 hours. The entire infrastructure of camp fire management revolves around producing, banking, protecting, and reviving this coal bed.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
The controlled use of fire is attested at Wonderwerk Cave, South Africa (~1.0 Ma, Francesco Berna et al. 2012, *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*), where micro-morphological analysis of sediments revealed burned bone and heated plant material in consistent spatial association with occupation debris. The sediment was never exposed to temperatures exceeding 700 degrees C, ruling out natural fire penetration and indicating controlled, low-temperature hearth use.

At Gesher Benot Ya'aqov, Israel (~790,000 ya), Nira Alperson-Afil and Naama Goren-Inbar (2010, *Science*) documented distinct spatial clustering of burned flint microartifacts around specific activity zones, demonstrating that *Homo erectus* or early *Homo sapiens* maintained fire in fixed locations rather than using it ephemerally.

The Qesem Cave record (400,000-200,000 ya, Israel) provides the strongest evidence for habitual, sustained hearth use. Ruth Shahack-Gross and colleagues (2014, *Journal of Archaeological Science*) identified thick deposits of wood ash concentrated in a central zone of the cave, reused over thousands of years. The hearth was not rebuilt each time - it was maintained in the same spot across hundreds or thousands of occupation events. This is the archaeological signature of fire management as a cultural institution rather than a technical event.

Ethnographic studies of Australian Aboriginal fire maintenance document the practice of carrying live coals in slow-burning bark bundles during travel between camps (Rhys Jones 1969, *The Geographical Magazine*). The coal carrier - often an older woman or adolescent - is responsible for the group's fire continuity during transit. Losing the carried coal is a significant social failure because it imposes the cost of friction restart on the entire group.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Fuel hierarchy:**

| Fuel type | Purpose | Burn characteristics |
|-----------|---------|---------------------|
| Tinder (dry grass, bark fiber, cattail fluff) | Ignition from coal or spark | Burns fast (10-20 seconds), high flame, no coals |
| Kindling (pencil-width dry sticks) | Building initial flame structure | Burns 2-5 minutes, enough heat to ignite larger wood |
| Medium fuel (wrist-width sticks) | Sustaining active flame | Burns 15-30 minutes, begins producing small coals |
| Main fuel (forearm-width hardwood) | Producing the coal bed | Burns 1-3 hours, produces dense, long-lasting coals |
| Banking fuel (dense hardwood logs, 10+ cm diameter) | Overnight coal maintenance | Burns very slowly, maintains coal bed for 6-12 hours |

**Building the daily fire (from existing coal bed):**

1. In the morning, scrape the ash layer from the top of the coal bed using a stick. Expose the coals. They should glow red or orange when air reaches them. If they do not glow but are still warm to the hand held 5 cm above, they are viable
2. Place a tight bundle of tinder directly on the exposed coals. Lean close and blow steadily across the coals into the tinder. Blow parallel to the ground, not downward. The coals need air flowing across their surface
3. When the tinder ignites (10-30 seconds if coals are good), add kindling in a tepee arrangement around and above the flame. Do not smother the tinder - leave air gaps
4. Once the kindling has caught and is burning with stable flame (1-2 minutes), add medium fuel. Lay the sticks across each other, building upward, maintaining spacing for airflow
5. When medium fuel produces its own coals (15-20 minutes), add the first piece of main fuel. Hardwood: oak, acacia, ironwood. Never softwood for the base - softwood (pine, willow) burns hot and fast but produces poor coals

**Banking the fire for overnight:**

1. After the evening cooking is done, collapse the flame structure by pushing all partially burned wood toward the center
2. Add one or two pieces of the largest, densest hardwood available. Position them parallel, close together, touching the existing coal bed
3. Cover the entire fire base with a 5-8 cm layer of wood ash. The ash is an insulator. It reduces oxygen flow (slowing combustion) while trapping heat (maintaining temperature)
4. In the morning, the ash-covered log will have burned down to a dense coal core at 300-500 degrees C. This is your restart point

**Fire safety and positioning:**

| Rule | Why |
|------|-----|
| 3-meter clearance from vegetation overhead | Rising sparks ignite dry canopy. A canopy fire above your sleeping camp is lethal |
| Shallow fire pit (10-15 cm deep) in windy conditions | Below grade reduces wind turbulence that scatters coals |
| Wind-facing reflector wall (stacked stones or green logs) | Directs heat toward sleeping area, shields flame from wind |
| Fire position upwind of sleeping area | Smoke drifts over sleepers, repelling mosquitoes and biting flies. Downwind fire means insects and no smoke benefit |
| Never build fire against or inside a living tree | Tree ignites, burns for hours, collapses unpredictably |

## Materials
<!-- @field: materials -->

| Resource | Purpose | Notes |
|----------|---------|-------|
| Hardwood (oak, acacia, ironwood, eucalyptus) | Main fuel and banking fuel | Dense wood = dense coals = longer burn time |
| Dry grass, shredded bark, cattail fluff | Tinder for reignition | Collect and keep dry in camp. A palm-sized bundle per restart |
| Wood ash | Insulating layer for banking | Produced by the fire itself. Accumulates naturally. Do not discard |
| Flat stones or green logs | Wind reflector, heat director | Arrange in a crescent behind the fire, opening toward sleeping area |
| Bark tube or green-leaf bundle | Coal transport between camps | Must be slow-burning, dense material. Replaced every 2-3 hours on the move |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Cooking:** Fire doubles the caloric bioavailability of starchy tubers (from 30-50% digestible raw to 80-95% cooked) and makes collagen-rich connective tissue in meat digestible. A group with sustained fire eats half the food for the same energy as a group eating raw
- **Water treatment:** Bringing water to a rolling boil for 1 minute kills virtually all pathogenic bacteria, protozoa, and viruses. This requires a sustained fire with a container - impossible without managed fire infrastructure
- **Predator deterrence:** Large cats, hyenas, and wild dogs avoid active fire. A ring of firelight extending 10-15 meters from the hearth defines a zone of safety for sleeping humans. Without fire, night is predator territory
- **Insect control:** Smoke from green or damp wood drives away mosquitoes (malaria, dengue) and biting flies (trypanosomiasis). In tropical environments, this is not comfort - it is disease prevention
- **Tool hardening:** Fire-hardening wooden spear points and digging sticks (5-8 minutes above coals) increases surface hardness 30-40%. Requires a stable, predictable heat source, not a flaring uncontrolled fire

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->
It is getting dark. The temperature is dropping. You found fire this morning - a smoldering stump from a lightning strike two days ago. You transferred a coal into a nest of dry grass, blew it into flame, and you have had fire all day. You cooked tubers. You boiled water. The fire is your most valuable possession and you are about to sleep for 8 hours.

If you let this fire die, you will spend the first 2 hours of tomorrow morning trying to restart it with a bow drill. That is 2 hours of food-gathering time lost. If it is raining tomorrow, you may not restart it at all.

Bank the fire now, before you are too tired to do it properly.

Push all the partially burned wood toward the center of the fire pit. Every stick, every ember, everything that still has heat in it - consolidate it. You want one concentrated mass, not a scattered ring of dying fragments.

Take the largest, densest piece of wood you have. It should be forearm-thick and from a hardwood tree - heavy, solid, no punky rot, no resinous pine. Lay it directly on the consolidated coal bed, touching the coals along its full length. If you have a second large piece, lay it parallel, 5 cm away from the first. The gap between them focuses heat.

Now cover everything - coals, logs, surrounding embers - with a thick layer of ash from the fire pit edges. Scoop it over with a flat stick or a bark slab. 5-8 cm thick. Do not be cautious with the ash. More is better. You are burying the fire in an insulating blanket that will trap its heat while starving it of just enough oxygen to slow combustion without killing it.

Go to sleep. The fire will not flame. It will not spark. It will not spread. It will sit under that ash blanket and burn with invisible slowness for the next 8-10 hours, converting that hardwood log into a bed of dense, orange coals at 400 degrees C.

In the morning, scrape the ash back with a stick. The coals will look gray and dead. Hold your hand 5 cm above them. If you feel heat, the fire is alive. Drop a fist-sized bundle of dry grass directly on the coals. Bend down and blow steadily across the surface. In 30-60 seconds, the grass will catch. Add kindling. The fire is back.

You did not restart the fire. You never let it die.
