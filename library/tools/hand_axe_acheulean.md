# Hand Axe (Acheulean Biface)

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

The hand axe is a large, bifacially flaked stone tool shaped into a symmetrical teardrop or oval form with a continuous cutting edge around most of the perimeter. It is the Swiss Army knife of the Paleolithic - used for butchering, chopping wood, scraping hides, digging roots, and cracking bone. Unlike earlier Oldowan choppers (crude cores with a few flakes removed), the Acheulean hand axe shows deliberate bilateral symmetry, controlled thinning, and planned final form. It represents the first standardized, mentally pre-conceived tool design in human history. A well-made hand axe can butcher a full antelope carcass, split a femur for marrow, chop through 5 cm of green wood, and scrape a hide clean - all without resharpening. The technology persisted for over 1.5 million years, making it the longest-lived tool design ever produced.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

The oldest known hand axes come from Konso (Ethiopia), dated to roughly 1.75 million years ago, and Kokiselei (Kenya, 1.76 Ma), associated with Homo erectus. The technology is named after Saint-Acheul (France), where the first specimens were identified in 1859 by Jacques Boucher de Perthes. By 1.0 Ma, hand axes appear across Africa, the Middle East, India, and Europe, carried by migrating Homo erectus populations. The Boxgrove site (England, 500,000 BP) preserves exceptional hand axes alongside butchered horse remains, demonstrating direct use in large-game processing. Late Acheulean hand axes (300,000-200,000 BP) from sites like Caddington (England) show the introduction of soft-hammer percussion using bone or antler, producing remarkably thin, refined specimens with feather-thin edges. The tradition fades in Africa around 200,000 BP as Middle Stone Age prepared-core technologies emerge, but persists in parts of India until roughly 125,000 BP.

## Construction
<!-- @field: construction -->

**Material selection:**

| Material | Quality | Fracture type | Availability |
|----------|---------|---------------|-------------|
| Flint/chert | Excellent | Conchoidal, predictable | Europe, Middle East |
| Obsidian | Superior edge, fragile | Conchoidal, very sharp | Volcanic regions |
| Quartzite | Good, tough | Semi-conchoidal | Global |
| Basalt | Adequate, coarse | Irregular, harder to control | Global |
| Fine-grained sandstone | Poor | Granular, crumbles | Avoid if alternatives exist |

**Step-by-step construction:**

**Stage 1 - Blank preparation (hard hammer):**

1. Select a cobble or thick flake 15-25 cm long, 10-15 cm wide.
2. Using a hammerstone (hard, round cobble, 300-500 g), strike the edge at roughly 45 degrees.
3. Remove large cortex flakes from both faces, alternating sides.
4. Goal: establish a rough biconvex cross-section with an irregular edge.
5. Remove 15-25 large flakes. This takes 5-10 minutes.

**Stage 2 - Primary shaping (hard hammer):**

1. Continue with hard hammer, now striking closer to the edge.
2. Thin the cross-section to roughly 3:1 length-to-thickness ratio.
3. Begin defining the teardrop outline - wider at the butt, tapered at the tip.
4. If a hinge fracture occurs (flake stops short, leaves a step), strike from the opposite face to remove it.
5. Remove 20-40 medium flakes. This takes 10-20 minutes.

**Stage 3 - Refinement (soft hammer):**

1. Switch to a soft hammer: dense bone (preferably leg bone of large animal) or hardwood baton.
2. Platform preparation: use the edge of a small stone to abrade a tiny flat platform on the edge where you want to strike.
3. Strike with soft hammer at a lower angle (20-30 degrees) with a pushing motion.
4. Soft hammer produces thin, flat, invasive flakes that cross further toward the centre of the piece.
5. Work around the entire perimeter, alternating faces.
6. Goal: thin, symmetrical cross-section with sharp continuous edge.
7. Remove 30-60 thinning flakes. This takes 20-40 minutes.

**Stage 4 - Edge finishing:**

1. Use the edge of a flat stone or antler tine to apply pressure-flaking along the cutting edge.
2. Push small flakes off by pressing firmly and rolling the tool against the flaking tool.
3. This creates a regular, saw-tooth-fine edge.
4. Leave the butt end slightly thicker and rounder for comfortable hand grip.

**Total production time:** 40-90 minutes for a skilled knapper. Beginners: 3-5 hours with many failures.

**Common failures and fixes:**

| Problem | Cause | Fix |
|---------|-------|-----|
| Too thick, will not thin | Striking too close to edge, steep angle | Strike further from edge, lower angle |
| Hinge fracture (step on surface) | Striking angle too steep, not enough force | Strike from opposite face to remove step |
| Breaks in half | Hidden internal flaw, or too thin already | Start over with new blank |
| Asymmetric outline | Removing too much from one side | Flip and work the underworked face |

## Operation & Maintenance
<!-- @field: operation -->

**Grip positions:**

- **Power grip:** Wrap full hand around butt end, thumb on flat face. For chopping, heavy butchering.
- **Precision grip:** Pinch between thumb and fingers near the tip. For scraping, fine cutting.
- **Palm grip:** Flat against palm, fingers curled over edge. For hide scraping.

**Resharpening:** When edges dull from use, remove a few small flakes with a hammerstone to expose fresh sharp surfaces. A well-made hand axe can be resharpened 10-20 times before becoming too small.

**Safety:** Always cut away from the body. The edge is sharper than a steel knife when fresh-knapped. A slip can cut to the bone.

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Butchering:** Primary tool for dismembering carcasses, separating joints, slicing muscles. One hand axe can process an entire large animal.
- **Woodworking:** Chopping small branches, splitting kindling, shaping digging sticks and spear shafts.
- **Hide preparation:** Scraping fat and membrane from inner surface of animal hides.
- **Digging:** Point driven into soil for root extraction, loosening clay.
- **Bone breaking:** Striking bone margins against an anvil stone to access marrow.
- **Defence:** Last-resort hand weapon. The pointed end can penetrate deeply.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

You already have crude Oldowan choppers and sharp flakes. The hand axe is the upgrade. It takes longer to make but it does everything - butcher, chop, scrape, dig, split - and it lasts through an entire carcass processing session without needing replacement.

Find the best stone within walking distance. Flint is ideal, but any fine-grained rock that breaks with smooth, shell-like fractures works. You need a piece roughly the size of your forearm, thick enough to be at least 5 cm at the centre.

Start with a heavy hammerstone. Strike the edge at 45 degrees, knocking off large flakes from alternating faces. You are creating a biconvex cross-section - rounded on both faces, thin at the edges. Work around the entire perimeter. After 20-30 flakes, you should have a rough teardrop shape - pointed at one end, wider at the other.

Now switch to a bone baton or hardwood club. Before each strike, grind a tiny flat platform on the edge with a small stone. Hit the platform at a shallow angle with a firm push-strike. The soft hammer sends thin flakes skimming across the face, thinning the piece without breaking it. This is the hardest skill to learn. Expect to break your first 5-10 attempts. Keep the fragments - they still make functional cutting flakes.

When successful, you will have a symmetrical, flat, teardrop-shaped tool with a continuous sharp edge around most of the perimeter. Leave the base end slightly thicker for grip comfort. This single tool replaces the dozen crude flakes you have been using. It will butcher, chop branch wood, scrape hides clean, and dig into hard soil. Make two - one to use, one as backup.

A skilled knapper eventually produces these in under an hour. You are not skilled yet. Allocate a full afternoon and enjoy the failures. Each broken piece teaches you about flake propagation, platform angle, and force control. This is the most important manufacturing skill of the next million years.
