# Oldowan Industry

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

The Oldowan industry is the earliest standardised stone tool tradition, characterised by the systematic reduction of cobbles through hard-hammer percussion to produce sharp-edged flakes and modified cores. Named after Olduvai Gorge (Tanzania) where Louis Leakey first described the assemblage in the 1930s, the Oldowan represents a cognitive revolution: the ability to envision a useful shape inside a raw stone, plan a sequence of strikes to extract it, and replicate the process consistently. An Oldowan toolkit is deceptively simple, typically consisting of cores (the worked stone), flakes (the sharp pieces struck off), and hammerstones (the striking tool). But experimental replication by Nicholas Toth (1985) demonstrated that producing even a basic Oldowan flake requires understanding of conchoidal fracture mechanics, platform angles, and force control that exceeds the capabilities of all non-human primates. The thin, sharp flakes are the real product: they cut meat 10x faster than teeth, slice through hide that fingernails cannot penetrate, and process plant material with precision impossible by hand alone.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

The oldest confirmed Oldowan tools, dated to 2.6 Ma, come from Gona (Ethiopia), described by Sileshi Semaw (1997). These artefacts show controlled flaking with consistent striking angles of 60-80 degrees, indicating the knappers already understood platform geometry rather than randomly bashing rocks together. However, the 2015 discovery of Lomekwian tools at West Turkana (Kenya, 3.3 Ma) by Sonia Harmand pushed the origin of intentional stone tool production back 700,000 years before Gona, though Lomekwian technology is cruder and may represent an independent earlier tradition. At Koobi Fora (Kenya, 1.5 Ma), Toth's refitting studies (reassembling flakes back onto their parent cores) revealed that knappers transported cores up to 13 km from their stone source to butchery sites, demonstrating logistical planning. At Kanjera South (Kenya, 2.0 Ma), Thomas Plummer (2009) found Oldowan flakes associated with butchered bovid remains from multiple size classes, confirming that the tools were functionally linked to systematic meat processing across a wide dietary range.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->

**Raw material selection:**

| Stone type | Quality | Where to find | Key properties |
|------------|---------|---------------|----------------|
| Basalt | Good | River cobbles, volcanic outcrops | Tough, medium-fine grain, holds edge |
| Quartzite | Good | Riverbeds, metamorphic outcrops | Hard, conchoidal fracture, durable |
| Chert/flint | Excellent | Limestone beds, river gravel | Fine grain, very sharp edges, brittle |
| Obsidian | Excellent | Volcanic regions only | Glass-like, sharpest edges possible, fragile |
| Quartz | Acceptable | Veins in bedrock, river gravel | Unpredictable fracture, but widely available |

**Testing a cobble before committing:**

1. Tap it with another stone. A ring or clear tone indicates homogeneous, knappable material. A dull thud means internal fractures or grain changes. Reject dull stones.
2. Look for a natural flat surface or angular edge. This is your striking platform. Without a platform, you cannot control the flake.
3. Size: the ideal core fits comfortably in one hand, roughly 8-15 cm diameter. Too small and you cannot grip it. Too large and you waste energy on material you do not need.

**Producing Oldowan flakes (step by step):**

1. Select a hammerstone: a dense, rounded cobble that fits in your dominant hand, 200-500 g. River-worn granite or basalt is ideal.
2. Hold the core in your non-dominant hand, resting it on your thigh or a leather pad (or folded vegetation).
3. Identify the platform: a flat or slightly angled surface near the edge of the core. The angle between the platform and the surface you want to remove the flake from should be less than 90 degrees (ideally 60-80 degrees).
4. Strike the platform decisively with the hammerstone. Hit just inside the edge (5-10 mm from the lip), not directly on the edge. The blow should travel through the platform into the body of the core.
5. Use a snapping wrist motion, not a full arm swing. Force comes from wrist acceleration, not muscle power.
6. A successful strike produces a flake with a bulb of percussion (the swelling on the inner face where the force entered), a sharp edge along the margins, and a conchoidal ripple pattern.
7. Rotate the core to expose new platforms created by the previous removal. Each flake scar creates a new surface to strike from.
8. Continue removing flakes around the core's circumference. A single cobble yields 5-15 usable flakes.

**Common errors and fixes:**

| Error | Cause | Fix |
|-------|-------|-----|
| Flake shatters into fragments | Striking too hard or internal fractures in stone | Reduce force, test stone quality first |
| Flat, thick chunk instead of thin flake | Platform angle too steep (>90 degrees) | Rotate core to find shallower angle |
| Hammerstone slides off platform | Striking too far from edge or wet surface | Strike 5-10 mm inside edge, dry surfaces |
| Hinge fracture (flake curls and stops) | Insufficient force or striking too far from edge | Increase wrist snap, move strike point closer to edge |
| Core becomes too round to knap further | All viable platforms exhausted | Discard core, begin new cobble |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Butchering:** A fresh Oldowan flake slices through animal hide in 2-3 strokes, compared to 20+ minutes of tearing with teeth and fingernails. Flakes cut tendon, separate joints, and strip meat from bone with surgical precision.
- **Marrow access:** Modified cores (choppers) crack long bones efficiently. A single heavy blow with a chopper on a long bone shaft exposes 200-400 calories of marrow per bone.
- **Plant processing:** Flakes scrape bark for fibre, cut tuber stems, split tough seed pods, and shave wooden digging sticks to a point.
- **Hide preparation:** Scraper-type flakes remove fat and connective tissue from animal skins, the first step toward making coverings for warmth or carrying.
- **Wood shaping:** Flakes carve, scrape, and sharpen wooden tools, including digging sticks and early spear shafts, creating compound technology.
- **Defence:** A sharp flake fitted into a wooden handle or gripped directly becomes a slashing weapon effective against both animals and rival groups.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

You need a sharp edge. Right now, today, before anything else. Without a cutting tool, you cannot process meat, cut cordage, scrape wood, split hide, or do half of what survival demands. Your fingernails and teeth are pathetically inadequate for these tasks. A stone flake fixes this in under a minute.

Go to the nearest riverbed or rocky area. Pick up cobbles and tap them together. Listen. A clear ringing tone means solid, knappable stone. A dull thud means cracks inside, reject it. Choose one cobble the size of your fist with at least one flat surface or angled edge. This is your core. Choose a second, rounder, denser cobble that fits comfortably in your striking hand. This is your hammerstone.

Sit down. Place the core on your thigh with a pad of folded leaves or bark to protect your leg. Hold it firmly with your non-dominant hand. Look for the angle where the flat surface meets the core's body. This edge should be less than a right angle, closer to 60 degrees if possible.

Now strike. Not with your whole arm. Snap your wrist. Hit the platform surface about a finger-width inside the edge, driving the force downward and into the body of the stone. If you do it right, a thin, sharp chip will pop off the far side. That chip is your tool. Its edges are sharper than any broken glass you have ever seen.

Pick it up carefully by the thick end (the platform end with the bulge). Run the sharp edge along a piece of wood. It shaves cleanly. Run it along a strip of bark. It slices. This edge will cut meat, split plant fibre, scrape a stick to a point, and strip hide from a carcass.

Make five or six flakes from one cobble. Keep the best three. Carry them wrapped in a leaf or bark envelope. You now have the most important survival technology in human history. Two and a half million years of your ancestors made exactly these tools. They worked then. They work now.

When a flake dulls, do not try to resharpen it. Discard it and strike a new one. Flakes are disposable. The raw material is everywhere. A fresh edge is always better than a reworked one.

Start making flakes every day. In a week your success rate will double. In a month you will feel the stone's internal structure through the hammerstone and predict exactly where the flake will detach before you strike. That is the skill that separated your ancestors from every other animal on Earth.
