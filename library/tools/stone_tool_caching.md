# Stone Tool Caching
<!-- @field: description -->
The transport and deliberate storage of stone tools and raw lithic material represents one of the earliest documented behaviors showing multi-step planning in hominins. Rather than manufacturing tools only at the moment of use and abandoning them immediately after, some early hominins carried tools across the landscape and deposited them at key locations - sleeping sites, butchery locations, water sources - for future retrieval.

Archaeological evidence of stone tool transport over distances of 10 to 90 kilometers has been identified at East African sites dating to 1.8-2.5 Ma. This behavior implies spatial memory of both the tool cache location and the raw material source, anticipation of future needs, and an understanding that the effort of transport is worth the benefit of having a tool available when needed. It is a cognitively demanding behavior that marks a significant threshold in hominin planning capacity.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
The transport of lithic raw material is one of the most precisely measurable behaviors in the archaeological record, because geologists can identify the specific rock outcrops from which individual tools were made. At Olduvai Gorge (Tanzania), Mary Leakey documented in the 1970s that some Oldowan tools found at butchery sites (Bed I, ~1.8 Ma) were made from lava or quartzite that could only have come from specific outcrops 3 to 14 kilometers away. The hominins did not simply make tools at the quarry and use them locally - they transported finished tools or raw nodules to sites where they anticipated needing them.

More striking evidence comes from FxJj50, a site in the Koobi Fora region of Kenya (~1.5 Ma), where hominins transported basalt nodules from a source 12 km distant, while local quartzite was available. This selective transport - choosing quality material over proximity - shows sophisticated evaluation of raw material properties.

The Swartkrans site in South Africa (~1.8 Ma) contains accumulations of quartzite flakes at spots that appear to have been regularly used sleeping/activity locations, suggesting deliberate maintenance of tool kits at recurring camp sites over extended periods.

## Caching Strategy
<!-- @field: procedure -->
Effective stone tool caching requires evaluation of three factors: quality of the material, weight versus utility tradeoff, and the memory or marking of cache locations.

**Raw material evaluation before transport:**

| Property | How to Test | Preferred | Reject |
|----------|-------------|-----------|--------|
| Grain size | Inspect fracture surface | Fine-grained, glassy | Coarse, granular |
| Knapping quality | Strike test piece against another stone | Conchoidal fracture, sharp edge | Crumbles, irregular fracture |
| Weight | Handle the nodule | Dense, compact | Excessively heavy for edge area |
| Weathering | Surface inspection | Fresh, unweathered cortex | Deep cracks, chalky exterior |
| Size | Visual assessment | Fist-sized to head-sized | Smaller than fist (too small to knap) |

**Transport decisions:**

1. **Finished tools** - carry 2-4 flakes or cores already prepared; heavier but ready to use
2. **Raw nodules** - carry 1-2 quality nodules; lighter per tool potential, requires knapping on arrival
3. **Combined** - carry 1 raw nodule and 2 pre-made flakes; practical field compromise

**Cache placement principles:**

1. Place caches at features you will reliably return to: sleeping rocks, waterholes, high points with visibility
2. Mark the cache location using a distinctive large stone placed on top, or a crack in a specific rock
3. Cache in shelter from rain (under rock overhang) to prevent tool scatter
4. Cache away from areas where large animals congregate (predator trails, dense bush) to reduce disturbance
5. Deposit caches in pairs at two nearby locations - if one is disturbed, the other survives

**Carrying methods:**

| Method | Capacity | Best For |
|--------|----------|----------|
| Hand carry | 1-3 nodules | Short distances under 2 km |
| Tucked under arm against body | 1-2 larger pieces | Medium distances |
| Bark tray or leaf bundle | 4-8 flakes | Finished tools, sharp edges managed |
| Animal skin wrap | 2-4 kg of material | Longer transport with sharp flakes |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Butchery site readiness:** Carrying 2-3 sharp flakes to a carcass location means butchering can begin immediately on arrival, before scavengers crowd in - the most critical time constraint in opportunistic carnivory.
- **Camp maintenance:** Maintaining a stock of ready tools at a recurring sleeping site means the group is never without cutting tools at the end of an exhausting day.
- **Dry season range extension:** Caching tools at water sources 20-50 km apart allows ranging through dry-season territory without having to carry a full toolkit, reducing the burden of long-distance travel.
- **Social tool sharing:** A cache maintained by one group member benefits all. Proto-economic behavior emerges from the practice of creating shared tool deposits.

| Weight of typical carrying load | 0.5-2 kg | Balance between utility and travel cost |
| Flakes per suitable nodule | 5-15 | Depends on core size and skill |
| Tool lifespan before re-sharpening needed | 30-60 min active use | Cutting meat and plant material |
| Cache survival rate (open ground) | Low | Need sheltered placement under rock |
| Optimal raw nodule size | 8-15 cm diameter | Manageable weight, adequate flake yield |

## Day 0 — Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0 -->
You have a good stone tool right now. You are at a carcass site, 8 kilometers from where you sleep.

Do not leave the tool here when you go. Do not throw it away when you are done.

Pick the two sharpest flakes from what you made today. Wrap them in a large leaf. Carry them back. Set them under the overhang where you sleep, in the back corner, covered by a flat stone.

Tomorrow when you need to cut something and there is no good stone nearby, they will be there.

When you find a good outcrop of dark fine-grained stone - take more than you need today. Carry 2 extra nodules. Leave one at the waterhole a day's walk east. You will need it in the dry season when there is no time to look for new stone.

The tool you cached last week is worth more than three you might find tomorrow.
