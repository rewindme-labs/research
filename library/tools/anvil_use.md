# Anvil Use

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

Anvil use is the employment of a stable flat stone as a working platform upon which to crack nuts, crush bones, perform knapping, and process tough materials by combining hammerstone and anvil into a composite tool system. This is a cognitive milestone: two objects, neither useful alone for the task, become effective in combination. A nut cannot be cracked by pressing it against the ground (insufficient hardness) or by striking it in the hand (insufficient stability). Placing the nut on a hard flat surface and striking with a hammerstone solves both problems simultaneously. Chimpanzees at Bossou (Guinea) use stone anvils with natural concavities to prevent nuts from rolling during cracking, and select flat-topped stones over rounded ones at a rate of 85% (Matsuzawa, 1994). This is material evaluation and composite tool use predating manufactured technology by millions of years.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

Tetsuro Matsuzawa's longitudinal study at Bossou (1994-2020) produced the most detailed record of primate anvil use: chimpanzees transport hammerstones up to 500 m to specific anvil sites, show preference for granite anvils with natural depressions, and occasionally stack a small stone on top of a larger anvil to create a more stable platform (meta-tool use). At Kanjera South (Kenya, 2.0 Ma), Thomas Plummer identified pitted anvil stones with wear patterns consistent with repeated nut cracking and bone percussion alongside Oldowan flakes. The Gona sites (Ethiopia, 2.6 Ma) include flat basalt blocks showing battering on upper surfaces surrounded by knapping debris, indicating that anvils served both food processing and stone tool manufacture from the earliest Oldowan period. Bipolar knapping, where a core is placed on an anvil and struck from above, is a technique that extends the usable range of stone materials by enabling reduction of small or awkwardly shaped cobbles impossible to knap freehand.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->

**Selecting an anvil stone:**

| Property | Ideal | Acceptable | Reject |
|----------|-------|------------|--------|
| Surface | Flat or slightly concave | Mostly flat with small bumps | Rounded, convex |
| Size | Larger than work item, 20-40 cm | Fits between legs when seated | Too small to be stable |
| Weight | Heavy enough not to move under impact | Requires effort to shift | Slides or rocks under impact |
| Material | Hard, dense (granite, quartzite, basalt) | Any hard rock | Soft stone (sandstone, shale) |
| Stability | Embedded in ground or self-stabilising | Can be wedged stable with smaller rocks | Wobbles, tips over |

**Anvil applications:**

| Task | Anvil setup | Hammerstone action |
|------|-------------|-------------------|
| Nut cracking | Nut centred on flat anvil | Single sharp blow, moderate force |
| Bone marrow extraction | Long bone across anvil at thinnest section | Heavy repeated blows |
| Bipolar knapping | Small core upright on anvil | Strike directly from above |
| Seed/grain grinding | Seeds on rough-surfaced anvil | Grinding stone pressed and rotated in circular motion |
| Meat tenderising | Meat flat on anvil | Repeated moderate pounding |
| Pigment grinding | Ochre/mineral chunk on flat anvil | Crushing then grinding to powder |

**Nut cracking technique (detailed):**

1. Seat yourself with the anvil between your legs or at comfortable arm's reach on a stable surface.
2. Place the nut in the centre of the anvil. If the anvil has a natural depression or concavity, place the nut there (prevents rolling).
3. Grip the hammerstone in your dominant hand. Weight 200-500 g, fitting comfortably in the palm.
4. Stabilise the nut with thumb and forefinger of non-dominant hand. Remove fingers just before striking.
5. Strike with a wrist snap, not a full arm swing. Controlled force prevents shell fragments from flying into eyes.
6. One precise blow cracks the shell. Multiple lighter blows are less effective because the nut absorbs energy by moving slightly.
7. Remove the kernel. If fragments remain in the shell, use a thin stick or thorn to extract.

**Bipolar knapping technique:**

1. Select a small cobble or quartz pebble too small for freehand knapping.
2. Place it upright on the anvil, one end touching the anvil surface.
3. Strike the top end directly downward with a heavy hammerstone.
4. The force splits the cobble between the hammerstone blow and the anvil resistance, producing sharp fragments from materials otherwise unusable.
5. Quartz, vein quartz, and small river pebbles respond well to bipolar technique when they resist conventional knapping.

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Food access:** Nuts enclosed in hard shells (mongongo, marula, palm) contain 500-700 kcal per 100g of kernel. Without anvil cracking, this caloric resource is inaccessible. A Bossou chimp cracks 17-20 nuts per hour using anvil technique.
- **Marrow extraction:** Long bone marrow (786 kcal/100g) requires the bone to be split against a hard surface. Smashing a bone against the ground wastes energy and scatters fragments. An anvil concentrates the force precisely.
- **Tool production:** Bipolar knapping extends the usable raw material range by enabling reduction of quartz, small cobbles, and irregular shapes that conventional freehand knapping cannot process.
- **Processing station:** A permanent anvil at camp serves as the food processing hub for incoming raw materials: nuts, bones, hard seeds, tubers, and pigments all processed at the same station.
- **Teaching platform:** Anvil-based tasks are visible and repeatable, making them ideal for observational learning. The learner can see hammerstone angle, strike force, and result simultaneously.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

Find a flat rock. Wide, heavy, hard, and stable. This is your anvil and it changes everything.

Place it where you spend time at camp. Between your legs when seated, on a cleared patch of ground, or on a larger rock at a comfortable working height if available. Wedge smaller stones under its edges if it wobbles. A wobbling anvil wastes energy because force goes into rocking instead of cracking.

Put a nut on it. Hold the nut with your fingertips, hammerstone in your other hand. Strike once, firmly, with a wrist snap. Not a swing from the shoulder. Control matters more than power. The shell cracks, the kernel is exposed, and you have 600 calories per 100 grams of the highest-density plant food available.

Put a long bone on it. Lay the bone across the anvil at the thinnest part of the shaft. Hit it hard. Repeatedly if needed. It splits. The marrow inside is 786 calories per 100 grams. Scoop it out with a stick or your fingers.

If you have small stones that are too rounded or too small to knap by holding in your hand, try bipolar technique. Stand the small stone upright on the anvil, one end touching. Strike the top end straight down with your heaviest hammerstone. The stone shatters between the two hard surfaces and produces sharp fragments. Some of these fragments will have usable cutting edges. This technique works on quartz that defeats every other knapping method.

Set up your anvil as a permanent station at camp. Every food item that comes in passes through the anvil: bones cracked, nuts opened, seeds crushed, tubers pounded. One person working the anvil while others forage creates a production line that maximises caloric extraction from every food source your group finds.
