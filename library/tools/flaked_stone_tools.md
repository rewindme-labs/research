# Flaked Stone Tools (Lithic Knapping)

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->
Flaked stone tools are cutting, scraping, and piercing implements produced by fracturing rock to create sharp edges  -  the oldest technology in human history and the foundation of every subsequent technological development. The technique (knapping) exploits conchoidal fracture: when a hard, homogeneous, fine-grained stone (flint, chert, obsidian, quartzite) is struck at the correct angle with sufficient force, it fractures in a predictable cone-shaped pattern, producing flakes with edges sharper than surgical steel (obsidian edges are 3-5 nanometers thick, vs. 30-50 nm for a steel scalpel blade). The knapper controls flake size, shape, and edge geometry by varying the striking angle (platform angle), the striking force, the contact point, and the type of hammer used (hard stone vs. soft antler/bone). This is NOT random rock-smashing  -  it is a precise, learned skill that represents the first evidence of abstract planning, material science, and transmitted knowledge in the hominin lineage.

**Major tool traditions (chronological):**
| Industry | Date | Type | Key tool |
|----------|------|------|----------|
| Lomekwian | 3.3 Ma | Earliest known | Crude flakes, battered cobbles |
| Oldowan | 2.6 Ma | Simple core/flake | Chopper, sharp flake |
| Acheulean | 1.7 Ma - 200 ka | Bifacial | Handaxe (teardrop, symmetrical) |
| Mousterian | 300-30 ka | Prepared core | Levallois flakes (predetermined shape) |

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
The oldest known stone tools are from Lomekwi 3, Kenya (~3.3 million years ago)  -  predating the genus *Homo* and likely made by *Kenyanthropus platyops* or *Australopithecus*. The Oldowan industry (~2.6 Ma, first identified at Olduvai Gorge, Tanzania, by Louis and Mary Leakey) represents the first standardized toolkit: *Homo habilis* systematically produced sharp flakes by striking cobbles against an anvil or with a hammerstone. These simple flakes were sufficient to butcher large carcasses, scrape hides, and process plant materials  -  enabling the dietary shift to high-protein consumption that fueled brain expansion.

The Acheulean handaxe (~1.7 Ma - 200 ka) is arguably the most successful single tool design in history  -  used virtually unchanged for over 1.5 million years across Africa, Europe, and Asia by *Homo erectus* and later *Homo heidelbergensis*. These teardrop-shaped bifacial tools were produced by reducing a core from both sides (bifacial reduction), creating a symmetrical, multi-purpose hand-held tool for cutting, chopping, digging, and scraping. The transition from Oldowan to Acheulean required a leap in cognitive planning  -  the knapper had to envision the final shape inside the raw cobble and remove material accordingly.

Levallois technology (Mousterian, ~300 ka, associated with Neanderthals and early *Homo sapiens*) represents the next cognitive revolution: the "prepared core" technique. The knapper shapes the entire top surface of a core to a specific convex geometry ("turtle-back"), then removes a single predetermined flake from the prepared surface. The resulting flake has a predictable size, shape, and edge geometry  -  essentially a pre-programmed manufacturing process. This is recursive planning: the knapper removes stone NOT to make the current tool, but to set up the stone for a FUTURE removal.

Upper Paleolithic blade industries (~50 ka) produced long, narrow blades 5-10× more cutting edge per kilogram of stone than Acheulean handaxes  -  a massive increase in material efficiency. Microliths (tiny geometric stone elements) were hafted into composite tools: arrows, sickles, and spears with replaceable stone inserts.

## Construction
<!-- @field: construction -->
**Raw materials (ranked by quality):**
| Stone | Quality | Properties | Where to find |
|-------|---------|-----------|---------------|
| Obsidian | Exceptional | Volcanic glass, sharpest possible edge (3-5 nm), brittle | Volcanic regions |
| Flint/chert | Excellent | Cryptocrystalline silica, tough, sharp, available | Chalk/limestone formations, riverbeds |
| Jasper | Good | Silica, colorful, slightly granular | River gravels |
| Quartzite | Fair | Metamorphic sandstone, tough but coarser edge | Common everywhere |
| Basalt | Fair | Fine-grained volcanic, durable | Volcanic regions |
| Quartz | Poor | Crystalline, fractures unpredictably | Very common |
| Granite, sandstone | Unsuitable | Too granular, no conchoidal fracture |  -  |

**Test:** Strike a candidate rock with another hard stone. If it fractures with a smooth, curved (conchoidal) surface and the edge is sharp enough to cut skin  -  it's suitable.

**Tools needed:**
1. **Hammerstone:** A smooth, hard, rounded river cobble, 5-10cm diameter, fits in the palm. Weight ~300-800g. Used for hard-hammer percussion.
2. **Soft hammer:** Antler tine, dense bone, hard wood baton. Removes thinner, more controlled flakes than a hammerstone.
3. **Pressure flaker:** Antler tip, copper point, bone point. Pressed (not struck) against the edge to remove tiny shaping flakes for final refinement.
4. **Knee pad:** Thick leather or folded cloth on the thigh  -  the core rests on the thigh during knapping.

**Technique 1  -  Simple flake tool (Oldowan, 5 minutes):**
1. Select a fist-sized cobble of flint/chert/quartzite. This is your "core."
2. Identify a platform  -  a flat or slightly angled surface where the hammerstone will strike.
3. Hold the core in your non-dominant hand, resting on your padded thigh, with the platform edge extending beyond your knee.
4. Strike the platform edge sharply with the hammerstone at ~60° angle, directing the blow INTO the core mass (not tangentially). The blow should land within 1cm of the edge.
5. A flake detaches  -  the platform end (striking platform, bulb of percussion) is thick; the distal end (opposite) is thin and razor-sharp.
6. The flake IS your tool. The sharp edge cuts meat, hide, cordage, and wood immediately. No further modification needed.
7. Rotate the core and repeat  -  one cobble yields 10-30 usable flakes.

**Technique 2  -  Bifacial handaxe (Acheulean, 15-30 minutes):**
1. Select a large flat cobble or thick flake as your "blank."
2. Using the hammerstone, remove large flakes from both faces alternately (bifacial reduction) to establish the rough teardrop shape. Strike at low angles (less than 60°) to remove broad, thinning flakes.
3. Switch to a soft hammer (antler) for thinning flakes  -  these are flatter and travel further across the face, creating a thinner, more symmetrical cross-section.
4. Refine the edge with pressure flaking  -  press the antler tip against the edge and lever outward to pop off tiny corrective flakes.
5. Target dimensions: 10-20cm long, 5-8cm wide, 2-3cm thick at center, edges sharp all around.

**Technique 3  -  Levallois prepared core (advanced, 20-40 minutes):**
1. Shape a disk-shaped core by removing flakes around the perimeter (like peeling an orange).
2. Shape the top surface into a convex "turtle-back" by removing radial flakes from the perimeter.
3. Prepare a striking platform on one end by removing a small flake.
4. Strike the prepared platform  -  a single, large, thin flake with a predetermined shape detaches from the prepared surface. This flake is the product; the core is either exhausted or prepared again.

## Operation & Maintenance
<!-- @field: operation -->
**Using a flake tool:**
- Hold between thumb and fingers (pinch grip) for fine cutting.
- For heavy chopping: hold at the thick (bulb) end and cut with the thin distal edge.
- **Avoid striking hard materials at steep angles**  -  the thin edge chips and shatters. Use a slicing motion, not a chopping motion, for best edge preservation.
- **Retouching:** When an edge dulls, press-flake small chips from one face (unifacial retouch) to re-expose a sharp edge. This is resharpening, not remaking.

**Safety:**
- Stone flakes are SHARP  -  fresh obsidian or flint edges will cut to the bone before you feel pain.
- Always knap over your lap (padded), never between bare legs or over bare feet.
- Wear eye protection if available  -  stone chips fly unpredictably during knapping.
- Knapping debris (debitage) is dangerously sharp  -  clear it from walking paths.

**Hafting (attaching stone to a handle):**
1. Split the end of a wooden handle/shaft.
2. Insert the stone tool's base (tang) into the split.
3. Bind tightly with cordage (sinew, plant fiber, rawhide).
4. Seal the binding with pine pitch/resin adhesive (heated pine sap + charcoal powder).
5. Result: hafted axe, hafted knife, spear point  -  dramatically more force delivery and ergonomics than hand-held stone.

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
**Butchering:** Skinning, disjointing, filleting, and defleshing large game carcasses. A single obsidian flake can butcher an entire deer.

**Woodworking:** Shaping shafts, points, handles, and structural elements. Splitting, shaving, and carving wood.

**Hide processing:** Scraping fat and membrane from hides for leather production.

**Plant processing:** Cutting cordage fibers, harvesting reed/grass, stripping bark, opening nuts and seed pods.

**Fire preparation:** Cutting and splitting kindling, scraping tinder, striking sparks (flint + iron pyrite = sparks).

**Digging:** Handaxes double as digging tools for roots, tubers, and burrowing prey.

**Weapon production:** Spear points, arrow points, knife blades  -  the cutting/piercing components of all pre-metal weapons.

## Day 0  -  Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->
Flaked stone tools are a Day 0, Hour 0 absolute priority  -  they are the FIRST technology, the one that unlocks everything else. Without a cutting edge, you cannot: butcher meat, split wood for fire, cut cordage for shelter, sharpen a digging stick, make a spear, process hide for clothing, or harvest plant fibers. A sharp stone is the difference between a naked primate and a technologically capable human.

**Hour 0 Protocol:**

1. **Find a river.** Riverbeds are nature's hardware store  -  millions of years of water erosion have tumbled, sorted, and concentrated the best knapping stones. Look for smooth, fine-grained cobbles: flint (gray/dark, waxy sheen), chert (similar to flint, often brown/red), quartzite (white/gray, sugary texture), obsidian (black volcanic glass  -  unmistakable). Test candidate rocks: strike two together. If the fracture surface is smooth and curved (conchoidal), you have a winner.

2. **Make your first tool in 2 minutes.** Pick up a fist-sized cobble. Find a flat edge. Strike it sharply with another hard rock at 60° angle, 1cm from the edge. A flake pops off. That flake is your first knife. Test it: it should slice through grass, skin, or your thumbnail with minimal pressure. If it does  -  you now have cutting capability. Everything else follows.

3. **Make 10 flakes, not 1 perfect tool.** Beginners obsess over making one beautiful handaxe and break 5 cobbles trying. Instead: produce MANY simple sharp flakes quickly. Quantity defeats quality at the survival stage. A handful of sharp flakes enables: butchering (1 flake), fire prep (1 flake), cordage cutting (1 flake), wood shaping (2 flakes), spear point (1 flake). Six flakes = six capabilities = survival toolkit.

4. **Keep a hammerstone on your body at all times.** A smooth, palm-sized river cobble in your pocket is the only manufactured tool you need to carry. With it, you can produce cutting tools from any suitable stone you encounter. The hammerstone is the mother tool  -  the tool that makes all other tools.

5. **Hafting multiplies everything.** Once you have cordage (see: cordage entry) and pitch (heated pine resin + charcoal), attach a stone blade to a wooden handle. A hand-held flake is a knife. A hafted flake is an AXE  -  it delivers 5-10× more force because the handle acts as a lever. The transition from hand-held stone to hafted stone tool is the single largest jump in tool effectiveness in human history.

Stone tools sustained human survival for 3.3 million years before metals existed. They are not a "primitive fallback"  -  they are the most proven, most reliable, most universally available technology on Earth. Learn to make them. They will never run out of ammunition, never need batteries, never rust, and never jam.
