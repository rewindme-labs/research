# Water Container

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->
Without a container, you drink where water is and go thirsty everywhere else. Every meter you walk from a water source is a meter further from your next drink. Your foraging radius is limited to a circle around the nearest spring, stream, or seep well - perhaps 3-5 km before dehydration forces you back. Your persistence hunting range is zero, because you cannot carry the 3-4 liters you need for a midday pursuit. Your camp must sit within 200 meters of water, even if better shelter, better toolstone, or better foraging exists 2 km away on a dry ridge.

A container changes the arithmetic. A single bark trough holding 3 liters extends your operational radius by 8-10 km in hot conditions. An animal stomach bag holding 5 liters allows a full-day foraging trip into waterless terrain. A set of four sealed ostrich eggshell canteens cached along a travel route transforms a 40 km crossing of dry savanna from suicidal into routine.

The container is not a convenience. It is the technology that decouples movement from water proximity. Every other expansion of hominin range - into drier habitats, across seasonal deserts, along coastlines between freshwater sources - depends on it.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
Containers made from organic materials rarely survive in the archaeological record. The oldest direct evidence of container technology is the use of marine shells as pigment containers at Blombos Cave, South Africa (~100,000 ya, Henshilwood et al. 2011, *Science*) - but these are processing vessels, not water carriers. The oldest dedicated water containers in the record are ostrich eggshell canteens from Howiesons Poort and Still Bay contexts in southern Africa (~60,000-70,000 ya), though the behavior almost certainly extends much further back.

The indirect evidence is strong. Homo erectus populations occupied Dmanisi, Georgia (~1.8 Ma) and the Levantine corridor, environments with seasonal water scarcity that would require water transport for multi-day travel between sites. The Acheulean site distributions across the Saharan and Arabian corridors from 1.0-0.5 Ma imply crossings of waterless terrain that are impossible without portable water.

Ethnographic data provides the full picture. The San of the Kalahari use ostrich eggshell canteens as their primary water storage technology, caching filled shells in buried clusters along travel routes - a water logistics system spanning hundreds of kilometers (Silberbauer 1981; Lee 1979). Australian Aboriginal peoples carry water in bark troughs, wooden bowls, and animal-skin bags across some of the driest terrain on Earth. The Hadza use gourds, bark, and animal stomachs interchangeably depending on availability.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Container types by material availability:**

| Material | Container form | Capacity | Durability | Time to make |
|----------|---------------|----------|------------|-------------|
| Bark (birch, eucalyptus, acacia) | Folded trough, sealed with resin | 1-5 liters | Days to weeks (degrades when dry) | 20-40 minutes |
| Animal stomach | Tied bag | 3-8 liters (depending on animal size) | Weeks if kept moist | 15-30 minutes |
| Ostrich eggshell | Canteen with plugged hole | 1-1.5 liters | Years if not cracked | 30-60 minutes |
| Gourd (Lagenaria siceraria) | Dried hollow gourd | 0.5-5 liters | Months to years | Grows naturally; 10 min to prepare |
| Intestine (large herbivore) | Tied tube | 1-3 liters | Days | 10-15 minutes |
| Rawhide (untanned) | Folded pouch or bag | 2-6 liters | Weeks to months | 30-60 minutes |

**Making a bark trough (fastest, most universal):**

1. Find a living tree with bark that peels in large sheets. Birch, certain eucalyptus species, and some acacias work. The bark must come off in a single piece without tearing into strips
2. Cut a rectangle of bark approximately 40 x 60 cm using a stone flake. Score the outline first, then peel inward from one edge
3. Fold the shorter sides upward to form walls 10-15 cm high. Pinch the corners together
4. Pin each corner closed with a sharpened twig pushed through both layers of folded bark, or tie with a strip of inner bark fiber
5. If tree resin is available (acacia gum, pine pitch), smear it along the seams and corner folds to waterproof. Without resin, the trough will leak slowly but hold water long enough to carry 1-2 km

**Processing an animal stomach as a water bag:**

1. Remove the stomach from the carcass intact. Cut at the esophageal junction and the pyloric sphincter
2. Turn inside out. Scrape the inner mucous lining with a stone flake - remove as much tissue as possible to reduce bacterial growth
3. Rinse with clean water (or sand-scrub if water is scarce)
4. Tie the smaller opening (pylorus) shut with a strip of sinew, hide, or bark cord. Tie tight - double knot, then fold the tied end back and bind again
5. Fill through the larger opening (esophageal end). Tie this end loosely enough that you can untie it to drink, but tight enough to prevent spilling while walking
6. Carry by looping a cord or bark strip around the tied neck and slinging over one shoulder

**Making an ostrich eggshell canteen:**

1. Locate an intact ostrich egg (abandoned nest or fresh). Empty eggs from hatched nests also work if undamaged
2. Punch a hole in one end: place the egg on soft ground, position a pointed stone tool at the pole, and tap with a hammerstone. Controlled, light taps. The hole should be 1.5-2 cm diameter - large enough to pour water in and out, small enough to plug
3. Drain the contents (eat them - one egg contains ~2,000 kcal)
4. Rinse the interior with clean water. Shake vigorously with a handful of sand inside to scour
5. Plug with a roll of grass, a wooden plug, or a wad of beeswax. The plug must seal tightly enough that the canteen can be carried inverted without leaking
6. Capacity: 1-1.5 liters per shell. Carry multiples in a net bag or padded hide sling

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Extended foraging radius:** A 3-liter container extends operational range from a 3-5 km radius (limited by return-trip hydration) to 8-15 km, tripling accessible foraging area and available resource patches
- **Persistence hunting logistics:** A midday pursuit in 40 degree C heat requires 3-5 liters of water over 3-5 hours. Without carried water, the hunter collapses before the prey does. The water container makes persistence hunting viable in open terrain away from streams
- **Camp siting flexibility:** Groups with water containers can camp on defensible high ground, near toolstone outcrops, or adjacent to productive foraging patches regardless of water proximity. Water is fetched and carried rather than lived beside
- **Water caching for travel routes:** Buried or sheltered containers at intervals along known travel routes allow crossing of waterless terrain that would otherwise be impassable. The San cache ostrich eggshell canteens at 10-15 km intervals across the Kalahari
- **Cooking:** Boiling water requires a container that can hold liquid near or over fire. Bark troughs cannot be placed in flame but work with hot-stone boiling. Stomach bags tolerate brief heat. This enables a cooking method that produces safe drinking water and extracts nutrients from bone and tough plant matter

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->
There is a tuber patch 8 km east. You know it is there because you passed through it three days ago and marked the location by a distinctive split boulder. The patch has enough food for three days of full group provisioning. But between here and there, there is no water. Eight kilometers of open grassland under direct sun. You will sweat 1.5 liters per hour walking at a moderate pace with a load. Round trip is 16 km. That is 5 hours minimum, 7.5 liters of water.

You cannot carry 7.5 liters of water in your hands.

Find a tree with bark that peels. Score a rectangle as large as you can manage with a stone flake - 40 by 60 cm at minimum. Peel it carefully from the trunk in one piece. Fold the short sides up to make walls. Pinch each corner and push a thorn or sharpened twig through both layers to hold the fold. You now have an open trough.

If you have access to acacia gum or any sticky tree resin, smear it along the seams. It will slow the leak. Without resin, the trough will weep through the fold gaps but will hold enough water for 30 minutes of walking before it drains below useful volume.

Fill the trough at the stream. Carry it balanced in both hands or cradled in the crook of one arm. Walk. Drink from it as you go. When you reach the tuber patch, dig. Fill your carrying sling with roots. Refill the trough from the seep at the base of the split boulder if one exists. If not, you are working on the water you carried.

Come back. Drink the rest on the return trip.

Tomorrow, kill something with a stomach. Clean it, tie one end, and fill it with water. Sling it over your shoulder. It holds 5 liters, does not leak, and frees both your hands. The day after that, you can go 12 km.

Every container you make extends the territory you can use. Every territory you cannot reach is food you cannot eat.
