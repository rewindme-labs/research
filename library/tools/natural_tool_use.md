# Natural Tool Use

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

Natural tool use is the selection and application of unmodified objects from the environment to achieve a task that hands alone cannot accomplish. A stick probes a termite mound. A stone cracks a nut. A leaf cups water. No shaping, no manufacturing, no alteration of the object - the user recognizes that a naturally occurring item has a property (hardness, length, sharpness, absorbency) that solves an immediate problem. This is the cognitive foundation of all technology. Tool use is observed across primates: chimpanzees at Gombe (Tanzania) fish for termites with grass stems (Goodall, 1964), capuchins in Brazil crack palm nuts with river cobbles weighing up to 1 kg (Fragaszy, 2004), and New Caledonian crows fashion hooked probes from pandanus leaves. In hominins, unmodified tool use predates manufactured tools by millions of years and remains the most accessible technology in any survival situation.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

Jane Goodall's 1960 observation of chimpanzee David Greybeard using a grass stem to extract termites at Gombe Stream (Tanzania) redefined the human-animal boundary. Louis Leakey famously responded: "Now we must redefine tool, redefine man, or accept chimpanzees as humans." At Bossou (Guinea), Tetsuro Matsuzawa documented chimpanzees selecting specific stone types for nut cracking, preferring granite hammerstones over softer laterite and choosing anvil stones with natural concavities to prevent nut roll-off (1994). At Olduvai Gorge (Tanzania), unmodified manuports (stones transported to a site but not knapped) appear alongside manufactured Oldowan tools at 1.8 Ma, suggesting that early Homo carried useful natural objects alongside manufactured ones. The 3.3 Ma Lomekwian site at West Turkana (Kenya) shows tool use at the boundary between unmodified and manufactured, with heavy cobbles used as passive hammers and anvils.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->

**Categories of natural tools:**

| Tool type | Natural object | Task solved |
|-----------|---------------|-------------|
| Hammer | Dense cobble, round hardwood knot | Cracking nuts, breaking bones, pounding |
| Probe | Straight stick, grass stem, vine | Extracting insects, testing holes, measuring depth |
| Lever | Sturdy branch, flat stone | Prying bark, lifting rocks, digging |
| Scoop | Curved bark, large leaf, shell | Carrying water, collecting small items |
| Club | Dense branch with natural weight | Defence, killing small game |
| Sponge | Moss, crumpled leaves, lichen | Absorbing water from crevices |
| Shield | Flat bark slab, large leaf | Sun shade, rain protection, carrying tray |

**Selecting a natural tool:**

1. Define the task. What physical property do you need? Hardness for cracking, length for reaching, flexibility for probing, absorption for collecting water.
2. Scan the immediate environment within 20 m. River beds provide hard cobbles. Forest floors provide sticks and bark. Grasslands provide stems.
3. Test the candidate. A hammerstone should feel dense and fit comfortably in one hand. A probe should not snap when flexed. A lever should not bend under moderate pressure.
4. If the first object fails, analyse why. Too light? Too flexible? Too short? Adjust selection criteria and try again.
5. Retain a useful tool. Carry it. Bring it back to camp. A known-good hammerstone saves 10 minutes of searching next time.

**Improvised tool combinations (no modification required):**

1. Place a nut on a flat anvil stone. Strike with a hammerstone. Two unmodified tools working together.
2. Dip a long stick into a stream to test depth before crossing. One tool, one data point that saves a life.
3. Stack flat stones to elevate food off the ground, keeping it from ants. Multiple tools, one system.
4. Jam a sturdy branch under a boulder and lever it up to check for sheltering insects, grubs, or scorpions beneath.

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Immediate food access:** A cobble hammerstone cracks bones for marrow, splits nuts, and crushes hard-shelled invertebrates. Zero manufacturing time, immediate caloric return.
- **Water collection:** A bark tray or large concave leaf scoops water from shallow pools inaccessible to cupped hands. Moss squeezed over the mouth collects dew.
- **Threat response:** A dense branch serves as a club for defence against predators or rival groups. Thrown cobbles deter mid-sized predators from 10-15 m.
- **Exploration:** A probing stick tests suspicious ground (bogs, holes, snake lairs) before committing body weight. It extends your sensory reach by 1-2 m.
- **Foundation skill:** Every manufactured tool began as a natural tool. Learning to recognise useful properties in raw materials is the prerequisite for all subsequent technology.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

Look at the ground around you. Everything you need to survive the next hour is already there. You do not need to build anything, knap anything, or invent anything. You need to pick things up and use them.

Find a rock. A hard one, round, heavy in the hand, the size of your fist. This is your hammer. It cracks bones, smashes nuts, drives pointed sticks into the ground, and defends your life if something gets too close. Carry it everywhere. Sleep with it next to your hand.

Find a stick. Straight, as long as your arm, thick enough not to snap when you push on it. This is your probe. Push it into holes before reaching in with your fingers. Scorpions, spiders, and snakes live in holes. Your stick finds them before your hand does. Use it to test mud depth at river crossings. Use it to poke into termite mounds and pull out protein.

Find a flat rock the size of a dinner plate. This is your anvil. Place nuts, bones, seeds, and shellfish on it and strike with your hammerstone. Two objects that cost you nothing just became a food processing station.

Peel a sheet of bark from a dead tree. Cup it. You now have a water container, a food tray, and a carrying vessel. Fill it with berries, grubs, or water and bring it back to camp.

Every object you pick up teaches you something about what works and what does not. That dense dark cobble cracked the bone cleanly. The pale one shattered. Remember that. The green stick bent when you pushed. The dead grey one held firm. Remember that. This is the beginning of material science and it starts this minute, with whatever is at your feet.
