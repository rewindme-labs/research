# Lithic Knapping

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->
You have teeth that cannot pierce hide, nails that cannot cut sinew, hands that cannot crack bone. Without a sharp edge, you are a 60 kg primate with no natural weapons and no way to process 90% of the calories around you. Lithic knapping solves this problem with physics: strike a fine-grained stone at the correct angle, and a fracture wave propagates through the material in a predictable cone, detaching a flake with an edge sharper than anything biology ever produced. Obsidian flakes have been measured at 3 nanometers edge width. Surgical steel sits at 200-300 nanometers. A fresh knapped flake is, at the molecular level, the sharpest cutting tool on Earth.

The technology spans the entire arc of human existence. The earliest known stone tools are 3.3 million years old, predating the genus *Homo* entirely. The most recent traditional knappers were still producing stone blades in the 20th century in parts of Ethiopia and Australia. No other technology in history has remained in continuous use for that duration. The reason is simple: the raw material is everywhere, the physics are universal, and the product is immediately functional.

A beginner can produce a usable cutting flake in under 10 minutes. An expert Oldowan knapper can reduce a cobble to 15-20 sharp flakes in less than 5 minutes, each one capable of butchering a carcass, scraping a hide, or sharpening a wooden spear. The flakes are disposable. The skill is not.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
The oldest confirmed stone tools are the Lomekwian implements from Lomekwi 3, West Turkana, Kenya (3.3 Ma), reported by Sonia Harmand and colleagues in *Nature* (2015). These are crude by later standards - heavy, irregular, produced by smashing cobbles against anvil stones - but they are unmistakably intentional. The makers were not *Homo*. They were likely *Kenyanthropus platyops* or a late australopithecine, which means that the impulse to fracture stone for a sharp edge predates our entire genus by at least 500,000 years.

The Oldowan industry, first systematically described from Gona, Ethiopia (~2.6 Ma, Sileshi Semaw, 2003, *Journal of Human Evolution*), represents the transition from opportunistic bashing to deliberate, repeatable technique. Oldowan knappers selected specific stone types from outcrops up to 10 km from their activity sites, carried them, and reduced them using freehand percussion with a hammerstone. The cognitive leap is significant: the knapper must visualize the flake inside the core before striking, hold multiple spatial variables (angle, force, platform geometry) simultaneously, and adapt in real time when strikes fail.

Mary Leakey's taxonomy of the Olduvai Gorge assemblages (1971) classified the major Oldowan tool forms: choppers (unifacially flaked cores), discoids, polyhedrons, and the flakes themselves. Nicholas Toth's experimental replication work (1985) demonstrated that the flakes, not the cores, were the primary target product in most cases - the cores were the waste, not the tools. This inverted decades of archaeological assumption.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Step 1 - Raw material selection:**

The single most important decision in knapping is the stone you start with. Bad material cannot produce good tools regardless of technique.

| Rock Type | Suitability | How to identify |
|-----------|------------|-----------------|
| Flint / Chert | Excellent | Waxy or dull surface, extremely fine grain, no visible crystals. Rings sharply when struck |
| Obsidian | Excellent | Glassy, black or dark green, conchoidal fracture visible on natural breaks |
| Quartzite | Good | Fine-grained, white to gray, heavier than it looks. Granular texture under magnification |
| Basalt | Good | Dense, dark, uniform. Common in volcanic regions. Tests well with ring |
| Granite | Poor | Visible crystals (quartz, feldspar, mica). Fractures irregularly. Reject immediately |
| Sandstone | Poor | Gritty, crumbles under impact. Cannot hold a sharp edge |
| Limestone | Variable | Only the densest, finest-grained forms work. Most are too soft |

**The ring test:** Hold a candidate stone in one hand and strike it with another dense stone. Good knapping stone produces a clear, high-pitched ring. Poor material produces a flat thud. This test takes three seconds and saves hours of frustration.

**Step 2 - Freehand percussion (basic flake production):**

1. Select a core stone between the size of a tennis ball and a grapefruit. Hold it in your non-dominant hand, resting on your palm and thigh
2. Identify a platform: a surface that meets the face you want to flake at an angle less than 90 degrees. If no angle is less than 90, create one by knocking off a small chip first
3. Position the hammerstone strike point 5-15 mm back from the platform edge. Not on the edge itself - behind it
4. Strike with a sharp, controlled blow at 60-80 degrees from the platform surface. The motion comes from the wrist, not the elbow. Follow through - do not pull back at contact
5. A successful strike produces a flake with a smooth concave ventral surface, a bulb of percussion below the impact point, and a sharp feathered edge along at least one margin
6. If the flake does not detach cleanly, rotate the core and try a different platform. Do not strike the same failed spot twice

**Diagnosing failures:**

| What happened | Why | What to change |
|--------------|-----|----------------|
| Step fracture: flat, stepped termination | Platform angle too steep (above 90 degrees) | Find or create a sharper platform angle |
| Hinge fracture: rolls over and stops | Strike too far from edge, or insufficient force | Move strike closer to edge. Hit harder |
| Shatter: core breaks into irregular chunks | Internal flaw in stone, or material is granite/sandstone | Discard. Select better material |
| Nothing detaches | Wrong angle or insufficient force | Recheck angle. Strike harder with wrist snap |

**Step 3 - Oldowan core reduction:**

1. Begin with a rounded river cobble of flint, chert, quartzite, or basalt
2. Strike the first flake from any natural platform edge on the cobble
3. The scar left by the first flake creates a new platform for the second. Strike the second flake from this platform
4. Work around the circumference of the core, each removal creating the next platform
5. Continue until the core becomes too small to hold safely, or you have enough flakes
6. The remaining core is itself a functional tool: a chopper, useful for heavy scraping or breaking bone

## Materials
<!-- @field: materials -->

| Item | Purpose | Alternatives |
|------|---------|-------------|
| Core stone (8-15 cm diameter) | Raw material for flaking | Any fine-grained stone that passes the ring test |
| Hammerstone (400-700g) | Percussion tool for striking | Dense, palm-sized river cobble. No cracks, no flat faces |
| Bark pad or folded hide | Protects the holding hand from sharp edges | Fresh leaves folded thick. Discard when cut through |
| Flat anvil stone | Supports core for bipolar percussion (optional) | Any flat, stable rock surface |

**Improvisation:** If no ideal hammerstone is available, a dense hardwood burl or a large piece of antler can serve as a soft hammer, producing thinner, more controlled flakes. If no fine-grained stone exists in the area, coarse quartzite and even dense river cobbles can produce crude but functional edges through bipolar percussion: place the core on an anvil stone and strike the top with a heavy hammerstone. The resulting fragments are irregular but often sharp enough to cut skin and sinew.

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Carcass processing:** A fresh flake cuts through hide, muscle, tendon, and cartilage as effectively as a modern steel knife. Toth's 1985 experiments showed that two Oldowan flakes and 15 minutes of work could fully deflesh and disarticulate a goat carcass. The flake edge dulls after 30-60 minutes of continuous cutting, at which point you make another one
- **Plant processing:** Cutting fibrous stalks, splitting bamboo, opening tough seed pods, scraping edible cambium from bark. The scraping action is particularly effective with the thicker dorsal edge of a flake, which resists chipping
- **Woodworking:** Shaping a digging stick, sharpening a thrusting spear, notching a throwing stick. A large flake held between thumb and forefinger functions as a drawknife, shaving wood in long controlled strokes
- **Hide preparation:** Scraping subcutaneous fat and membrane from a fresh hide requires a sharp, sturdy edge held perpendicular to the skin surface. A thick flake or the edge of the reduced core works well for this

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->
There is a dead antelope 200 meters from where you are standing. A leopard killed it at dawn and a hyena clan drove the leopard off at midday. The hyenas have eaten the organs and the face. They left 15 kg of muscle on the bones because they gorge and sleep before returning. You have approximately 90 minutes before they come back. That carcass contains 20,000 calories of protein and fat. You cannot bite through the hide. You cannot tear the tendons with your hands. You need a blade, and you need it now.

Walk to the nearest riverbed or rocky outcrop. Pick up stones. You are looking for dark, smooth, heavy, fine-grained rocks with no visible crystals. Not white. Not chalky. Not gritty. Dark and smooth and dense. Pick up two that feel heavier than they look. Hold one in each hand. Strike them together, edge to edge, sharply. Listen. You need a ring - a clear, high note. A thud means bad material. Discard it and try the next one.

When you find the ring, the larger stone is your core and the smaller is your hammerstone. Look at the core. Find a corner where two surfaces meet at a sharp angle - less than 90 degrees, like the corner of a book. Place the hammerstone 10 mm back from that edge. Strike downward and inward, firmly, with a snap of the wrist. Do not swing from the shoulder. The motion is compact: wrist and forearm. Follow through the blow.

A curved chip will detach from the core and land in your lap or on the ground beside you. Pick it up. The inside face - the concave surface with the small bump near the top - is smooth. The edge where this surface meets the outer cortex of the stone is your cutting edge. Run your thumb lightly along it without pressing. If it catches your skin, it will cut meat.

Go to the carcass. Hold the flake between your thumb and two fingers, cutting edge down. Press the edge into the hide where the shoulder meets the ribcage and draw it toward you in a firm stroke. The hide will part. Peel it back with one hand and cut the exposed muscle away from the bone in long strokes, pulling the meat as you cut.

Work fast. You do not own this carcass. The hyenas will return. Every minute you spend here is 200 grams of meat and fat stripped from bone and stuffed into your carrying sling.

When the flake dulls - you will feel it start to drag instead of slice - drop it and make another one from the same core. It takes 30 seconds. You are not manufacturing a precious tool. You are producing disposable blades from an inexhaustible supply of raw material that is lying on the ground in every riverbed on the continent.

Leave with the meat. Leave the bones for the hyenas. Take the core with you. It has more flakes in it, and the next carcass will not wait either.
