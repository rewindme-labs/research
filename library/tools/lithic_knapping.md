# Lithic Knapping
<!-- @field: description -->
Lithic knapping is the intentional fracturing of stone to produce sharp-edged flakes and shaped cores usable as cutting tools. It is the oldest continuously practiced technology in human history, spanning from the earliest Lomekwian tools (~3.3 Ma) through the Oldowan (2.6-1.7 Ma) and Acheulean (1.7-0.3 Ma) industries. A knapped stone flake is sharper than any metal blade at the microscopic edge level - obsidian flakes have been measured at 3 nanometers edge width, compared to surgical steel at 200-300 nanometers.

The fundamental physics of knapping relies on the conchoidal fracture properties of fine-grained, isotropic stones: when struck with sufficient force at a precise angle, a fracture wave propagates through the stone in a predictable cone shape, detaching a flake with a sharp ventral edge. This requires both the right raw material and the correct biomechanical technique. It is a skill requiring years of practice to master, but basic functional flakes can be produced by a beginner within hours of instruction.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
The oldest known stone tools are the Lomekwian implements from Lomekwi 3, West Turkana, Kenya (3.3 Ma), reported by Sonia Harmand et al. (2015, *Nature*). These tools predate *Homo* entirely, suggesting that *Kenyanthropus platyops* or a late *Australopithecus* species produced them. The tools include cores, flakes, and potential anvil stones - demonstrating that percussion flaking was established before the emergence of the genus *Homo*.

The subsequent Oldowan industry (first confirmed at Gona, Ethiopia, ~2.6 Ma, reported by Sileshi Semaw in 2003, *Journal of Human Evolution*) represents the first systematic, widespread stone tool industry. Oldowan toolmakers - likely early *Homo habilis* - selected appropriate raw materials from specific outcrops, transported them to activity sites, and systematically reduced cores to produce sharp flakes.

Mary Leakey's analysis of the Olduvai Gorge assemblages in the 1970s established the foundational taxonomy of Oldowan tools: choppers (unifacially flaked), discoids, spheroids, and the flakes removed from them. Experimental replication studies (Nicholas Toth, 1985) confirmed that even novice knappers using Oldowan techniques could rapidly produce effective cutting tools from appropriate raw material.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Step 1 - Raw material selection (critical):**

| Rock Type | Suitability | Identifying Features |
|-----------|------------|---------------------|
| Flint / Chert | Excellent | Dull to waxy surface, extremely fine-grained, rings when struck |
| Obsidian | Excellent | Glassy black, natural volcanic glass |
| Quartzite | Good | Granular but fine-grained, white-gray, harder than quartz |
| Basalt | Good | Dense black volcanic rock, uniform grain |
| Granite | Poor | Coarse grain causes irregular fracture |
| Sandstone | Poor | Crumbles rather than fractures conchoidally |
| Limestone | Variable | Only the finest-grained dense varieties work |

**Test a stone before committing:** Strike two stones together - the sound should ring clearly. A dull thud means poor fracture properties. Inspect the surface: it should be smooth with no visible grain texture.

**Step 2 - Freehand percussion (basic flake production):**

1. Hold the core (the larger stone) in your non-dominant hand, supported by your palm
2. Find a platform edge on the core - a surface that makes an angle of less than 90 degrees with the face you want to remove a flake from
3. Strike the platform edge 5-10 mm back from the edge with the hammerstone, at approximately 70-80 degrees from the core surface
4. The blow should be sharp, controlled, and delivered with a slightly downward and inward trajectory
5. A successful strike detaches a flake with a concave ventral face (the inside of the cone) and a bulb of percussion just below the striking point
6. If the flake does not detach fully, do not force it - rotate the core and select a new platform angle

**Reading failed strikes:**

| Problem | Cause | Fix |
|---------|-------|-----|
| Step fracture (flat, stepped edge) | Platform angle too steep (>90 degrees) | Select a sharper platform angle |
| Hinge fracture (curved, abrupt termination) | Strike too far back from edge | Move strike point closer to edge |
| No detachment | Insufficient force or incorrect angle | Increase force, recheck platform angle |
| Core shatters | Crack in raw material, or granite/limestone | Test stone first, discard cracked material |

**Step 3 - Simple core reduction (Oldowan method):**

1. Begin with a rounded cobble of suitable raw material
2. Strike along the perimeter of one face to detach a series of flakes, each creating a new platform for the next
3. Work around the core edge, maintaining a consistent striking angle
4. Continue until the core is exhausted or until you have sufficient flakes for your task
5. Both the flakes (sharp edges) and the reduced core (useful as a chopper) are functional tools

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Butchery:** A fresh knapped flake is adequate for deflesheing a large animal carcass, separating hide from muscle, and cutting through tendons and cartilage. Modern studies (Toth, 1985) showed Oldowan flakes outperform many modern metal knives for meat processing.
- **Plant processing:** Cutting fibrous plants, opening seed pods, splitting bamboo, and scraping bark all benefit from a sharp stone edge.
- **Wood working:** A stone flake can shave a wooden branch to a point (digging stick, throwing club) in minutes.
- **Hide working:** Scraping hair from a freshly removed hide requires a sharp, sturdy edge - a thick stone flake serves effectively.

## Day 0 — Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0 -->
You need to cut something and you have no blade.

Find a riverbed. Look for dark, smooth, dense stones - not granite with visible crystals, not soft white chalky rock. Pick up two that feel heavy for their size. Strike one against the other edge to edge - sharply, at an angle. Listen for a ring, not a thud.

When the ring is right, grip the core in your non-dominant hand. Find an edge that angles at less than 90 degrees - a sharp corner. Strike it 10 mm back from the edge with the other stone, firmly, at about 70 degrees downward and inward. A curved chip will pop off. The inner face of that chip is your cutting edge.

Test the edge on your forearm hair. If it shaves hair, it will cut meat and rope.

Your knife weighs 30 grams and took 2 minutes to make. Make three more and keep them.
