# Digging Stick

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->
The digging stick is a straight wooden shaft, 80-120 cm long, with one or both ends sharpened or fire-hardened to a blunt point. It extends the reach and leverage of the human hand into compacted soil to extract underground storage organs (USOs) - tubers, corms, bulbs, rhizomes - that are completely inaccessible to bare-handed excavation in hard clay or rocky ground.

The tool is absurdly simple. Any green branch of adequate diameter can be sharpened in 15 minutes with a stone flake or 5 minutes of abrasion against a rough rock. No knapping skill, no fire, no composite construction. A 6-year-old can make one. And yet the digging stick is one of the most calorically productive tools in the forager toolkit, because the food it accesses - starchy underground tubers - is among the densest energy reserves in the savanna environment: 400-800 kcal per kg of raw tuber, invisible from the surface, available year-round including the dry season when above-ground plant foods collapse.

A skilled forager with a good digging stick in productive habitat can extract 8-12 kg of USOs in a 3-4 hour morning session. That is 3,000-10,000 kcal. No other low-technology foraging method in the savanna produces that return rate on a consistent, daily basis.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
Wooden tools do not survive in the archaeological record under normal conditions. The earliest confirmed pointed wooden objects are from Kalambo Falls, Zambia (~476,000 ya, Larry Barham et al. 2023, *Nature*) and Schoningen, Germany (~400,000 ya, Hartmut Thieme 1997). These are spears and shaped sticks, not confirmed digging sticks, but functional equivalents certainly existed far earlier.

The indirect evidence is strong. Isotopic analysis of australopithecine and early *Homo* dental enamel reveals C4 signatures consistent with significant consumption of underground plant parts (Matt Sponheimer et al. 2013, *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*). Microwear on hominin teeth from 2-3 Ma shows abrasion patterns consistent with gritty, starchy foods (USOs retain soil particles even after brushing). The distribution of Oldowan sites correlates with habitats rich in USO-bearing plants. The simplest explanation: hominins were digging up tubers with sticks from the very base of the lineage.

In chimpanzees, the use of rigid sticks to excavate underground bee nests and to probe for tubers in rocky soil has been documented at multiple field sites (Crickette Sanz et al. 2004; Jill Pruetz and Paco Bertolani 2007). The tool concept does not require human-level cognition. It predates the genus *Homo* with certainty.

Ethnographic universality clinches the case. Every documented forager culture uses digging sticks: the Hadza of Tanzania, the San of the Kalahari, Australian Aboriginal peoples, Amazonian groups. The tool is older than fire, older than stone knapping, older than language. It is the baseline.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Step 1 - Selecting the blank:**

| Criterion | Target | Why |
|-----------|--------|-----|
| Wood density | Medium-hard (not softwood, not ironwood) | Softwood crushes against rock; ironwood is too brittle and heavy to carry |
| Diameter | 2.5-4 cm | Thinner flexes under load; thicker cannot be gripped firmly one-handed |
| Length | 80-120 cm | Short enough to thrust with body weight; long enough for leverage |
| Straightness | Straight in the working 30 cm | Curves in the upper shaft are tolerable. Curves in the working end misdirect force |
| Condition | Green or recently dead | Bone-dry wood snaps on the first hard thrust. Rotten wood crumbles |

**Step 2 - Shaping the point:**

*Without fire:*
1. Hold the working end against a rough sandstone rock at a 20-30 degree angle
2. Grind in a circular motion, rotating the stick 90 degrees every 8-10 strokes
3. Work to a blunt, rounded point approximately 1.5-2 cm in diameter. Not needle-sharp (splinters under impact) and not flat (cannot penetrate)
4. Time: 5-15 minutes depending on wood hardness and rock grit

*With fire (stronger, more durable):*
1. Hold the working end 3-5 cm above a bed of hot coals. Not in flame - above coals
2. Rotate slowly, one quarter turn every 10 seconds. Maintain steady heat for 5-8 minutes
3. The surface darkens and becomes aromatic. Do not let it char black - that means burning, not hardening
4. Remove while warm and scrape the toasted outer layer with a stone flake. The exposed surface beneath is case-hardened - 30-40% more resistant to splitting and compression than untreated green wood
5. Re-sharpen the point by abrasion after hardening

**Step 3 - Digging technique:**

| Substrate | Technique | Body position |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|
| Sandy soil | Insert at 45 degrees, lever toward you | Kneeling, one knee forward for leverage |
| Hard clay | Vertical thrust, pull and repeat | Standing, driving body weight through arms into stick |
| Rocky ground | Short jabbing strikes around obstruction, not sustained pressure | Crouching, two-handed grip near the point |
| Root-dense soil | Circle around the plant, multiple angles, lever inward | Kneeling, working circumference before pulling |

**Critical rules:**
1. Dig at the base of the visible plant, where the stem enters the ground. The tuber sits at the root-stem junction, not directly below the center of the leaf spread
2. After penetrating 10-15 cm, switch from thrusting to levering. Lever the soil and root laterally to break it free rather than driving straight down through the tuber (which damages it)
3. Work in a circle around the stem: 4-6 insertion points at different angles. Pry inward from each. The tuber shifts and loosens without breaking apart

| Rough sandstone rock | Abrasion for shaping point | Any coarse-grained flat stone. Quartzite, granite, laterite |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **USO extraction:** The primary use. Tubers, corms, and bulbs in compacted savanna soil are invisible from the surface and inaccessible without a lever tool. A skilled forager with a digging stick in good habitat produces 3,000-10,000 kcal in a morning - the highest sustained caloric return of any non-hunting foraging method
- **Waterhole excavation:** The same technique applied to dry riverbeds. Drive the stick into sand or clay to test for subsurface moisture, then excavate a seep well by circling and levering. The digging stick is the primary tool for dry-season water access in arid environments
- **Termite mound access:** Driving the hardened point through the surface crust of a termite mound creates an insertion channel for a fine probe used in termite fishing
- **Post holes for shelter construction:** Loosening compacted soil for upright posts in lean-to and windbreak construction, where the ground is too hard for bare-hand excavation
- **Defensive use:** A fire-hardened point can inflict significant puncture wounds at close range. Not a primary weapon, but functional in an emergency

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->
You can see the long, narrow leaves of a wild yam emerging from cracked red clay. The tuber is 30-40 cm below the surface. You have tried digging with your hands. You broke two fingernails and moved 5 cm of soil in 10 minutes. The clay is dry, compacted, and harder than your fingers.

Cut a branch from the nearest hardwood tree. Straight. Thick as two fingers together. Long as your arm plus your forearm. Trim off side branches with a stone flake or by snapping them and twisting. Find a rough rock - any rock with gritty texture. Grind one end of the branch against the rock in a circular motion, rotating the stick every few strokes. Ten minutes. You now have a blunt point.

Kneel at the base of the yam plant. Not above the leaf center - at the base, where the stem enters the ground. That is where the tuber starts. Drive the point into the clay at a 45-degree angle, pushing down with both hands and your body weight. It resists. Push harder. It breaks through.

Pull the stick toward you. The clay levers apart around the insertion point. Drive it in again, 10 cm clockwise from the first hole. Lever again. Work a circle of 4-5 holes around the stem. Each time you lever, the soil around the root loosens.

After the fourth or fifth insertion, reach into the loosened earth with your hand and feel the tuber. It is the size of your forearm. Grip it where it meets the stem and pull upward while levering with the stick from the opposite side. It comes free in a shower of clay fragments.

Brush off the dirt. Eat it raw if you have no fire - wild yam is edible uncooked, though bitter and starchy. Better cooked: roast on coals for 20 minutes until the inside is soft and the bitterness fades.

You just extracted 600 kcal of dense starch from soil that your bare hands could not penetrate. The entire tool took 10 minutes to make. If you have fire tonight, spend 5 minutes holding the point above the coals to harden it. Tomorrow's digging will go faster and the point will last a week instead of a day.

Move to the next plant. There are 30 more in this patch.
