# Digging Stick
<!-- @field: description -->
The digging stick is the most fundamental foraging tool in the human repertoire: a straight wooden shaft, 60-120 cm long, with one or both ends sharpened or fire-hardened to a robust point. It extends the reach and leverage of the human hand into the soil to extract underground food sources — tubers, corms, bulbs, roots — that would otherwise be inaccessible without prolonged bare-hand excavation in hard or compacted ground.

The digging stick requires no knapping skill, no fire (though fire improves it), and no composite construction. Any green or semi-dry branch of appropriate diameter can be deployed immediately. Yet despite its simplicity, it is one of the most calorie-unlocking tools in the forager toolkit: underground storage organs (USOs) are among the densest energy reserves in the savanna environment, and the digging stick is what makes them consistently accessible.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
Wooden digging sticks do not preserve archaeologically under normal conditions. The earliest confirmed wooden tools are the Schöningen spears (~400,000 ya, Germany), but functional analogues certainly existed far earlier. Indirect evidence of USO exploitation — the wear patterns on hominin teeth, isotope analysis suggesting starchy plant consumption, and the distribution of Oldowan sites in areas rich in USO-bearing plants — suggests digging stick use from at least 2-3 Ma.

The oldest surviving possible digging stick is a pointed wooden object from Kalambo Falls, Zambia (~476,000 ya, Barham et al. 2023). More substantially, ethnographic universality of the digging stick across all documented forager cultures makes its deep antiquity virtually certain. The Hadza of Tanzania, the San of the Kalahari, Australian Aboriginal peoples, and Amazonian foragers all use versions of the same basic tool.

In chimpanzees, the use of rigid sticks for excavation of underground bee nests and tubers in rocky soils has been documented at multiple sites (Sanz et al. 2004; Pruetz and Bertolani 2007). This confirms that the tool concept itself does not require a level of cognition above that of other great apes, strongly suggesting it was present at the very base of the hominin lineage.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Step 1 — Blank selection:**

| Criterion | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| Wood density | Medium-hard. Hardwood preferred (does not crush against rock or root) |
| Diameter | 2.5-4 cm. Thin enough to grip firmly; thick enough not to flex under load |
| Length | 80-110 cm. Long enough for downward thrust with body weight |
| Straightness | Straight along the working 30 cm of one end. Minor curves in the upper shaft acceptable |
| Condition | Green-cut or recently dead (not rotten). Bone dry = brittle under impact |

**Step 2 — Working end preparation:**

*Option A — Abrasion (no fire):*
1. Hold the working end of the stick against a rough sandstone rock
2. Grind in a circular motion, rotating the stick every 5-10 strokes
3. Work to a blunt rounded point — not needle-sharp (splinters), but concentrated into a 1.5-2 cm diameter tip
4. Time: 5-15 minutes per end

*Option B — Fire-hardening (stronger, slower):*
1. Place the working end 3-5 cm above live coals (not in flame)
2. Rotate slowly every 10-15 seconds for 5-10 minutes
3. The wood surface will darken slightly and become aromatic
4. Remove. While still warm, scrape the charred outer layer with a stone flake — this reveals a hardened, dense surface beneath
5. The hardened end is 30-40% more impact-resistant than untreated green wood

**Step 3 — Use technique:**

| Substrate | Technique | Body position |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|
| Loose sandy soil | Insert at 45°, lever toward you | Kneeling, one knee forward |
| Compacted clay | Perpendicular downward thrust, repeat | Standing, using body weight |
| Rocky ground or roots | Short sharp strikes at the obstruction, not sustained pressure | Crouching, two-handed grip |
| Termite mound | Perpendicular punch strokes to penetrate surface | Standing, overhead |

**General rules:**
1. Always thrust downward at the base of the plant stem, not in the center of the visible growth — the USO is at the root-stem junction, not directly below the leaves
2. After initial penetration (10-15 cm), switch to a levering motion to pop the USO loose rather than driving down through it
3. Work in a circle around the plant rather than one straight hole — multiple approach angles free a large root structure

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **USO extraction:** Tubers, corms, bulbs, and deep roots in compacted savanna soil are inaccessible without a digging stick or equivalent. A skilled forager with a good stick can extract 8-12 kg of USOs in a morning's work — a caloric return unavailable by any other low-technology method.
- **Waterhole digging:** The same stick used for tuber extraction can drive into dry riverbeds to test substrate moisture and begin excavation of subsurface water sources.
- **Termite access:** Driving the point through a termite mound surface creates an insertion channel for the fine extraction probe used in termite fishing.
- **Shelter construction:** The digging stick can drive post holes for lean-to shelters and windbreaks where the soil is too hard for hand excavation.
- **Defensive last resort:** A fire-hardened point can inflict significant injury at close range. Not a weapon of choice, but a backup.

## Day 0 — Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0 -->
You can see the leaves of the wild yam coming up through the grass. The tuber is 40 cm below the surface in hard clay.

Cut a branch as thick as two fingers together. Straight as you can find. About as long as your arm plus your forearm. Find a rough rock and grind one end to a blunt point in a circle — 10 minutes, rotating steadily.

Kneel at the base of the plant — not above the center, but where the stem meets the ground. Drive the point into the soil at a 45-degree angle away from you. It will resist. Apply body weight. It breaks through.

Work in a circle around the stem, 6-8 insertions. Then lever inward from each insertion point. The tuber will shift.

Reach in and pull.

If you have fire, spend 8 minutes holding the tip over the coals, rotating. Scrape the char off. That tip will last 10 times longer.

One stick, made in 20 minutes, gives you access to food that was completely invisible from the surface.
