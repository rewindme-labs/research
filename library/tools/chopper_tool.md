# Chopper Tool
<!-- @field: description -->
The chopper is the simplest deliberately shaped stone tool - a rounded cobble or nodule from which a series of flakes has been removed from one face to create a sinuous, sharp edge at one end. Unlike a flake (which is a byproduct of knapping), the chopper is the core itself, shaped intentionally for use. It is heavier and more robust than any flake, making it the tool of choice for tasks requiring sustained force: chopping through thick bone, splitting green wood, breaking open hard-shelled nuts, and rough-cutting woody plant stems.

The chopper is the defining tool of the Oldowan industry (~2.6-1.7 Ma) and appears in the hands of early *Homo habilis* across East and South Africa. Its production requires fewer cognitive steps than a hand axe but more intentionality than simple flake use: the maker must select a suitable cobble, identify a working face, and deliver a series of strikes in the correct sequence to produce a functional edge without shattering the core.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
Mary Leakey's systematic classification of Olduvai Gorge lithics (published in *Olduvai Gorge*, 1971) established the chopper as the typological cornerstone of the Oldowan industry. She distinguished between unifacial choppers (flakes removed from one face only), bifacial choppers (flakes from both faces, creating a more acute edge), and polyhedrons (multiple worked faces). The assemblages from Bed I, Olduvai (~1.8-1.9 Ma) and Koobi Fora (~1.9 Ma) show that choppers were manufactured from local lava cobbles collected from stream channels.

Experimental replication by Nicholas Toth (1985, *Journal of Human Evolution*) demonstrated that an experienced knapper can produce a functional chopper from a suitable cobble in 30-90 seconds. Toth also confirmed through use-wear analysis that Oldowan choppers show edge damage consistent with wood-chopping and bone-breaking, not merely butchering.

The oldest tools attributed to the Oldowan - from Gona, Ethiopia, at ~2.6 Ma (Semaw et al. 2003) - include both choppers and flakes, suggesting that both tool types were intentionally produced from the beginning of the industry.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Cobble selection:**

| Criterion | Ideal | Reject |
|-----------|-------|--------|
| Rock type | Basalt, quartzite, quartzite cobble | Granite, sandstone |
| Size | Fist to two-fist size (400-900g) | Too large to grip; too small to produce edge |
| Shape | Rounded, no existing cracks | Already fractured |
| Test (ring test) | Rings when struck against another stone | Dull thud = internal cracks |

**Production sequence (unifacial chopper):**

1. Hold the cobble in your non-dominant hand, with the face you want to work facing up
2. Select a natural platform on the cobble edge - a surface angling at less than 90 degrees to the working face
3. Strike the platform with a hammerstone, removing a flake. The scar becomes a new platform for the next strike.
4. Strike adjacent to the first scar, using the flake scar as a new platform - work along the edge
5. After 4-8 strikes, a sinuous, sharp edge should develop along one end of the cobble
6. The opposite end remains smooth for grip

**Using the chopper:**

| Task | Technique | Edge Use |
|------|-----------|---------|
| Chopping green wood | Downward swinging blow | Full edge impact |
| Splitting bone (not for marrow - use hammerstone) | Prying and levering | Insert edge into crack and twist |
| Nut cracking | Not a chopper task | Use hammerstone on anvil instead |
| Rough cutting fibrous stems | Repeated hacking blows | Working edge |
| Digging (in hard ground) | Driving point-end into soil | Edge and point combined |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Wood processing:** Chopping branches for shelter construction, fire wood, digging sticks, and throwing clubs requires a robust edge that can sustain repeated downward impact without shattering - a task that destroys thin flakes in seconds.
- **Large bone processing:** Breaking thick skulls, pelves, and large epiphyses (bone ends) for marrow access requires the mass and robustness of a chopper rather than the delicacy of a flake.
- **Emergency digging:** In compacted soil, a chopper can be used to chip and lever out tubers or dig initial holes where a digging stick cannot penetrate.
- **Processing fibrous plants:** Hacking through agave-type fibrous leaves, palm fronds, and thick bark requires the chopper's edge mass to cut through rather than simply compress the fibers.

## Day 0 — Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0 -->
You need to cut a branch as thick as your wrist. Your flakes are shredding against it.

Find a river cobble - rounded, dense, fist-to-two-fist size. Dark basalt or grey quartzite. Strike it against another stone to check: it should ring, not thud.

Find a natural corner on the cobble. Strike it 5 times along the edge, each blow just ahead of the last scar. A jagged, sharp edge will develop along one end.

Now hold the smooth end and swing the sharp end into the branch. Hard, downward, repeated. Four or five blows will cut through a wrist-thick green branch.

This tool took 60 seconds to make. You could have spent that 60 seconds failing with a flake that breaks on the second blow.

Heavy work needs a heavy tool.
