# Hafting
## Description
<!-- @field: description -->
Hafting is the attachment of a stone, bone, or shell tool element to a wooden or bone handle, creating a composite tool with significantly greater mechanical effectiveness than either component alone. The key advantage is leverage: a hand-held 10 cm flake requires the user's arm to be within 10 cm of the target; the same flake mounted on a 150 cm shaft can be swung at high velocity with the mechanical amplification of the lever arm, producing far greater impact force and allowing distance from dangerous prey. Hafting transformed tools from hand-extensions into true weapons and high-efficiency implements.

The earliest confirmed hafted tools date to approximately 200,000 years ago (hafted points from Sibudu Cave, South Africa, and Kathu Pan, South Africa), but indirect evidence of hafting adhesive production appears as early as 500,000 years ago (birch tar distillation evidence from Europe). The cognitive and motor demands of hafting are substantially greater than flake production alone, requiring multi-step planning, appropriate material selection, and the ability to evaluate the mechanical fit between handle and tool element before use.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
The Sibudu Cave site in KwaZulu-Natal, South Africa (analyzed by Lyn Wadley and colleagues, 2009, *Science*) provided the clearest early evidence for composite tool manufacture. Ochre and plant-derived gum adhesive residues found on backed microliths at the site (~65,000 years ago) confirmed that these small, geometrically shaped points were hafted rather than hand-held. Earlier sites present more ambiguous evidence.

The Kathu Pan 1 site in the Northern Cape, South Africa, produced shaped stone points at ~500,000 years ago (Wilkins et al. 2012, *Science*) that showed impact fractures consistent with hafted spear use. This pushed the evidence for hafted projectiles substantially deeper into the Middle Pleistocene.

European Neanderthals are confirmed to have hafted tools using birch tar as adhesive (Schmid et al. 2019, *PNAS*, from Inden-Altdorf, Germany, ~200 ka), and possibly pine resin in earlier periods. Their hafted scrapers and points show wear patterns at the hafted zones consistent with sustained use with handles, not hand-held use.

Ethnographic hafting practices are extremely diverse. Australian Aboriginals use grass-tree (*Xanthorrhoea*) resin mixed with beeswax, heated to workable consistency. Amazonian peoples use tree sap (Hevea latex) thickened by heat. East African peoples historically used animal fat and charcoal mixtures. The consistent principle is heat-reactive adhesive plus mechanical securing (lashing with plant fiber).

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Step 1 - Prepare the tool element:**

The stone element must have a blunt, thick base for insertion into a haft socket or notch, with a sharp working end. Sharp-all-around flakes do not haft well - the mounting end must be deliberately blunted:

1. Blunt the base by heavy-handed percussion retouch on both faces until the base is 8-12mm thick and no longer sharp
2. Ensure the base has parallel or slightly convergent sides for a secure grip in the haft
3. Width of the base should match the haft socket width (test before adhesive application)

**Step 2 - Prepare the haft:**

*Side-notch (for scrapers and small knives):*
1. Select a handle 15-30 cm long, 15-20mm diameter hardwood
2. Cut a shallow notch at one end using a stone flake, 5-8mm deep, matching the tool width
3. The tool slides into the notch flush with the handle surface

*Split-stick haft (for larger tools):*
1. Select a green branch 30-150 cm long, 20-30mm diameter
2. Split the insertion end longitudinally 6-10 cm deep using a flake
3. Insert a temporary wedge to keep the split open
4. Insert the tool element base into the split
5. Remove the wedge - the spring of the wood grips the tool

**Step 3 - Adhesive application:**

*Plant resin adhesive (simplest):*
1. Collect pine or acacia resin from trunk exudates - yellow to amber translucent material
2. Heat resin gently over coals until liquid - do not overheat (will char and lose adhesive strength)
3. Add powdered charcoal or ochre 20-30% by volume (increases working range and durability)
4. Apply molten mixture to the join area with a stick
5. Insert tool element into position while adhesive is still hot and pliable
6. Allow to cool completely before testing

**Step 4 - Lashing:**

Adhesive alone is insufficient for high-impact use. Always lash the haft joint:

1. Prepare lashing: wet plant fiber strips (bark fiber, grass, sinew) or dampened strips of hide
2. Wrap tightly around the haft joint in a cross-pattern, overlapping each wrap 50%
3. Begin below the joint, wrap up and over the tool element base, then back down
4. Tie off with a half-hitch knot
5. As plant fiber dries, it contracts, increasing grip on the joint

## Materials
<!-- @field: materials -->

| Item | Purpose | Alternatives |
|------|---------|-------------|
| Hardwood handle blank | Primary haft material | Any dense hardwood, 15-150 cm depending on tool type |
| Stone flake (sharp) | Cutting haft notch | Any sharp edge |
| Plant resin or animal fat/charcoal mix | Adhesive | Pine resin, acacia resin, grass-tree resin |
| Heat source (coals) | Liquefying adhesive | Small coal bed from fire, no open flame needed |
| Plant fiber, sinew, or dampened bark | Lashing | Bark strips, dried grass twisted into cord |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Spear/thrusting weapon:** A 150 cm shaft with a stone point or fire-hardened tip can kill or seriously injure large game from 2-3 meters - creating distance between the hunter and dangerous prey at the moment of strike. The mechanical leverage increases penetrating force by 5-10x compared to a hand-held stone.
- **Axe (hand axe hafted):** A large core tool hafted to a 40-50 cm handle functions as a chopping tool for wood and bone, with the handle preventing hand injury and increasing downward force.
- **Knife:** A hafted flake scraper with an 18-20 cm handle dramatically reduces hand fatigue during extended butchering or hide-working sessions.
- **Digging tool:** A pointed stone element hafted to a 100 cm handle extends reach and increases leverage for excavating tubers or prying large rocks.

## Day 0  -  Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->
You have a good stone point. It is sharp enough but your hand is 5 cm from whatever you are stabbing. That is too close.

Find a straight green branch, wrist-thick, 150 cm long. Split one end 8 cm deep with your flake. Push the wide base of your stone point into the split. The wood grips it.

Find a pine tree or acacia with amber resin oozing from the bark. Collect a handful. Make a small coal bed. Hold the resin over it on a stick until it becomes liquid. Mix in powdered charcoal from your fire - about a quarter of the volume. Stir until uniform.

Drip the hot mixture around the join where stone meets wood. Work quickly - it cools fast.

While it cools, tear strips of bark or inner fiber. Wind them tightly around the joint - 10 wraps, overlapping. Tie off.

Let it set completely. Test it by striking the point hard against a tree trunk. If the point does not rotate or pull out, it is ready.

You now have a spear. The difference between your hand reaching something and this reaching something is 140 centimeters. That 140 centimeters might be your life.
