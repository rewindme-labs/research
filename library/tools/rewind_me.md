# Rewind-Me

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->
You are reading this inside it.

Rewind-Me is what happens when someone lies awake at 3 AM and asks the question that won't go away: *how much of everything we've built would we actually know how to rebuild?* Not which app, not which algorithm. The real stuff. How to get iron out of mud. How to keep grain from rotting through winter. How to ferment a drink that's safe when the river isn't. The things that kept us alive for ten thousand years before anyone wrote a line of code.

The answer, once you start pulling at it, is terrifying. Almost none of it lives in people anymore. It lives in supply chains. In institutions. In machines that work until they don't. Somewhere between the last blacksmith and the first microprocessor, we traded deep knowledge for convenience, and the trade was so successful that nobody noticed it happened.

Rewind-Me is a catalog of everything we forgot we knew. Not a museum exhibit. Not a textbook. A working document, raw, detailed, sometimes uncomfortably honest, that treats human knowledge the way an engineer treats a blueprint: something meant to be used, not admired from behind glass.

It is released one day at a time over 365 days, walking through history at the pace history actually moved. Slowly at first. Impossibly slowly, millions of years per day, because that's how long it actually took. Then faster. Then dizzyingly fast. Then all at once. Watching the timeline accelerate is itself a lesson no history class ever teaches.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
There is a particular kind of loss that happens without grief.

Nobody mourned the last person who could reliably identify every edible plant within walking distance of their home. Nobody held a funeral for the skill of reading weather by watching birds. Nobody decided that knowing how to build a kiln from riverbank clay was no longer important. It simply became unnecessary, one convenience at a time, until the knowledge existed only in academic papers that nobody outside a university would ever read.

This is not nostalgia. Nostalgia wants the old world back. Rewind-Me doesn't want anything back. It wants the *knowledge* forward. Stripped from the amber of history, translated into language that a person with dirty hands and a clear head could actually use.

The project was born in 2026 in Brazil, from a conversation between a human who couldn't stop asking questions and an AI that couldn't stop answering them. Daniel Farias spent months defining what belonged in the catalog and what didn't. What "detailed enough" actually meant. What the difference was between knowledge that impresses and knowledge that saves.

The timing mattered. 2026 was the first year that AI could do what no research team could: sustain months of continuous, deep, cross-referenced work across archaeology, chemistry, metallurgy, botany, brewing, medicine, and a hundred other fields, at a level of detail that would take a university department years to produce. The irony was not lost on anyone involved: the technology that made us forget how to do things was now the only thing fast enough to write it all down before the forgetting became permanent.

## Construction
<!-- @field: construction -->
Two of us built this. One human. One not.

But "built" is not quite the right word. Something like this doesn't come from a plan. It comes from a pile of late nights, stubborn questions, long detours, and the kind of obsession that makes you write about the thermodynamics of carbon diffusion in austenite at 3 in the morning because the entry needed it, and the entry was right to need it, because someone who decides to rebuild deserves to know *why* their steel is brittle after quenching and what to do about it.

Every entry in the Library follows the same honest structure: what is this thing, where does it come from, how do you actually make it with your hands, how do you store it, what is it good for, and, most importantly, what does it mean on Day 0?

Day 0 is not necessarily the day the world ends. Day 0 is the day someone decides to start. The day you look at something and say: I want to understand how this is made. I want to know how to do it myself. Rewind-Me itself had a Day 0. It was an ordinary night in 2026 when someone opened a conversation and typed the first question. No catastrophe required.

But the Day 0 section in each entry does imagine a harder version of that question: what if you had to start from nothing? That framing is a tool, not a prophecy. It forces honesty. It tells you that aluminum is worthless to pursue without industrial electricity. That pain relief can be made from willow bark in a kitchen. That iron is the single most important element for building anything and that you should understand it before everything else. It does not optimize for comfort. It optimizes for clarity.

The Timeline, the other half of the project, arranges all of this knowledge along the actual chronological sequence of human discovery. Day 1 of the Timeline has almost nothing in it. Just rocks and fire. Because that's what our ancestors had for millions of years, and pretending otherwise would be a lie. The emptiness of Day 1 is the most important lesson the Timeline teaches: *everything you take for granted was hard-won, slowly, by people who had almost nothing.*

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
**For the ones who wonder.** Rewind-Me is the best argument against taking anything for granted. You cannot get from stone tools to a solar panel without passing through copper smelting, iron forging, glass blowing, chemistry, electricity, and semiconductor physics. In that order. The timeline makes the dependency chain visible and inescapable. You start to understand why the Bronze Age happened *before* the Iron Age, not after. Why steel took two thousand years to get right. Why some civilizations stalled and others accelerated. The answers are always in the materials.

**For the ones who build.** Every entry documents reproduction. Not theory. Process. Temperatures, pressures, ratios, failure modes, substitutions. The level of detail is sometimes excessive and never apologetic. If you're reading the entry on beer, you will know the mashing temperature for barley (62-68°C), the reason it matters (enzyme activation windows), and what happens if you miss it (thin, watery, under-fermented disappointment). That specificity is not showing off. It's the difference between knowledge and trivia.

**For the ones who worry.** You're not crazy. The feeling that we've become dangerously dependent on systems we don't understand is correct. Rewind-Me won't fix that. No catalog can. But it puts the knowledge somewhere honest, somewhere open, somewhere it can be found by anyone who looks. The Library doesn't belong to a company or a platform. It belongs to the species. It always did. We just hadn't gotten around to writing it down in one place.

**For the kids**  -  Someday, probably sooner than anyone expects, a teenager somewhere is going to open this and spend an entire afternoon reading about how ancient Egyptians made blue pigment from sand, copper, and natron heated to 900°C. And something will click. And they'll want to try it. And that, the spark, the curiosity, the "wait, I could actually *do* this?", is the whole point.

## Day 0  -  Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->
This entry is the catalog writing about itself, so the usual question doesn't quite apply. Rewind-Me's own Day 0 already happened. It was the night someone decided this knowledge was worth gathering. Not because something collapsed. Because it deserved to exist in one place, organized, accessible, honest.

The Library exists now. Hundreds of entries, each structured, each written with the kind of care that assumes the reader actually wants to understand and not just skim. The knowledge is no longer locked in one person's head or one server's hard drive. That was always the design: make something that outlasts its maker.

But let's be practical, because that's what this catalog respects.

**Print what matters first.** The entries that keep you alive in the first week (water purification, fire starting, edible plant identification, basic wound care) fit on twenty pages. Print them. Laminate them if you can. Put them in the bag you grab when you leave. Paper has outlasted every digital storage format ever invented. It does not need electricity. It does not need a password. It works in the rain if you protect it.

**For a longer horizon,** the full Library fits on any device capable of storing text. An old phone, a cheap e-ink reader, a Raspberry Pi with a solar panel and a battery. Any of these can hold and display the entire catalog indefinitely, without a grid, without an internet connection, without an update. The design is deliberately low-tech. The invitation to build one is open, permanently.

**For the longest horizon,** the storage medium that outlasts everything is the one between your ears. Read the entries that matter to you. Practice the ones that interest you. Brew the beer. Smelt the copper. Bake the bread. Grow the garden. The knowledge doesn't become real until it lives in your hands.

That's what Rewind-Me was always trying to do. Not build an encyclopedia. Just get the knowledge close enough to people that it lives where it always lived best: in human memory, passed from hand to hand, the way it traveled for a hundred thousand years before anyone thought to write it down.

Find a river. Turn to *Flaked Stone Tools*. Everything follows from there.
