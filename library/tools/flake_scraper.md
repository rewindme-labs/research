# Flake Scraper
## Description
<!-- @field: description -->
The flake scraper is the first deliberately retouched stone tool in the archaeological record: a stone flake whose dorsal edge has been shaped by secondary percussion or pressure to create a durable, angled scraping edge. While an unretouched flake has a very sharp but fragile edge suited for cutting, a retouched scraper has a more robust edge that can sustain the lateral pressure required for sustained scraping tasks - working hides, shaping wooden shafts, removing bark, and scraping tubers.

The step from simply using flakes as struck to deliberately modifying their edges to improve performance for specific tasks represents a significant cognitive threshold: it requires understanding that a tool can be improved, that the modification serves a future purpose, and that the effort of retouching is worth the performance gain. This is one of the earliest documented examples of tool improvement rather than mere tool use.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
Side scrapers are ubiquitous in Oldowan and Acheulean assemblages (2.6-0.3 Ma). Nicholas Toth's experimental replication studies (1985, *Journal of Human Evolution*) confirmed that retouched scrapers outperform unretouched flakes for sustained scraping tasks by a factor of 3-5 in terms of edge durability before failure. The investment in retouching (typically 30-60 seconds of careful percussion) pays back in extended usable life.

The Swartkrans site in South Africa (~1.8-1.5 Ma) contains large quantities of bone tools alongside stone scrapers - researchers C.K. Brain and A. Sillen (1988, *Nature*) identified bone points showing distinctive wear patterns consistent with digging and hide-working, suggesting that *Homo* at Swartkrans was engaged in complex multi-tool task sequences including hide processing.

Among modern stone-tool-using peoples (until recently), hide scrapers were among the most carefully maintained and curated tools. Inuit women maintained end scrapers (*ulu* variants) throughout their lifetimes, resharpening them repeatedly. San women used hafted scrapers for hide preparation, replacing only the flake insert while maintaining the handle indefinitely.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Step 1 - Select the right flake for scraper production:**

A good scraper blank has:
- One edge with an angle between 45 and 70 degrees from the ventral face (not too steep, not too shallow)
- Sufficient length on that edge (5+ cm) for effective scraping
- Thickness sufficient to support retouching without shattering (3+ mm at the edge)

**Step 2 - Retouching the edge:**

Two methods, choose based on available materials:

*Percussion retouching (hard hammer):*
1. Hold the flake on your palm, dorsal face up, with the edge to be retouched facing away from you
2. Use a small, dense hammerstone (100-200g) and strike the dorsal face at the edge, 2-3 mm back from the edge
3. Each blow removes a tiny flake from the dorsal surface, creating a stepped or scaled pattern
4. Work along the entire edge in overlapping strikes, creating a uniform scraping angle
5. The resulting edge is more durable but slightly blunter than the original - optimized for scraping, not cutting

*Pressure flaking (antler or bone tine):*
1. Hold the flake dorsal face up on a leather pad
2. Press the tip of a hard antler tine or bone point firmly against the edge, 1-2 mm from the edge tip
3. Apply steady inward pressure while rocking the tine slightly to lever off tiny flakes
4. This produces finer, more controlled retouch than percussion
5. Requires more skill but produces a cleaner edge geometry

**Step 3 - Using the scraper:**

| Task | Edge Angle | Stroke Direction | Pressure |
|------|-----------|-----------------|---------|
| Hide scraping (removing fat/membrane) | 45-55 degrees | Pushing away from body | Moderate-heavy |
| Wood shaping (removing bark, rounding shaft) | 60-70 degrees | Pulling toward body | Heavy |
| Tuber preparation (peeling) | 40-50 degrees | Pulling toward body | Light-moderate |
| Bone scraping (removing periosteum) | 55-65 degrees | Either direction | Heavy |

| Hammerstone (100-200g) | Hard hammer retouching | Small, controlled for precision |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Hide processing:** The primary task requiring a scraper rather than a sharp flake. Removing fat, connective tissue, and membrane from a freshly removed hide requires sustained lateral pressure over the entire hide surface - a task that would destroy an unretouched flake edge in minutes but a scraper can sustain for an hour.
- **Wood shaping:** Shaping a wooden shaft, thinning a digging stick, or preparing wooden components for composite tools all require a robust scraping edge that can work against hard material.
- **Food preparation:** Peeling tubers and roots, scraping seeds from grass heads, and removing bark from edible inner bark sources all benefit from a scraper's controlled edge.
- **Ritual and hygiene:** Scrapers are documented ethnographically for hair removal, wound edge preparation, and surface cleaning tasks in hunter-gatherer contexts.

## Day 0  -  Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->
You have a fresh hide from yesterday's kill. You need to scrape the fat and membrane off the inside before it dries into a stiff board.

Your sharp flakes will do it for 2 minutes and then the edge will crumble.

Take a thicker flake - one 4-5mm thick at the edge, with a natural angle of about 60 degrees. Find a small stone the size of an egg. Strike the thick edge of your flake from the top, 2-3mm back from the edge, repeatedly along its length. You are not trying to take a big flake off - you are removing tiny chips that regularize the edge and make it steeper and more durable.

Now press that edge against the inner surface of the hide at a 45-degree angle. Push forward with firm pressure. A curl of fat will rise in front of the edge.

This edge will last 20 minutes of scraping before it needs attention. Rework it for 30 seconds and continue.

The hide that takes 2 hours to scrape clean will last you years. The scraper that lets you do it properly takes 2 minutes to make.
