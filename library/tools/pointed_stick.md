# Pointed Stick

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

The pointed stick is the simplest manufactured tool: a straight branch sharpened at one end by scraping with a stone flake or hardened in fire. It is simultaneously a digging implement (extracting tubers, probing ground), a thrusting weapon (close-range defence and hunting), a probing tool (testing water depth, snake holes), and a structural element (shelter framing, fence stakes). The cognitive step from using an unmodified branch to deliberately shaping it into a point represents the transition from natural tool use to manufacturing. Chimpanzees at Fongoli (Senegal) sharpen sticks with their teeth and use them to spear bushbabies in tree hollows (Pruetz & Bertolani, 2007), one of the only documented cases of non-human weapon manufacture for hunting. Fire-hardening the tip transforms a workable but easily blunted wooden point into a durable tool that penetrates soil and hide with significantly less force.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

The Schoningen spears (Germany, 300,000 BP) are the oldest surviving manufactured wooden weapons: 10 spruce spears 1.8-2.5 m long, scraped smooth with stone tools, with the heaviest point at the forward third for aerodynamic balance identical to modern competition javelins (Thieme, 1997). However, wooden tools rarely survive, and the pointed stick tradition likely extends to the earliest hominins. The Clacton spear point (England, 400,000 BP) is a yew wood point with clear scraping marks. At Lehringen (Germany, 125,000 BP), a yew spear 2.4 m long was found between the ribs of a straight-tusked elephant skeleton. The Fongoli chimpanzee spearing behaviour documented by Pruetz pushes the conceptual origin of pointed stick use back to our common ancestor. Among the Hadza, simple pointed digging sticks are women's primary foraging tool, used daily for tuber extraction, and are replaced every 2-3 days from locally available hardwood.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->

**Selecting wood for a pointed stick:**

| Property | Why it matters | How to test |
|----------|---------------|-------------|
| Straightness | Aerodynamics (throwing), leverage (digging) | Sight along the length like a rifle barrel |
| Hardness | Tip durability under use | Thumbnail pressure test: no dent = hardwood |
| Grain direction | Resistance to splitting | Check for spiral grain (reject) vs straight grain (accept) |
| Diameter | Balance between strength and weight | 2-4 cm for digging sticks, 3-5 cm for thrusting spears |
| Length | Task specific | 1 m for digging, 1.5-2.5 m for spears |
| Moisture | Green wood bends; dry wood snaps | Slight flex without cracking = ideal |

**Best wood species (where available):**

| Species | Properties | Best for |
|---------|------------|----------|
| Acacia | Very hard, straight, dense | Spear, digging stick |
| Olive (Olea) | Extremely hard, fine-grained | Spear tips, handles |
| Ironwood (various) | Hardest available, heavy | Digging sticks, clubs |
| Yew | Flexible, tough, straight | Throwing spears |
| Ash/hickory | Hard, resilient, shock-absorbing | Multi-purpose |
| Bamboo (if available) | Hollow, lightweight, splits cleanly | Temporary spears |

**Manufacturing a pointed stick:**

1. Select a straight branch 1-2.5 m long, 2-5 cm diameter, from a hardwood species.
2. Trim all side branches flush using a stone flake. Cut at an angle to prevent snag points.
3. Sharpen the tip: use a stone flake to scrape at a shallow angle, rotating the stick as you work. Create a gradual taper over the final 10-15 cm, not a pencil-point (too fragile).
4. Smooth the shaft by scraping along the length to remove bark and rough spots. A smooth shaft reduces friction in soil and prevents hand blisters during use.
5. Fire-harden the tip (if fire available): hold the sharpened tip 10-15 cm above glowing coals (not in flames). Rotate slowly and continuously for 3-5 minutes. The wood surface dries, compresses, and darkens. It should be toasted brown, never charred black (charred wood is brittle).
6. Test the tip by pressing into compacted soil. A properly hardened tip penetrates cleanly. An unhardened tip deforms and splinters.

**Maintenance:**

1. Resharpen the tip daily during heavy use. A stone flake scrapes the tip back to a point in 30 seconds.
2. Replace the stick every 2-5 days in hard-soil digging use. Wood tips wear down progressively.
3. Store pointed sticks upright, tip up, leaning against shelter. Tips touching damp ground absorb water and soften.

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Tuber extraction:** The pointed stick is the primary tool for accessing underground storage organs (USOs). Insert the tip next to the tuber stem, lever sideways to loosen soil, then extract by hand. Daily tuber foraging in the Kalahari produces 1,200-2,000 kcal using a simple digging stick.
- **Self-defence:** A 2 m hardwood spear held with both hands deters most medium predators. The reach advantage (human arm + spear = 3+ m) keeps teeth and claws at distance. Two people with spears facing a lone predator almost always win the standoff.
- **Thrusting weapon:** Against trapped, exhausted, or injured prey animals, a fire-hardened pointed stick delivers lethal thrusts. Not a thrown weapon at this stage, but a close-range finishing tool.
- **Shelter construction:** Pointed stakes driven into the ground form the frame for windbreaks, lean-to shelters, and predator fencing (thornbush perimeters).
- **Probing:** Before stepping into murky water, tall grass, or dark holes, a pointed stick probes ahead. The 1-2 m extension of reach detects threats before your body encounters them.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

Find the hardest, straightest branch you can get. Arm's length at minimum, longer if you want a weapon. As thick as your thumb to two fingers wide. Bend it slightly to test: it should resist bending and spring back.

Strip the side branches with a sharp stone flake. Cut them flush so nothing snags your hand during use. Then scrape the tip at a shallow angle, rotating the stick as you go. You want a gradual tapered point over the last 10-15 cm, not a needle point. A needle point snaps on first contact with soil or bone.

If you have fire, harden the tip. Hold it above glowing coals, not in the flames. Rotate slowly. Watch the colour change from pale to golden brown. This takes 3-5 minutes of patient turning. When it is uniformly brown and the surface feels harder and smoother, it is done. If it turns black, you went too far and created charcoal that crumbles. Start over with a new section.

You now have the most versatile tool in the primitive arsenal. Stab it into the ground next to a tuber stem and lever sideways to loosen the earth. Extract tubers that contain 90-200 calories per root, 10-20 roots per hour.

Hold it with both hands when a predator approaches. Point the tip at its face. Your reach with the stick exceeds its reach with its jaws. Step forward, not backward. Jabbing forward communicates aggression that most predators are not willing to test.

Drive it into the ground as a tent stake. Lean branches against it. You have the beginning of a shelter frame.

Probe every hole, puddle, and shadow with it before reaching in with your hand. Your stick encounters the snake before your fingers do.

Make two. Carry one as your primary tool. Leave one at camp as a spare. When the first one breaks or dulls beyond resharpening, the backup is immediate. Replace the backup that evening. Never have fewer than two pointed sticks in your possession.
