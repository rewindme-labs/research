# Primitive Woodworking

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

Primitive woodworking is the shaping of wood using stone tools, fire, water, and abrasion. Long before metal saws and chisels, hominids split logs with stone wedges, scraped surfaces smooth with flake tools, carved points by charring and scraping, bent green wood with steam and weight, and drilled holes by rotating hardwood awls against softwood targets. Wood is the most accessible structural material on earth - lighter than stone, stronger than bone in tension, shapeable when green, rigid when dry. A skilled worker with a hammerstone, a sharp flake, and patience can produce everything from digging sticks to shelter frames to watercraft within the first weeks of a survival scenario.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

The earliest direct evidence of woodworking is the Schoningen spears (Germany, c. 300,000 BP) - eight complete spruce throwing spears, each 1.8-2.5 m long, with the balance point at the front third, scraped smooth and fire-hardened at the tip. The workmanship implies a long prior tradition. The Clacton spear tip (England, c. 400,000 BP) is the oldest surviving wooden artifact showing deliberate shaping. At Kalambo Falls (Zambia, c. 200,000 BP), waterlogged deposits preserve shaped wooden implements including a club, digging sticks, and what appears to be a bark container. The Lehringen lance (Germany, c. 125,000 BP) was found embedded in elephant ribs, demonstrating functional weapon craft. Aboriginal Australians produced boomerangs, shields, and multi-piece composite tools entirely with stone blades and fire, maintaining woodworking traditions spanning 40,000+ years.

## Construction
<!-- @field: construction -->

**Wood selection:**

| Property | Best species | Adequate | Avoid |
|----------|-------------|----------|-------|
| Straight grain | Ash, spruce, yew, bamboo | Pine, birch, poplar | Knotty or twisted grain |
| Hardness for tools | Oak, ironwood, olive | Beech, maple | Soft pines, balsa |
| Flexibility for bending | Willow, hazel, green ash | Young oak, elm | Dry hardwoods |
| Fire-hardening response | Hardwoods with dense grain | Most deciduous species | Resinous softwoods (catch fire) |
| Rot resistance | Cedar, yew, black locust | Oak, walnut | Poplar, birch, willow |

**Core techniques:**

**1. Splitting (fastest way to process logs):**
- Select a straight-grained log 10-30 cm diameter.
- Carve or find a hardwood wedge (15-20 cm, sharp edge).
- Place wedge in a natural crack or start one with a sharp stone at the log end.
- Strike wedge with a heavy hammerstone (1-2 kg).
- As the split opens, insert a second wedge further along.
- Continue leap-frogging wedges to split the full length.

**2. Scraping and smoothing:**
- Use a large flake tool (obsidian, flint, or quartzite with a long, sharp edge).
- Hold flake at 60-80 degree angle to the wood surface.
- Pull toward you in long strokes, removing thin curls.
- For fine work, use the edge of a fresh flake at a steeper angle.
- Finish with sandstone or dried shark skin rubbed along the grain.

**3. Carving and shaping:**
- Score a circumferential groove around a branch with a sharp flake to control the break point.
- Chop with a hand axe or heavy flake for rough shaping.
- Whittle with a small flake for detail work - always cut away from the body.
- For hollowing (bowls, dugouts): char the area with hot coals, scrape away charred wood with a flake, repeat.

**4. Fire-hardening:**
- Carve the point or surface to final shape while green.
- Hold 10-15 cm above hot coals (not in flames), rotating constantly.
- The surface should darken to golden-brown, not blacken.
- Process takes 10-20 minutes per surface.
- The heat drives out moisture and cross-links lignin, increasing surface hardness by 30-50%.

**5. Bending:**
- Select green (living) wood, ideally willow, hazel, or young ash.
- Heat over steam (suspend over a container of boiling water) for 15-30 minutes per cm of thickness.
- Bend slowly to desired curve while hot.
- Tie in position with cordage and allow to dry 3-7 days.
- The set becomes permanent once fully dry.

**6. Drilling:**
- Select a hard, straight stick (10-15 cm long, pencil-thick) as the drill.
- Shape one end to a rounded point.
- Place on softer wood surface, press down and rotate rapidly between palms.
- For faster drilling: use a bow drill mechanism (cord wrapped around drill, powered by back-and-forth bow motion).
- Add fine sand as abrasive into the hole for faster cutting.

## Operation & Maintenance
<!-- @field: operation -->

- **Green vs dry wood:** Green wood (freshly cut, high moisture) carves easily but shrinks and cracks as it dries. Dry wood is harder to work but dimensionally stable. For tools that need to hold shape (handles, frames), rough-carve green and finish after drying.
- **Preventing cracks:** Seal end-grain with mud, fat, or pine resin immediately after cutting to slow uneven drying.
- **Storage:** Keep finished wooden tools out of direct sun and rain. Rub periodically with rendered animal fat to prevent drying and cracking.
- **Repairs:** Splits can be bound with wet rawhide that shrinks tight as it dries. Loose joints can be re-wedged.
- **Tool edge maintenance:** Re-sharpen stone scraping tools frequently - a dull flake tears wood fibers instead of cutting cleanly.

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Weapons:** Spears, clubs, throwing sticks, digging sticks, shield blanks
- **Shelter:** Ridge poles, rafters, wall frames, door frames, stakes
- **Containers:** Bowls (burned and scraped), troughs, mortars
- **Transportation:** Travois poles, raft frames, paddle blanks, sled runners
- **Fire:** Hearth boards and spindles for friction fire, fuel processing
- **Agriculture:** Digging sticks, hoe handles, fence posts, drying racks

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

You need wood for everything - shelter poles, digging sticks, weapon shafts, fire hearth boards, containers. Here is how to start processing it with nothing but stone tools.

Find a straight branch or sapling 5-8 cm thick. Score a ring around it with a sharp stone flake where you want to cut. Keep scoring, rotating the branch, cutting deeper into the same groove. When you are halfway through, bend it sharply and it will snap clean at the score line. This is your basic length-control technique - no saw needed.

To split a thicker log for planks or half-rounds, make a hardwood wedge first. Find a dense branch, shape one end to a thin edge by scraping with a flake. Place the wedge in the end of your log and hammer it with a fist-sized stone. Drive it in until the split starts running down the grain. Insert a second wedge (a stick works temporarily) further along the opening crack to hold it while you drive the first one deeper. Work your way down the log.

For shaping and smoothing, hold a sharp flake at a steep angle to the wood and pull it toward you like a drawknife. Long, steady strokes. The goal is thin curls, not gouging. A freshly knapped flint flake is sharper than a steel knife - use it.

To harden a spear point or digging stick tip, hold the shaped end above hot coals - close enough that oil sizzles out, far enough that it does not catch fire. Rotate constantly for 15 minutes until the surface is golden-brown and the wood has a hard, glassy sound when you tap it.

To make a bowl from a section of log, place hot coals on the surface where you want the hollow. Let them burn a shallow depression, scrape out the char with a flake, place more coals. Repeat. In a full day of work you can produce a functional eating bowl that holds 500 ml.

Every piece of wood you work teaches you about grain direction, hardness, moisture, and species behaviour. There is no substitute for repetition. Start with digging sticks and spear shafts - they are forgiving shapes. Graduate to hafted tools and containers as your control improves.
