# Lean-To Shelter
<!-- @field: description -->
A lean-to is the simplest constructed shelter: a single angled roof plane that deflects rain and wind from one direction, providing adequate protection for overnight sleeping in temperate to warm conditions. It requires no advanced technique, no tools beyond what hands and simple stone flakes can provide, and can be constructed by one person in 1-2 hours from materials present in most woodland or forest environments.

The lean-to's strength is speed and simplicity; its limitation is directional protection only. It excels when wind and rain come from a consistent direction — as in most weather systems — and when positioned with its open face away from the prevailing wind. In cold conditions, a fire in front of the open face turns the lean-to's reflective back wall into a radiant heat concentrator, dramatically improving thermal efficiency.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
Lean-to structures are among the most archaeologically ambiguous shelters to detect, because their components — poles, bark, leaves — decompose completely without leaving durable traces. Post holes and stone foundation lines are the primary archaeological signatures of early constructed shelters, but these require floors of sufficient permanence and weight to create detectable impressions.

The earliest confirmed hominin-constructed shelters are the oval stone rings from Olduvai Gorge (Bed I, ~1.8 Ma) interpreted by Mary Leakey as possible windbreak bases, and the wooden structure from Terra Amata, France (~400,000 ya) — though the Terra Amata interpretation remains debated. Chimpanzee nest construction, which involves bending and weaving branches into a sleeping platform, provides a behavioral analog for the cognitive prerequisites of lean-to construction.

Ethnographic documentation across forager societies shows lean-to structures as the standard emergency and temporary shelter across Africa, Australia, and the Americas — built in one sitting, used for a few nights, then abandoned or maintained as a dry-season camp.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Site selection:**

| Factor | Ideal | Reject |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Wind direction | Back of shelter faces prevailing wind | Open face into wind |
| Ground | Flat, well-drained | Slope (water flows under you), hollow (collects water) |
| Overhead | Clear or sparse canopy (avoids drip lines) | Dense canopy directly above (drip concentrates) |
| Proximity to materials | Within 50m of poles, thatch | Far from materials (construction takes too long) |
| Predator access | Open ground approach — visible from inside | Dense bush at back (leopard approach cover) |

**Construction sequence:**

**1. Ridge pole:**
- Find or cut a straight pole 3-4 m long, 5-8 cm diameter
- Prop one end against a forked tree branch or two upright poles at 1.5-1.8 m height
- Rest the other end on the ground or a low support at 30-40 cm height
- This pole defines the roof slope angle (30-40 degrees ideal for rain runoff)

**2. Rafter poles:**
- Lean 8-12 poles against the ridge pole, parallel and evenly spaced 20-30 cm apart
- Poles should extend 30 cm past the ridge pole at the top
- At the bottom, push the butt ends 10 cm into the soil (prevents sliding)
- Lash rafters to the ridge pole with cordage if available; lean angle alone holds them adequately for short-term use

**3. Thatching:**
- Begin at the bottom (eave) and work upward — each layer overlaps the one below, directing water outward
- Use long-stemmed grass, broad leaves, ferns, bark slabs, or any dense, overlapping plant material
- Layer thickness: 15-20 cm minimum for rain resistance; 30+ cm for cold/extended rain
- Tuck bundles under each rafter pole before the next layer — this keys them in place without lashing
- Final layer at the ridge: fold material over the top and weight with a pole laid across

**4. Side walls (optional, improves wind protection):**
- Lean additional poles vertically at each open side
- Thatch over them the same way as the roof

**5. Sleeping bed:**
- Never sleep directly on soil — ground conductive heat loss is severe
- Lay 10-20 cm of dry leaf litter, grass, or bark inside the shelter
- The insulation layer under you is as important as the insulation above you

## Materials
<!-- @field: materials -->

| Item | Purpose | Quantity for 1-person lean-to |
|------|---------|-------------------------------|
| Ridge pole (3-4 m) | Primary structure | 1 |
| Rafter poles (2.5-3 m) | Roof support | 8-12 |
| Upright support poles (1.5-2 m) | Ridge pole support (if no forked tree) | 2 |
| Long-stemmed grass or broad leaves | Thatching material | Large armful (50-80 bundles) |
| Cordage | Lashing (optional) | 2-3 meters |
| Dry leaf litter or grass | Ground insulation layer | 20+ cm depth |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Rain protection:** A properly thatched lean-to with 20+ cm of material sheds heavy rain completely when the roof slope is adequate (30+ degrees) and the thatching is layered correctly from bottom to top.
- **Wind protection:** The solid back wall of a lean-to deflects wind around the sleeping area; with a fire at the open front, the combination of wind block and reflected radiant heat makes the lean-to comfortable in temperatures well below zero.
- **Extended camp construction:** A lean-to built for one night can be maintained, improved, and extended into a multi-week camp structure with additional walls, a fire pit, and a food cache area.
- **Group construction speed:** Two people can construct a 3-person lean-to in under an hour. Dividing labor — one person gathers poles while the other gathers thatch — is significantly faster than sequential single-person construction.

## Technical Data
<!-- @field: technical_data -->

| Parameter | Value | Notes |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| Construction time (1 person) | 1-2 hours | With material within 50 m |
| Optimal roof slope | 30-45 degrees | Below 30 = rain doesn't run off; above 45 = reduced interior height |
| Minimum thatch thickness | 15-20 cm | For rain resistance in moderate rain |
| Ground insulation depth | 10-20 cm | Reduces conductive heat loss by 60-80% |
| Ridge pole height | 1.5-1.8 m | Allows sitting inside with clearance |
| Lean-to interior volume (1 person) | ~1.5 m x 2 m | Adequate for sleep and equipment storage |
| Fire-reflector thermal gain | Significant | Open face toward fire roughly doubles radiated heat |
| Oldest confirmed shelter | ~1.8 Ma | Olduvai Gorge, debated |

## Day 0 — Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0 -->
It is going to rain tonight. You have 90 minutes before dark.

Find a tree with a natural fork at 1.5 meters height. This is your ridge pole support. If none exists, push two upright poles into the ground 2 meters apart with forked tops, 1.5 meters high.

Lay your longest straight pole across the fork — one end high, one end on the ground. That is your roof angle.

Lean 10 poles against it, parallel, 25 cm apart. Push the bottom ends into the ground.

Now thatch. Start at the bottom. Grab a bundle of long grass. Push the stems under the lowest rafter. Lay the next bundle above it, overlapping the first by half its length. Work upward, each bundle covering the roots of the one below. The rain must always hit the top of a bundle before it hits anything below.

When you reach the top, fold a bundle over the ridge and weight it.

Inside, pile 15 cm of dry leaves.

Light a fire at the open front.

The rain will run off the roof above you. The fire will warm the air in front of you. The ground insulation will stop the cold coming up from below.

You spent 90 minutes and tonight you are dry.
