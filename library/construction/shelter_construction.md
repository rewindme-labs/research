# Shelter Construction

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

Shelter construction is the deliberate building of physical structures for protection from wind, rain, sun, cold, and predators using available natural materials. It is the transition from finding natural shelter (caves, overhangs) to creating it where none exists. The simplest form is a windbreak: a row of branches leaned against a support and covered with vegetation. The most sophisticated Paleolithic structures include the DK stone circle at Olduvai Gorge (1.8 Ma, interpreted by Mary Leakey as a hut base), the Terra Amata shelters (France, 400,000 BP), and the mammoth bone houses of Mezhirich (Ukraine, 15,000 BP). Shelter construction requires understanding of structural principles (load bearing, wind resistance, water shedding), material properties (flexibility, durability, weather resistance), and site engineering (drainage, orientation, ventilation).

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

The DK site at Olduvai Gorge (1.8 Ma) contains a rough circle of basalt blocks 4 m in diameter that Mary Leakey interpreted as the base of a shelter or windbreak. While this interpretation is debated, it represents the earliest potential evidence of construction. At Terra Amata (France, 400,000 BP), de Lumley excavated post-hole impressions and stone weight patterns suggesting oval hut structures 8-15 m long, with central hearths. The chimps of Gombe and Mahale build nightly sleeping nests from bent branches and leaf mattresses in 3-5 minutes, demonstrating basic construction behaviour in our primate lineage. Among the Hadza (Tanzania), camp shelters take 30-60 minutes to build from local materials: a framework of bent saplings covered with grass, sufficient for dry-season use and rebuilt at each new camp location every 2-3 weeks.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->

**Shelter types by complexity:**

| Type | Build time | Materials | Protection | People housed |
|------|-----------|-----------|------------|---------------|
| Windbreak | 15-30 min | Branches, brush | Wind, partial rain | 2-4 |
| Lean-to | 30-60 min | Poles, leaves/grass | Wind, rain, sun | 2-4 |
| A-frame | 1-2 hours | Ridge pole, rafters, thatching | Full weather | 2-3 |
| Dome/wickiup | 2-4 hours | Bent saplings, thatching | Full weather, wind | 4-8 |
| Semi-permanent hut | 4-8 hours | Posts, frame, thick thatching | All weather, extended use | 4-10 |

**Building a lean-to shelter (most practical first build):**

1. Select a ridgepole: a strong straight branch 3-4 m long, 5-8 cm diameter. Hardwood preferred.
2. Rest one end on a natural support: a tree fork, rock ledge, or crossed stake, 1-1.5 m above the ground. The other end rests on the ground.
3. Lean rafters (straight branches) along both sides of the ridgepole at 45-degree angles, spacing them 15-20 cm apart.
4. Starting from the bottom, layer thatching material over the rafters: large leaves, grass bundles, fern fronds, bark sheets. Overlap each layer like roof tiles so water runs down and off.
5. Build the thatch 10-15 cm thick. Thin thatch leaks. Test by having someone inside while you pour water over the outside.
6. Leave the downwind end open for entry. The upwind end should be solid to block prevailing wind.
7. Line the interior floor with dry vegetation 15+ cm deep for insulation.

**Building a windbreak (emergency shelter):**

1. Find or create two vertical supports 2 m apart: tree trunks, driven stakes, or rock faces.
2. Lean branches densely between the supports, tilted at 60-70 degrees from horizontal.
3. Stack brush, leaves, and grass against the branch framework from the windward side.
4. Height should reach at least 1.5 m.
5. Build the fire 1-2 m in front of the windbreak on the downwind side. Heat reflects from the windbreak onto sleeping occupants.

**Site preparation:**

1. Clear all sharp objects and level the ground surface.
2. Dig a shallow drainage groove around the uphill perimeter to divert rain runoff.
3. Orient the shelter opening away from prevailing wind (typically east in most regions to catch morning sun).
4. Position the fire between the opening and the prevailing wind direction for consistent smoke dispersal.

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Thermal protection:** A well-thatched lean-to with fire reduces overnight heat loss by 60-80% compared to sleeping in the open. In temperate climates, this is the difference between functional sleep and hypothermia.
- **Rain protection:** 15 cm of layered thatch sheds all but the heaviest tropical downpours. Keeping people and equipment dry prevents hypothermia, tool rust, fire extinguishment, and morale collapse.
- **Predator deterrence:** An enclosed shelter with a fire at the entrance creates a defensible position. The structure limits attack angles and the fire deters approach.
- **Social space:** Constructed shelters create defined living spaces that organise group activity: sleeping areas, storage, tool production zones. This spatial organisation improves efficiency and reduces conflict.
- **Camp permanence:** A group that builds semi-permanent shelters invests in a location, enabling multi-week stays that maximise resource extraction from the surrounding area before relocation.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

If there is no cave, no overhang, and no rock shelter nearby, you build one. Tonight. Before dark. This is not optional in any climate where the temperature drops below 15 C at night.

Find the simplest structure first: a windbreak. Two trees or driven stakes, 2 m apart. Lean every branch you can find against them, dense enough that you cannot see through. Stack brush and grass against the windward side until it is solid. Build your fire 1.5 m in front of it. You now have a heat reflector that bounces firelight and warmth back onto you. A windbreak takes 15-30 minutes and saves your life on cold nights.

When you have more time, build a lean-to. Find a strong straight branch as long as you are tall plus another arm's length. Rest one end in a tree fork or on a rock shelf at chest height. The other end touches the ground. Lean shorter branches along both sides at 45 degrees, spaced a hand's width apart. Now thatch it.

Thatching is the critical step. Gather arm-loads of large leaves, grass bundles, fern fronds, or bark strips. Lay them on the frame starting from the bottom, overlapping each layer above the one below, like roof tiles. Water runs down each layer and off the bottom instead of dripping through. Build the thatch 15 cm thick. Test it: have someone inside while you pour water over the outside. If they stay dry, you are done. If not, add more thatch.

Line the inside floor with 15 cm of dry grass, leaves, or pine needles. You will sleep on this. Ground contact steals body heat at a rate that wakes you every hour. Insulation stops it.

Dig a shallow groove around the uphill side of your shelter. Even a 5 cm trench diverts rainwater runoff around your shelter instead of through it. You will thank yourself at 2 AM when it rains.

Orient the opening away from the prevailing wind. In most places, wind comes from the west or northwest. Open your shelter to the east. Morning sun warms you. Wind hits the solid back.

Improve the structure each day. Add more thatch. Extend the sides. Build up the windbreak. A shelter that takes 30 minutes on day one becomes legitimately comfortable by day five with 15 minutes of daily improvement.
