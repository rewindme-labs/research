# Natural Shelter

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

Natural shelter is the identification and use of unmodified environmental features for protection from weather, predators, and temperature extremes. Caves, rock overhangs, cliff recesses, dense tree canopies, fallen log hollows, boulder clusters, and thicket interiors all provide protection that requires zero construction effort. Before hominins could build, they had to find. The cognitive task is pattern recognition: scanning a landscape and identifying which formations offer overhead cover, wind blocking, drainage, defensibility, and escape routes. Baboons sleep on cliff faces for predator protection. Chimpanzees select large trees with broad canopies for night nests. Early hominins used the same strategy, and natural shelters remained the primary living sites for millions of years. The caves at Sterkfontein (South Africa, 3+ Ma) contain Australopithecus fossils accumulated over hundreds of thousands of years, indicating sustained, repeated use of the same natural shelter.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

The Sterkfontein cave system (South Africa) preserves Australopithecus africanus remains from 3.0 to 2.0 Ma, making it one of the longest-occupied natural shelters in the hominin record. However, taphonomic analysis by C.K. Brain (1981) at Swartkrans revealed that many hominin remains in caves were deposited by leopards who dragged kills into trees overhanging cave entrances, a reminder that caves attract hominins and predators alike. At Wonderwerk Cave (South Africa), evidence of hominin occupation stretches back to 2 Ma with continuous use across hundreds of thousands of years, including the earliest interior cave fire evidence at 1 Ma (Berna, 2012). Rock shelters (overhangs without enclosure) served as the dominant shelter type in tropical and subtropical environments: Kalambo Falls (Zambia, 300,000 BP) shows repeated occupation of a lakeside rock shelter with associated tools and fire evidence.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->

**Evaluating a natural shelter (scoring system):**

| Criterion | Ideal | Acceptable | Reject |
|-----------|-------|------------|--------|
| Overhead cover | Full rock/canopy ceiling | Partial cover, 50%+ | Open sky |
| Wind exposure | Blocked on 2-3 sides | Blocked on 1 side | Fully exposed |
| Drainage | Elevated or sloped floor | Level, dry | Depression (water collects) |
| Predator signs | None within 50 m | Old signs (weeks+) | Fresh scat, tracks, kills |
| Escape routes | 2+ exit directions | 1 exit with clear sightline | Dead-end (box canyon, deep cave) |
| Overhead hazard | Solid roof, no loose rock | Minor instability | Active rockfall, cracked ceiling |
| Insect/snake presence | None visible | Minor (manageable) | Wasp nest, snake den, ant colony |

**Types of natural shelter and their properties:**

| Shelter type | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|-------------|------------|---------------|
| Cave | Full weather protection, thermal mass | Predator lairs, no light, poor ventilation, flooding |
| Rock overhang | Rain protection, open air flow, daylight | Wind exposure on open side, limited space |
| Boulder cluster | Wind break, multiple hiding points | No rain protection, gaps admit snakes |
| Dense canopy (tree) | Rain deflection, elevated sleeping | No wind protection, falling risk, insect exposure |
| Fallen log hollow | Wind and rain protection, concealment | Limited space, insect/rot issues, fire risk |
| Thicket interior | Full concealment, predator deterrent (thorns) | Poor visibility, entry/exit difficulty |

**Cave assessment protocol:**

1. Approach cautiously. Listen for 30 seconds at the entrance. Growling, rustling, or hissing means occupied.
2. Check entrance floor for fresh scat, tracks, or bone fragments. Fresh predator signs (hyena coprolites are white, leopard scat dark and segmented, bear scat large with plant matter) mean the occupant will return.
3. Throw a stone deep into the cave. Wait. If nothing moves or vocalises after 60 seconds, enter slowly with a fire brand or lit torch.
4. Check ceiling for stability. Cracked rock, stalactites over sleeping areas, or water seepage from above indicate problems.
5. Check floor slope. Water should not flow into the cave during rain. If the entrance is lower than the interior, water will pool inside.
6. Test ventilation. Light a small smoky fire near the entrance. Smoke should exit, not accumulate inside.

## Materials & Improvisation
<!-- @field: materials -->

| Material | Source | Shelter improvement |
|----------|--------|---------------------|
| Brush/branches | Deadfall, live cutting | Block open sides of overhang |
| Grass/leaf litter | Surrounding ground | Floor insulation (15+ cm depth) |
| Flat stones | Local surface | Seal gaps in boulder clusters |
| Fire | Campfire at entrance | Predator deterrent, warmth, light |
| Thorny branches | Acacia, bramble | Entrance barrier in caves/overhangs |
| Bark sheets | Dead trees | Wind screen, rain deflector for open sides |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Immediate survival:** A natural shelter found in the first hour eliminates the most lethal short-term exposure risk. Hypothermia kills in hours; a rock overhang with fire eliminates it.
- **Predator defence:** Caves and overhangs reduce the attack perimeter from 360 degrees to a narrow front. One person with a fire at the entrance can defend a group of 10.
- **Energy conservation:** Building shelter from scratch costs 500-1,500 calories of labour. Finding a natural shelter costs walking energy only. The saved calories sustain you an extra day.
- **Extended occupation:** Good natural shelters support multi-day or seasonal camps. Water sources near caves at Sterkfontein enabled continuous occupation across thousands of years.
- **Social space:** Sheltered areas with overhead protection and fire become the social hub: food processing, tool making, teaching, and night-time discussion happen under cover.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

Tonight you need a roof and a wall. You do not have time to build either. Find them.

Look at the nearest rock face, cliff, or outcrop. Walk along its base. Rock overhangs, even shallow ones that extend 1 m from the wall, block rain and cut wind speed by two thirds. Deeper recesses that extend 2-3 m create liveable spaces. Clean the floor of sharp stones, check for animal signs, and claim it.

If no rock formations exist, find the densest tree cluster within walking distance. Three or four tightly spaced trees with interlocking canopy deflect 50-70% of rainfall. The centre of the cluster is windbreak on multiple sides.

If you find a cave, stop at the entrance. Listen. Throw a rock inside and wait a full minute. Nothing? Step in with light. Check the floor for fresh scat, bones, or claw marks on walls at waist height (leopard sign). Check the ceiling for loose rock. Check the slope of the floor: if it tilts inward, rainwater will flood you. Walk out and check the terrain above the entrance for drainage channels that could pour water in during storms.

Once you claim your shelter, do two things immediately. First, pile 15 cm of dry grass, leaves, or pine needles on the floor where you will sleep. The ground steals body heat at a rate that kills. Insulation stops it. Second, build a fire between you and the opening. The fire does three things: it warms you, it lights the entrance so you see what approaches, and it terrifies almost everything that might approach.

Do not go deep into caves without a reliable light source. Deep caves are completely dark and disorientation happens in minutes. Stay near the entrance where daylight and airflow reach.

A natural shelter found in the first hour of survival is worth more than a constructed shelter built in six hours of exhausting labour. Find it first. Improve it later.
