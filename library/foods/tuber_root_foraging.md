# Tuber and Root Foraging
<!-- @field: description -->
Underground storage organs - tubers, corms, rhizomes, and bulbs - represent the most calorie-dense, predictable, and drought-resistant food source available to early hominins on the African savanna. Unlike fruit, which is seasonal and ephemeral, underground organs persist through dry seasons, store starch and sugars in concentrated form, and remain accessible year-round. Paleoanthropologists estimate that tubers may have constituted 30 to 50 percent of the diet of early *Australopithecus* and *Homo* populations in East Africa, particularly during the dry season when fruit and game were scarce.

The shift toward a tuber-heavy diet is one of the most significant dietary transitions in human evolution. Richard Wrangham's "Cooking Hypothesis" (2009) and others have argued that the reliable caloric supply from starchy underground organs allowed hominins to sustain larger bodies and, eventually, larger brains. Even without fire, raw tubers provide substantial energy: the wild potato-yam (*Dioscorea* spp.) yields 100-150 kcal per 100g, the tigernut sedge (*Cyperus esculentus*) yields 460 kcal per 100g dry weight.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
Isotope analysis of hominid tooth enamel from East African fossil sites (Australopithecus sediba, ~2 Ma; Paranthropus boisei, ~2.3 Ma) reveals heavy C4 plant consumption consistent with sedge corms (*Cyperus* spp.) and grassy savanna plants. A 2014 study by Amanda Henry et al. (*PLOS ONE*) identified starch granules consistent with underground storage organs on Oldowan stone tools from Swartkrans, South Africa (~1.8 Ma), demonstrating that early *Homo* was actively processing tubers against stone surfaces.

The tigernut sedge (*Cyperus esculentus*), a semi-aquatic plant whose small starchy corms are rich in fat, fiber, and starch, has been proposed by researchers including Nathaniel Dominy (2012) as a "keystone food" for the *Paranthropus* lineage - explaining their robust jaw morphology adapted for crushing fibrous underground material. Modern wild-type tigernuts still grow abundantly in semi-arid East African lakeshores where early hominins concentrated.

Ethnographic data from the Hadza hunter-gatherers of Tanzania shows that women spend 2-4 hours daily excavating tubers (*//ekwa*, *tafabe*, and *shumwa* in Hadza terminology), providing 50-70% of total caloric intake during dry season. This mirrors what archaeological evidence suggests for Pliocene hominins.

## Identification and Selection
<!-- @field: procedure -->
The ability to locate and identify edible underground organs is a learned skill transmitted socially over years. The following are the most important signs:

**Surface indicators of underground organs:**

| Plant Feature | What it Indicates | How to Read It |
|---------------|-------------------|----------------|
| Yellowed or dying stem in dry season | Active storage organ below | Dig at the stem base, 5-25 cm deep |
| Star-shaped leaf rosette flat to ground | Likely corm (crocus-type) | Dig 8-15 cm directly below rosette center |
| Thick, fleshy stem base | Rhizome or tuber cluster | Probe soil outward from stem 10-30 cm radius |
| Sedge grass near dry watercourse | *Cyperus* corms (tigernut) | Excavate along root zone, corms at 5-15 cm |
| Characteristic smell when soil disturbed | Aromatic rhizome | Identify before consuming - not all are edible |

**Toxicity screening (critical):**

1. Crush a small amount and smell - sharp bitter smell suggests alkaloids (e.g., Dioscorea raw, *Colchicum* bulbs - DEADLY)
2. Touch to lip for 30 seconds - tingling or burning = reject
3. Chew a pea-sized piece, hold in mouth 5 minutes without swallowing - any burning = reject
4. Swallow a pea-sized amount, wait 8 hours - nausea, vomiting = reject
5. Eat 50g portion, wait 24 hours before consuming freely

**Safe priority species in East African savanna:**

| Species | Edible Part | Calories (per 100g raw) | Season |
|---------|-------------|--------------------------|--------|
| *Cyperus esculentus* (tigernut) | Corm | 460 kcal (dry) | Year-round |
| *Typha* spp. (cattail) | Rhizome, pollen | 80-100 kcal | Wet margins |
| *Dioscorea* spp. (wild yam) | Tuber | 100-150 kcal | Seasonal |
| *Hypoxis hemerocallidea* | Corm | 60-80 kcal | Dry season |
| *Raphionacme* spp. | Water tuber | 30-50 kcal + water | Dry season critical |

## Excavation Technique
<!-- @field: materials -->
The digging stick is the only required tool - a hardwood branch, 60-120 cm long, 2-4 cm diameter, with one end shaped to a blunt point against stone.

**Excavation sequence:**

1. Locate the stem or leaf cluster of the target plant
2. Clear surface debris to expose the stem base
3. Insert the digging stick at a 30-45 degree angle, 5 cm from the stem
4. Apply downward and outward pressure to loosen soil - do not simply stab downward (severs the tuber)
5. Work around the stem in a circle, progressively loosening the soil plug
6. Once the plug is loose, extract by gripping the stem and lifting, or reach in and pry upward with fingers
7. Brush soil from the organ and inspect for rot or mold before consuming

**Tools:**

| Item | Purpose | Primitive Alternative |
|------|---------|----------------------|
| Hardwood digging stick | Loosen and lever compacted soil | Any hardwood branch, fire-hardened tip preferable |
| Flat stone | Scrape dirt from organ surface | Any flat rock |
| Bark tray or leaf bundle | Carry excavated material | Large leaves folded into cone |
| Sharp flake | Peel or slice tuber | Any sharp-edged stone flake |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Dry season caloric baseline:** When fruit and game disappear for months, tubers provide the stable caloric foundation that keeps the group alive. A skilled forager can excavate 2-4 kg of tubers per hour in good ground, yielding 500-1500 kcal per hour of work.
- **Water content in drought:** Species like *Raphionacme* and wild yams contain 60-80% water by weight. During severe drought, excavating these organs provides critical hydration when surface water is absent.
- **Year-round predictability:** Unlike seasonal fruits, underground organs persist in place through dry seasons. A patch identified in one wet season can be re-harvested in subsequent years.
- **Group provisioning:** Tubers are portable and can be carried back to camp in quantity, enabling provisioning of young, elderly, and injured group members who cannot forage.

## Day 0 — Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0 -->
You have no food. The savanna is dry. You need calories.

Find a dry streambed or lakeshore margin. Look for sedge grass - the thin, triangular-stemmed grass that grows in dense clumps in moist ground depressions. Follow the dried bed until you see it. Dig 10 cm down directly beside the stem cluster. You are looking for small brown corms the size of a large marble (tigernut sedge) or fibrous yellowish clumps (wild yam). Extract them. Brush off dirt. Do the lip test - hold a small piece to your lower lip for 30 seconds. No burning? Chew a pea-sized piece without swallowing. No burning, no numbness? Swallow a small amount and wait. If you feel fine after 2 hours, eat freely.

A single hour of focused excavation in good ground will yield 1,000-2,000 calories. That is the difference between walking out tomorrow or not.
