# Bone Marrow Extraction
## Description
<!-- @field: description -->
Bone marrow is one of the most calorie-dense foods accessible to a hominin on the African savanna - a single femur from a large bovid (wildebeest, zebra) contains 200-400 grams of pure fat-rich marrow yielding 1,400-2,800 kcal. Unlike muscle meat, which spoils rapidly in tropical heat and is consumed quickly by competing carnivores, bone marrow remains protected inside the bone and accessible even from carcasses that have been defleshed by lions, leopards, and hyenas. Early hominins exploited this competitive gap: access to the caloric treasure locked inside bones that no other competitor could open.

This strategy - termed "power scavenging" by paleoanthropologist Henry Bunn - may have been the primary meat-access strategy for *Australopithecus* and early *Homo* before active hunting became viable. The key tool is the hammerstone: a dense, rounded cobble of hard rock used to deliver focused percussive force to crack open long bones and access the marrow cavity. This is among the oldest documented tool uses, preceding even flaked stone tools.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
The oldest evidence of hominin bone marrow extraction dates to 3.4 Ma at Dikika, Ethiopia, where Shannon McPherron et al. (2010, *Nature*) reported cut marks and percussion marks on bovid bones consistent with stone tool use - predating the first formally recognized Oldowan tools by nearly a million years. While the Dikika finds remain debated, clear evidence of systematic marrow extraction appears at Olduvai Gorge, Tanzania (1.8 Ma) and Koobi Fora, Kenya (1.9 Ma), where thousands of bone fragments bear the characteristic percussion marks, impact notches, and marrow cavity exposure typical of deliberate hammerstone cracking.

Analyses of the Olduvai fauna by Pat Shipman (1984) and later Henry Bunn (2001) showed that hominins accessed limb bones - femur, tibia, humerus - primarily for their marrow content, leaving characteristic cone-shaped percussion pits on the shaft and spiral fractures consistent with green bone impact. Large bovid limb bones appear at frequencies far exceeding their representation in the local fauna, indicating deliberate selection and transport to processing sites.

Isotope analysis of early hominin enamel (particularly *Paranthropus* and early *Homo* specimens from South Africa and Ethiopia) shows significant animal protein consumption beginning around 2.5-3.0 Ma, consistent with marrow exploitation as a major caloric contribution.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
Marrow extraction requires only a hammerstone - no other tool is necessary, though a sharp flake is useful for final marrow removal.

**Bone selection:**

| Bone Type | Marrow Quality | Cracking Difficulty | Best Source Animal |
|-----------|---------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Femur (thigh) | Excellent (fat-rich) | High (thick cortex) | Bovid, equid |
| Tibia (shin) | Excellent | Medium | Any large ungulate |
| Humerus (upper arm) | Good | Medium | Any large ungulate |
| Radius/Ulna | Good | Low (thinner cortex) | Any ungulate |
| Metacarpals/Metatarsals | Fair | Low | Most ungulates |
| Skull | Brain (similar nutrition) | Variable | Any |
| Rib | Poor (minimal marrow) | Low | Skip - not worth effort |

**Hammerstone selection:**

1. Choose a dense, hard stone without cracks - quartzite, basalt, or granite
2. Oval or spherical shape, 400-800 grams (comfortably gripped in one hand)
3. Test by striking one rock against another - a good hammerstone produces a clear ring, not a dull thud
4. Rounded edges reduce risk of injury to the striking hand
5. Keep 2-3 hammerstones at the processing site - if one chips or splits, work continues

**Extraction sequence:**

1. Stabilize the bone on a flat anvil stone or compacted earth surface
2. Identify the bone shaft (diaphysis) - the long, cylindrical middle section
3. Strike the shaft at a 45-60 degree angle with the hammerstone, using a sharp, controlled downward blow - not a glancing strike
4. Target the same point with 3-5 firm blows to develop an initial impact notch
5. The cortex (outer bone wall) will fracture inward in a cone shape - spiral fractures will propagate along the shaft
6. Once the first crack penetrates the cortex, rotate the bone 45 degrees and strike adjacent to the crack
7. Continue until the bone opens with a longitudinal split, exposing the marrow cavity
8. Extract marrow with a finger, thin stick, or bone splinter
9. Lick the bone cavity clean - the marrow adheres to the inner cortex surface

**From a partially defleshed carcass:**

1. Arrive at the carcass site with hammerstones in hand - do not spend time searching for stones at the site
2. Identify and detach the most marrow-rich limb bones first (femur, tibia) using a sharp flake
3. Work rapidly - other scavengers will arrive within 10-20 minutes of your presence
4. Extract on-site or carry bones to a safer, less exposed processing location
5. Process long bones before breaking small bones - prioritize caloric yield per time unit

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **High-calorie emergency food:** Marrow from two large bovid limb bones (femur + tibia) can provide 3,000-5,000 kcal - enough for an adult's full daily requirements from a carcass that has already been abandoned by large carnivores.
- **Competitive advantage over other scavengers:** Hyenas, vultures, jackals, and wild dogs cannot crack large long bones. By developing this capability, early hominins occupied a caloric niche inaccessible to all competing scavengers.
- **Brain extraction:** The same percussion technique applied to the skull opens the braincase, providing brain tissue (~1,400 kcal, rich in essential fatty acids including DHA linked to neurological development).
- **Dry season survival:** During lean periods when live prey is difficult to catch and fruit is absent, abandoned carcasses with intact long bones represent a reliable caloric resource that other species are leaving unused.

## Day 0  -  Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->
You found a carcass. The lions ate the meat last night. The vultures took the scraps this morning. There is nothing left visible.

Look at the legs. The large bones - the thigh, the shin - are still intact. Inside those bones is marrow.

Pick up a dense round stone before you approach the carcass. Find a flat stone on the ground near the site. Stabilize the leg bone across it.

Strike the shaft of the bone hard, at an angle. Not a glancing blow - a downward strike aimed at one spot. Four or five times in the same spot. You will hear the bone crack. It will split open along a spiral fracture.

Scoop out the yellow-white paste inside with a finger or a stick. It tastes like fat because it is pure fat. Eat it immediately.

Do this to both legs. If there is a skull, open it the same way at the back of the cranium. Brain tissue is inside.

You will leave this carcass with 2,000 to 4,000 calories. Everyone else left with nothing.

The round stone you brought made the difference.
