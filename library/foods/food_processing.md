# Food Processing

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

Food processing is the mechanical and thermal preparation of raw materials to make them edible, digestible, or safer to consume. Cracking nuts, smashing bones for marrow, pounding tough roots, stripping bark, peeling fruits, mashing insects, and removing toxins through soaking or leaching all fall under this category. Processing increases the caloric return from any food item by 30-60% because it physically breaks cell walls, denatures proteins, and gelatinises starches that the human gut cannot access in raw form. Richard Wrangham's "cooking hypothesis" (2009) argues that food processing, eventually including fire-based cooking, was the primary driver of human brain expansion by providing the caloric surplus required to fuel a metabolically expensive organ. But long before controlled fire, hominins processed food mechanically using stones, wooden implements, and their own teeth.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

At Kanjera South (Kenya, 2.0 Ma), Thomas Plummer found stone anvils with pitting consistent with repeated nut cracking alongside animal bone fragments showing percussion damage for marrow extraction. Both activities represent systematic food processing. At Olduvai Gorge (1.8 Ma), microwear analysis of stone flakes by Lawrence Keeley (1980) detected plant polish patterns indicating that some tools were used to process tubers and fibrous vegetation rather than meat alone. The 790,000 BP site at Gesher Benot Ya'aqov (Israel) provides the earliest firm evidence of cooking, with charred seeds and wood in concentrations consistent with hearths (Goren-Inbar, 2004). Among modern San Bushmen, women spend 2-3 hours daily processing mongongo nuts using hammerstone and anvil, extracting 600 kcal per session from a food that would be inaccessible without tools.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->

**Processing techniques by food type:**

| Food | Raw state | Processing method | Result |
|------|-----------|-------------------|--------|
| Nuts (hard shell) | Inedible, shell intact | Hammerstone on anvil | Cracked, kernel accessible |
| Long bones | Marrow sealed inside | Percussive fracture with heavy stone | Marrow exposed, 800 kcal/100g |
| Tubers | Tough, fibrous, sometimes toxic | Pounding, soaking, roasting | Softened, detoxified, 30-60% more digestible |
| Tough meat/sinew | Difficult to chew | Pounding between stones | Tenderised, chewable, faster digestion |
| Fibrous plants/bark | Indigestible raw | Stripping, soaking, pounding | Fibre separated, edible portion exposed |
| Seeds/grains | Hard coat, poor digestibility | Grinding between flat stones | Flour-like paste, highly digestible |
| Insects (large) | Exoskeleton, bitter | Removing head/legs/wings, mashing | Protein paste, reduced bitterness |

**Basic processing station setup:**

1. Find an anvil stone: flat, stable, larger than your hand, embedded in ground or heavy enough not to move.
2. Find a hammerstone: dense, rounded, 200-500g, fits comfortably in your dominant hand.
3. Clear the area around the anvil of debris. Processed food fragments scatter and you need to collect them.
4. Position yourself seated with the anvil between your legs or at comfortable arm height on a rock.
5. Place the food item in the centre of the anvil. Strike with controlled force. For nuts, one sharp blow. For bones, repeated heavy strikes on the thinnest section of the shaft.

**Detoxification through water processing:**

1. Slice or pound the food item into thin pieces to increase surface area.
2. Submerge in running water (stream) for 6-24 hours. Running water leaches water-soluble toxins faster than still water.
3. If no running water is available, soak in a container of still water, changing the water every 4-6 hours.
4. Test a small piece after processing using the standard edibility test (wrist, lip, tongue, wait).
5. Foods requiring leaching: acorns (tannins), bitter manioc (cyanide), cycad seeds (cycasin), some yam species (dioscorine).

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Caloric multiplication:** Processing increases usable calories from tubers by 30-60% and makes bone marrow (786 kcal/100g) accessible. A group that processes food extracts significantly more energy from the same foraging territory.
- **Dietary safety:** Many calorie-rich plant foods contain toxins that processing neutralises. Without leaching, acorns cause kidney damage. Without pounding, bitter manioc releases lethal doses of hydrogen cyanide.
- **Feeding dependents:** Pre-masticated and pounded food is the only way to feed infants past weaning age and individuals with dental injuries. Processing creates soft foods from hard inputs.
- **Food storage:** Dried, pounded meat (early pemmican equivalent) and cracked, dried nuts store longer than whole raw items. Processing is the first step toward food preservation.
- **Time efficiency:** A group with a dedicated processor at camp converts raw inputs from multiple foraging parties simultaneously, reducing overall food-to-mouth time.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

Everything you eat today can give you more nutrition if you process it first. Your gut is not designed for raw unprocessed food. It works, but it wastes 30-60% of the calories locked inside plant cell walls and tough protein structures.

Find two rocks. One flat and stable, one round and heavy in your hand. This is your food processing station. It weighs nothing to set up and it changes every meal.

Put a nut on the flat rock. Hit it with the round one. Not a timid tap. A sharp controlled blow. The shell cracks, the kernel comes out. That kernel has fat and protein your brain needs today.

Find a long bone from any carcass. Put it on your anvil stone and strike the thinnest part of the shaft with your heaviest hammerstone. It will split. Inside is marrow. Scoop it out with a stick or your finger. Eat it immediately. This is the highest calorie density food available to you without fire.

Got tubers? Pound them. Place the tuber on the anvil and smash it with the hammerstone until it is a fibrous mash. This breaks the cell walls and makes the starch accessible to your digestive enzymes. You just increased the caloric yield by a third. If you have fire, throw the pounded mass into the coals for 10 minutes. Now you doubled the yield.

If a plant food is bitter, that bitterness is usually a toxin. DO NOT ignore bitter taste. Slice the food thin, soak it in running water for a full day, changing the water if it is still. This leaches out most water-soluble poisons. Test again after soaking. Still bitter? Discard it.

Set up your processing station at camp in a central location. Whoever stays at camp processes whatever the foragers bring in. Raw materials come in, edible food goes out. This is a production line and it works the same way it worked three million years ago.
