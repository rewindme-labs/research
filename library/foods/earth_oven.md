# Earth Oven

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->
An open fire chars the outside of food and leaves the inside raw. It loses 70-80% of its heat to the air. It requires constant attention, cannot cook more than a few items simultaneously, and shuts down in rain or wind. The earth oven solves all of these problems with a hole in the ground and a pile of hot rocks.

The principle is simple: heat stones in a fire until they reach 300-500 degrees C. Transfer them into a lined pit. Place food on or between the stones. Cover the pit with earth. The soil acts as insulation, trapping the heat inside the sealed chamber. The food cooks by a combination of radiant heat from the stones, conducted heat from direct contact, and steam generated by moisture in the food and any added water. Temperatures inside the sealed pit stabilize at 150-200 degrees C and remain there for 4-8 hours without any additional fuel.

The result is slow, even, all-around cooking that renders tough tubers soft and fully digestible, converts collagen in bone and sinew into gelatin, and sterilizes meat through to the center without burning the surface. A single earth oven pit 60 cm across can cook 10-15 kg of food in one batch - enough to feed a group of 15-20 for an entire day - while the people who built it go off to forage, hunt, or sleep. No other cooking method available to early hominins offers this combination of capacity, fuel efficiency, and unattended operation.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
The earth oven is archaeologically invisible in its simplest form - a pit, some rocks, and food leave little trace after the organic material decomposes. However, fire-cracked rock (FCR) concentrations are among the most common features in Middle and Late Pleistocene sites, and many are interpreted as the remains of stone-boiling or pit-cooking activities. FCR occurs when stones heated to high temperatures and then rapidly cooled (by contact with moist food or water) fracture along internal flaws. Accumulations of angular, thermally fractured cobbles in defined spatial clusters are diagnostic.

The Klasies River Mouth site, South Africa (~120,000-60,000 ya, Hilary Deacon 1989), contains dense concentrations of fire-cracked rock in association with hearths and shellfish remains, suggesting organized cooking beyond simple roasting. The Pinnacle Point caves (164,000 ya, Curtis Marean 2007) show evidence of systematic heat application to marine resources.

Ethnographically, the earth oven is nearly universal. Polynesian cultures call it the *umu* or *hangi* - entire pigs, taro, and breadfruit cooked underground for community feasts. Australian Aboriginal peoples have used ground ovens for at least 30,000 years (documented by Rhys Jones and others). The *pachamanca* in the Andes, the *kalua* in Hawaii, and the New England clambake are all descendants of the same basic technology. The convergent invention across every continent except Antarctica confirms that it is not a single cultural tradition but a solution that any group with fire and rocks will eventually discover.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Step 1 - Dig the pit:**

| Pit size | Food capacity | Group size served |
|----------|--------------|-------------------|
| 40 cm diameter, 30 cm deep | 3-5 kg | 5-8 people |
| 60 cm diameter, 40 cm deep | 8-15 kg | 15-20 people |
| 100 cm diameter, 50 cm deep | 20-40 kg | 30+ people |

1. Select a location with stable, non-sandy soil. Sandy soil collapses when heated. Clay or packed earth works best
2. Dig with a digging stick and hands. Keep the excavated earth in a mound beside the pit - you will use it to seal the oven
3. The pit should be roughly hemispherical: wider at the top, curving to a rounded bottom. Sharp corners create cold spots

**Step 2 - Select and heat the stones:**

| Stone type | Suitability | Risk |
|-----------|------------|------|
| Dense river cobbles (basalt, granite) | Excellent | Low - heat evenly, retain heat for hours |
| Sandstone | Poor | HIGH - can explode when heated due to trapped moisture |
| Limestone | Acceptable | May crack but does not explode. Chemical changes above 800 C |
| Slate/shale | DANGEROUS | Layers trap moisture. Explosive fragmentation risk |
| Quartz/quartzite | Good | May crack but fragments are not dangerous |

**CRITICAL:** Never use stones taken from a river bottom or stream bed without testing. Waterlogged stones can contain trapped moisture that expands explosively when heated. Test by placing a single stone at the edge of the fire first. If it cracks with a sharp pop in the first 10 minutes, reject all stones from that source.

1. Collect 15-25 stones the size of a fist to a grapefruit. Dense, dry, heavy for their size
2. Build a hot fire in or adjacent to the pit. Stack the stones in the fire bed, surrounded by fuel
3. Burn a full hardwood fire for 1-2 hours until the stones glow red or orange in the coals. They need to reach 300-500 degrees C

**Step 3 - Load the oven:**

1. Rake out any unburned wood and excess ash from the pit, leaving the hot stones in the bottom
2. Place a layer of green leaves, grass, or seaweed over the stones. This creates steam and prevents food from contacting stone directly (which chars)
3. Place the food on the leaf layer. Large items (whole tubers, meat joints) on the bottom nearest the heat. Smaller items on top. Wrap delicate items (fish, small roots) in leaf packets
4. Add a second layer of green leaves or grass over the food
5. If additional moisture is desired, pour 200-500 mL of water over the leaf layer. It will flash to steam when it reaches the stones
6. Cover the entire pit with the excavated earth mound. Pack it firm. Seal any cracks or steam vents. The oven must be airtight for maximum heat retention

**Step 4 - Wait:**

| Food type | Cooking time (sealed pit) |
|-----------|--------------------------|
| Root vegetables (yam, taro, cassava) | 2-4 hours |
| Large meat joint (bone-in leg) | 4-6 hours |
| Whole fish (leaf-wrapped) | 1-2 hours |
| Shellfish | 30-60 minutes |
| Mixed load (tubers + meat) | 3-5 hours |

Do not open the oven to check. Every opening releases trapped heat and extends cooking time. Trust the process.

**Step 5 - Open and serve:**

1. Scrape the earth layer off carefully, starting from one edge
2. Remove the top leaf layer. Steam will escape - keep your face to the side
3. Extract food with sticks or by hand (edges will be cool enough; center will be hot)

## Materials

| Item | Purpose | Alternatives |
|------|---------|-------------|
| Digging stick | Excavating the pit | Hands and a flat stone work for small pits |
| Dense stones (15-25, fist to grapefruit size) | Heat storage medium | Must be dry, dense, non-porous |
| Hardwood fuel | Heating the stones | Any wood works but hardwood holds flame longer |
| Green leaves, grass, or seaweed | Steam layer and food protection | Bark strips, wet hide |
| Loose earth | Sealing the oven | The soil you excavated from the pit |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Batch cooking for groups:** One oven session cooks 10-15 kg of food for an entire day's group consumption. No one needs to tend the fire. The cook builds the oven in the morning, loads it, seals it, and goes foraging. Dinner is ready when the group returns
- **Caloric extraction from tubers:** Raw starchy tubers are 30-50% digestible. Slow underground cooking with steam breaks down cell walls and gelatinizes starch, raising digestibility to 85-95%. The same tuber yields nearly twice the usable calories cooked in an earth oven vs. eaten raw
- **Tough-meat processing:** Collagen in connective tissue, tendon, and bone begins converting to gelatin at 70 degrees C with sustained heat. An earth oven holds meat at 150-200 degrees C for 4-6 hours - long enough to render the toughest scavenged joint into soft, chewable food
- **Fuel efficiency:** A 2-hour fire heats stones that cook for 6 hours. The earth insulation loses heat at roughly 10-15 degrees C per hour. No other method stretches a single fire's energy output this far
- **Wet-weather cooking:** Once sealed, the oven is underground and immune to rain and wind. Open-fire cooking shuts down in heavy weather. Pit cooking does not

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->
You have 12 kg of tubers and a hind leg from a scavenged carcass. Your group is 14 people. An open fire will cook 2 kg at a time, requiring someone to sit and tend it for 3 hours while everyone else forages. You cannot afford to lose a forager to cooking duty.

Dig a pit. 60 cm across, 40 cm deep. Use your digging stick. Pile the earth beside the hole.

Collect 20 dense, dry river cobbles. Not from the water - from the dry bank above the waterline. Fist-sized to grapefruit-sized. Stack them in the pit. Build a fire on top of and around them. Burn hardwood for 90 minutes. The stones will glow dull red in the coal bed.

While the stones heat, collect two armloads of green leaves. Large, broad leaves are best. Grass works. Anything green and wet.

When the stones are glowing, rake out the burning wood and ash with a long stick. Leave the stones in the bottom. Drop a thick layer of green leaves over them. Steam will hiss immediately. Place the tubers on the leaf bed, packed tight. Put the meat leg on top, bone pointing up. Cover everything with the second armload of leaves.

Shovel the earth pile back over the top. Pack it with your hands. Seal every crack. No steam should escape. If you see a wisp, pack more earth on that spot.

Walk away. Go forage for 4 hours. The oven does not need you.

Come back. Scrape off the earth. Pull back the top leaves. Steam pours out. The tubers are soft through to the center - a stick slides into them with no resistance. The meat is falling off the bone. The connective tissue has dissolved into gelatin. The marrow in the bone has liquefied.

Feed 14 people from a hole in the ground that cooked itself while you were gone.
