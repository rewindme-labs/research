# Insect & Larval Foraging

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->
Entomophagy (eating insects) is not a survival novelty but the default ancestral human diet. Before spears, before traps, before fire-assisted hunting, insects and their larvae were the most reliably accessible concentrated animal protein on Earth. They require no tools to harvest, no fire to make edible in most cases, and they exist in extraordinary density in every terrestrial biome. A single termite mound can contain 1-3 million workers; one square meter of rotting log can contain hundreds of beetle larvae; a swarm of locusts can strip a field and simultaneously provide thousands of calories per hunter.

Insects averaged across species contain 40-70% protein by dry weight, 10-35% fat (including essential fatty acids), and significant micronutrients including iron, zinc, B12, and omega-3 fatty acids. Per kilogram of edible protein produced, insects require 12 times less feed than cattle, 4 times less than chickens, and emit 100 times fewer greenhouse gases. To a starving human with no other option, these statistics are irrelevant. What matters is that insects are always there, they cannot outrun you, and they do not fight back.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
The earliest direct evidence of human insect consumption is associated with Oldowan tool sites in Africa dating to approximately 1.9 million years ago, where modified bone tools show wear patterns consistent with extracting termite larvae from mounds - nearly identical to the probe-and-extract technique observed in chimpanzees today. By the time of *Homo sapiens*, insect foraging was a universal behavior.

Archaeological evidence from Hinds Cave, Texas (c. 8,000 BCE) shows large-scale consumption of sotol moth larvae and other insects, preserved in coprolites. The Hebrew Bible (Leviticus 11:22) specifically permits consumption of four species of locust, indicating that insect eating was normalized enough to require explicit religious regulation. Aristotle wrote in *Historia Animalium* (350 BCE) that the larvae of the cicada were "most savory when the creature is about to become a nymph" - he was describing the culinary preference for a specific insect developmental stage.

In traditional cultures across Africa, Asia, Latin America, and Oceania, insect consumption was never abandoned. The Paiute of the Great Basin harvested pine moths and alkali flies in industrial quantities. The Aztecs consumed algae cakes (*tecuitlatl*) mixed with insect eggs. In 19th-century Japan, boiled silkworm pupae (*sanagi*) remained a standard market food. Today, 2 billion people in 80+ countries regularly consume insects as part of their diet.

## Ingredients & Preparation
<!-- @field: preparation -->
**Identification - highest-value targets:**

| Insect Type | Where Found | Season | Preparation | Calories/100g |
|-------------|------------|--------|-------------|---------------|
| Termites (workers/alates) | Termite mounds, rotting logs, clay soil | Year-round (workers); rainy season (alates) | Raw, roasted, or sun-dried | 100-120 kcal |
| Beetle larvae (Coleoptera) | Rotting logs, under bark, in soil | Year-round | Raw, roasted, boiled | 130-160 kcal |
| Grasshoppers / locusts | Tall grass, crop fields | Summer/dry season | Roasted, sun-dried (remove wings, legs) | 100-120 kcal |
| Caterpillars (hairless) | Leaves, tree bark | Spring/summer | Boiled, roasted (squeeze gut contents first) | 90-120 kcal |
| Ant eggs/pupae | Under flat rocks, in ant colonies | Spring | Raw, cooked | 100 kcal |
| Cicada nymphs | Soil around tree roots, emerging at night | Species-dependent | Roasted whole | 120 kcal |
| Silkworm pupae | Cocoons in mulberry trees | Summer | Boiled or roasted | 100 kcal |
| Cricket | Under rocks, logs, leaf litter | Year-round in warm climates | Roasted, boiled | 120 kcal |

**Harvesting techniques:**

*Termites:*
1. Insert a thin stick, straw, or your finger into a termite mound gallery opening. Wait 30-60 seconds for soldiers to grab onto it, then withdraw slowly.
2. For winged alates (flying termites emerging after rain): place a container under a mound entrance. Shake the mound. Alates emerge by the hundreds.
3. Dig into a rotting log to find larvae (large, white, C-shaped grubs of longhorn beetles) - the single highest-calorie primitive food per unit of effort.

*Grasshoppers/locusts:*
1. Collect at dawn when cold and sluggish; at night, sweep tall grass with both arms. In large swarms, simply scoop into a container.
2. Remove wings and hind legs (spines can scratch throat). Remove the gut tube by grasping the head and pulling slowly - the gut pulls through cleanly.
3. Roast on hot coals or a flat heated rock for 2-3 minutes until crispy.

*Beetle larvae (grubs):*
1. Split open rotting logs systematically. Large white grubs (Cerambycidae, Scarabaeidae) are the prize.
2. Edible raw or roasted over coals for 3-5 minutes. Raw has more moisture; roasted is crunchier and safer if concerned about gut parasites.

**Safety rules:**
- AVOID: brightly colored insects (warning coloration = likely toxic), hairy caterpillars (urticating hairs cause severe allergic reactions), insects that smell like bitter almonds (cyanide compounds), any insect at the site of a pesticide application.
- SAFE indicators: dull brown/black/green coloration, found in large aggregations (predators avoid toxic species; mass aggregations indicate palatability), related species are known edible.
- Cooking destroys most parasites. Raw consumption of grasshoppers and grubs is generally safe, but boiling or roasting reduces risk essentially to zero.

## Preservation & Storage
<!-- @field: storage -->
- **Roasting:** Spread on a flat hot rock over coals. 3-5 minutes until crispy. Product is shelf-stable for several days in dry conditions.
- **Sun-drying:** Spread insects in a thin layer on flat rocks in direct sun. Turn once after 4-6 hours. Fully dried insects (wings and legs removed) store for weeks.
- **Smoking:** Hang over a low smoky fire. The combined drying and smoke compounds extend shelf life significantly and add flavor.
- **Pounding:** Dried insects can be ground with a grinding stone into a protein-dense flour that mixes into porridge, flatbread, or any starchy base.

Fresh insects begin to decompose within hours in warm conditions. Prioritize processing within the same day of harvest.

Insects are particularly valuable as a source of **complete protein** (all essential amino acids), omega-3 fatty acids (especially in caterpillars and grubs), and chitin (a prebiotic fiber that feeds gut bacteria). Iron in insects is in heme form (highly bioavailable), making them superior to most plant iron sources for preventing anemia.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->
You have been out of food for 24 hours. Hunting will take days to pay off. Foraging edible plants requires identification time you do not have. But right now, under any log within 30 meters of where you are standing, there are calories waiting to be picked up with your fingers.

**The first thing to do:** Find a rotting log - brown, soft, beginning to crumble. Not freshly fallen (too hard, few larvae), not fully composted (too far gone). The in-between stage, where the outer surface is starting to soften and peel. Roll it over. Pull the bark away. Look for large, white, C-shaped grubs (beetle larvae) ranging from fingernail-sized to finger-sized. These are your highest-calorie immediate food source, 130-160 kcal per 100g of clean tissue.

You can eat them raw. The taste is mild, slightly nutty, sometimes milky. If the idea is unacceptable, skewer 3-4 on a thin stick and hold over coals for 3 minutes until golden. They will taste like crispy bacon fat.

**If you are near grassland or any field of tall dry grass:** Sweep both arms through the grass at dawn when insects are cold and sluggish. You will collect dozens of grasshoppers per minute. Remove the large hind legs (they have backward-facing spines), twist off the wings, pull the head slowly to extract the gut tube, and roast on a flat hot rock for 2-3 minutes. A handful (100g) provides more protein than a chicken egg.

**Near any termite mound:** Push a thin stick into a gallery entrance and wait. Within 60 seconds, soldier termites will grip the stick. Pull slowly. You can harvest hundreds this way in 10 minutes. They are edible raw - sharp, slightly acidic taste. Roasted, they taste like popcorn. Traditional Congolese cuisine fries them in their own body fat with salt.

Do not be precious about this. All the protein you need on Day 1 is in the soil, under logs, in mounds, and in the grass around you. The barrier is entirely psychological. Overcome it once, and you will never look at a rotting log the same way again.
