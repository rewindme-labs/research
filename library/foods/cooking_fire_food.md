# Cooking (Fire-Based Food Preparation)

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

Cooking is the application of heat to food to make it safer, more digestible, and more nutritious. Raw meat carries parasites and bacteria. Raw tubers contain resistant starch that passes through the gut undigested. Raw legumes contain lectins that cause severe vomiting. Heat solves all three problems. Cooking denatures proteins (making them 30-50% more bioavailable), gelatinizes starch (increasing caloric extraction by 20-40%), kills pathogens (Salmonella dies at 74C, Trichinella at 77C), and destroys anti-nutrients (trypsin inhibitors, lectins, cyanogenic glycosides). The Maillard reaction (amino acids + sugars at 140-165C) produces the flavour compounds that make cooked food appealing. Richard Wrangham's "cooking hypothesis" argues that controlled cooking was the key driver of human brain expansion, providing the caloric surplus needed to support a metabolically expensive 1400g brain.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

The earliest evidence of cooking is debated. Hearth deposits at Wonderwerk Cave (South Africa, c. 1.0 Ma) contain burned bone fragments and ash layers suggesting repeated fire use for food processing. Qesem Cave (Israel, c. 400,000 BP) preserves burned animal bones in controlled hearth contexts associated with Homo sapiens ancestors. By the time of Neanderthals (300,000-40,000 BP), cooking was universal - dental calculus from Shanidar Cave (Iraq) and El Sidron (Spain) shows cooked starch granules. The earliest evidence of boiling (using heated stones dropped into water containers) dates to roughly 25,000 BP at European Upper Paleolithic sites. Earth-oven cooking (heated stones in a pit) appears independently across Polynesia, the Americas, and Australia, reflecting convergent discovery of indirect-heat methods.

## Ingredients & Preparation
<!-- @field: preparation -->

**Core cooking methods (no pots, no metal):**

| Method | Setup | Best for | Temperature | Time |
|--------|-------|----------|-------------|------|
| Direct roasting | Meat on a stick over coals | Small cuts, fish, tubers | 200-300C surface | 10-30 min |
| Ember cooking | Wrap in leaves, bury in coals | Root vegetables, eggs, small fish | 150-200C | 20-60 min |
| Stone-slab grilling | Flat stone heated in fire, food placed on top | Thin-sliced meat, flatbread, insects | 180-250C surface | 5-15 min |
| Earth oven (hangi/imu) | Pit with heated stones, food layered, covered with earth | Large cuts, whole animals, tough roots | 100-150C | 2-8 hours |
| Stone boiling | Drop fire-heated stones into water in bark/hide/stomach container | Soups, bone broth, grain porridge | 100C | 30-120 min |
| Smoking | Hang food above low, smoky fire (green wood) | Meat preservation, fish | 50-80C | 4-24 hours |
| Ash baking | Wrap dough/tuber in leaves, bury in hot ash | Flatbread, yams, taro | 150-200C | 15-45 min |

**Direct roasting procedure:**

1. Build fire, let it burn down to a deep coal bed (30-60 min).
2. Push flames aside - cook over coals, not flames. Flames cause charring.
3. Skewer meat on a green (living) hardwood stick 1-2 cm thick.
4. Hold 15-20 cm above coals, rotate quarter-turn every 2-3 minutes.
5. Internal temperature must reach 74C minimum for poultry, 63C for whole cuts of red meat, 74C for ground/processed meat.
6. Test: pierce thickest part - juices should run clear, not pink. Meat should be grey-brown throughout.

**Stone boiling procedure:**

1. Collect 10-15 river cobbles, fist-sized (avoid limestone - it can explode).
2. Heat stones in a fire for 30-45 minutes until glowing.
3. Prepare a container: bark trough, animal stomach, hollowed log, or a hole lined with animal hide.
4. Fill container with water.
5. Use two green sticks as tongs to transfer hot stones into the water.
6. Water will begin boiling within 1-2 minutes per stone.
7. Add food (meat chunks, roots, greens, crushed grain).
8. Replace cooled stones with fresh hot ones every 10-15 minutes.
9. A sustained boil for 20-30 minutes is sufficient for most foods.

**Earth oven procedure:**

1. Dig a pit 60 cm deep, 80 cm wide.
2. Line the bottom with fist-sized rocks.
3. Build a large fire on top of the rocks and burn for 2-3 hours.
4. Remove fire remnants, leaving scorching-hot rocks.
5. Layer: green leaves (banana, taro, seaweed), then food, then more leaves.
6. Cover with earth mounded 15-20 cm above ground level.
7. Leave sealed for 4-8 hours depending on food volume.
8. Excavate carefully - steam will escape.

## Preservation & Storage
<!-- @field: storage -->

- **Cooked meat shelf life (tropical):** 8-12 hours without refrigeration. Consume immediately or smoke/dry.
- **Smoked meat:** 1-4 weeks depending on thickness and humidity.
- **Cooked roots/tubers:** 24-48 hours.
- **Bone broth:** 12-24 hours. Reboil to extend by another 12 hours.
- **Sign of spoilage:** Sour smell, slime texture, green/grey discoloration. Do not eat.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

You have fire. Now use it to transform your food situation. Raw meat gives you roughly half its potential calories because your gut cannot fully digest uncooked protein. Raw tubers pass through your system largely intact. Cooking changes the equation by 30-50%.

Start with direct roasting. Let your fire burn to coals - no flames. Skewer meat chunks on a green stick (do not use dry wood - it catches fire). Hold 15 cm above the coals and rotate slowly. The meat is done when juices run clear and the inside is grey-brown, not pink. This is not about taste - it is about killing parasites. A single Trichinella larva in undercooked pork can cause weeks of muscle pain and potentially death.

For roots and tubers, wrap them in large green leaves and push them into the edge of the coal bed. Cover lightly with ash. Check after 30 minutes - pierce with a stick, and if it slides through easily like soft clay, the starch is gelatinized and ready. Pull out, peel off charred leaves, eat. You just doubled the calories you extract from that tuber compared to eating it raw.

For boiling without a pot, use stone boiling. Collect 10 fist-sized river cobbles. Heat them in your fire for 30 minutes until they glow. Dig a small depression, line it with a large piece of bark or animal hide, fill with water. Transfer hot stones in with two sticks used as chopsticks. The water will boil violently. Add meat scraps, crushed bones (for marrow fat), greens, anything edible. Keep replacing cooled stones with hot ones. After 30 minutes of sustained boil, you have a calorie-dense broth that extracts nutrients from materials you could never chew raw - bone marrow, tendons, cartilage.

Cook everything. The only exceptions are ripe fruits, some leafy greens you have confirmed safe, and clean water you have already boiled. In a world without antibiotics, food-borne infection is a death sentence. Heat is your medicine.
