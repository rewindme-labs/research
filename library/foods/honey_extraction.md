# Honey Extraction

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

Honey extraction is the location of wild bee nests and the retrieval of honey, beeswax, and bee larvae, one of the most calorie-dense and nutrient-rich wild food sources available. Honey provides 304 kcal per 100 g of pure carbohydrate energy with trace minerals and antimicrobial compounds. Bee larvae add protein and fat. The practice requires knowledge of bee species behaviour, nest location strategies, techniques for suppressing defensive stinging, and timing of extraction to minimise damage to the colony for future harvesting. Chimpanzees at Loango (Gabon) use tool sets of up to 5 different implements to access bee nests (Boesch, 2009): a thick pounding stick to break the entrance, a thin perforator to pierce internal chambers, and a dipping wand to extract honey. The mutualistic relationship between humans and the greater honeyguide bird (Indicator indicator) represents one of the most remarkable interspecies cooperations in nature: the bird leads humans to bee nests in exchange for access to wax and larvae exposed during extraction.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

The 8,000-year-old Cueva de la Arana rock painting (Spain) depicts a human figure climbing a ladder to collect honey from a cliff-face nest, confirming that systematic honey collection is an ancient practice. However, evidence from chimpanzee tool-assisted honey extraction and the deep evolutionary relationship with honeyguide birds suggests the practice extends to the earliest hominins. Claire Spottiswoode's playback experiments in Mozambique (2016) demonstrated that Yao honey-hunters use a specific "brr-hm" call to recruit honeyguide birds, and birds using this call increased the hunter's probability of finding a nest by a factor of 3. The honeyguide relationship exists across sub-Saharan Africa and may represent co-evolved mutualism spanning millions of years.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->

**Locating bee nests:**

| Method | Reliability | Range |
|--------|-------------|-------|
| Follow honeyguide bird | Very high (triples success rate) | 500 m-2 km |
| Watch bee flight lines at water sources | High | 200-500 m from water |
| Listen for buzzing near tree hollows | Moderate | 5-20 m |
| Look for nest entrance (small dark hole with traffic) | Moderate | Visual range |
| Check known productive trees seasonally | High (repeat harvesting) | Home range |

**Bee behaviour reading:**

1. At a water source, watch which direction bees fly when departing with water. They fly directly toward the nest. Follow that line.
2. Laden bees (carrying pollen or water) fly slower and lower. Following their flight line with your eyes narrows down nest direction.
3. Near a suspected nest tree, stand still and watch for bee traffic entering/exiting a small hole. Nest entrances are typically 2-4 cm diameter.

**Extraction procedure:**

1. Prepare a smoke bundle: green leaves wrapped around a glowing coal or small fire, bound with bark strip or vine. The bundle should produce thick, slow smoke, not flames.
2. Approach the nest from below if in a tree. Smoke rises, so hold the smoke source below the entrance and let it fill the cavity.
3. Wait 3-5 minutes. Smoke suppresses bee aggression by triggering a gorging response (bees fill with honey in preparation for possible hive abandonment), making them docile.
4. Widen the entrance if needed using a stout stick as a chisel. Do not destroy the entire cavity.
5. Reach in or use a long stick to break off honeycomb sections. Pull out comb, shake off remaining bees, and place in a bark container.
6. Take no more than half the comb. Leaving half ensures the colony survives and replenishes for future harvesting.
7. Leave the wax and empty comb near the base of the tree if honeyguide birds led you. This maintains the mutualistic relationship.

**Sting management:**

| Action | Effectiveness |
|--------|--------------|
| Thick smoke at nest entrance | Reduces stinging by 80-90% |
| Cover head and neck (leaf wrapping) | Protects most vulnerable areas |
| Quick, calm movements | Fewer sting triggers than erratic swatting |
| Scrape stingers (do not squeeze) | Reduces venom injection by 50% |
| Mud paste on stings | Reduces swelling and pain |
| If swarmed: walk away steadily, do not run into brush | Bees disengage at 50-100 m |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Energy boost:** Honey provides immediate high-energy carbohydrates. 500 g of honey delivers 1,500 kcal, enough to sustain intense activity for a full day. No other wild food matches this energy density in readily digestible form.
- **Wound treatment:** Honey's natural hydrogen peroxide content, low pH, and high osmolarity give it antimicrobial properties. Applied to wounds, it inhibits bacterial growth and promotes healing. Documented in both ancient Egyptian medicine and modern clinical studies.
- **Beeswax utility:** Harvested beeswax waterproofs containers, seals tool hafting joints, and serves as fuel for sustained light/heat.
- **Bee larvae nutrition:** Larvae are 37% protein and 13% fat, providing nutritional diversity that honey alone lacks.
- **Sustainable resource:** A colony that retains 50% of its comb rebuilds within weeks. Properly managed wild hives provide recurring harvests season after season.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

Honey is the highest energy food you can find without hunting. 500 grams gives you 1,500 calories of instant fuel. Finding it is the challenge.

Go to the nearest water source in the morning. Sit and watch for bees. They come to drink, especially in dry weather. When a bee finishes drinking, it lifts off and flies in a direct line toward its nest. Follow that compass heading. Walk 200 m in that direction and look for more bees going the same way. You are getting closer.

Near the nest tree, you will hear buzzing and see bees entering and exiting a small hole, usually in a hollow trunk or branch cavity. Stand still and confirm the location before approaching.

Make a smoke bundle. Take a handful of green leaves. Wrap them around a glowing coal from your fire. Bind loosely with a strip of bark. The bundle should produce thick white smoke without catching fire.

Hold the smoke below the nest entrance. Smoke rises and fills the cavity. Wait three to five minutes. The bees calm down. They are not gone but they are manageable.

Pry the entrance wider with a sturdy stick. Reach in and break off chunks of honeycomb. Pull them out, shake bees off gently, and place in a bark tray. Take no more than half of what is in there. The colony needs the other half to survive and rebuild. You want this nest to be productive next month.

If a honeyguide bird chatters at you and flies in short hops ahead of you, follow it. These birds lead humans to bee nests because they cannot open the nests themselves. They eat the wax and larvae you expose. When you finish, leave the empty comb and wax scraps at the base of the tree. This keeps the partnership alive.

If you get stung, scrape the stinger out sideways with your fingernail or a flat stone edge. Do not pinch and pull because that squeezes more venom in. Rub mud on the sting for pain relief. If you are being swarmed, walk away steadily in a straight line. Do not run into bushes, the bees follow and you get tangled. They give up after 50-100 m.
