# Carrion Detection

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

Carrion detection is the skill of locating animal carcasses through environmental signals: circling vultures, predator feeding sounds, scent carried by wind, insect swarm behaviour, and behavioural cues from other scavengers. For early hominins who lacked the speed to hunt large game and the weapons for reliable kills, scavenging was a critical protein acquisition strategy. A zebra killed by a lion provides 50,000-80,000 calories of meat and marrow, most of which the lion abandons after eating its fill. The hominin who arrives first after the predator departs (or who drives the predator off) gains massive caloric return for minimal energy investment. But the window is narrow: hyenas, jackals, and vultures compete aggressively for scavenging opportunities. Speed of detection determines success.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

At FLK Zinj (Olduvai Gorge, 1.75 Ma), butchery marks on large ungulate bones analysed by Henry Bunn (1981) showed that stone tool cut marks overlay carnivore tooth marks on some bones, indicating that hominins accessed carcasses after predator kills. However, cut marks underlay tooth marks on other bones, suggesting early access before carnivores. This mixed pattern indicates competitive scavenging. At Koobi Fora (Kenya, 1.5 Ma), assemblages dominated by limb bones (high marrow content) with few axial bones suggest selective transport: hominins grabbed the most calorie-dense parts and left before predators returned. Ethnographic evidence from the Hadza (Tanzania) documents active scavenging: Hadza hunters follow vulture descents to locate lion kills and routinely confront lions to steal carcasses, using group display and fire brands (O'Connell, Hawkes & Blurton Jones, 1988).

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->

**Detecting a carcass from distance:**

| Signal | Range | Reliability | What it means |
|--------|-------|-------------|---------------|
| Vulture spiral (circling column) | 5-15 km visible | Very high | Large animal dead or dying below |
| Vulture descent (dropping from height) | 2-5 km | Very high | Carcass confirmed, vultures landing |
| Predator sounds (growling, crunching) | 200-500 m | High | Active feeding, carcass present |
| Scent (decay, blood) | 50-500 m downwind | Moderate | Carcass present, age indicated by smell strength |
| Fly swarm (dense black cloud) | 50-200 m | High | Carcass present, possibly older |
| Marabou storks gathered (ground) | 500 m-2 km | High | Large carcass, predators may have left |
| Jackals/hyenas moving in one direction | 1-3 km | Moderate | Following scent trail to a kill |

**Approach protocol (danger hierarchy):**

1. Scan from 200+ m. Identify: is a predator still on the carcass? If a lion, leopard, or bear is feeding, do not approach without a group of 5+ armed adults and fire.
2. If vultures and small scavengers only: approach quickly. They flee from humans. Speed matters because hyenas arrive within 10-30 minutes of a vulture concentration.
3. Assess carcass freshness: body temperature (warm = very fresh), eye condition (clear = fresh, cloudy = 12+ hours), abdominal bloating (none = fresh, swollen = 24+ hours), maggot presence (none = under 12 hours, visible = 24+ hours in warm climate).
4. Harvest in priority order: liver and organs first (highest nutrition, spoil fastest), then hindquarter muscle, then marrow-bearing long bones. Cut and carry, do not process at the carcass site.
5. Leave within 15-20 minutes. Prolonged presence at a carcass attracts every predator and scavenger in the area.

**Vulture reading guide:**

| Vulture behaviour | What it indicates |
|-------------------|-------------------|
| High circling (thermal riding) | Searching, no carcass found yet |
| Tight spiral, descending | Carcass spotted, confirmed |
| Sitting on ground, wings spread | Recent arrival, waiting turns or drying wings |
| Fighting on ground | Active feeding, carcass has accessible meat |
| Sitting in trees, full crops | Already fed, carcass may be stripped |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Caloric windfall:** A single scavenged large ungulate carcass provides 50,000-80,000 kcal, enough to feed a group of 10 for 3-5 days. No other foraging activity offers comparable return per event.
- **Fat access:** Predators consume muscle and organ meat but often leave marrow-bearing long bones, brain in the skull, and subcutaneous fat. These are the highest-quality energy sources and the ones hominins could uniquely access with stone tools.
- **Low energy cost:** Walking 3-5 km to a carcass detected by vulture-watching costs 150-300 kcal. The return can be 10,000+ kcal. This is among the highest return-on-investment activities available.
- **Predator intelligence:** Regular scavenging builds detailed knowledge of predator behaviour: kill patterns, feeding duration, abandonment timing, and territory. This intelligence protects the group and improves hunting strategy.
- **Ecological integration:** Hominins inserting themselves into the scavenging guild (vultures, hyenas, jackals) occupy a unique niche: too smart and too social for other scavengers to dominate, capable of arriving before competitors using cognitive rather than olfactory detection.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

Watch the sky. Large birds circling in a tight column are vultures riding a thermal directly above something dead. If they start descending, something large has died within the last few hours. That is your signal. Move toward it now, because every other scavenger within 5 km received the same signal.

Go in a group of four or more. Carry cutting tools and something to carry meat in. Bring a fire brand if you have one. Move quickly.

When you can see the carcass, stop at 200 m and assess. If a large predator is still feeding, wait. Lions generally feed for 30-60 minutes then rest nearby. You can potentially drive a resting lion off a carcass with a group display of 5+ shouting people with fire and thrown rocks. You cannot drive a feeding lion from anything. Leopards will drag kills into trees and you will never get them.

If only vultures and small scavengers are present, move in fast. They flee from approaching humans. Get to the carcass and start cutting immediately.

Priority one: liver and organs. They have the highest nutrient density and spoil the fastest. Cut them free and hand to your carrier.

Priority two: hindquarter muscle. Cut along the joint lines to remove the largest pieces fastest. Do not try to debone perfectly. Speed matters more than efficiency.

Priority three: long bones. Crack them at the site if you have a hammerstone, or carry them back to camp. Each long bone holds 200-400 calories of marrow that nothing else at the carcass can access.

You have 15 minutes. After that, hyenas arrive in numbers you cannot manage. Grab what you have and go. Walk back to camp at steady pace. Do not run. Running with meat attracts pursuit.

Make this a systematic practice. Every morning, spend 2 minutes scanning the sky from the highest point near camp. Learn what vulture behaviour looks like from your location. Within a week you will spot the difference between searching vultures (wide, high circles) and descending vultures (tight spiral, dropping altitude) without conscious effort.
