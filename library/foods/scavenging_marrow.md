# Scavenging & Bone Marrow Extraction

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->
Scavenging is the opportunistic acquisition of animal flesh and nutrients from carcasses killed by other predators or by natural causes. It was the first form of animal protein consumption practiced by hominins, predating hunting by at least 1.5 million years. Bone marrow extraction is scavenging's most calorie-dense technique: using a hammerstone to fracture large mammal bones and access the lipid-rich marrow cavity, which is often available even after larger predators have consumed all accessible meat.

Bone marrow is 50-96% fat by wet weight, with a caloric density of approximately 780 kcal per 100g of pure marrow - more calorie-dense than most seeds and nuts. For a brain requiring 20% of daily caloric intake and a body under extreme physical stress, this concentrated fat was likely the prehistoric equivalent of a modern energy gel. The argument that increased access to marrow fat drove early hominid brain expansion remains one of the most compelling hypotheses in paleoanthropology.

The key skill is not consuming the carcass itself (often stripped by primary predators) but correctly reading the scene to determine: (1) whether it is safe to approach, (2) whether sufficient nutritional value remains, and (3) how to maximize caloric extraction from what is left.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
The oldest direct evidence of hominin scavenging comes from Dikika, Ethiopia, dated to 3.39 million years ago: cut marks on animal bones made by stone tools, discovered in 2010, belong to *Australopithecus afarensis* (the species of "Lucy") and push the origin of meat-eating and tool use back 800,000 years earlier than previously established. The bones of a large bovid and a horse-like animal show marks consistent with deliberate flesh removal by a stone flake.

At Olduvai Gorge, Tanzania, the Oldowan assemblage (1.8-1.2 Ma) contains thousands of cut-marked bones alongside hammerstones used for percussion to split long bones and access marrow. Analysis of which bones are marked (the proximal ends of limb bones, where marrow density is highest) demonstrates that early hominins systematically prioritized high-fat marrow over muscle meat - behavior consistent with a secondary scavenger arriving at a carcass after carnivores had removed most flesh.

The late-arrival hypothesis (humans scavenging after lions, hyenas, and vultures) was challenged by 1990s excavations at Olduvai, which showed hominin cut marks beneath carnivore tooth marks in many specimens, indicating that hominins sometimes reached carcasses before carnivores. Competitive scavenging by small groups throwing stones to drive off lions from a fresh kill may have been a key early behavior.

## Ingredients & Preparation
<!-- @field: preparation -->
**Reading the scene - approach assessment:**

| Signal | Meaning | Action |
|--------|---------|--------|
| Circling vultures at height | Carcass detected, predator probably still present | Wait, observe from distance |
| Vultures spiraling down and landing | Predator likely departed; safe to approach | Move in with group |
| Marabou storks and jackals at carcass | Secondary scavengers; large predators gone | Approach with noise to drive off |
| No birds, one large predator visible | Do not approach | Move on |
| Strong meat/decay smell with no birds | Old carcass, advanced decomposition | Bones may still have marrow; flesh not usable |
| Fresh blood smell, no birds yet | Very fresh; predator likely within 500m | Dangerous; group approach with noise |

**Safety assessment of the carcass:**

- **Fresh (killed <6 hours):** Flesh is safe if cooked. Smell: fresh blood. Flies just beginning to land.
- **1-2 days:** Surface flesh may be spoiled in hot climates; deep muscle near bone still usable if cooked thoroughly. Smell: sour, beginning fermentation.
- **3-5 days (hot climate):** Flesh unsafe without thorough cooking. Maggots present. Bones and marrow still fully usable.
- **1+ week:** Flesh inedible. Bones intact for marrow extraction.
- **AVOID:** Bloated carcass with greenish discoloration of skin (botulinum toxin risk); carcass of an animal with obvious disease signs (sores, unusual discharge); carcass in stagnant water.

**Marrow extraction procedure:**

1. Identify the highest-value bones: femur (thighbone), tibia, humerus (upper forearm). These have the largest marrow cavities. A single femur from a wildebeest contains 150-250g of marrow.

2. If meat remains on the bone, strip it first with an edge tool (sharpened stone flake). Even partially decomposed meat can be cooked safely if brought to vigorous boil for 10+ minutes.

3. Find a hammerstone: a dense, rounded river cobble weighing 0.5-1.5 kg. Find the natural weakest point of the bone (slightly flattened sections, existing crack lines).

4. Place the long bone on a flat, stable stone anvil. Strike the bone sharply at the mid-shaft with the hammerstone using a controlled overhead blow. Do not use glancing strikes - direct perpendicular impact splinters the bone cleanly.

5. Two to four strikes will fracture the mid-shaft diaphysis. Pull the bone apart. The marrow appears as a yellow (if fresh and fat-rich) or darker (if the individual was malnourished) cylindrical column inside.

6. Scoop or suck the marrow directly. Alternatively, place bone fragments marrow-side down on a flat rock over fire; the marrow melts and drips out for collection.

7. Do not discard the bone fragments. Boil them for 2-3 hours to extract bone broth: a collagen-rich liquid containing dissolved minerals, some remaining fat, and sufficient gelatin to be nutritionally significant.

## Preservation & Storage
<!-- @field: storage -->
- Marrow rendered by heat stores for days to weeks in a sealed container (gut sealed with resin, hollow bone capped with bark).
- Cooked meat from a carcass can be preserved by smoking or sun-drying within hours of cooking.
- Bone broth (from boiling split bones) stores for 24 hours without refrigeration; re-boil daily to prevent spoilage.
- Rendering animal fat from marrow and fatty tissue creates tallow, which stores for weeks to months.

### Priority order of consumption from a carcass:
1. **Liver** (most nutrient-dense organ, consume first as it spoils fastest)
2. **Marrow** (most calorie-dense, accessible even on old carcasses)
3. **Brain** (high in DHA; consume within 24h of death)
4. **Muscle meat** (protein; cook thoroughly)
5. **Bone broth** (minerals and collagen from any bones)

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->
You do not have to hunt today. Something else already did it for you.

Look for vultures. From any elevated position, watch the sky. Circling vultures are reading thermals above a carcass. When they begin to drop and land, the kill site has been vacated by the primary predator. Move toward the site with your group, making noise. Clapping, shouting, throwing stones - jackals and marabou storks will scatter immediately.

**What you find:**
In most cases, large carnivores (lions, hyenas) consume the soft organs and muscle meat. They rarely crack the long bones for marrow. Every skeleton they leave behind still contains a complete week's worth of concentrated calories in the marrow cavities. This is yours.

**What to do in 20 minutes:**

1. If any liver remains (dark reddish-brown, lobed organ in the abdominal cavity), eat it first. Tear off a fist-sized piece and consume it immediately. One liver from a zebra contains more B12 than 100 meals of plant food. Do not cook it if you need to eat immediately - it is safe raw if the animal died within the last 12 hours.

2. Find the longest intact leg bones. Place the femur on the largest flat rock you can find. Pick up a heavy rounded cobble (your hammerstone). Strike the mid-shaft perpendicularly, hard. Two to four firm blows will crack it. Pull it open. The inside yellow column is marrow - pure fat calories. Eat it directly or melt it over a heated rock.

3. Gather all the cracked and split bones into a container (folded bark, stomach cavity of the animal itself). Add water. Boil for as long as you can. The resulting broth contains dissolved collagen, minerals, and fat droplets. Drink it. This is bone broth, the first survival food in human history.

4. Any remaining muscle meat that smells sour but not rotten: cook it at a vigorous boil for at least 10 minutes. The heat destroys the bacteria causing the smell. The protein is fully intact.

The skeleton a lion abandons is not waste. For the first 3 million years of hominin history, it was lunch.
