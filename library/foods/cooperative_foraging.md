# Cooperative Foraging

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

Cooperative foraging is the coordinated collection of food by multiple individuals working together, sharing information about resource locations, dividing search areas to maximise coverage, and pooling findings for group distribution. It differs from individual foraging by exploiting group size: 6 foragers spreading across 3 km2 in communication range discover 4-6x more resource patches per hour than a single individual walking the same area. When one discovers a productive fruiting tree, a vocal signal brings others to exploit the find before it spoils or competitors arrive. This is the gathering equivalent of cooperative hunting. Among the Hadza (Tanzania), women forage in groups of 3-8 and share real-time information on tuber patch productivity, berry concentrations, and baobab fruit availability, with group foraging yielding 40-60% higher per-capita returns than solo foraging (Hawkes, O'Connell & Blurton Jones, 1997).

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

Chimpanzees at Budongo Forest (Uganda) produce "food calls" when discovering rich fruit patches, recruiting group members from up to 500 m away (Slocombe & Zuberbuehler, 2005). The caller adjusts call rate based on food quantity, giving more calls for larger patches, demonstrating that cooperative foraging with quality signalling exists in the primate lineage. At Kanjera South (Kenya, 2.0 Ma), the diversity of animal species and plant residues associated with stone tools at a single location suggests that Homo habilis foraging parties were bringing resources from multiple ecological zones to a central processing area, implying coordinated collection across a wide area. Among the Ache (Paraguay), documented by Kim Hill (1993), group foraging parties of 8-15 share all collected food equally at day's end regardless of individual contribution, creating an insurance system against daily variance.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->

**Organising a coordinated forage:**

| Step | Action | Benefit |
|------|--------|---------|
| 1. Morning briefing | Assign sectors: "Team A goes east, Team B goes south" | Prevents overlap, maximises coverage |
| 2. Spacing | Teams spread 200-500 m apart within signalling range | Covers more ground |
| 3. Discovery call | Finder calls group with specific signal for food type | Fast recruitment to productive patches |
| 4. Harvest | Group concentrates on best patch, fills carrying capacity | Efficient exploitation before spoilage |
| 5. Rotate | Move to next sector when current patch depleted | Systematic area coverage |
| 6. Return | All teams return to camp before dusk with pooled resources | Centralised processing and sharing |

**Communication during foraging:**

| Signal | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| Short repeated whistle | "Found something, come look" |
| Long sustained call | "Large quantity found, bring carriers" |
| Two clapped rocks | "Direction check, where are you?" |
| Response clap | "I am here, this direction" |
| Silence (extended) | "Check on me, possible problem" |

**Maximising foraging return:**

1. Send the person with the best plant knowledge as lead scout. Their identification speed reduces wasted effort on non-food items.
2. Carry containers (bark trays, hide bags, leaf wraps). Hands-only foraging wastes 30-40% of potential harvest through dropping and inability to carry volume.
3. Process nothing in the field unless necessary. Carry raw material back to camp where dedicated processors convert it efficiently.
4. Track foraging returns by area. If the south sector produced 3x the food of the east sector yesterday, send more people south today.
5. Forage different areas on consecutive days. Rotating patches prevents resource depletion and allows regrowth.

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Caloric yield:** Cooperative foraging produces 40-60% more calories per person than solo foraging. The information-sharing alone accounts for 20-30% of this increase.
- **Safety in numbers:** Foraging groups of 3+ deter predators that attack solitary individuals. The group serves as both foraging team and mutual protection.
- **Discovery rate:** Six eyes scanning for food find 4-6x more patches per hour than one pair of eyes. Rare or hidden resources (underground tubers, camouflaged fruits) are more likely to be noticed.
- **Seasonal buffering:** When one forager's specialty plant is out of season, another forager's knowledge of alternative species fills the gap. Diverse expertise within the group covers more of the calendar.
- **Social economy:** Sharing all foraged food equally at day's end eliminates the risk of individual bad luck. The person who found nothing today eats from others' success. Tomorrow the roles may reverse.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

Do not forage alone. Two people covering separate ground and sharing what they find will outproduce two people walking the same path by 40-60%. Three people do even better. Six is optimal. Beyond eight you start losing coordination.

Before leaving camp, split into teams and assign directions. Point at the terrain: "You two go toward the ridge. We go toward the river. Meet back here when the sun is there." Simple sector division prevents two teams from picking the same berry bush while a productive area goes unexplored.

Agree on calls before splitting up. One short whistle means "I found something, come check." One long call means "Big find, bring containers." Two rock claps back and forth means "Where are you?" Practice once. Go.

When someone finds a productive patch, everyone converges and harvests until it is depleted or containers are full. Do not spread the harvest across 5 small patches when one large patch can fill your needs. Concentrate effort, maximise extraction, move on.

Carry containers. Bark trays, folded large leaves, hide bags, anything that holds volume. Foraging with hands only wastes a third of what you find because you drop items, cannot carry enough, and have to make multiple trips. A single bark tray quadruples what one person brings back.

At camp in the evening, pool everything. All food goes to the centre. The processor sorts, prepares, and distributes equally. The person who found the motherload today feeds the person who found nothing. Tomorrow it reverses. This is not generosity. This is insurance. Over a week, equal sharing outperforms individual hoarding because daily foraging variance is enormous and no individual has consistently good days.
