# Termite and Ant Extraction
## Description
<!-- @field: description -->
Termites and ants are among the most nutritionally dense, energy-efficient, and seasonally predictable animal foods available on the African savanna. A single termite mound may contain 1-3 million workers and soldiers, each containing 14-16% protein and 30-40% fat by dry weight. Unlike hunting, which requires skill, risk, and cooperation, termite extraction can be accomplished by a single individual with a single stick in minutes. The caloric return per unit of foraging effort is exceptionally high.

The practice of dipping sticks or grass stems into termite mounds to extract workers is documented in chimpanzees (*Pan troglodytes*) across multiple African populations - one of the clearest examples of tool use in non-human primates, discovered by Jane Goodall at Gombe in 1960. For early hominins, this technique was almost certainly practiced, and the ability to harvest termites systematically provided a reliable protein source independent of hunting success or plant food availability.

Army ant columns provide an alternative but riskier insect food source: accessible as the column is moving, but dangerous to handle in large quantities due to their powerful mandibles. Soldier termites are defensible in small numbers. Flying termite alates (reproductive winged termites), which emerge in massive swarms after rain events, represent an extraordinary windfall food opportunity.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
Jane Goodall's 1964 paper in *Nature* ("Tool-using and aimed throwing in a community of free-living chimpanzees") documented the first confirmed tool use by non-humans: chimpanzees selecting, transporting, and modifying grass stems and thin twigs for termite extraction at Gombe, Tanzania. This discovery redefined the human-animal boundary at a stroke. Goodall's mentor Louis Leakey reportedly said: "Now we must redefine tool, redefine man, or accept chimpanzees as humans."

Subsequent studies across Africa showed that termite extraction is culturally variable in chimpanzees: Gombe chimps use long flexible probes; Bossou (Guinea) chimps use shorter stiffer probes; Goualougo Triangle (Congo) chimps use multi-component tool sets including a puncturing tool to open the mound and a probe to extract termites. This cultural variability is itself significant - it demonstrates learned, transmitted technology.

Nutritional analysis of common termites (*Macrotermes* spp., the dominant mound-building genus in East Africa) by van Huis et al. (2013, FAO) shows: protein 30-37% dry weight, fat 35-44% dry weight, caloric density 300-400 kcal per 100g dry weight. The winged alates, which swarm after rain events, are particularly rich: up to 620 kcal per 100g in some analyses.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Standard termite extraction (dip probe method):**

1. Locate an active termite mound - look for fresh soil deposits around the mound surface (worker maintenance activity), soldier termites patrolling at entrances, or the characteristic earthy smell of fresh excavation
2. Select a probe: a thin, flexible stem or twig, 30-80 cm long, 3-6 mm diameter, stripped of leaves
3. Identify a termite tunnel entrance - small holes or fresh soil around cracks in the mound surface
4. Insert the probe gently into the entrance, 10-20 cm deep, and leave it still for 30-60 seconds
5. Soldier termites will bite onto the probe - this is their defensive behavior
6. Withdraw the probe slowly and steadily - rapid withdrawal causes soldiers to release
7. Consume soldiers directly from the probe by drawing it through your lips
8. Repeat insertion into the same entrance or different ones

**Termite mound selection:**

| Mound Type | Productivity | Notes |
|------------|-------------|-------|
| *Macrotermes* (large cathedral mounds, >1m) | High | Most productive, also hardest mound surface |
| *Odontotermes* (smaller, soil mounds) | Medium | Easier to probe, less soldier density |
| *Trinervitermes* (small, rounded) | Low | Small workers, low extraction rate |
| Abandoned mound | Zero | No termites remain |

**Flying alate collection (windfall event):**

1. Flying alates emerge from mounds in massive swarms typically at dusk following the first heavy rain after dry season
2. The emergence lasts 30-90 minutes
3. Collection method: hold a bark tray or cupped leaves below the mound exit holes - alates fall directly into the container
4. Alternative: gather from ground surface where alates shed wings and congregate
5. Process: remove wings if desired (they are edible but bulky), consume directly or store briefly in covered container
6. A single 30-minute alate harvest can yield 200-500 grams of insect protein and fat

**Army ant access:**

1. Locate a moving army ant column by sound (audible rustling in leaf litter) or by following birds that follow columns to catch flushed prey
2. Use a stick to scoop handfuls of workers from the edge of the column (not the center - higher soldier density)
3. Crush immediately between stones or in the mouth to prevent biting
4. Do not attempt to collect from nests - army ant colonies will attack en masse

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Reliable daily protein top-up:** Even when hunting fails and large plant foods are unavailable, a 20-minute session at a productive termite mound provides meaningful protein and fat supplementation to an otherwise carbohydrate-heavy diet.
- **Children's supplementary nutrition:** Termite extraction is learnable by children as young as 4-5 years old. The activity provides independent nutrition acquisition skills and dietary supplementation without adult assistance.
- **Dry season protein source:** Large insects persist through dry seasons when many other food sources contract, providing a caloric buffer during lean periods.
- **Emergency windfall:** Flying alate swarms represent one of the highest caloric density collection events available without any hunting. A group with knowledge of local mound emergence timing can collect kilograms of food in under an hour.

## Day 0  -  Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->
You have been walking all morning and found nothing worth eating. The termite mounds near the dry streambed are as big as your torso.

Stop. You have protein right in front of you.

Find a thin stick or pull a grass stem. Strip the leaves. Push it into one of the small holes in the mound's surface, gently, 15 centimeters deep. Hold it still. Wait 30 seconds.

Pull it out slowly. Soldier termites will be clamped to it with their jaws. Draw the stick between your lips. Repeat.

It takes about 20 minutes to collect a meaningful handful. Eat them as you go.

If it has just rained and you see winged termites emerging from the mound in a cloud, stop everything and collect as many as you can. Hold a large leaf below the emergence holes. They fall in. A 30-minute swarm can give you more food than a full day of plant foraging.

This is not a meal. It is a supplement. Combined with tubers and whatever plant food you have, it is the difference between a day where you have energy and a day where you are running low.
