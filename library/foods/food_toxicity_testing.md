# Wild Food Toxicity Testing
## Description
<!-- @field: description -->
Of the approximately 350,000 known plant species, roughly 700 are lethally toxic to humans and thousands more cause serious illness. In an environment without accumulated cultural knowledge about which plants are safe, the ability to systematically evaluate unknown food items - slowing down the process of adoption rather than either refusing all new foods or accepting them incautiously - is a critical survival skill with deep evolutionary roots.

The toxicity-testing protocol described here is a formalized version of an empirical process that hominins developed over millions of years of dietary expansion. It operates on the principle that most plant toxins are dose-dependent: a tiny amount will cause detectable symptoms before a fatal amount is absorbed. By staging exposure incrementally and waiting between each stage, the tester can detect danger signals before consuming a harmful quantity.

This protocol is used today by survival instructors and is taught by the U.S. Army Survival Manual (FM 3-05.70), indigenous training programs across multiple continents, and wilderness medicine curricula. It is not foolproof - some toxins cause delayed onset reactions - but it dramatically reduces the risk of fatal plant poisoning.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
Evidence of early hominin dietary breadth comes from dental calculus (fossilized dental plaque) analysis. A 2014 study by Amanda Henry et al. (*PLOS ONE*) analyzing calculus from *Australopithecus sediba* (~1.98 Ma) found evidence of consumption of bark, woody plants, and plant silicates - material suggesting a diet that included experimentally tried plants rather than a fixed set of known safe species.

Comparative primate studies are instructive. Chimpanzees (*Pan troglodytes*) exhibit cautious food neophobia - they observe group members eating new foods before trying them, reject foods that cause negative reactions in others, and consume small initial quantities of unfamiliar items. This social learning-based toxicity filtering has been documented extensively by Jane Goodall (Gombe, 1960s-present) and William McGrew.

Indigenous knowledge systems worldwide have independently converged on similar testing protocols. The !Kung San of the Kalahari, Aboriginal Australians, and Amazonian forest peoples all describe step-by-step tasting protocols for unfamiliar plants, with equivalent logic: incremental exposure, sensory evaluation at each stage, and time delays between stages.

## Testing Protocol
<!-- @field: procedure -->
Apply this protocol to any unfamiliar food. Do not skip steps or compress the timeline.

**Pre-contact assessment:**

| Check | What You're Looking For | Reject If |
|-------|------------------------|-----------|
| Visual | Unusual color, mold, insect damage | Mold visible, bright red/purple berries with no local primate consumption |
| Smell (crush a small piece) | Sharp bitter almonds (cyanide), acrid chemical smell | Almond or peach-pit smell = cyanide risk |
| Context | Animals eating this? Insects present on it? | Nothing eats it in the area |

**Note:** Animals eating a plant is a positive signal but not a guarantee - some animals (especially birds) tolerate toxins humans cannot.

**Skin contact test:**

1. Crush a small piece of the plant
2. Rub the juice on the inside of your wrist or inner elbow
3. Wait 15 minutes
4. If burning, itching, rash, or swelling develops: **REJECT - do not eat**
5. If no reaction: proceed to lip test

**Lip contact test:**

1. Touch the plant material to your lower lip for 30 seconds
2. Do not chew or swallow
3. Wait 3 minutes
4. Signs to watch for: tingling, burning, numbness, swelling
5. If any sign present: **REJECT**
6. If clear: proceed to mouth test

**Mouth test:**

1. Place a pea-sized piece in your mouth
2. Chew lightly - do not swallow
3. Hold for 5 minutes
4. Watch for: burning, bitter astringency, excessive salivation, numbness, throat tightening
5. Spit out. Rinse mouth with water.
6. Wait 8 hours. Eat nothing else from this food source during this period.
7. If no effects after 8 hours: proceed to swallow test

**Swallow test:**

1. Swallow a small amount (1-2 teaspoons of plant material)
2. Wait 8 hours. Eat nothing else from this source.
3. Monitor for: nausea, vomiting, abdominal cramps, dizziness, visual changes, excessive thirst
4. If any symptoms develop: **REJECT permanently**
5. If clear after 8 hours: the food is provisionally safe for consumption

**Full consumption:**

1. Eat a normal portion (50-100g)
2. Wait 24 hours before consuming freely
3. Some toxins (e.g., phytolacca alkaloids in pokeweed) are cumulative - a first portion may seem safe while repeated consumption causes illness

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Dietary expansion:** The protocol allows safe addition of new plant species to the diet as you move into new territory or as seasons change the available food base.
- **Famine conditions:** When familiar foods are exhausted, systematic testing of unfamiliar plants provides a structured pathway to accessing emergency nutrition without fatal gambling.
- **Social knowledge transfer:** Completed tests should be shared across the group. One person's successful test result protects everyone. Failed tests with mild symptoms (quickly detected) warn others away from the same plant.
- **Geographic adaptation:** Moving through unfamiliar ecosystems requires rapid building of local plant knowledge. The testing protocol is the systematic method for this.

## Technical Data
<!-- @field: technical_data -->

| Parameter | Value | Notes |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| Known toxic plant species | ~700 lethally toxic | Of ~350,000 known plant species |
| Minimum total protocol time | 24-48 hours | For a single new food item |
| Skin contact test duration | 15 minutes | Detect contact toxins and irritants |
| Lip contact test duration | 30 seconds + 3 min wait | Detect mucous membrane irritants |
| Mouth hold duration | 5 minutes | Detect oral alkaloids and tannins |
| Post-swallow wait | 8 hours | Detect absorbed toxins with delayed onset |
| Cyanide detection (bitter almond smell) | Pre-contact | Reject before any skin contact |
| Amatoxin detection (death cap mushroom) | Not detectable by taste/smell | Delayed 6-24 hours onset - highest risk food |
| False safety rate (birds eating toxic berries) | Common | Bird metabolisms tolerate many human-toxic compounds |

## Day 0  -  Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->
You are hungry. You found a plant you do not recognize. Your instinct says eat it.

Do not eat it yet.

Take 2 minutes right now. The time you spend here could save your life.

Step 1: Crush a small piece. Does it smell like bitter almonds or peach pits? If yes - drop it and walk away. That is cyanide.

Step 2: Rub the juice on your inner wrist. Wait 15 minutes. Burning? Reject.

Step 3: Touch it to your lip for 30 seconds. Tingling? Reject.

Step 4: Chew a pea-sized piece for 5 minutes. Do not swallow. Burning or numbness in your mouth? Spit, rinse, reject.

Step 5: Swallow a teaspoon. Wait 8 hours. Eat nothing else from this plant.

If you feel fine after 8 hours, eat a normal portion and wait 24 more hours.

This takes time. But the plant that looked like a meal and killed you in 6 hours also looked safe. The protocol is the difference.
