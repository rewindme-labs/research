# Dietary Expansion

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

Dietary expansion is the progressive broadening of food sources beyond the ancestral fruit-and-leaf diet of forest-dwelling primates toward an omnivorous strategy that includes animal protein, marrow, organs, underground storage organs (tubers, corms), aquatic resources, insects, eggs, and honey. This shift was not a single event but a million-year process driven by habitat change, tool development, and social cooperation. As East African forests fragmented into mosaic woodland-savanna during the Pliocene, hominins that exploited the widest dietary range survived dry seasons that killed specialists. Dietary expansion fuelled brain growth (animal fat provides essential omega-3 fatty acids and DHA), extended ranging distance, and created the caloric surplus that allowed longer juvenile dependency and therefore longer learning periods.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

Stable carbon isotope analysis of Australopithecus africanus teeth from Makapansgat (South Africa, 3.0 Ma) by Matt Sponheimer and Julia Lee-Thorp (1999) revealed a C4 signature indicating significant consumption of savanna-derived foods (grasses, sedges, or animals eating them), distinct from the C3 forest-fruit diet of earlier apes. At Dikika (Ethiopia, 3.39 Ma), cut marks on ungulate bones reported by Shannon McPherron (2010) suggest stone tool-assisted meat processing by Australopithecus afarensis, pushing animal food use back nearly a million years before Homo. Bone marrow extraction sites at Koobi Fora (1.5 Ma) show systematic hammerstone percussion patterns on long bones, indicating targeted caloric extraction. Henry Bunn (1981) demonstrated that early Homo at Olduvai Gorge preferentially butchered high-value limb bones from large ungulates, indicating dietary selectivity rather than random scavenging.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->

**Food category expansion sequence:**

| Stage | Foods added | Access method | Caloric yield |
|-------|------------|---------------|---------------|
| Baseline | Fruits, leaves, bark | Hand picking | Low-moderate |
| Stage 1 | Insects, grubs, eggs | Digging, branch probing | Moderate (high protein) |
| Stage 2 | Scavenged meat, marrow | Stone tools for cutting and cracking | High |
| Stage 3 | Tubers, corms, bulbs | Digging sticks, fire for detoxification | High (starch) |
| Stage 4 | Aquatic resources (fish, shellfish, crabs) | Hand catching, trapping | Very high per effort |
| Stage 5 | Hunted game | Weapons, cooperative pursuit | Variable, feast-or-famine |

**Identifying new food sources in unfamiliar terrain:**

1. Watch what animals eat. Baboons are generalist omnivores with a diet overlap of 40-60% with humans. If a troop feeds on something, it is worth investigating.
2. Test unknown plants using the universal edibility test: rub on inner wrist, wait 15 minutes. If no reaction, touch to lip corner, wait 15 minutes. Place a small piece on tongue without swallowing, wait 15 minutes. Chew and spit, wait 4 hours. Only then eat a small portion and wait 8 hours.
3. Prioritise calorie-dense foods: animal fat, marrow, nuts, tubers, honey. Leafy greens fill the stomach but provide minimal calories.
4. Process everything possible. Pounding, soaking, and cooking (when available) break down plant toxins and cell walls, releasing 30-60% more calories from the same food.

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Caloric insurance:** Broad diet means no single food failure causes starvation. If game is scarce, tubers sustain. If tubers are seasonal, insects and marrow fill the gap.
- **Brain fuel:** The human brain consumes 20% of basal metabolic energy. Animal fat and marrow provide concentrated calories and essential fatty acids (DHA, arachidonic acid) required for neural tissue development.
- **Range expansion:** Dietary flexibility allows colonization of new biomes. Coastal, forest, savanna, and mountain environments each offer different food combinations, but a flexible diet exploits them all.
- **Seasonal buffering:** Underground storage organs (tubers) are available year-round in savanna environments. They serve as the fallback food when preferred items are seasonally absent.
- **Weaning food:** Processed soft foods (pounded tubers, chewed meat) allow earlier weaning of infants, reducing inter-birth intervals and increasing group reproductive rate.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

Eat everything. Your survival depends on abandoning any concept of what food should look like, taste like, or feel like in your mouth. The person who eats only what they recognize starves. The person who eats whatever contains calories lives.

Start with what is certain. Crack open every long bone you find from any carcass, no matter how old, as long as it does not smell of active rot. Use a rock. Smash the shaft midway. The marrow inside is the most calorie-dense food available to you without tools: 786 calories per 100 grams. Scoop it out with your fingers. Eat it raw.

Dig. Get a sturdy stick, sharpen the tip with a rock, and probe soft ground near water. Tubers grow where the soil is damp. When your stick hits resistance, dig around it with your hands. Wash the tuber. If you have fire, roast it in coals for 15-20 minutes. If you do not have fire, eat it raw but chew thoroughly. Your gut will extract less without cooking, but less is better than nothing.

Turn over every log and rock. Grubs, beetles, termites, ants. They are protein and fat in a package that does not run away. Termite mounds are goldmines. Break a hole in the side, insert a wet stick, wait 30 seconds, pull it out covered in termites. Eat them off the stick. They taste like nuts. Some of them actually do.

Check every bird nest you can reach. Eggs are complete nutrition. Eat them raw by cracking a small hole and drinking the contents. Do not cook eggs over open flame unless you have a container, they explode.

Do not eat anything with white sap, a bitter almond smell, or three-leaflet compound leaves until you have tested it using the wrist-lip-tongue protocol over 24 hours. Everything else, try it. Your ancestors did not survive by being picky. Neither will you.
