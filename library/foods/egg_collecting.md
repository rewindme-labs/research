# Bird & Reptile Egg Collecting

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->
Egg collecting is the foraging and consumption of wild bird and reptile eggs. It requires no trapping, no killing of prey, no fire in many cases, and minimal tools. An egg is a nutritionally complete package containing high-quality protein, essential fats, fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K), and all the micronutrients required to build a complete organism. Per gram of edible food obtained for effort invested, wild eggs are among the highest-return foraging activities available in most environments.

Unlike hunting, eggs are found at predictable locations (nesting sites), do not require pursuit, and cannot escape. Unlike plant foraging, eggs contain complete protein (all essential amino acids in balanced ratios). Unlike insect foraging, eggs are psychologically accessible to most people without conditioning. This combination makes egg collecting the rational first priority for accessible animal protein in the early days of any survival situation.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
Eggshell fragments found at Diepkloof Rock Shelter, South Africa (c. 60,000 BCE) represent ostrich egg shells used as water containers, indicating that eggs were collected and consumed at least this far back and that the shells were considered a durable container material. Ostrich eggs contain approximately 2,000 calories each - equivalent to roughly 24 chicken eggs - and were clearly an important caloric resource in that environment.

Paleolithic cave art at La Pileta, Spain (c. 25,000 BCE) includes what some archaeologists interpret as bird nests with eggs, suggesting cognitive mapping of nesting locations as food resources. Across every documented hunter-gatherer culture, egg collecting is described as a standard, systematic foraging activity with formalized knowledge of which species nest where, in which season, and how to access the nests safely.

The ancient Egyptians kept geese specifically for egg production by 3000 BCE, and goose eggs appear in food offerings in tomb paintings at Saqqara. The domestication of chickens from red junglefowl (*Gallus gallus*) in Southeast Asia around 8000 BCE was initially motivated by ritual cockfighting rather than egg production - egg farming as a primary purpose dates to approximately 1400 BCE in China. The wild ancestor, however, was being egg-raided long before domestication.

## Ingredients & Preparation
<!-- @field: preparation -->
**Locating eggs - species-specific strategies:**

| Species Type | Nest Location | Season | Signs |
|-------------|--------------|--------|-------|
| Ground-nesting birds (lapwings, quail, plovers, geese) | Open grass, beach, bare earth | Spring/summer | Shallow scrape surrounded by nothing; bird runs decoy when flushed |
| Tree-nesting birds (pigeons, doves, raptors) | Tree forks, cliff ledges | Spring/summer | Sticks, feathers accumulated; adult sits still |
| Cliff-nesting seabirds (gannets, guillemots, murres) | Rock ledges near coastlines | Late spring | Dense colonies; distinctive guano below |
| Freshwater waterbirds (ducks, herons, coots) | Reed beds, floating vegetation | Spring | Nest visible from boat or bank; flushing adult |
| Reptile eggs (turtles, lizards, snakes) | Loose soil, sand, rotting vegetation | Summer | Disturbance marks in sand; warm spots in composted leaves |
| Ostrich | Open savanna scrape | Species-specific | Enormous 2kg eggs in ground nest; male incubates at night |

**Freshness testing - the float test:**

Fill a container of water deep enough to submerge the egg completely.
- **Sinks and lies flat on the bottom:** Very fresh (within 1-5 days). Best quality.
- **Sinks but stands on one end:** 1-2 weeks old. Still fully edible.
- **Floats:** Air cell has expanded due to decomposition. Do not eat. (Exception: very large eggs like ostrich may float slightly even when fresh due to shell porosity.)

**Freshness testing without water - the shake test:**
Hold the egg horizontally and shake gently. A fresh egg makes no sound. A stale egg has a detectable liquid-sloshing sensation inside.

**Candling (to check development):**
Hold the egg up to a bright light source. A completely clear interior = no embryo, fully edible. Dark mass visible = embryo present (still edible in many cultures at early stages; discard if late-stage developed).

**Cooking methods (primitive):**

| Method | Description | Best For |
|--------|-------------|----------|
| Roasting in shell | Place egg directly in warm (not flame-hot) coals, pointed end down. Turn every 60 seconds for 8-10 minutes. | All egg sizes |
| Hot rock steaming | Heat a flat rock, crack egg onto surface, fold a wet leaf over to create steam chamber | Small-medium eggs |
| Stone boiling | Crack eggs into a bark bowl with water; add fire-heated rocks until water boils | Multiple small eggs |
| Raw consumption | Safe for fresh eggs; all nutrients intact; traditional in many cultures | Very fresh eggs only |
| In-shell boiling | Submerge in water; boil for 10 minutes for full hard set | Any egg with access to a container |

## Preservation & Storage
<!-- @field: storage -->
- **Unwashed eggs** (with natural cuticle bloom intact): Store at cool ambient temperature for 2-4 weeks.
- **Washed eggs:** Must be used within 3-5 days. Washing removes the porous shell's natural seal.
- **Coating with oil, fat, or liquid wax** (applied to shell): Seals pores; extends ambient storage to 6-8 weeks. Traditional Chinese method used for millennia.
- **Mineral lime solution (water glass):** Eggs submerged in dilute calcium hydroxide solution store for 6-9 months. Traditional preservation method in Europe and China.
- **Smoking:** Not practical for whole eggs; broken and dried egg solids can be smoked.
- **Signs of spoilage:** Sulfurous smell upon cracking, black/green discoloration of yolk, gelatinous or lumpy white.

Eggs are among the few wild foods that provide significant Vitamin D and Vitamin B12 without supplements. Choline (critical for brain function) is one of the most commonly deficient nutrients in prehistorical diets; eggs are the most concentrated natural source.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->
You need protein today without a hunt. Walk slowly through any open grassland, marsh edge, or beach and flush ground-nesting birds. They will explode from the grass ahead of your feet and land within 50 meters, then pretend to be injured (broken-wing display) to draw you away from the nest. Walk back toward where they flushed from. The nest is a shallow scrape in the ground with 3-8 eggs, speckled brown and nearly invisible.

Before you touch them: drop one in water (any still water in a cupped bark piece or the hollow of a rock). If it sinks flat, it is fresh. Take all of them.

**Immediate cooking (no container):** Place eggs in the embers of a fire, pointed end down. Turn gently every 60 seconds. After 8-10 minutes, the shell will be slightly scorched. Crack open - the white will be set and the yolk firm. Do not let them sit directly in active flame or they will explode. Warm coals only.

**No fire:** Crack the egg and consume raw. Wild bird eggs are safe raw when fresh (passed the float test). The yolk is nutritionally superior raw (heat denatures some B vitamins and reduces biotin absorption from the white). This is the fastest protein delivery possible.

**In tree-nesting areas:** Look for sticks accumulated in tree forks, cliff ledges, or thick vegetation. Adults sit completely still when incubating. Approach the nest from below and observe the adult: if it flies off directly without the broken-wing display, it is not a ground nester. Climb only if the nest is clearly accessible. Never take more than half the eggs in any active nest - the adult will abandon an empty nest but will often continue incubating a partially depleted clutch.

**Reptile eggs:** In summer, probe loose sandy soil in sun-exposed south-facing banks. Turtle eggs are round, leathery, golf-ball sized, and buried 15-30cm deep. They are nutritionally equivalent to bird eggs and safe to eat raw or cooked. Snake eggs are similar but elongated. The freshness float test applies equally.

A productive nest site found on Day 2 may still be productive on Day 5 if you do not overharvest it. Map the location. Return in 3 days.
