# Meat Sun-Drying
<!-- @field: description -->
Meat sun-drying is the simplest form of food preservation: exposing thin strips of fresh meat to direct sunlight and warm air until the water content is reduced to below 15%, inhibiting microbial growth and enzymatic spoilage. The process requires no fire, no salt, and no containers - only thin slicing technique, elevated placement for air circulation, and sufficient sunlight and low humidity. The result is a lightweight, shelf-stable protein food that can be carried and consumed over days to weeks.

In tropical savanna environments like East Africa, where daytime temperatures regularly exceed 30-35°C and relative humidity drops below 40% during dry season, meat can be dried to safe preservation levels in 6-24 hours. This makes sun-drying viable even without any technological infrastructure beyond a stone flake for slicing. The ability to preserve meat from a large carcass - which a small group cannot consume before spoilage in hot weather - dramatically changes the caloric calculus of a successful kill or scavenging event.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
Sun-dried meat, in various traditional forms, is found across virtually every culture that has ever lived in hot, seasonally dry environments. The Maasai (*nyama*), the San (!Kung *eland* dried meat), the North American Plains peoples (pemmican precursor), the Inca (charqui, the origin of the word "jerky"), and South African Boers (biltong) all independently developed meat drying as a primary preservation technique. The convergence is not coincidental: in any environment with sun, wind, and dry air, meat drying is the obvious, zero-infrastructure response to the problem of perishable surplus.

Archaeological evidence for deliberate meat drying is difficult to distinguish from natural desiccation, but site distribution patterns suggest it was practiced well before the advent of fire control. David Lubell's analysis of North African Aterian sites (~80-40 ka) found processed bone assemblages consistent with meat removal and transport, implying some preservation method for meat taken from carcasses at kill sites.

The Hadza of Tanzania are documented by Frank Marlowe (2010, *Cambridge Studies in Biological and Evolutionary Anthropology*) to dry meat from large game on branches in open sunlight when a kill exceeds immediate consumption capacity. The technique is treated as obvious and basic rather than specialized knowledge.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Step 1 - Assessment:**
Meat is suitable for drying if it is fresh (killed or scavenged within 2-4 hours in hot weather). Meat already showing slime or off-smell should be consumed immediately rather than dried - it will spoil further during drying rather than being preserved.

**Step 2 - Slicing:**

| Slice Parameter | Target | Effect of Getting Wrong |
|----------------|--------|------------------------|
| Thickness | 3-6 mm | Thicker = longer drying, risk of interior spoilage |
| Length | 10-20 cm | Longer strips dry more evenly |
| Direction | With the grain (along muscle fibers) | Against the grain = crumbles when dry |
| Fat removal | Remove most visible fat | Fat goes rancid faster than lean meat |

Slice with the sharpest available stone flake, working systematically from the outer surface toward the bone. Slice parallel to the muscle fiber direction, not across it.

**Step 3 - Placement:**

1. Lay strips on rocks, branches, or grass tufts elevated off the ground (prevents insect and ground-level animal access)
2. Maximum sun exposure - face the meat toward midday sun
3. Ensure air circulation under and around each strip - no overlapping pieces
4. At night, bring strips inside (rock overhang, tree branch) to prevent re-hydration from dew
5. Return to direct sun at dawn

**Step 4 - Assessment of completeness:**

| State | Condition | Action |
|-------|-----------|--------|
| Leathery, flexible, no moisture when pressed | Partially dried | Continue - not yet safe to store |
| Rigid, dark brown, breaks rather than bends | Fully dried | Safe to store and transport |
| White/gray mold visible | Spoiled during drying | Discard |
| Strong off-smell | Failed drying | Discard |

**Environmental conditions:**

| Condition | Drying Time | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| Hot sun, 35°C+, <40% humidity | 6-12 hours | Ideal savanna dry season conditions |
| Warm sun, 25-30°C, <60% humidity | 12-24 hours | Adequate for one-day drying |
| Overcast, humid | 24-48 hours | Risk of spoilage increases significantly |
| Night alone (no sun) | Not viable | Cold slows drying, dew rehydrates |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Carcass surplus preservation:** A single wildebeest provides 100-150 kg of edible meat - far more than a small group can eat in 24 hours before hot-weather spoilage. Drying 20-30 kg of the surplus converts ephemeral surplus into 7-10 days of portable food.
- **Travel provisioning:** Dried meat is 70-75% lighter than fresh meat of equal nutritional content (water removed). A group moving to a new location can carry several days of food without the weight penalty of fresh meat.
- **Lean season reserve:** Dried meat stored in cool, dry conditions (rock crevices, dense shade) can remain palatable for 1-4 weeks in savanna conditions, providing a caloric buffer during hunting failures.
- **Energy concentration for long movements:** A 30g piece of dried meat provides approximately 90 kcal - equivalent to 120g of fresh meat - in a portable, zero-spoilage format.

## Technical Data
<!-- @field: technical_data -->

| Parameter | Value | Notes |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| Water content of fresh meat | 70-75% | Drops to <15% in fully dried state |
| Weight reduction during drying | 65-75% | A 1 kg fresh strip becomes ~250-300g dried |
| Caloric density increase | 3-4x per gram | Concentration of protein and fat as water removed |
| Optimal slice thickness | 3-6 mm | Thin enough for same-day drying in good conditions |
| Shelf life (fully dried, dry conditions) | 1-4 weeks | Without salt or smoke |
| Bacteria inhibition threshold | <15% water content | Standard food preservation parameter |
| Drying time (ideal conditions) | 6-12 hours | East African dry season, direct sun |
| Fat shelf life (dried) | 1-2 weeks | Fat goes rancid faster than lean - remove before drying |

## Day 0 — Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0 -->
You have more meat than you can eat today. Tomorrow it will be rotten.

Take your stone flake. Slice as much of the remaining meat as you can into strips 4-5 mm thick. Slice with the grain - along the muscle fibers, not across. Remove visible fat from each strip.

Find the brightest patch of sunlight near your camp. Lay the strips across branches or flat rocks in direct sun. Do not stack them. Leave gaps between each strip.

Come back in 3 hours and turn them over. If they are still soft and wet, leave them longer. If they are becoming dark and leathery, they are working.

Before dark, gather them and bring them inside your shelter or into your sleeping tree. Dew will rehydrate them overnight.

Lay them back in the sun at first light.

After a second day in strong sun, the strips should be rigid and break when bent. That meat is now safe to carry. It weighs a quarter of what it did fresh. It will feed you for days.

You just multiplied today's kill into next week's food supply.
