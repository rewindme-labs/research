# Infant Rearing

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

Infant rearing is the sustained care of offspring from birth through nutritional independence: carrying, nursing, feeding, protecting, thermoregulating, and transmitting survival behaviours. Hominin infants are uniquely helpless compared to other primates. A newborn chimpanzee clings to its mother independently within hours. A human newborn cannot hold its own head up for weeks and cannot walk for a year. This extreme altriciality (developmental helplessness) is the biological cost of large brains passing through a bipedal pelvis. The payoff is an extended juvenile learning period that allows complex skill acquisition. In foraging societies, childcare is not exclusively maternal. Sarah Hrdy's "cooperative breeding" model (2009) demonstrates that allomothers (grandmothers, siblings, aunts, unrelated females) share nursing, carrying, and feeding duties, enabling mothers to resume foraging sooner and reducing inter-birth intervals.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

The Dikika infant (Australopithecus afarensis, Ethiopia, 3.3 Ma), described by Zeresenay Alemseged (2006), preserves the skeleton of a 3-year-old with a brain size of roughly 330 cc representing about 70% of adult capacity, indicating that brain growth continued well past birth like modern humans. This confirms extended infant dependency in the australopithecine lineage. Studies of dental microstructure in Homo erectus from Mojokerto (Java, 1.8 Ma) by Christopher Dean (2001) indicate growth rates intermediate between apes and modern humans, suggesting a childhood dependency period of 10-12 years. Among the Hadza (Tanzania), documented by Kristen Hawkes (1998), post-menopausal grandmothers provide 30-40% of caloric needs for weaned grandchildren through tuber gathering, supporting the "grandmother hypothesis" that extended lifespan evolved specifically to support grandchild rearing.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->

**Infant carrying methods (no manufactured equipment):**

| Method | Position | Age range | Hands free? |
|--------|----------|-----------|-------------|
| Cradle carry | Arms crossed under infant | 0-3 months | No |
| Hip carry | One arm supporting infant on hip bone | 3-12 months | One hand |
| Back carry (skin/bark sling) | Infant against back, bark strip across chest | 4+ months | Both hands |
| Piggyback (toddler) | Child grips rider's shoulders | 2-4 years | Both hands |
| Walking alongside (supervised) | Child walks, adult within arm's reach | 3+ years | Both hands |

**Feeding progression:**

| Stage | Age | Food type | Method |
|-------|-----|-----------|--------|
| Exclusive nursing | 0-6 months | Breastmilk only | On demand, 8-12 times daily |
| Introduction | 6-9 months | Pre-chewed soft tuber, pounded fruit | Mother or allomother pre-masticates and transfers mouth-to-mouth |
| Mixed feeding | 9-18 months | Breastmilk + mashed tubers, soft meat, insect paste | Direct feeding + continued nursing |
| Gradual weaning | 18-36 months | Increasing solid food, decreasing nursing | Child eats group food, nursing for comfort |
| Independence | 36+ months | Full group diet (soft items preferred) | Self-feeding with supervision |

**Thermoregulation for infants:**

1. Newborns lose heat rapidly through their large head surface area. Keep the head covered with a leaf wrap or soft bark.
2. Skin-to-skin contact is the most effective warming method. Hold infant against bare chest, cover both with a hide or bark sheet.
3. At night, the infant sleeps between two adults. Body heat from both sides prevents hypothermia even without fire.
4. In extreme heat, keep infant in shade at all times. Wet a cloth or moss and dampen the infant's head and limbs. Encourage frequent nursing for hydration.

**Protection priorities:**

1. Never set an infant on the ground unattended. Ground-level threats include snakes, scorpions, ants, and small predators.
2. At camp, designate a specific person as infant watcher at all times. This role rotates but never goes unfilled.
3. During group movement, the infant carrier walks in the centre of the group, surrounded by adults.
4. If a predator approaches, the infant carrier and one escort retreat while the rest of the group displays.

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Population survival:** A group that loses infants to exposure, predation, or malnutrition within one generation faces extinction. Infant survival rate directly determines group viability.
- **Knowledge transmission:** The extended dependency period (3-5 years of intensive care, 10-15 years of skill learning) is the window for cultural transmission. Longer childhood enables more complex technology and social behaviour.
- **Cooperative bonding:** Shared infant care creates reciprocal obligations among group members. The allomother who carries your child today expects the same service tomorrow. This network strengthens group cohesion.
- **Maternal foraging efficiency:** A mother freed from constant carrying by an allomother gathers 40-60% more food per day (Hadza data, Hawkes 1998). The extra calories feed both mother and infant.
- **Demographic advantage:** Groups with effective cooperative rearing achieve inter-birth intervals of 3-4 years versus 5-6 years for exclusively maternal care. Over a generation, this doubles population growth rate.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

If there is a baby in your group, every other priority drops one rank. Shelter, warmth, and food for the infant come before comfort for adults.

The baby stays off the ground at all times. Snakes, ants, scorpions, and cold ground are all lethal to something that cannot roll away. One person holds the infant or it rests on a raised platform of stacked vegetation at least 30 cm off the ground.

Nursing mothers need extra water and extra food. She is producing 500 calories a day of milk on top of her own survival needs. Feed her first. If she stops producing milk before the infant can eat solid food, the infant dies. There is no alternative formula.

When the mother needs to forage, another adult takes the infant. This is not optional. It is the system that allowed our species to exist. Wrap a wide strip of bark or soft hide around the carrier's chest and across one shoulder. Place the baby against the back with its weight resting on the carrier's hips. Secure with the bark strip. Both hands are now free.

Start introducing soft food at six months. Chew a piece of cooked tuber until it is paste. Transfer it to the infant's mouth with your finger. This is pre-mastication and every human culture practiced it until commercial baby food appeared. The enzymes in your saliva begin digesting the food before the infant swallows it.

At night, the infant sleeps between two warm bodies. Not near the fire where a rolling log could reach. Between adults, skin contact, covered with whatever insulation you have. This is how infant thermoregulation worked for three million years and it works tonight.

Assign the infant watching role every morning the same way you assign foraging roles. Say the name out loud. That person does not leave camp while on infant duty. When they need relief, they hand over to a named replacement. The gap between one watcher leaving and another arriving is the gap where infants die.
