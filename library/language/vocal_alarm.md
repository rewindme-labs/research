# Vocal Alarm Systems

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

Vocal alarm systems are differentiated vocalisations that encode information about specific threat types, enabling group members to execute appropriate responses without seeing the threat themselves. Vervet monkeys produce three acoustically distinct alarm calls: a short staccato bark for leopards (response: climb a tree), a low-pitched chuckle for eagles (response: look up and hide under cover), and a high-pitched chattering for snakes (response: stand upright and scan the ground). Each call triggers a specific, correct evasive behaviour in group members who hear but cannot see the predator (Seyfarth, Cheney & Marler, 1980). This is referential signalling: the call refers to an external object, not merely the caller's emotional state. Early hominins, with vocal tracts intermediate between apes and modern humans, almost certainly possessed differentiated alarm calls that conveyed threat category, urgency, and possibly direction.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

Robert Seyfarth and Dorothy Cheney's playback experiments with vervet monkeys at Amboseli (Kenya, 1980) proved that alarm calls are not simply expressions of fear but function as labels for predator categories. Juveniles initially make errors (eagle alarm for a falling leaf) and are corrected through parental response, confirming social learning of call meanings. Klaus Zuberbuehler's research on Diana monkeys in Tai Forest (2000) demonstrated that alarm calls encode both predator type and urgency level, with distinct acoustic modifications for close versus distant threats. Campbell's monkeys add suffix-like syllables to root calls to modify meaning, the closest documented analogue to morphological structure in animal communication (Ouattara, 2009). Chimpanzee alarm calls at Budongo (Uganda) include "hoo" calls that referentially indicate snakes, with listeners responding appropriately even without visual confirmation (Crockford, 2012).

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->

**Establishing a human alarm call system:**

| Call type | Sound | Meaning | Correct response |
|-----------|-------|---------|------------------|
| Short sharp bark (repeated) | "HAH-HAH-HAH" | Ground predator approaching | Stand together, grab weapons, face threat |
| Sustained high scream | Long ascending cry | Aerial threat or falling object | Cover head, get under canopy/overhang |
| Low rapid grunt | "UH-UH-UH-UH" | Snake or ground-level danger | Freeze, visually scan around feet |
| Single long tone | Extended "OHHH" | Unknown threat, investigate | Stop movement, group together, assess |
| Two-tone rising | "OH-EEE" repeated | Hostile humans approaching | Defensive formation, weapons ready |
| Rapid high chirps | "IP-IP-IP-IP" | Danger passed, all clear | Resume activity, maintain vigilance |

**Implementation rules:**

1. On the first day of group formation, agree on call types and responses. Rehearse each call and response 3 times.
2. The person who spots the threat gives the call. Do not wait for confirmation. False alarms cost minor energy. Missed warnings cost lives.
3. Everyone responds to the call immediately, even if they cannot see the threat themselves. Trust the caller.
4. After responding, the caller adds directional information by pointing or giving a secondary call.
5. Children learn calls through exposure and imitation. By age 4-5 in foraging societies, children produce and respond to alarm calls correctly.
6. False alarm rate above 30% degrades response. If one person consistently gives false alarms, the group stops responding. Accuracy matters.

**Night alarm protocol:**

1. The night watch gives alarm calls at reduced volume but increased repetition rate. Sleeping individuals need 3-5 seconds to wake and orient.
2. A night ground-predator call should be accompanied by fire intensification (throw fuel on the fire immediately).
3. Group members orient toward the fire for light, not away from it into darkness.

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Predator survival:** A differentiated alarm for "leopard" versus "snake" triggers opposite responses (climb versus freeze). A generic "danger" call leaves individuals guessing and responding incorrectly half the time.
- **Group coordination speed:** An alarm call propagates across a spread-out foraging party in 1-2 seconds. Physical signals (running, gesturing) take 10-30 seconds to reach all members. Speed determines survival in ambush scenarios.
- **Night survival:** Vocal alarms are the only communication channel in darkness. Visual gestures are useless. A well-practised call system functions 24 hours.
- **Child safety:** Children too young to assess threats respond reflexively to alarm calls. The mother's alarm cry triggers immediate proximity-seeking in infants, a reflex preserved in modern human startle response.
- **Collective defence:** The alarm call itself deters some predators. A group vocalising simultaneously projects size and alertness that solitary prey cannot match. Lions abandon hunts when alerted prey mob-vocalises.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

Agree on your alarm calls tonight. Before you sleep. This cannot wait.

You need at minimum three calls. One for ground predators (lion, hyena, wolf, bear). One for snakes or ground-level hazards. One for hostile humans. Make each sound distinct enough that a half-asleep person at 3 AM can tell them apart without thinking.

Ground predator: a sharp repeated bark. HAH-HAH-HAH. Yell it aggressively. The response is everyone stands, grabs the nearest weapon, and faces outward. Do not run. Running from a predator that has not committed to a charge triggers the chase response.

Snake or ground danger: a low rapid grunt. UH-UH-UH. The response is freeze. Look at the ground around your feet. Do not step backward until you have visually confirmed clear ground behind you.

Hostile humans: a rising two-tone call. OH-EEE, OH-EEE. The response is group together, weapons visible, maintain eye contact with the approaching group. Project strength without aggression.

Practise all three right now. Have each person give each call. Have each person perform the correct response. Do this until the response is automatic. In a real emergency you will not think. You will react. The reaction must be correct.

The person on night watch gives the alarm. When they call, everyone moves. No questioning, no hesitation, no verifying. The watch saw something and that is enough. False alarms cost you 30 seconds of sleep. Missed alarms cost everything.

When you hear an alarm, move toward the fire. The fire is your orientation point in darkness. It is your weapon against predators. It is your light source for threat assessment. Moving away from fire into darkness is moving into the predator's advantage. Never do it.
