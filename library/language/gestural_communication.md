# Gestural Communication

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

Gestural communication is the transmission of information through body posture, hand movements, facial expressions, eye direction, and physical demonstration without vocalisation. It is the proto-language that preceded speech and persists as a parallel communication channel in all human cultures. Great apes use over 60 distinct gestures with consistent meanings across populations (Hobaiter and Byrne, 2014): arm raise for "climb on me", directed scratching for "groom here", leaf clipping as attention-getting, and ground slap as play invitation. Early hominins almost certainly had a richer gestural vocabulary than modern apes, combined with bipedal posture that freed both hands for simultaneous signalling. In survival contexts, gestural communication enables silent coordination during hunting, threat response coordination without alerting predators, and teaching of physical skills through demonstration.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

Michael Tomasello's research at the Max Planck Institute (2007) demonstrated that great apes use gestures intentionally and flexibly, adjusting their signals based on whether the recipient is watching and whether previous attempts succeeded, a level of communicative sophistication previously attributed only to humans. At Gombe (Tanzania), Goodall documented chimpanzee begging gestures with extended palm-up hand position, identical to the human begging gesture across all cultures, suggesting deep evolutionary conservation. Michael Corballis (2002) proposed the "gesture-first" hypothesis of language evolution, arguing that manual gestures provided the cognitive scaffolding for vocal language. Archaeological evidence is necessarily indirect, but the symmetrical hand axes of Homo erectus (1.7 Ma onward) require bilateral hand coordination and spatial planning that imply demonstrative teaching through gesture.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->

**Essential survival gestures (establish with your group immediately):**

| Gesture | Meaning | When to use |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| Flat hand raised, palm forward | STOP / FREEZE | Predator spotted, danger ahead |
| Pointing with extended arm | LOOK THERE | Resource, threat, or direction indication |
| Beckoning wave toward self | COME HERE | Safe to approach, assistance needed |
| Both palms pressed down repeatedly | GET DOWN / HIDE | Immediate concealment needed |
| Fist raised overhead | ATTENTION / GATHER | Group assembly, important announcement |
| Hand across throat (sliding) | DANGER / DO NOT | Deadly hazard, prohibition |
| Finger to lips | SILENCE | Stealth required, predator nearby |
| Arm sweeping left/right | GO THAT DIRECTION | Flanking movement, route change |
| Two fingers pointing at eyes, then at target | WATCH THAT | Sustained observation needed |
| Open-close hand rapidly | HURRY | Time pressure, urgency |

**Silent hunting communication protocol:**

1. Before departing camp, rehearse 5-6 core signals with the hunting party. Confirm every person understands each gesture.
2. Designate a lead signaller (usually the tracker in front position). All others watch the lead for instructions.
3. During stalk: lead raises fist (stop), points (prey location), sweeps arm (flanking direction), presses palms down (conceal).
4. Attack signal: lead drops arm sharply downward from overhead. This is unambiguous and visible at 50+ m.
5. After kill: raised fist with pumping motion (success, come help). Both arms crossed overhead (abort, retreat).

**Teaching through demonstration (gestural pedagogy):**

1. Sit facing the learner. Ensure eye contact.
2. Perform the action slowly, exaggerating each step. When knapping, lift the hammerstone high, pause before striking, show the angle clearly.
3. Point to critical details: the platform angle, the striking point, the correct grip.
4. Guide the learner's hands physically through the motion. Physical contact carries more precise information than observation alone.
5. Nod for correct attempts. Shake head and demonstrate again for errors. No vocalisation needed.

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Silent hunting:** Vocal communication alerts prey. A gesture vocabulary of 10 signals coordinates a 4-person hunting party over 50 m of visual range without producing sound.
- **Predator response:** The time between spotting a predator and the group reacting determines survival. A raised palm (STOP) communicates instantly across 30 m. Shouting the same message also alerts the predator.
- **Inter-group encounters:** Gestural signals can communicate peaceful intent (open palms, weapons lowered) or threat (weapons raised, chest expansion) before vocal range, reducing the risk of ambush through misunderstanding.
- **Teaching:** Physical skills transfer 2-3x faster through hands-on guided demonstration than through verbal instruction alone. Early hominins teaching knapping, foraging, and processing relied entirely on gestural pedagogy.
- **Infant communication:** Human infants gesture meaningfully (pointing, reaching, waving) from 9-12 months, 6-12 months before first words. This channel was likely the primary parent-infant communication mode for millions of years.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

Your mouth will get you killed. Every sound you make carries hundreds of metres to predators and prey alike. Your hands are silent and visible at 50 metres. Learn to talk with them.

Sit your group down right now. Establish these five signals. Rehearse each one until everyone recognises it instantly:

Flat palm raised: STOP. Everything freezes. No exceptions. 

Point with full arm extension: LOOK THERE. Every head turns to where the finger points.

Both palms pushing down: GET DOWN. Everyone drops to a crouch or prone immediately.

Beckoning wave: COME HERE. Safe to approach.

Finger to lips: SILENCE. No talking, no movement, breathe through your nose only.

Five signals. Learn them in ten minutes. They will coordinate your group during hunting, alert everyone to danger without alerting the danger to you, and transfer information faster than any whispered conversation.

When you teach someone a physical skill, stop talking. Sit across from them, make eye contact, and show them with your hands. Slow every step down to half speed. Exaggerate every movement. When they try and fail, take their hands and guide them through the motion. Physical guidance carries more information than any words you could choose.

When you meet strangers, your body speaks before your voice does. Weapons down, palms open and visible, posture relaxed but upright. This is the universal signal for "I am not a threat." Every culture on earth reads this the same way. Getting it wrong is lethal.

Add new gestures as you need them. The group that hunted together yesterday will invent specific signals for situations they encountered. By the end of the first week, your gesture vocabulary should cover directions, quantities (one finger, two fingers, full hand for many), time (point at sun position for when), and emotional states (nod for good, head shake for bad, shrug for unknown). You are building a language. It does not need grammar. It needs clarity and consistency.
