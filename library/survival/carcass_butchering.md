# Carcass Butchering
<!-- @field: description -->
Carcass butchering is the systematic dismemberment of a dead animal to extract maximum edible material - muscle, organ, fat, bone marrow, and brain - in the minimum time and with the minimum tool investment. For early hominins, butchering speed was a critical variable: at a carcass site on the African savanna, large carnivores (lions, hyenas), scavenging birds (vultures, marabou storks), and jackals will arrive within minutes. A group that can extract and transport the most valuable portions before displacement has a significant survival advantage.

The anatomical logic of butchering follows the distribution of caloric density and spoilage rate. Organs (liver, heart, kidneys) are the highest caloric density, spoil fastest, and should be consumed first on-site. Muscle meat from the haunches and back is the bulk of the carcass and should be portioned and carried. Long bones should be cracked for marrow before leaving or at a safe processing site. Hide is secondary unless immediate shelter or clothing material is needed.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
The oldest confirmed butchering evidence consists of cut marks on animal bones from the Dikika site, Ethiopia (~3.4 Ma), and systematically from Olduvai Gorge, Tanzania (~1.8 Ma) and Koobi Fora, Kenya (~1.9 Ma). Cut mark analysis by Pat Shipman (1984) and Henry Bunn (1986) showed that early hominins accessed carcasses early - before large carnivores in many cases - based on the position of cut marks relative to carnivore tooth marks: when human cut marks underlie carnivore gnaw marks, the hominins got there first.

Experimental butchery studies by Kathy Schick and Nicholas Toth (1993, *Making Silent Stones Speak*) demonstrated that a single Oldowan flake can disarticulate a large bovid (wildebeest-sized) carcass completely in 20-30 minutes. The same task performed with bare hands would take 3-4 hours and yield dramatically less meat. The efficiency gain from even the simplest stone tool is enormous.

Zooarchaeological analysis of bovid limb bone representation at East African Oldowan sites shows consistent over-representation of marrow-rich long bones relative to their frequency in the living fauna, indicating deliberate selection and transport of these elements for marrow extraction - demonstrating that early hominins understood the anatomy of their prey well enough to make selective decisions under time pressure.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Priority sequence (time-sensitive):**

| Priority | Anatomical Location | Why | Time Available |
|----------|--------------------|----|----------------|
| 1 | Liver, heart, lungs (thoracic/abdominal cavity) | Highest nutrition, fastest spoilage | Eat on-site immediately |
| 2 | Hindquarters (femur + hindleg muscle) | Largest muscle mass | Transport first |
| 3 | Forelimbs + shoulder | Major muscle mass | Transport second |
| 4 | Back/loin | Rich muscle, near spine | Transport if time remains |
| 5 | Long bones (femur, tibia, humerus) | Marrow extraction | Crack at camp or on-site |
| 6 | Hide | Material utility | Only if time permits |
| 7 | Brain (skull) | Rich in DHA fat | Crack skull at camp |

**Disarticulation sequence:**

1. **Open the body cavity:** Insert a sharp flake into the soft tissue of the lower abdomen, cut upward along the midline. Avoid puncturing the stomach/intestines (smell will attract scavengers and the contents are toxic). Pull organs out directly - liver and heart first.

2. **Remove hindlimbs:** Locate the hip joint where the femoral head meets the pelvis. Flex the limb to expose the joint capsule. Cut through the connective tissue at the joint. With the flake inserted into the joint, lever the femoral head out. The entire hindlimb can now be separated.

3. **Remove forelimbs:** The shoulder joint in ungulates is not a ball-and-socket joint - it is held by muscle alone. Cut through the triceps and surrounding muscle from the inside of the axilla (armpit) while pulling the forelimb outward. The entire limb separates cleanly.

4. **Remove muscle from limbs:** Score the muscle longitudinally along each side of the bone. Insert fingers to peel the muscle away. The major muscle groups (quadriceps, hamstrings, gluteals) come off in large coherent masses.

5. **Crack long bones (see: *Bone Marrow Extraction*):** Carry complete bones to a safe location or crack on-site with a hammerstone.

**Tool requirements:**

| Task | Tool | Edge Type |
|------|------|-----------|
| Opening body cavity | Sharp thin flake | Very sharp, thin edge |
| Joint disarticulation | Sharp flake, slightly thicker | Needs to withstand prying force |
| Muscle separation from bone | Sharp flake | Long cutting edge |
| Hide removal | Sharp flake with retouched edge | Sustained scraping |
| Bone cracking | Hammerstone (400-800g) | Percussion, no edge required |

## Materials
<!-- @field: materials -->

| Item | Purpose | Notes |
|------|---------|-------|
| 2-3 sharp stone flakes | Cutting, joint separation, muscle removal | Bring from camp; no time to knap at carcass |
| Hammerstone | Bone marrow extraction | Dense cobble, 400-800g |
| Bark trays or large leaves | Carrying meat portions | Pre-made before arrival |
| Group of 3-5 individuals | Speed, defense of carcass, transport | Single individual at large carcass is risky |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Maximizing caloric yield from opportunistic kills:** A wildebeest provides 80-120 kg of edible material. In 25-30 minutes of systematic butchering, a skilled group can remove and transport 30-50 kg of the highest-value portions before being displaced by carnivores.
- **Scavenging efficiency:** When arriving at a carnivore kill, the priority portions are different than a fresh kill - focus on long bones (marrow intact), any remaining organ material not yet consumed, and accessible muscle at joint areas.
- **Knowledge of animal anatomy:** Effective butchering requires mental mapping of the animal's skeleton, joint locations, and major muscle groups. This knowledge develops over years of practice and is transmitted explicitly within groups.
- **Tool preparation ahead of time:** Bringing pre-made flakes to the carcass site rather than knapping on arrival saves critical minutes during the most time-pressured phase of butchering.

## Day 0 — Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0 -->
You found a fresh carcass. You have 10-15 minutes before hyenas arrive. You have 3 flakes and a hammerstone.

Move fast. Here is the order:

Open the belly. Cut from the navel upward on the midline. Pull out the liver - dark red, large, lobed. Eat a piece now. Give pieces to everyone present. It will not survive the journey back.

Go to the hip. Flex the hindleg back. Cut into the joint where the leg meets the pelvis. Work the flake around the joint capsule. Pull outward while cutting. The leg separates. Hand it to someone to carry.

Do the other hindleg. Do both forelegs - cut from the inside of the armpit, pull outward.

Pick up the long bones if you can carry them. Crack them at camp.

If you hear hyenas, take what you have and leave now. Fighting a clan of 20 hyenas for the remaining carcass is a bad trade.

Everything you carried out is food. Everything you left behind is theirs.
