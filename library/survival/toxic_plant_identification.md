# Toxic Plant Identification & Food Avoidance

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

Roughly 10-15% of plant species contain compounds toxic enough to cause illness or death in humans. In a survival context without medical support, ingesting the wrong plant can kill within hours (water hemlock, castor bean) or cause debilitating vomiting and diarrhea that leads to fatal dehydration. Toxic compounds include alkaloids (nicotine, strychnine, atropine), glycosides (cyanogenic compounds in cherry pits, cassava), oxalates (in rhubarb leaves, dumb cane), and lectins (in raw kidney beans). The Universal Edibility Test (UET) is a systematic protocol for testing unknown plants through progressive exposure - skin, lip, tongue, small taste, small meal - with wait periods between each stage. No single visual rule like "red means danger" is reliable. The only reliable approach is the protocol.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

Plant toxin knowledge is among the oldest forms of transmitted information. Chimpanzees at Mahale Mountains (Tanzania) avoid specific Strychnos species despite eating closely related ones, as documented by Toshisada Nishida (1994), suggesting pre-human origins of toxin avoidance. Neanderthal dental calculus from El Sidron cave (Spain, 49,000 BP) contains traces of bitter yarrow and chamomile but shows avoidance of toxic Ranunculus species growing nearby. The Ebers Papyrus (Egypt, 1550 BC) lists plants "not to be eaten" including hemlock, henbane, and belladonna. Indigenous Amazonian peoples developed processing techniques to remove cyanide from cassava - a multi-day soaking and heating process that makes an otherwise lethal staple edible, documented as early as 8,000 BP in archaeological contexts.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->

**Universal warning signs (avoid immediately):**

| Feature | Examples | Toxin class |
|---------|----------|-------------|
| Milky or coloured sap | Euphorbia, manchineel, oleander | Diterpenes, cardiac glycosides |
| Bitter or burning taste | Alkaloid-containing plants | Alkaloids |
| Almond-like smell in crushed leaves | Cherry laurel, cassava | Cyanogenic glycosides |
| Shiny black or white berries | Belladonna, baneberry, pokeweed | Various alkaloids |
| Umbrella-shaped flower clusters (Apiaceae family) | Hemlock, water hemlock | Cicutoxin, coniine |
| Three-leaflet pattern | Poison ivy/oak (skin), some toxic legumes | Urushiol, lectins |
| Seeds inside pods that rattle | Castor bean, jequirity bean | Ricin, abrin |
| Fungal fruiting body with white gills + ring + cup base | Amanita (death cap, destroying angel) | Amatoxins |

**Universal Edibility Test (UET) protocol:**

| Stage | Action | Wait time | Stop if |
|-------|--------|-----------|---------|
| 0 | Fast for 8 hours (empty stomach baseline) | - | - |
| 1 | Crush plant part, rub on inner wrist | 15 min | Redness, rash, burning |
| 2 | Touch to corner of lip | 15 min | Tingling, numbness, burning |
| 3 | Place on tongue tip, do not swallow | 15 min | Bitter taste, burning, numbness |
| 4 | Chew small piece, spit out | 15 min | Nausea, odd sensation |
| 5 | Swallow one small piece (thumbnail size) | 8 hours | Any GI distress, dizziness |
| 6 | Eat small handful | 8 hours | Any symptoms |
| 7 | If no reactions, consider edible in that preparation | - | - |

**Critical rules for the UET:**

1. Test only ONE plant part at a time (leaf, root, fruit, seed are different).
2. Raw and cooked versions are separate tests.
3. Only one person tests - never the whole group.
4. Keep a sample of the tested plant for reference.
5. If any negative reaction occurs at any stage, induce vomiting (if within 30 min of ingestion) and drink large quantities of water.

**Poisoning first response:**

1. Identify what was eaten. Keep a sample or remember the location.
2. If within 30 minutes of ingestion: induce vomiting by stimulating the gag reflex with a finger at the back of the throat.
3. Drink activated charcoal if available (crush charcoal from hardwood fire into water, drink). Charcoal adsorbs many toxins.
4. Drink large volumes of clean water to dilute and flush.
5. Do NOT induce vomiting for caustic or petroleum substances.
6. Monitor breathing, pulse, and consciousness for 24 hours.

## Materials & Improvisation
<!-- @field: materials -->

| Material | Source | Purpose |
|----------|--------|---------|
| Charcoal (hardwood) | Campfire remnants, crushed fine | Emergency toxin adsorbent |
| Clean water | Stream, filtered, boiled | Dilution and flushing |
| Reference samples | Pressed leaves, dried specimens | Build a "known safe" and "known toxic" collection |
| Storage container | Bark, shell, gourd | Separated storage for test material |

**Building a local plant knowledge base:**

- Observe what animals eat. Primates share many toxin sensitivities with humans. If local monkeys eat a fruit freely, it is more likely safe (not guaranteed).
- Observe what animals avoid. Goats eat almost anything - if goats refuse a plant, be very cautious.
- Track and record every plant tested through the UET, marking the location and result.

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Food safety:** Prevents fatal poisoning from unknown plants during foraging expansion into unfamiliar territory.
- **Water safety:** Some aquatic plants (water hemlock, blue-green algae) contaminate water sources. Recognizing them prevents contact poisoning.
- **Processing toxic staples:** Many nutrient-rich plants (cassava, taro, acorns) are toxic raw but safe after processing (soaking, boiling, leaching, fermentation). Understanding this unlocks high-calorie food sources.
- **Poison applications:** Toxin knowledge enables arrow and spear poison (curare, Diamphotoxin), fish poison (rotenone from specific roots), and insect repellent.
- **Medicine:** Many toxic plants are medicinal at low doses (digitalis from foxglove for heart conditions, quinine from cinchona bark for malaria).

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

Hunger is dangerous, but eating the wrong thing is worse. A single bite of water hemlock root can kill you in 2 hours with violent seizures. There is no field antidote. Your first rule: never eat anything you cannot identify with certainty.

Start with what you know. Dandelions, clover, grass seeds, cattail roots, pine needles for tea - these are safe across most of the world. If you recognize nothing around you, use the Universal Edibility Test.

Pick one plant. Choose the most abundant species - if it passes, you have a food source. Take one part only (just the leaf, or just the root). Crush it and rub the juice on your inner wrist. Wait 15 minutes. No rash, no burning? Touch it to the corner of your lip. Wait 15 minutes. Nothing? Place a small piece on your tongue. Wait. Chew a tiny piece and spit it out. Wait. Swallow a thumbnail-sized piece and wait 8 full hours. If at any point you feel numbness, burning, nausea, or dizziness - stop immediately, drink water, eat crushed charcoal from your fire mixed into water.

Remember the absolute avoidance signs: milky sap, almond smell from crushed leaves, bitter or burning taste, umbrella-shaped white flower clusters, and mushrooms with white gills plus a ring on the stem plus a cup at the base. These patterns cover the most lethal plant families on earth.

Build your knowledge base every day. Test one new plant while subsisting on known-safe foods. Within two weeks you will have 5-10 confirmed safe species for your area. Mark the location of toxic plants with a pile of stones so you do not revisit them. This slow, methodical approach keeps you alive while the impulsive approach kills.
