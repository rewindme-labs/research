# Habitat Adaptation

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

Habitat adaptation is the adjustment of survival strategies when moving between distinct ecological zones: open savanna, woodland edges, riverine forest, rocky highlands, coastal margins, and arid scrubland. Each biome offers a different resource profile and presents different threats. Savanna provides visibility and game but little shade and distant water. Forest provides food diversity and cover but limited visibility and dangerous floor-level encounters. Riverine zones provide water, dense food, and tool materials but concentrate predators. The ability to read each habitat's unique characteristics and modify behaviour accordingly is what allowed Homo to colonise every terrestrial biome on Earth. Generalists survive where specialists perish.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

The distribution of Homo erectus sites across Africa, the Caucasus (Dmanisi, 1.8 Ma), Southeast Asia (Java, 1.6 Ma), and China (Zhoukoudian, 780,000 BP) demonstrates successful habitat adaptation across tropical savanna, temperate forest, volcanic highland, and monsoon coast environments. At Olduvai Gorge (1.8 Ma), site distribution across lake margin, river channel, and adjacent upland plateau within the same stratigraphic level shows Homo habilis exploiting multiple habitat types within daily ranging distance (Blumenschine & Peters, 1998). The Omo-Kibish (Ethiopia, 195,000 BP) and Jebel Irhoud (Morocco, 300,000 BP) Homo sapiens sites span dramatically different climatic zones, confirming that behavioural flexibility rather than genetic specialisation drove range expansion.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->

**Habitat assessment matrix:**

| Habitat | Primary food | Water | Shelter | Main threat | Movement strategy |
|---------|-------------|-------|---------|-------------|-------------------|
| Open savanna | Game, tubers | Seasonal rivers, pans | Minimal, need construction | Predators (visibility cuts both ways) | Travel at midday when predators rest |
| Woodland edge | Fruits, nuts, small game | Seasonal streams | Trees, natural cover | Snakes, leopard | Forage at edges, retreat to trees |
| Riverine forest | Fish, shellfish, plants | River (permanent) | Dense canopy | Crocodiles, hippos | Never camp at water level |
| Rocky highland | Game, tubers, stone | Springs, rain catchment | Caves, overhangs | Exposure, rockfall | Use shelter formations, cache tools |
| Coastal margin | Shellfish, fish, seabirds | Freshwater inlets | Dune caves, rock shelters | Tides, storms | Time foraging to tidal cycle |
| Arid scrubland | Root tubers, insects, game | Rare springs, dew | Minimal | Dehydration, heat | Travel at night, shelter midday |

**Transitioning between habitats:**

1. Scout before committing. Send 2 people to explore the new habitat for half a day before moving the group. They assess water, shelter, food availability, predator signs, and terrain.
2. Carry essentials from the old habitat. Water containers, proven tools, fire-starting materials, and emergency food. The new habitat may not provide these immediately.
3. Establish camp near the habitat boundary initially. This allows retreat to known territory if the new area proves unsuitable.
4. Test food sources cautiously. Different habitats contain different plant species. Apply the standard edibility test to unfamiliar plants even if they resemble known species.
5. Observe local animal behaviour. Resident animals have already solved the habitat's challenges. Watch what they eat, where they drink, and where they shelter.

**Seasonal habitat cycling:**

| Season | Preferred habitat | Reason |
|--------|------------------|--------|
| Wet season | Higher ground, woodland edge | Avoid flooding, abundant fruit/nut crops |
| Dry season | Riverine zones, permanent water | Only reliable water source, concentrated game |
| Hot season | Forest canopy or coastal | Shade, water access, reduced heat stress |
| Cool/temperate season | Open savanna | Optimal movement conditions, dispersed game |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Range expansion:** Groups that adapt across habitat types exploit 3-5x the territory of habitat specialists. This buffers against local resource failure and supports larger populations.
- **Seasonal optimisation:** Moving between habitats to match resource peaks maximises caloric intake: coastal shellfish in summer, highland game in autumn, riverine fish in dry season.
- **Extinction resistance:** Habitat specialists go extinct when their biome changes. Generalists shift to alternative habitats and persist. This is the primary reason Homo sapiens survived climate oscillations that eliminated Neanderthals from Europe.
- **Innovation pressure:** Each new habitat presents novel problems that drive tool innovation, food processing development, and social organisation adaptation. Habitat diversity accelerates cultural evolution.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

Look at where you are right now. Is it open ground? Forest? Near water? Rocky? Each type gives you different things and threatens you with different dangers. Adapt to what this specific place offers.

If you are in open grassland, your advantages are visibility and movement speed. Your problems are no cover from sun, wind, or predators. Find elevated ground for camp. Travel during midday heat when predators rest. Build windbreaks from whatever you can drag together and get water sorted as your first priority because it may be far away.

If you are at a forest edge, you have food diversity and cover but limited visibility. Forage along the edge where light reaches and fruit grows. Retreat into the trees for safety (most large predators cannot follow you up). Watch the ground carefully for snakes. Sound travels strangely in forest and you may not hear approaching danger until it is close.

If you are near a river, you have the most valuable location possible: permanent water, fish, shellfish, dense vegetation, and tool stone in the riverbed. Your dangers are concentrated here too: crocodiles, hippos, malaria mosquitoes, flooding. Camp above the flood line, never at water level. Fill water containers from elevated banks, never kneeling at the edge.

When seasons change, be ready to move. The water hole that sustained you in the wet season dries in the dry season. The fruit trees that fed you in summer are bare in winter. If you have mapped your territory properly, you know where the dry-season water is, where the winter food alternatives grow, and which shelter sites serve each season.

The groups that survive are not the strongest. They are the most flexible. When the habitat changes, change with it. When the habitat you are in stops providing, move to one that does. Stubbornness and attachment to a single location is how groups starve.
