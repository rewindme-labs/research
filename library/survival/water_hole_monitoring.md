# Water Hole Monitoring
<!-- @field: description -->
A permanent water hole in the dry season is simultaneously the most critical resource and the most dangerous location on the East African savanna. Every animal in the region must drink - prey species, predators, and hominins alike converge on the same limited water points. Lions, leopards, hyenas, and crocodiles all learn to ambush prey at water holes, making these locations peak predation risk zones, especially at dawn, dusk, and night.

Water hole monitoring is the practice of systematically observing a water source before approaching, timing approach for minimum predation risk, approaching in group formation with sentinels, and drinking efficiently before withdrawing to a safer location. It converts a high-risk necessity into a manageable daily task through observation and behavioral discipline. Groups that approach water holes carelessly lose members; groups that monitor systematically drink safely every day.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
The importance of water hole management for early hominins is reflected in site distributions. Analysis of *Australopithecus* and early *Homo* fossil sites across East Africa by Anna Kay Behrensmeyer and colleagues consistently shows clustering within 1-3 km of paleolake margins, ancient spring lines, and river channels - suggesting that hominin group territories were organized around reliable water access.

Cave sites in South Africa (Sterkfontein, Swartkrans, Kromdraai) that have produced *Australopithecus africanus* and *Paranthropus robustus* fossils are now understood as carnivore den accumulations - hominins that were killed and carried to these sites, primarily by leopards. The taphonomic analysis by C.K. Brain (1981, *The Hunters or the Hunted?*) suggests that many of these kills occurred at or near water sources, where leopards established predictable ambush positions.

Modern ethological observation at Amboseli (Kenya) by Cynthia Moss and colleagues has documented that elephant herds approach water holes only after extensive olfactory testing from upwind positions and visual scanning from elevated ground, before committing to descent to the water's edge - a behavior directly analogous to what is proposed for early hominins.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Phase 1 - Remote observation (approach 200-300m from water):**

1. Approach from upwind and from elevated ground if possible
2. Stop at 200-300 meters and scan the water hole and surroundings for 5-10 minutes
3. Check for:
   - Predator presence (lion lying near water, leopard in tree above water, crocodile at edge)
   - Prey animal behavior: are wildebeest/zebra drinking calmly or alert at distance? Alert prey = predator nearby
   - Vultures in trees near water (indicates recent kill, carnivores present)
   - Unusual silence of birds

**Indicators at the water hole:**

| Signal | Meaning | Action |
|--------|---------|--------|
| Prey animals drinking calmly | No immediate predator threat | Proceed with approach |
| Prey animals at distance, not approaching | Predator at water or approach route | Wait, continue monitoring |
| Prey animals fleeing from water | Active predator approaching or attacking | Do not approach |
| Crocodile/hippo visible in water | Dangerous, do not enter water | Drink from edge at maximum distance |
| Vultures circling/in trees | Recent kill, carnivores nearby | High caution, may approach after 30 min |
| Cattle egrets or wading birds at edge | Generally safe | Positive signal |

**Phase 2 - Staged approach:**

1. Approach in group formation, not single file, with sentinels at front
2. Advance to 100 meters, stop, scan again (2-3 minutes)
3. If clear, advance to water's edge as a group, not sequentially
4. Sentinels maintain peripheral watch while others drink
5. Drinkers alternate - never all drinking simultaneously

**Phase 3 - Drinking protocol:**

1. Drink from the downwind side of the water hole (predators typically upwind)
2. Avoid dense vegetation at water edge - leopard ambush sites
3. Do not wade in - crocodile risk in any water deeper than knee in East Africa
4. Drinkers kneel briefly, face up and scan between mouthfuls - do not bury face in water for extended periods
5. Collect water in containers for camp (gourd, animal stomach) while at water
6. Complete all collection and drinking efficiently - minimize time at the water's edge

**Timing:**

| Time of Day | Risk Level | Notes |
|-------------|-----------|-------|
| Dawn (first light) | High | Nocturnal predators returning, transitional |
| Mid-morning (08:00-10:00) | Low | Best time to drink in dry season |
| Midday | Low-medium | Hot, predators resting in shade |
| Late afternoon (16:00-18:00) | Medium-high | Predators beginning to stir |
| Dusk | Very high | Peak hunting time |
| Night | Extreme | Do not approach water at night |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Daily hydration security:** In dry season, the water hole may be the only water source within a day's walk. Systematic monitoring protocol ensures the group can drink daily without losing members.
- **Information gathering:** Regular visits to the water hole provide information on animal movements, predator territories, and which prey species are concentrated in the area - critical intelligence for hunting planning.
- **Social intersection:** Water holes are where groups with overlapping territories encounter each other. Monitoring includes watching for other hominin groups and reading whether their behavior signals conflict or peaceful coexistence.
- **Container water:** Collecting water in containers during each visit reduces visit frequency, lowering cumulative predation risk exposure.

## Technical Data
<!-- @field: technical_data -->

| Parameter | Value | Notes |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| Peak predation risk at water hole | Dawn and dusk | Corresponds to peak lion activity |
| Safest approach time | Mid-morning | 08:00-10:00 in East African conditions |
| Crocodile ambush range | Within 1-2 body lengths of water edge | Do not kneel within 3m of water in crocodile habitat |
| Detection range of lion at water hole | 50-200m | Wind-dependent; downwind of lion = no scent |
| Remote observation minimum time | 5-10 minutes | Before committing to approach |
| Prey animal behavior as indicator | Most reliable signal | Calm prey = safe; alert prey = predator nearby |
| Optimal approach group size | 5-10 individuals | Balance between safety and water hole displacement of other animals |
| Container collection capacity | 2-5 liters per person | With animal stomach, gourd, or bark container |

## Day 0 — Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0 -->
You are 300 meters from the water hole. Your mouth is dry. Your instinct says run to it.

Wait.

Stop here for 5 minutes. Look at the animals near the water. Are the zebra drinking or standing back at 100 meters, alert? If they are not drinking, something is wrong.

Check the trees overhanging the water. Do you see a leopard in the branches? Check the tall grass at the edge. Is it moving without wind?

If the zebra are calm and drinking, approach in your group, spread out, not in single file. Sentinels stay upright and watch. Drinkers drink fast, face up between mouthfuls.

Fill your containers while you are there. Fewer visits means less risk.

Do not approach at dusk. Do not approach at night. No amount of thirst is worth dying at the water's edge in the dark.

The animal that monitors before approaching has been drinking here for 3 million years. The animal that runs straight to the water is the reason the predators stay near the water.
