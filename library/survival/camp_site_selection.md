# Safe Camp Site Selection

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->
Camp site selection is the systematic evaluation and choice of a location for a base camp that will serve as the operational center for all subsequent survival activities. It is the single most consequential decision in the first week of any survival situation: a bad camp site costs energy, risks flooding, increases predator exposure, requires relocation, and demoralizes a group. A good camp site saves energy, protects against weather and predators, provides access to water and resources, and provides the stable base from which shelter, food production, fire management, and signaling can all be operated simultaneously.

The decision should be made by Day 4 at the latest. Before Day 4, a temporary night camp (trees, elevation, fire perimeter) is acceptable. After Day 4, the energy cost of constant relocation, combined with the physical demands of foraging and construction, makes a permanent base essential.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
Archaeological analysis of prehistoric base camps (Olduvai Gorge, Tanzania; Pinnacle Point, South Africa; Lazaret Cave, France) consistently reveals the same selection criteria across millions of years and multiple species: proximity to water (within 500m-1km), elevated position above the valley floor, wind protection from prevailing direction, and proximity to flint or other stone tool materials. These are not coincidences - they are the convergent solutions of multiple independent groups to the same optimization problem.

The Late Paleolithic site of Ohalo II (Sea of Galilee, Israel, c. 23,000 BCE) provides one of the most complete preserved base camp records. Six brush huts arranged around a central hearth, located on a lakeside terrace with 3m of elevation above water level, facing the prevailing east-southeasterly wind from the lake, with views of the entire basin. Hazelnut and acorn caches, fish bones, and grass seed grinding stones indicate that the site was occupied seasonally, abandoned, and re-occupied - meaning subsequent groups recognized and returned to the same optimal location.

Roman military engineering formalized camp site selection into a standardized procedure described in Vegetius's *Epitoma Rei Militaris* (390 CE): no camp within 1000 paces of forest or cover that enemies could use; water must be accessible but the camp elevated above it; prevailing wind must sweep across the camp, not lie stagnant within it; and the camp must have at least two separate exit routes in case of attack or fire. These criteria are functionally identical to the prehistoric selection criteria documented at Ohalo II, 23,000 years earlier.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Evaluation criteria (in priority order):**

**1. Water - the primary constraint**

| Distance to water | Assessment | Notes |
|------------------|------------|-------|
| 50-200m | Optimal | Close enough for daily access; far enough to avoid flood zone, insects, and predator ambush zones at the water's edge |
| 200-500m | Acceptable | Increases daily water-carrying effort but otherwise good |
| 500m-1km | Acceptable with effort | Consider water transport (bark containers, hollow log channels) |
| Under 50m | Not recommended | Flood risk; mosquito density; predator ambush zone; bank instability |
| Over 1km | Reject unless forced | Daily water run becomes primary energy expenditure |

- The water source should be flowing (spring or stream), not stagnant (parasite and bacteria concentration much higher in still water).
- Check for upstream contamination: carcasses, evidence of large animal wallowing, human/animal habitation upstream. Flowing water self-purifies at roughly 1 km per hour under natural conditions.

**2. Drainage and flood risk**

1. Look at the soil and vegetation. Flat, silty soil with moisture-loving plants (sedges, cattails near water, willows) indicates regular flooding.
2. Look for waterline marks on trees, rocks, or banks. The highest mark indicates the flood level. Your camp must be well above this, not adjacent to it.
3. Look at slope angle. A 2-5 degree slope away from the center of camp in at least two directions ensures rain water drains away, not pools underneath sleeping positions.
4. Avoid valley bottoms during any season when upland rain is possible. Flash floods from storms 30-50km away can reach valley floors with no warning.

**3. Wind and microclimate**

- Face the camp entrance away from the prevailing wind direction. This keeps smoke from the fire out of sleeping areas and reduces the cold wind chill effect on occupants.
- A slight embankment, rock face, or dense tree line 10-20m upwind provides an effective windbreak without blocking visibility.
- In cold environments: a south-facing slope (Northern Hemisphere) receives more solar radiation and stays 3-5°C warmer than a north-facing slope.
- In hot environments: north-facing or valley-shaded positions reduce heat load.
- Cold air drains downhill at night (temperature inversion). Camping at the bottom of a valley in a hollow is consistently 3-8°C colder at night than the surrounding hillside.

**4. Visibility and predator defense**

| Site Feature | Benefit |
|-------------|---------|
| Elevated position (10-30m above surroundings) | Visibility of approaching threats; detection range increases significantly |
| Clear sight line in primary threat directions | No cover within 50m for ambush approach |
| Single access point (cliff at back, river on one side) | Reduces number of perimeter directions to defend |
| Large trees nearby for elevated sleeping | Fallback if fire perimeter fails |
| No water hole within 100m | Water holes at night attract all local predators and their prey |

**5. Fuel, building material, and food resource proximity**

| Resource | Desired Distance |
|----------|----------------|
| Firewood (dead standing wood preferred) | Under 200m |
| Large construction timber (for shelter) | Under 300m |
| Dense brush or leaf litter (bedding insulation) | Under 200m |
| Known food resource (nut grove, shellfish bed, game trail) | Under 30 minutes travel |

**Camp layout once site is chosen:**

- Central fire position: 5-8m from sleeping area (heat radius without burning hazard)
- Sleeping area: upwind of fire (smoke goes away from sleepers)
- Water storage area: downhill from sleeping and cooking (runoff flows away)
- Food storage (if available): 50m+ from sleeping area in a separate location, cache raised off ground (predators and rodents)
- Latrine site: 50m+ downhill and downwind from camp; rotate positions regularly

## Materials & Improvisation
<!-- @field: materials -->
Camp selection requires no materials - only evaluation. The subsequent construction of shelter, fire perimeter, and storage uses:

| Need | Material | Notes |
|------|----------|-------|
| Site marker | Prominent rock cairn, carved tree mark | Enables finding camp after foraging excursions |
| Wind assessment | Wet finger, grass blade held up | Determines exact wind direction |
| Flood line detection | Debris line on vegetation; watermarks on rock | Establish how high past floods have reached |
| Ground drainage test | Pour water on ground; observe flow direction | Identifies low points and drainage channels |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Permanent base camp:** The camp selected by Day 4 should be designed for weeks of occupation. Invest the time in correct selection because relocation is enormously costly in lost construction work, established caches, and familiarity.
- **Temporary overnight camp:** Apply the drainage and predator proximity criteria at minimum. Wind and fuel criteria matter less for a single night.
- **Group camps:** Increased water consumption (minimum 2L per person per day for activity), increased noise, and increased fire demand all reinforce the importance of the water-proximity criterion.
- **Re-evaluation after major weather:** After significant storm, flood, or earthquake, re-evaluate. A formerly safe site may have a new upstream blockage creating flood risk.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->
You have been moving for 3 days. Tonight you stop moving and start building. But where you stop matters more than what you build.

Take 30 minutes before you commit to a site. Walk a 200m radius from your initial candidate location looking for these things:

**The three required checks:**

1. **Flood line.** Find the nearest water body. Walk its bank and look for the debris line on tree trunks and rocks - the line of mud, sticks, and dead leaves stuck at the same height in multiple places. That is where the water reached in the last major flood. Your camp must be above this. "Above" means both elevation (minimum 2-3m) and horizontal distance (minimum 50m). The flat, grassy area right next to the stream looks inviting. It floods.

2. **Wind direction.** Wet your finger and hold it up. Note which side of your finger gets cold. That is the wind direction. Position yourself so the wind will flow across your camp site, not collect in it. At night, cold air drains downhill and collects in valley hollows. If you are in a hollow, you will be 5-8°C colder than anyone 30m up the slope, burning more wood and sleeping less. Move upslope.

3. **Water access.** From your candidate site, walk toward your water source. Time it. More than 10 minutes means you will be carrying water multiple times per day in significant quantities. That is an enormous drain on energy that compounds every day. If the site is otherwise excellent but water is 15 minutes away, invest Day 5 in building a simple bark-channel or container water transport system rather than moving the camp.

**Once committed:**
Mark the site. Place a distinctive stone pile (cairn) visible from 50m in each approach direction. You will leave this camp to forage every day. You need to find it again, potentially exhausted, at dusk. The cairn takes 5 minutes to build and will matter more than you expect.

At the center of camp, clear a 6m diameter circle of all dead vegetation. This is your fire base. Tonight you build three perimeter fires. Tomorrow you build your shelter. The site decision is made. Stop second-guessing it and start building.
