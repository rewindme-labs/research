# Small Game Hunting
<!-- @field: description -->
While persistence hunting and large game scavenging provide high caloric yields, they are episodic and energetically demanding. Small game hunting — the capture of reptiles, rodents, ground birds, frogs, and similar animals — provides a more reliable, daily-accessible protein source requiring lower skill thresholds and no group coordination. A single individual with a throwing stick, improvised trap, or bare hands can secure 200-800 kcal of animal protein on most days in a productive savanna environment.

Small game is available in environments where large game is absent, scarce, or inaccessible. Lizards, skinks, and monitor lizards are present year-round in East African savanna. Rodents — particularly mole rats, grass rats, and ground squirrels — are abundant in most soil types. Ground birds (guineafowl, francolins, bustards) are catchable with throwing sticks and basic trapping. Even in lean seasons when nothing larger is available, an attentive forager can find small animal protein every day.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
The nutritional importance of small animals in early hominin diet is documented through multiple lines of evidence. Dental microwear analysis of *Homo habilis* specimens shows scratch patterns consistent with consumption of hard, brittle food items including small bones — consistent with consumption of whole small animals including lizards and rodents.

Zooarchaeological assemblages at Olduvai Gorge and Koobi Fora include microfauna (small mammal and reptile bones) alongside macrofauna, suggesting that hominins consumed a broad spectrum of animal sizes. A 2004 study by Charles Egeland and colleagues (*Journal of Human Evolution*) analyzing the Olduvai fauna showed that small animals represented a significant fraction of hominin dietary diversity even when large game was available.

The Hadza of Tanzania, among the most intensively studied contemporary foragers, consume a wide variety of small animals: small birds (caught in snares), rock hyraxes, tortoises, monitor lizards, and rodents taken opportunistically during plant-food collecting expeditions. Frank Marlowe (2010) estimated that small animals provided 5-15% of Hadza caloric intake by frequency of occurrence in the diet, with disproportionate micronutrient contribution.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Opportunistic capture — no tool required:**

Many small animals can be captured without tools when encountered in vulnerable situations:

| Animal | Situation | Capture Method |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| Lizard (most species) | Basking on rock, early morning cold | Approach slowly from behind, pin with hand |
| Snake (non-venomous) | After rain, crossing open ground | Pin head with a forked stick, grip behind head |
| Frog/toad | At water's edge at night | Hand capture in light |
| Tortoise | Open ground, any time | Flip and carry |
| Ground bird nest | Eggs found | Collect eggs directly |
| Mole rat tunnel | Active tunnel (fresh soil) | Block exits, dig and grab |

**Throwing stick technique:**

1. Select a stick 60-90 cm long, 3-4 cm diameter, with a natural slight curve or offset (not straight)
2. Grip one end, swing to throw in a rotating horizontal arc at knee-to-hip height
3. The rotating stick sweeps a wide path — effective against ground birds in grass, startled rodents, and lizards
4. Practice the horizontal arc — accuracy improves significantly after 20-30 throws
5. A flushed guineafowl or francolin running through grass can be stunned or killed at 5-10 meters

**Simple pit trap:**

1. Identify an active rodent runway — narrow path through grass with droppings and compressed vegetation
2. Dig a pit 20-30 cm deep, 20 cm diameter, directly on the runway
3. Cover lightly with dry grass stems arranged to look like the runway surface
4. Check every 1-2 hours — rodents following their habitual runway fall in and cannot climb out
5. A single afternoon of trapping with 5-6 pits yields 1-3 animals on a productive day

**Rock fall trap (for larger small game):**

1. Prop a large flat rock (5-10 kg) on a thin stick trigger
2. Tie bait (piece of fruit, insect, tuber) to the trigger stick
3. When animal pulls the bait, the trigger releases, the rock falls
4. Simple to construct, 15-minute setup; works unattended overnight

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Daily protein baseline:** While large game hunts are sporadic, small game capture can provide meaningful protein almost every day. Over a week, daily small game supplementation can provide as much total protein as a single large game event.
- **Solo foraging:** Large game hunting requires group coordination; small game hunting is viable for a single individual or a pair. This matters for individuals who are separated, scouting, or in territories where the group is dispersed.
- **Micronutrient richness:** Small animals — particularly liver, organs, and small bones — are dense in vitamins A, B12, iron, and zinc. These micronutrients are scarce in plant-heavy diets and critical for neurological function and growth.
- **Children's learning:** Small game hunting is learnable by children as young as 8-10 years old, providing early skill development, dietary independence, and caloric contribution to the group.

## Day 0 — Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0 -->
You have been walking all morning and found nothing large. But there are lizards on every rock.

The biggest one — the monitor, banded with yellow — is basking 3 meters away with its eyes half closed.

It is cold this morning. It is slow.

Move toward it slowly, keeping your body low. Do not look directly at its head — peripheral vision triggers flight faster than direct approach. Come from the side, not the front.

When you are within arm's length, your hand comes down hard on its back, just behind the head. Pin it. It will thrash. Hold on. A monitor's tail can hurt you but its legs cannot escape your grip behind the shoulder girdle.

This animal weighs 2 kg. It is 1,500 calories. It is your day's food in 30 seconds of attention.

If you see guineafowl in the grass ahead, pick up a curved stick. When they flush and run rather than fly — they often do — throw the stick in a horizontal spinning arc at knee height, sweeping through the flock. You may not kill one, but you may stun one.

Small animals are everywhere. You just have to be patient enough to see them.
