# Problem Solving

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

Problem solving is the cognitive process of recognizing an obstacle between current state and desired state, generating possible solutions, evaluating them against available resources and constraints, executing a plan, and modifying the approach when the first attempt fails. In the survival context, problem solving separates reactive behaviour (running from a predator) from strategic behaviour (building a barrier so the predator cannot enter camp). Early hominins demonstrated multi-step problem solving at least 2.6 Ma, when they began transporting raw stone material over distances of 10+ km to specific butchery locations, a chain of decisions requiring foresight: identify future need, select appropriate material, carry it at metabolic cost through territory, and apply it at the right moment. This capacity is the single cognitive ability that makes all other survival skills possible. Tools, fire, shelter, hunting, medicine, and social organization are all downstream products of structured problem solving.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

Experimental evidence from Kanzi the bonobo (Savage-Rumbaugh, 1994) demonstrates that great apes can solve novel tool-use problems but rarely chain more than two steps. The Lomekwian tools from West Turkana (Kenya, 3.3 Ma), described by Harmand (2015), show that pre-Homo hominins could solve the knapping problem: selecting a suitable hammerstone, identifying a striking platform on a core, and applying controlled force at the correct angle. At Olduvai Gorge (1.8 Ma), Nicholas Toth's refitting studies revealed that individual knappers experimented with multiple striking angles before finding the optimal one, indicating trial-and-error reasoning preserved in the stone record. By 500,000 BP, Homo heidelbergensis at Schoningen (Germany) produced wooden throwing spears that required at least 8 sequential manufacturing steps across multiple days, each dependent on the success of the previous one, a level of sequential planning comparable to modern engineering projects.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->

**The problem-solving cycle (applicable to any survival challenge):**

| Step | Action | Example |
|------|--------|---------|
| 1. Identify | Define the problem precisely | "Water source is 2 km away and the path crosses open ground with predator activity" |
| 2. Analyze | What resources and constraints exist? | "We have 4 people, carrying vessels, 3 hours of daylight" |
| 3. Generate | List at least 3 possible solutions | "A: go in force at midday. B: go at dawn when predators rest. C: find alternate water" |
| 4. Evaluate | Rank by risk, effort, and reliability | "B is lowest risk but requires early wake. C requires scouting investment" |
| 5. Execute | Commit to the best option and act | "Tomorrow: 2 people depart at first light with spears and vessels" |
| 6. Review | Did it work? What went wrong? | "Arrived safely but only found shallow water. Next time, bring digging tools" |

**Common survival problems and structured solutions:**

1. **Food shortage:** Map all food sources within 5 km radius. Rank by caloric yield per hour of effort. Allocate gatherers to highest-yield sources first. If all nearby sources depleted, send a two-person scouting team to survey 10 km radius before relocating camp.

2. **Tool breakage:** Maintain a raw material cache at camp. When primary tool breaks, switch to backup immediately while a dedicated knapper produces replacement. Never carry only one cutting tool.

3. **Injury to key member:** Cross-train all critical skills. If the tracker is injured, who is second-best? If nobody, that is a problem you solve today, not after the injury happens.

4. **Predator camping nearby:** Do not engage. Increase fire intensity at night. Increase group noise during day. If behaviour persists beyond 3 days, relocate camp rather than waiting for an attack.

5. **Lost direction:** Stop moving. Observe sun position (east morning, west evening). Look for terrain features you recognise. Climb the nearest high point for visual orientation. If truly lost, follow water downstream. Water leads to larger water, and larger water leads to game trails, campsites, and resources.

**Decision-making under time pressure:**

1. If you have less than 10 seconds to decide (charging animal, flash flood), default to the pre-planned response. This is why you rehearse emergency actions.
2. If you have 10 minutes (storm approaching, daylight fading), run through the 6-step cycle quickly. Skip step 3 if only one option is viable.
3. If you have hours (planning tomorrow's forage), invest time in steps 3 and 4. Better planning today saves energy and risk tomorrow.

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Route planning:** Evaluating multiple paths to a destination by terrain difficulty, predator risk, water availability, and distance. Choosing the 6 km safe route over the 4 km dangerous route is a problem-solving output.
- **Tool innovation:** Every new tool type began as a solution to a specific problem. "I need to cut hide but my flake is too small" leads to selecting a larger core and modifying technique.
- **Social conflict:** Disputes over food distribution, sleeping positions, or mating access require negotiated solutions. Groups that resolve conflicts through structured discussion survive; groups that resolve through violence fracture.
- **Environmental adaptation:** Moving into new biomes (coastal, mountainous, arid) requires solving unfamiliar problems: new predators, new food sources, new weather patterns. The groups that adapted fastest colonized the planet.
- **Contingency planning:** Pre-solving problems that have not occurred yet. "If the river floods, where do we go?" answered before the flood arrives saves the group.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

Every problem you face has a structure. Name the problem out loud. Say exactly what is wrong and what you need to change. "I need water and the nearest source is dangerous" is a problem you can solve. "Everything is terrible" is not. Be specific.

List your options. Force yourself to think of at least three. The first idea that comes to mind is usually the most obvious and often the worst. The third idea tends to be better because your brain has already rejected the easy answers. If you can only think of one option, you are not thinking hard enough. Ask someone else. A second perspective almost always reveals at least one path you missed.

Before committing, ask: what happens if this fails? If option A fails and there is no recovery, do not choose option A. Choose the option where failure is survivable and a second attempt is possible. In survival, reversible mistakes are acceptable. Irreversible mistakes are not.

After you act, review what happened. Did it work? Why? If it failed, where did it go wrong? This takes two minutes and saves hours of repeated failure. Groups that debrief outperform groups that do not. Every time.

Practice this every day on small decisions. Where to gather today. Which path to take to water. Where to position the fire ring. Structured thinking on small problems builds the mental pathways that save your life on large ones.

Plan for the problems that have not happened yet. Sit at the fire tonight and ask your group: what if the water dries up? What if a predator enters camp? What if someone breaks a leg? Answer those questions now, while you have time and calm. When the crisis hits, you will not think clearly. But you will remember the plan.
