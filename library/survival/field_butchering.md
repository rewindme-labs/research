# Field Butchering & Meat Processing

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->
Field butchering is the systematic process of converting an animal carcass into safely edible meat, preserved stores, and usable byproducts (hide, bone, sinew) while minimizing contamination from intestinal bacteria and spoilage. In a survival scenario, an animal is not just food; it is a walking hardware store. The hide provides shelter and clothing; the bones become tools, needles, and soup; the sinews become bowstrings and cordage; the fat provides calories and waterproofing. However, improper butchering can puncture the gastrointestinal tract, contaminating the meat with *E. coli* or *Salmonella*, turning a life-saving caloric windfall into a fatal diarrheal infection. Effectively, butchering converts muscle and organs tightly wrapped in a biological package into safely storable calories.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
Evidence of systematic butchering appears in the archaeological record around 2.5 million years ago with the *Oldowan* tool industry. Cut marks on fossilized animal bones from Bouri, Ethiopia (~2.5 Ma) and Dikika (~3.4 Ma, though debated) show that early hominins used sharp stone flakes to deflesh carcasses and hammerstones to crack marrow bones. Early butchering was largely associated with scavenging - waiting for a sabertooth cat to kill a prey animal and eat its fill, then rushing in to harvest the remaining meat and bone marrow before hyenas arrived. The ability to crack long bones for marrow gave hominins access to a crucial, predator-protected fat source that facilitated brain expansion.

Across all hunter-gatherer cultures, nothing is wasted. Native Americans utilized every part of the bison: bladders for water containers, hooves for glue, bones for scrapers. In polar regions, the Inuit rely almost entirely on butchered seals, caribou, and fish, eating organs raw to obtain Vitamin C that is otherwise absent in the Arctic environment. Before refrigeration, butchering was always immediately followed by preservation (drying, smoking, or salting).

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**1. Field Dressing (Evisceration) - Do this immediately after the kill:**
A dead animal bloats as gut bacteria generate gas, tainting the meat.
*   **Position:** Roll the animal onto its back, propping it up if necessary.
*   **The Cut:** Make a shallow incision from the breastbone down to the anus. **WARNING:** Do not puncture the stomach or intestines. Cut the hide and muscle layer only, using two fingers behind the blade to protect the internal organs.
*   **Ring the Anus:** Cut a circle around the anus (and reproductive organs). Tie it off with grass or cordage to prevent fecal material from spilling onto the meat. Pull it through into the pelvic cavity.
*   **Remove Organs:** Open the chest cavity. Slice the windpipe and esophagus. Pull the windpipe downward; the lungs, heart, liver, stomach, and intestines should roll out of the body cavity in one large mass.
*   **Save the Edibles:** Separate the liver, heart, and kidneys. **CRITICAL:** Check the liver. If it has white spots or is highly discolored, the animal was diseased; discard immediately. Remove the gallbladder (a small green sac attached to the liver) carefully; if it bursts, the bile will ruin the meat.

**2. Skinning:**
*   Hang the carcass by the hind legs if possible (requires rope and a strong tree branch).
*   Make incisions down the inside of each leg to meet the central belly cut.
*   Cut around the neck.
*   Pull the hide down, using your knife/flake to slice the white connective tissue (fascia) between the skin and muscle. If done right, the hide pulls off like a tight sweater. Keep the hide for tanning.

**3. Quartering:**
To make the meat transportable and cool it faster:
*   Cut the carcass in half down the spine (requires an axe or heavy chopper).
*   Remove the shoulders (front legs) by slicing through the muscle attaching them to the chest. There is no bone-to-bone joint holding the shoulder blade to the ribcage.
*   Remove the hams (hind legs) at the ball-and-socket hip joint.
*   Remove the backstraps (the long tender muscles running along either side of the spine).
*   Trim the neck and rib meat.

**4. Preservation (Smoking/Drying):**
*   Slice the meat into thin strips (no more than 0.5 cm thick). Remove all fat (fat goes rancid, lean meat dries).
*   Build a smoking rack of green wood over a smoldering, smoky fire (hardwoods only, no pine/conifers).
*   The smoke keeps flies off and deposits antimicrobial compounds. The heat dries the meat. This forms jerky, which can last months.

## Materials & Improvisation
<!-- @field: materials -->
*   **Cutting Tool:** A sharp knife. If you lack steel, a freshly knapped flint or obsidian flake (see: *Flaked Stone Tools*) is actually sharper than steel and will easily slice hide and muscle, though it chips against bone.
*   **Bone Breaker:** A heavy, smooth hammerstone to smash large leg bones for the marrow inside.
*   **Hanging Rig:** Cordage and a sturdy tree branch to hang the carcass, keeping it off the dirt and away from ground scavengers.
*   **Water:** Needed to wash your hands, NOT the meat. Washing meat spreads bacteria. Wipe blood off meat with grass or a clean cloth, but do not soak it.

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
*   **Caloric Harvest:** Converting hunted or scavenged animals into usable energy.
*   **Tool Procurement:** Accessing long bones for bone awls, needles, and flakers.
*   **Resource Maximization:** Brains are used to tan the hide; fat (tallow) can be rendered for torches, soap, and emergency calories.

## Technical Data
<!-- @field: technical_data -->
| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Gutting Time Limit | Within 1-2 hours of death to prevent bloat/taint |
| Usable Meat Yield | Approx. 35-40% of the animal's live weight |
| Drying Strip Thickness | < 0.5 cm to ensure inner moisture escapes |
| Marrow Caloric Density | ~800 kcal per 100g (pure fat) |
| Organ Priority | Liver (Vitamin A/B, iron), Heart (muscle), Kidneys |
| Dangerous Organs | Liver of polar bear/seal (Vitamin A poisoning), Gallbladder (bile taint), Intestines (feces load) |

## Day 0  -  Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->
You found a dead deer on the side of the road, or you managed to hunt something significant. It is Day 1. You cannot eat 100 pounds of meat tonight, and by tomorrow, the flies will have claimed it. You must systematically dismantle the animal before bacteria do.

**The Protocol:**
1. **Act Fast, Gut Fast:** Do not admire the animal. Cut from sternum to groin, carefully. If you nick the intestines and smell feces, immediately cut that section of meat away and discard it. Roll the guts out immediately to let the body cavity cool.
2. **Save the Heart and Liver (The Offal):** Cook and eat these first. They spoil the fastest and are incredibly rich in vitamins your body desperately needs right now. If the liver looks weird or spotted, abandon it - do not test pathogens on your own body.
3. **Keep the Meat Clean:** Do not butcher in the dirt. Keep the hide under the meat to act as a tarp, or hang it up.
4. **Fat is Fuel, Lean is for Storage:** Fat goes rancid quickly without cold temperatures. Cut the fat off. Render it (melt it in a pot) to make tallow for calories or lamps. Take the lean muscle, slice it impossibly thin, and drape it over a smoky fire. If you don't dry the bulk of the meat within 24 hours, you will lose it all to maggots and rot.
5. **Break the Bones:** Don't throw away the leg bones. Take a heavy rock, place the bone on another rock (an anvil), and smash it. Eat the greasy, pinkish-white marrow inside. Early humans conquered the world on bone marrow; you can survive the week on it.
