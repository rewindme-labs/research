# Throwing & Projectile Defence

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

Throwing is the oldest ranged weapon system. Before spears, before slings, before bows, hominids threw rocks. The ability to strike a target at distance serves two critical survival functions: killing small prey without a chase, and deterring predators without closing to contact range. Australopithecus and early Homo threw cobbles, chunks of hardwood, and handfuls of gravel with enough force to crack a skull at 15 m. The human shoulder joint, wrist flexibility, and hip rotation are specifically evolved for overhand throwing - no other primate matches this biomechanical design.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

Stone throwing predates all formal tool manufacture. At Olduvai Gorge (Tanzania), deposits dated to roughly 2.6 million years ago contain dense concentrations of rounded cobbles with no clear knapping purpose, interpreted by researcher Kathy Schick (1987) as cached throwing ammunition. The Dmanisi site (Georgia, 1.8 Ma) preserves similar cobble caches associated with Homo erectus butchery areas, suggesting deliberate stockpiling. South African cave sites such as Swartkrans (1.5 Ma) contain baboon skulls with depressed fractures consistent with thrown-stone impact rather than fall injuries. Ethnographic studies of Australian Aboriginal peoples document kangaroo hunting by groups throwing stones in coordinated barrages, achieving kill rates above 30% per hunt. The Tarahumara of Mexico throw stones at rabbits during persistence hunts with accuracy at 10-20 m.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->

**Selecting throwing stones:**

| Property | Ideal | Acceptable | Avoid |
|----------|-------|------------|-------|
| Shape | Egg-shaped, fits palm | Rounded, flat disc | Jagged, irregular |
| Weight | 200-400 g (fist-sized) | 100-600 g | Under 50 g or over 1 kg |
| Material | Dense river cobble | Hardwood knot, dried clay ball | Porous rock, sandstone |
| Surface | Smooth, dry | Lightly rough | Wet, mossy |

**Throwing technique (overhand):**

1. Grip the stone with three fingers around the widest circumference, thumb on top, pinky bracing base
2. Stand with non-dominant foot forward, body angled 45 degrees to target
3. Wind up by rotating hips backward, bringing throwing arm back behind the ear
4. Lead with hip rotation, then torso, then shoulder, then elbow, then wrist - this kinetic chain multiplies force
5. Release when the arm reaches roughly 2 o'clock position (slightly past vertical)
6. Follow through across the body

**For predator deterrence (priority scenario):**

1. Do not throw one stone - throw many in rapid succession
2. Gather 5-10 stones before engaging
3. Shout and make noise while throwing
4. Aim for the face and nose area - pain and surprise matter more than injury
5. If in a group, coordinate throws simultaneously - volume of fire overwhelms

**Accuracy training protocol:**

| Distance | Target size | Beginner hit rate | Skilled hit rate |
|----------|------------|-------------------|------------------|
| 5 m | Torso-sized rock | 80% | 98% |
| 10 m | Torso-sized rock | 40% | 85% |
| 15 m | Torso-sized rock | 15% | 60% |
| 20 m | Torso-sized rock | 5% | 35% |

## Materials & Improvisation
<!-- @field: materials -->

**Primary ammunition:**
- River cobbles (ideal density 2.5-2.8 g/cm3)
- Beach pebbles (smooth, aerodynamic)
- Broken rock fragments (less accurate but available everywhere)

**Field-expedient alternatives:**
- Hardwood balls: carve green wood into rough spheres, fire-harden the surface
- Sun-dried clay balls: mix clay with fine sand, form 5 cm spheres, dry 2-3 days in sun
- Coconut-sized chunks of termite mound (surprisingly dense)
- Frozen mud or ice balls in cold climates

**Ammunition caching:** Pile 20-30 stones at camp perimeter, near sleeping areas, and along trails. Restocking is continuous - pick up good throwing stones during any walk.

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Predator deterrence:** Leopards, hyenas, wild dogs retreat from coordinated stone throwing by groups of 4+. A single well-placed hit to the muzzle causes immediate withdrawal in most cases.
- **Small game hunting:** Rabbits, ground birds, lizards, roosting bats. Effective to 15 m for skilled throwers.
- **Driving game:** Throw stones into brush to flush hiding animals toward waiting hunters.
- **Tree-fruit harvesting:** Knock fruit, nuts, and honeycomb from branches too high to climb.
- **Inter-group conflict:** The original ranged weapon for territorial disputes.
- **Fishing:** Throw stones into shallow water to stun or kill fish in pools.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

You arrive with nothing. The first predator encounter can happen tonight. Before you build shelter, before you make tools, learn this: pick up the five best stones within arm's reach right now.

Walk to the nearest riverbed or rocky slope. Collect 20 fist-sized, egg-shaped cobbles. They need to be heavy enough to hurt but light enough to throw fast - if you can close your hand around it comfortably, it works. Pile 10 stones next to where you plan to sleep. Carry 3 in a folded skin or bark container whenever you move.

Practice immediately. Find a tree trunk roughly torso-width. Stand 5 paces away. Throw overhand - lead with your hips, not your arm. The power comes from the rotation of your whole body. Throw 20 times, collect, repeat. Move to 10 paces when you hit 8 out of 10 at 5 paces.

When a predator appears: do not throw one stone and wait. Pick up three stones. Throw the first at the face, the second immediately after at the body, the third at whatever is still visible. Scream while throwing. If you are in a group, everyone throws together on a count of three - synchronized impact is devastating. A leopard hit in the nose by a 300 g cobble at 10 m will not come back tonight.

Restock every day. Every walk is a chance to pick up ammunition. Build caches at the camp perimeter, at water sources, and at the base of fruit trees. This is the first and most reliable weapon you will ever have.
