# Zoopharmacognosy (Self-Medication)

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->
Zoopharmacognosy is the behavior in which non-human animals self-medicate by selecting and ingesting or topically applying plants, soils, insects, or psychoactive drugs to treat and prevent disease. Long before the cognitive leaps that produced shamanism, herbalism, or modern pharmacology, early hominids possessed the instinctual drive to combat parasites, neutralize toxins, and soothe digestive pain through specific, non-nutritional environmental foraging. The term was coined by biochemist Eloy Rodriguez and primatologist Richard Wrangham in 1993, but the behavior itself is ancient  -  predating the genus *Homo* entirely. This is the absolute root of human medicine. Knowing *how* to instinctively utilize earth and toxic plants is a primary survival mechanism when drinking unfiltered stream water and eating scavenged meat, since gastrointestinal parasites and bacterial infections were the leading cause of death for pre-modern humans.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
Evidence of this behavior is rampant in modern primates and extends logically to our earliest ancestors like *Australopithecus afarensis*. The landmark observation was made by Michael Huffman in 1987, when he documented a visibly ill chimpanzee (*Pan troglodytes*) named "Chausiku" at Mahale Mountains National Park, Tanzania, seeking out and consuming the bitter pith of the *Vernonia amygdalina* plant. This plant has virtually zero caloric value and is highly toxic in large doses, but correctly dosed, its sesquiterpene lactones paralyze intestinal nematode (roundworm) worms, purging them from the digestive tract within 24 hours. Chausiku's diarrhea and lethargy resolved completely within 20 hours of consumption.

Similarly, macaque monkeys, gorillas, and early humans engaged heavily in *geophagy* (the deliberate eating of dirt  -  specifically clay). Excavations at early hominid sites at Kalambo Falls, Zambia (~250,000 years ago), show evidence of clay harvesting near areas of plant processing, suggesting intentional detoxification. Indigenous peoples of the Peruvian Amazon have been documented eating clay alongside toxic wild potatoes for millennia  -  a practice that directly mirrors the macaw parrots who eat riverside clay to neutralize the toxins in unripe fruits.

Modern ethnographic evidence from hundreds of traditional cultures confirms the universality of geophagy. In the United States, "pica" (earth-eating) was widely practiced among enslaved African Americans and pregnant women in the rural South, often consisting of specific clay deposits known locally as "chalk"  -  a behavior that modern medicine dismissed as pathological until researchers demonstrated the clay's genuine detoxifying and mineral-supplementing properties. In sub-Saharan Africa, more than 30% of pregnant women practice geophagy, consuming kaolin-rich clays that bind dietary mycotoxins and provide calcium and iron supplementation.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Geophagy (Eating Clay for Toxin Neutralization):**
If you are forced to eat questionable scavenged meat, bitter green tubers (which often contain toxic alkaloids), or if you are suffering from severe diarrhea (which will kill you via dehydration in 3 days):

| Step | Action | Mechanism |
|------|--------|-----------|
| 1 | Locate clay below topsoil near riverbanks | Pure subsoil clay is free of surface bacteria and parasites |
| 2 | Identify the right type: smooth, greasy, non-gritty | Smectite/kaolin clays have the highest adsorption capacity |
| 3 | Consume a golf-ball-sized portion BEFORE eating toxic food | Clay arrives in stomach first, creating a chemical trap |
| 4 | The clay's negative charge binds positively charged toxins | Prevents alkaloids and enterotoxins from crossing intestinal wall |
| 5 | The entire clay-toxin complex is excreted harmlessly | Same mechanism as modern Kaopectate / bismuth subsalicylate |

1.  **Locate Clay:** Do not eat topsoil; it is full of active bacteria and parasite eggs. Dig down below the top layer near riverbanks or exposed hillsides to find pure, dense clay (kaolin or smectite). The ideal clay feels smooth and greasy when wet, not gritty.
2.  **Ingestion:** Consume a small handful (about the size of a golf ball) of the raw clay *before* eating the toxic or questionable food. This is critical  -  the clay must be in the stomach before the toxins arrive.
3.  **The Mechanism:** The clay particles carry a strong negative electrical charge. The toxic alkaloids in wild plants and the enterotoxins produced by bacteria in rotting meat carry positive charges. In the stomach, the clay chemically binds to the toxins (adsorption), preventing them from crossing the intestinal wall into the bloodstream. The entire mass is then safely passed out of the body. This is the exact same chemical mechanism as modern Pepto-Bismol (bismuth subsalicylate) and Kaopectate (kaolin/pectin).

**Mechanical Purging (The Leaf Swallowing Method):**
If you are infected with visible intestinal worms (causing severe abdominal pain and malnutrition despite eating):
1.  **Select Roughage:** Find leaves that have stiff, microscopic hairs (trichomes). Aspilia (*Aspilia mossambicensis*) is the species most commonly observed in chimpanzee self-medication, but any rough-surfaced, non-toxic leaf can work  -  fig leaves, certain grasses, or hairy-stemmed herbs.
2.  **Do Not Chew:** Fold the leaf in half so it is small enough to swallow whole, without chewing it.
3.  **The Mechanism:** The unchewed leaf passes into the intestines whole. The stiff, velcro-like hairs on the leaf scrape against the intestinal walls, physically snagging and dislodging adult parasitic worms, sweeping them completely out of the digestive tract. Huffman documented this behavior across multiple chimpanzee populations  -  the leaves are always swallowed whole, always found undigested in feces alongside expelled worms, and are only consumed when parasite loads are high.

**Topical Application (Insect Pharmacy):**
Recent research (2022, Alessandra Mascaro et al.) documented wild chimpanzees in Loango National Park, Gabon, catching flying insects and deliberately applying them to their own wounds and the wounds of other group members. The insects likely contain anti-inflammatory or antimicrobial compounds, representing a form of topical pharmaceutical application.

*   **Geophagy During Pregnancy:** The increased mineral demands of pregnancy (calcium, iron, zinc) and heightened sensitivity to dietary toxins (morning sickness is theorized to be a toxin-avoidance mechanism) explain why geophagy rates spike dramatically in pregnant women across all traditional cultures.

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
*   **Wound Care:** Packing open wounds with clean mud/clay to prevent insect vectoring and bacterial infection. The clay forms a physical barrier while its ionic properties inhibit bacterial growth.
*   **Dietary Expansion:** By neutralizing plant toxins with clay, early humans could safely consume a vast range of toxic tubers during famine, surviving when other animals starved. The Quechua of Peru routinely dip wild potatoes in clay slurry before eating  -  a practice dating back thousands of years.
*   **Parasite Management:** Regular consumption of rough vegetation and bitter plants suppresses intestinal parasite loads, improving nutrient absorption and preventing the chronic malnutrition that parasites cause.
*   **Emergency Poisoning Treatment:** Charcoal or clay consumed immediately after accidental ingestion of toxic plants can prevent lethal absorption of alkaloids and glycosides.
*   **Mineral Supplementation:** Specific clays provide bioavailable calcium, iron, and zinc  -  critical micronutrients that are difficult to obtain in sufficient quantities from wild foods alone.

## Day 0  -  Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->
It is Day 1. You drank water from a stagnant puddle two days ago. Today, your stomach is cramping violently. You have severe diarrhea. You are losing a liter of water an hour. You cannot keep anything down. Your kidneys will start to fail tomorrow if the fluid loss doesn't stop. You have no antibiotics, no loperamide, no hospital. But you have the ground beneath your feet, and that is enough.

**The Protocol:**
1.  **Find the Antidote:** Walk to the nearest steep bank, river edge, or deep hole. Push past the dark, organic topsoil. You are looking for the dense, sticky, brightly colored dirt underneath  -  red, yellow, or grey clay. It should feel smooth and greasy between your fingers, not gritty. If you crush it in your fist, it should hold its shape like modeling clay.
2.  **Consume:** Take a piece the size of a large coin. Put it in your mouth. Let it dissolve and swallow it. It will taste like dust and chalk. Do not be repulsed  -  this is the oldest medicine on Earth. Chimpanzees have been doing this for millions of years, and their gut health is superior to any modern human eating processed food.
3.  **The Binding:** Inside your violent stomach, the bacterial toxins that are irritating your intestinal lining and forcing the fluid purge will come into contact with the clay. The clay acts like a chemical magnet, locking onto the toxins through ionic bonding. This is not folk medicine  -  it is electrochemistry.
4.  **Wait:** Wait an hour. If you do not vomit, consume another piece of clay the same size. If clay is unavailable, crush some black charcoal from your campfire into fine powder  -  as fine as you can get it  -  mix it with a cup of water, and drink the slurry. The charcoal works by providing a massive internal surface area (300+ square meters per gram) onto which toxins adsorb.
5.  **Recovery:** The diarrhea will slow down and eventually stop as the clay coats the intestinal lining and locks up the poisons. Begin drinking tiny sips of clean water  -  not gulps, sips  -  to rehydrate. Add a pinch of ash to the water for potassium. You just used geology to cure a biological infection.
6.  **Prevention Going Forward:** Before eating any unfamiliar plant, bitter root, or questionable meat, eat a small portion of clay FIRST. This is your daily vitamin  -  the original dietary supplement. It expands the range of foods you can safely consume by an order of magnitude, and it may be the single difference between starving and surviving through winter.
