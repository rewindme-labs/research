# Predator Mobbing
## Description
<!-- @field: description -->
Predator mobbing is the coordinated group harassment of a known predator - approaching it closely, making aggressive displays, throwing objects, generating loud noise, and following it persistently until it abandons the area. Rather than fleeing, a group that mobs a predator takes the offensive, driving it away from sleeping sites, food caches, or vulnerable group members. This behavior converts the group from prey into a collective threat.

Mobbing works because most predators, even large ones, are risk-averse. A lion or leopard that normally dominates any single prey animal cannot afford an injury - an injured carnivore cannot hunt, and cannot hunt means death. Facing a coordinated group of 8-12 hominins screaming, throwing rocks, and advancing in formation, the cost-benefit calculation shifts decisively. Retreat costs the predator nothing; continuing the attack risks a serious wound. Most predators choose retreat.

The critical requirements are group size (minimum 6-8 adults), a known predator position, and the coordination to advance without individuals breaking and fleeing - because flight triggers pursuit instinct in predators that group advance suppresses.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
Mobbing behavior in non-human primates has been documented extensively. Chimpanzees (*Pan troglodytes*) have been observed mobbing leopards, pythons, and even rival chimpanzee groups with coordinated advance, branch breaking, projectile throwing, and sustained loud vocalizations. Jane Goodall documented at Gombe (Tanzania) in the 1960s that chimpanzee mobs successfully drove leopards from areas containing sleeping sites, and that leopards that had been mobbed avoided those areas for extended periods afterward.

Baboon troops routinely mob leopards in East Africa. Adult male baboons - which possess large canine teeth and substantial body mass (25-40 kg) - will approach an exposed leopard within 2-3 meters, barking loudly, making exaggerated threat displays. Since leopards that have been seriously wounded in mob encounters learn to avoid those troops, the behavior has clear selective benefit even at the individual level (mob participants face some risk but benefit from reduced predation on their own group).

Fossil evidence for stone throwing by early hominins remains indirect, but the throwing musculature and shoulder anatomy of *Homo habilis* and early *Homo erectus* was analyzed by Neil Roach et al. (2013, *Nature*) as being specifically adapted for high-speed throwing - a feature unique to humans among primates, enabling effective projectile use during mobbing scenarios.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
Mobbing is high-risk, high-reward. Never attempt without sufficient group size and a clear assessment of the predator's escape routes.

**When to mob (yes/no decision):**

| Situation | Decision | Reason |
|-----------|----------|--------|
| Leopard detected at night near sleeping tree | YES | Leopard at night is maximally dangerous; drive it away before it selects a target |
| Lion with a fresh kill, not looking at group | NO | Lion defending a kill will attack; circumnavigate at distance |
| Single leopard in open savanna visible at 50m | YES | Classic mobbing scenario with clear escape routes |
| Multiple lions or hyenas | NO | Numbers negate the mob's advantage |
| Snake at sleeping site | YES | Small-scale mob, appropriate for all sizes |
| Predator actively stalking a group member | YES | Immediate intervention required |

**Mob formation and approach:**

1. Alarm given - group assembles immediately. Count adults. If under 6, do not mob - retreat to trees.
2. Position largest adults at the front, juveniles and mothers at the rear.
3. Advance in a line, shoulder to shoulder - not a scattered crowd. The line is psychologically intimidating.
4. Begin generating maximum noise - coordinated vocalizations, rock striking, branch breaking.
5. Collect throwing objects during advance: rocks (fist-sized), sticks (1 meter).
6. Approach to within 20-30 meters, then hold position while throwing objects and vocalizing. Do not rush in.
7. If predator does not retreat within 2-3 minutes of sustained pressure at 20m, advance to 15m.
8. Continue following the retreating predator for 200-300 meters to discourage return.
9. Reassemble group, perform full body check of all members, assess injuries.

**Projectile throwing technique:**

| Object | Throw Type | Effective Range | Impact Force |
|--------|-----------|----------------|-------------|
| Fist-sized rock | Overhand | 15-25 m | Sufficient to cause pain/injury |
| Stick (1 m) | Javelin throw | 10-20 m | Startling, some impact |
| Handful of gravel | Underhand scatter | 5-10 m | Distraction, minimal impact |
| Large branch (2 kg) | Two-handed throw | 8-12 m | High impact, requires proximity |

| Loud coordinated vocalizations | Intimidation and group cohesion | Silence during mob is dangerous |
| Clear terrain knowledge | Escape route if mob fails | Know the nearest trees before engaging |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Sleeping site security:** Driving predators from an identified sleeping area before dark is the most efficient use of mobbing - it secures the night without the sustained vigilance cost.
- **Rescue of a seized group member:** If a predator has grabbed but not yet killed a group member, immediate coordinated mob with maximum aggression can force the predator to drop its prey. This has been observed in chimpanzees and baboons.
- **Territory maintenance:** Repeatedly mobbing predators that enter a core territory teaches those predators to avoid it, reducing predation pressure over time.
- **Predator identification:** Mobbing forces a predator into the open and keeps it visible long enough for the group to memorize its appearance - individual predators can be tracked and their behavior anticipated.

## Technical Data

| Parameter | Value | Notes |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| Minimum group size for effective mob | 6-8 adults | Below this, predator does not retreat reliably |
| Effective projectile range (thrown rock) | 15-25 m | Overlap with predator's charge range |
| Leopard charge speed | 60-70 km/h | Covers 20 m in ~1 second |
| Mob success rate (baboons vs. leopard) | ~80-90% | Predator retreats in documented observations |
| Maximum safe advance distance | 10-15 m (for terrestrial predator) | Within this, charge risk becomes significant |
| Duration of effective mob | 2-5 minutes | Most predators make retreat decision quickly |
| Predator area avoidance after mob | Days to weeks | Depends on individual predator's experience |
| Chimpanzee projectile throw distance | Up to 9 m | Less accurate than human throw |

## Day 0  -  Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->
There is a leopard in the tree 40 meters from where you sleep. It has been watching your group for 10 minutes. It is not leaving.

You have two choices: run (and it may select the slowest person to chase), or mob it now before dark.

Count your adults. If you have 6 or more, mob it.

Assemble your group into a line. Shoulder to shoulder. Pick up rocks as you walk - every person carries at least two fist-sized stones. Start screaming together. Not one person - all of you, at the same time, loud and sustained.

Walk toward it, not at it. Walk to 25 meters. Throw the first rocks. Keep screaming. The leopard's ears will flatten. It will look for an exit.

If it turns to leave, keep following it, screaming, for 300 meters. Do not let it settle in the next tree - drive it out of your area.

When you return, everyone counts. If everyone is present, you have done it correctly.

The predator is gone. Tonight, your sleeping tree is safe.
