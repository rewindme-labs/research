# Tree Sleeping
<!-- @field: description -->
For early hominins transitioning between arboreal and terrestrial lifestyles, sleeping in trees remained a critical survival strategy even as daytime activity shifted increasingly to the ground. The terrestrial savanna at night is dominated by large carnivores - lions, leopards, and hyenas - that have evolved specifically to hunt at low light. A bipedal hominin sleeping on the ground in this environment is extremely vulnerable. Trees provide physical separation from ground-level predators, early warning from disturbance of the branches and leaves, and advantaged defensive positions.

*Australopithecus afarensis* (3.7-2.9 Ma) retained shoulder joints and foot anatomy partially adapted for tree climbing - longer arms relative to body size, curved finger bones, and a more grasping foot morphology than later *Homo*. These features are now understood not as evolutionary relics but as maintained adaptations for a hybrid lifestyle that still required effective tree use at night. Even early *Homo habilis* likely used trees for night safety before fully committed terrestrial sleeping became viable with fire and group defense.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
The debate over whether early hominins slept in trees was substantially advanced by a 2012 study by Yohannes Haile-Selassie et al. in *PLOS ONE*, which analyzed the lower limb anatomy of *Australopithecus sediba* (~1.98 Ma). The study found that while sediba walked bipedally during the day, its foot anatomy still permitted effective tree climbing, suggesting deliberate nightly refuge-seeking in trees.

A 2016 study by Timothy Ryan and colleagues (*Journal of Human Evolution*) analyzed trabecular bone structure in *Australopithecus africanus* arm and leg bones, concluding that these hominins regularly loaded their arms with weight - consistent with regular climbing - in a pattern not seen in modern humans but similar to modern chimpanzees that sleep in trees nightly.

Comparative primate data is instructive: chimpanzees, our closest relatives, build new sleeping nests in trees every night, selecting trees with sufficient branch density to support a nest, height above 10 meters, and proximity to food sources. They rarely sleep in the same tree twice consecutively, reducing predictability for leopards that learn nest locations.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
Effective tree sleeping requires selecting the right tree, preparing a stable position, and maintaining awareness of exit routes.

**Tree selection criteria:**

| Criterion | Ideal | Acceptable | Reject |
|-----------|-------|-----------|--------|
| Height | 12-20 m | 8-12 m | Under 5 m (leopard can reach) |
| Branch structure | Multiple horizontal branches forming V-shapes | One large horizontal fork | Single vertical trunk only |
| Trunk access | Branches beginning at 2-3 m | Low-hanging branch at 1.5 m | No branches under 4 m |
| Visibility from ground | Partially obscured by canopy | Open but high | Directly visible at ground level |
| Stability | Alive, green | Recently dead but sturdy | Dry, cracking bark |

**Climbing approach:**

1. Approach the tree from downwind of the most likely predator approach direction (from water sources, dense bush)
2. Climb after sunset while visibility is still sufficient to assess branch stability
3. Never climb a tree you have not tested - shake lower branches vigorously before committing weight
4. Ascend to at least 8 meters - leopards can climb to 6-7 meters carrying prey
5. Test each branch before committing full body weight: push down sharply while holding the one above

**Sleeping position:**

1. Locate a fork where two substantial branches diverge from the main trunk - the most stable resting point
2. Position your back against the main trunk for support and stability
3. Wrap both arms around the trunk or drape them over branches - prevents rolling off during sleep
4. Keep legs draped over a branch on each side of the fork
5. If sleeping with a group, position 2-3 individuals in adjacent forks for mutual alarm

**Night descent - when to come down:**

- Only descend in full daylight once you can see the ground clearly for 20+ meters
- Call out loudly before descending - this alerts any predator that has been waiting and allows you to assess its response before committing to the descent

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Primary night defense:** For hominins without fire, trees are the only reliable barrier between the sleeping group and terrestrial large carnivores. A single leopard can kill and carry off an adult chimpanzee - they can do the same to a sleeping hominin.
- **Infant protection:** Infants and juveniles are the most vulnerable group members. Tree sleeping provides passive protection without requiring active vigilance throughout the night.
- **Recovery from injury:** An injured hominin who cannot move quickly at ground level has a significantly higher chance of surviving multiple nights in a tree than on the ground.
- **Scouting from height:** Before descending in the morning, the elevated position provides a surveillance advantage - scan the surrounding area for predators before committing to ground-level exposure.

## Day 0 — Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0 -->
The sun is 30 minutes from setting. You are on the open savanna. You do not have fire.

Find a tree right now. Not later - now, while you can still see. Look for one with branches starting below 3 meters. Shake the lower branches. If they hold your weight, climb.

Get to at least 8 meters. Find a fork where two thick branches meet the trunk. Sit into that fork with your back against the trunk. If you have a companion, they go into the next fork up or to the side.

Do not sleep on a single horizontal branch. You will fall.

Do not descend before full daylight. Leopards wait. They are patient. You can be more patient than a leopard because you are above the leopard.

The ground at night, without fire, belongs to them. The tree, tonight, belongs to you.
