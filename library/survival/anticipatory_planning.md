# Anticipatory Planning

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

Anticipatory planning is preparing for future needs before they become urgent: carrying tools for expected tasks, gathering food for later consumption, choosing routes with foresight of conditions ahead, caching supplies at strategic locations, and preparing responses to predictable emergencies. It requires mental time travel, the ability to project oneself into a future scenario and work backward to identify present actions that improve future outcomes. Thomas Suddendorf and Michael Corballis (2007) argued that this "prospective cognition" is one of the defining features of human intelligence. While some great apes show limited future planning (Santino the chimpanzee at Furuvik Zoo cached stones overnight for morning throwing at visitors, reported by Osvath, 2009), hominin anticipatory planning scaled to multi-day, multi-step logistics that no other species matches.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

At Koobi Fora (Kenya, 1.5 Ma), the transport of stone raw materials 2-13 km from source outcrops to butchery sites represents unambiguous anticipatory planning: the knapper foresaw a future cutting need, selected appropriate stone, carried it at metabolic cost, and applied it at the correct moment. This is logistical thinking identical in structure to modern supply chain management. At Schoningen (Germany, 300,000 BP), the crafting of 10 wooden spears (each requiring 8+ manufacturing steps over multiple days) in advance of a horse hunt at a lakeside killing ground represents multi-day strategic preparation. Among the !Kung San, documented by Richard Lee (1979), women plan foraging routes 2-3 days ahead based on seasonal fruiting calendars maintained entirely in memory, adjusting plans based on yesterday's rainfall, last week's harvest success, and expected plant maturation timing.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->

**Daily planning cycle:**

| Time | Planning action |
|------|----------------|
| Evening (before sleep) | Review today's results. Plan tomorrow: who goes where, what tools needed, what food to seek |
| Dawn | Confirm plan, check weather, adjust if conditions changed overnight |
| During activity | Monitor progress vs plan. Adjust in real-time if new information arrives |
| Return to camp | Assess outcomes. Feed results into tomorrow's planning |

**Strategic planning categories:**

| Horizon | What to plan | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| Today (0-12 hours) | Immediate tasks, assignments, routes | "We forage the east sector; return by this sun position" |
| This week (1-7 days) | Resource rotation, camp maintenance, scouting | "South sector needs 3 days rest before re-foraging" |
| This season (weeks-months) | Migration timing, cache building, territory expansion | "Water here dries in 6 weeks; scout alternative" |
| Annual (months) | Seasonal movement, inter-group relations, population | "Last year the herds moved south in the third moon" |

**Practical anticipatory actions:**

1. Carry backup tools. Never leave camp with only one cutting flake. A broken tool with no replacement turns a productive day into a wasted one.
2. Cache water along known travel routes. A filled container left at the midpoint of a long traverse eliminates the need to carry double the water weight.
3. Prepare fire-starting materials before they are needed. Dry tinder cached in the shelter means fire is available in minutes during an emergency, not after an hour of wet-weather searching.
4. Maintain a 2-day firewood supply at camp. Camp consumption estimation: 5-10 kg dry wood per night.
5. Pre-scout escape routes from every camp. When a predator arrives at 3 AM, there is no time to evaluate options.
6. Build tool caches at satellite foraging sites. A hammerstone and cutting flakes left at a distant foraging location eliminates the need to carry them every trip.

**Contingency planning (what-if scenarios):**

| Risk | Pre-planned response |
|------|---------------------|
| Water source dries | Pre-scouted alternative source, route memorised |
| Group member injured | Second-trained person for each critical role |
| Camp compromised (predator, flood) | Alternate camp site identified, fire materials cached there |
| Food shortage (3+ days) | Emergency fallback foods identified (insects, bark, roots) |
| Inter-group conflict | Retreat route planned, rally point designated |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Caloric efficiency:** A planned foraging day yields 30-50% more than a reactive one. Knowing which areas to visit in which order, based on previous results and seasonal timing, eliminates wasted travel.
- **Risk reduction:** Pre-planned responses to predictable emergencies (fire escapes, predator protocols, flood evacuation) reduce reaction time from minutes to seconds. Every second counts in crisis.
- **Tool logistics:** Carrying the right tools for expected tasks and caching extras at remote sites means you always have what you need without carrying a 10 kg toolkit everywhere.
- **Migration success:** Groups that plan seasonal movements 2-4 weeks ahead (based on resource depletion indicators) relocate smoothly. Groups that wait until resources are exhausted relocate in desperation.
- **Group cohesion:** A shared plan gives every group member clear purpose and direction. "We do not know what to do today" is a morale destruction that planning eliminates.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

Stop reacting and start planning. Right now, this evening, before you sleep, answer three questions for tomorrow:

Where are we going? Pick the direction and the target. Not "we will see what we find" but "we go to the ridge line to check the tuber patch and the water source."

What do we need? Count the tools. Two cutting flakes minimum, one digging stick, water container, carrying tray. If anything is missing, make it tonight, not tomorrow morning in daylight you could spend foraging.

What could go wrong? Name one thing. "A predator at the water source." Now answer it. "We approach from the high side with full visibility and weapons ready." This took 10 seconds. That 10-second contingency plan may save tomorrow.

Before you leave camp each morning, check weather. Feel wind direction. Look at clouds. If rain is likely, adjust your route to avoid flood-prone low ground and bring extra tinder wrapped in bark to keep dry.

Cache supplies on your routes. That hammerstone you use at the nut tree 3 km east? Leave it there. You do not need to carry it every trip. The kindling bundle at the alternate camp site? Leave it dry and covered. When you arrive in an emergency, fire is 5 minutes away instead of an hour of scrambling.

Every evening, debrief. What worked today? What failed? What runs out tomorrow? If the firewood at camp is down to one night's supply, someone collects wood tomorrow before anything else. If the water level at your source dropped since last visit, start scouting alternatives now, not when it is empty.

Planning is not a luxury for comfortable times. Planning is the survival strategy that separates groups that persist from groups that scramble from crisis to crisis until one kills them.
