# Concealment & Stealth Techniques

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

Concealment is the ability to avoid detection by predators, prey, or hostile groups. It operates on four sensory channels: visual (shape, colour, movement), auditory (footsteps, breathing, rustling), olfactory (body odour, smoke, sweat), and vibration (ground tremor from footfall). In the prehistoric context, concealment serves dual purposes - hiding from predators that outrun you, and approaching prey that outrun you. Every surviving hunter-gatherer culture has sophisticated stealth traditions passed from childhood. The San Bushmen of the Kalahari can approach antelope to within 15 m on open ground using patience, wind awareness, and micro-terrain. Concealment is not invisibility - it is the art of being unremarkable.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

Concealment behaviour predates humans entirely. Chimpanzees at Tai Forest (Ivory Coast) use suppressed vocalization and slow, crouched movement when stalking colobus monkeys, as documented by Christophe Boesch (1994). At Schoningen (Germany, 300,000 BP), the arrangement of horse remains near a lakeshore with wooden spears suggests ambush hunting from reed cover. Aboriginal Australians developed full-body mud camouflage for emu stalking, documented by Baldwin Spencer (1899) - they smeared clay matching the local soil colour and moved only when the bird's head was down feeding. The Hadza of Tanzania still practice a "freeze and crouch" technique when spotted by prey, remaining motionless for up to 20 minutes until the animal's vigilance drops.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->

**Visual concealment principles:**

| Principle | Method | Effect |
|-----------|--------|--------|
| Break silhouette | Crouch, lean against tree, avoid skyline | Eliminates recognizable human outline |
| Colour matching | Mud, ash, charcoal, leaf matter on skin | Blends with background |
| Stillness | Freeze when detected, move only when target looks away | Movement triggers prey/predator alertness |
| Shadow use | Stay in shadow, never move through open sun | Reduces contrast and glare from skin |
| Low approach | Crawl, belly-crawl, move behind terrain folds | Stays below sightline |

**Wind management (the most overlooked factor):**

1. Before any stalk, check wind direction: wet a finger, hold it up. The cool side faces the wind.
2. Always approach from downwind (wind blowing from prey/predator toward you).
3. If wind shifts during approach, stop. Wait or circle to re-establish downwind position.
4. Human scent carries 200-400 m on a steady breeze. No amount of visual camouflage compensates for wrong wind.

**Movement protocol for stalking:**

1. Plan your route before moving. Identify cover points every 5-10 m.
2. Move when the target is feeding, head down, or looking away.
3. Freeze instantly when the target raises its head. Do not lower your body - freeze in position.
4. Take 2-3 slow steps per movement burst. Place foot heel-first, roll to toe, transferring weight gradually.
5. Avoid stepping on dry sticks, loose gravel, or dry leaves.
6. Breathe through your nose, slowly. Mouth-breathing is louder.
7. Keep hands close to your body - swinging arms catch peripheral vision.

**Hiding from predators:**

| Situation | Action |
|-----------|--------|
| Open ground, predator approaching | Drop flat, face down, cover neck. Stillness is your only option. |
| Near a tree | Climb. Most large predators (lions, hyenas) cannot climb effectively. Leopards can. |
| Near water | Enter water waist-deep. Many predators will not follow. Crocodile zones excepted. |
| In tall grass | Freeze and crouch. Do not run unless the predator is committed to a charge. |
| In rocky terrain | Squeeze into a crevice or overhang too narrow for the predator. |

**Field camouflage application:**

1. Rub wet mud over all exposed skin - match local soil colour.
2. Rub charcoal under eyes and across brow ridge to reduce glare.
3. Tuck grass, leafy twigs, or fern fronds into hair and any bindings.
4. Avoid carrying anything shiny or reflective (wet stone tools, water containers).

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Predator evasion:** Freeze response combined with scent management gives survival advantage against ambush predators (leopards, crocodiles) and pursuit predators (wild dogs, hyenas).
- **Ambush hunting:** Position concealed near water sources, game trails, or salt licks. Wait for prey to approach within throwing/spear range (5-15 m).
- **Stalking for close approach:** Reduce stand-off distance for throwing stones or thrusting with a spear. The difference between a 30 m approach and a 10 m approach is the difference between a miss and a kill.
- **Scavenging:** Approach predator kills without triggering defensive aggression. Move slowly, low, from downwind.
- **Inter-group avoidance:** Detect hostile groups before being detected. Withdraw without confrontation.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

Your body is a beacon. Upright posture, pale or contrasting skin, swinging arms, heavy footsteps, and a scent cloud that drifts hundreds of metres downwind. Every predator within line of sight knows exactly where you are. Fix this now.

Go to the nearest mud source - riverbank, puddle, wet earth after rain. Scoop handfuls and rub it across your arms, legs, face, and neck. Match the colour of the ground around your camp. Grab a charred stick from any old fire and draw lines under your eyes and across your cheekbones. You just became 70% harder to see.

Learn the wind. Right now, wet your finger and hold it up. The cold side tells you where the wind is coming from. When you walk, keep the wind in your face. Everything behind you can smell you, everything in front cannot.

When something sees you, freeze. Do not duck, do not drop, do not flinch. Lock every muscle and hold. Count to thirty in your head. If it was a prey animal, it will relax and look away - then take two slow steps. If it was a predator deciding whether to charge, your stillness makes you ambiguous, and ambiguity makes predators cautious. Only run if the predator commits to a charge from under 30 m.

Move slowly. Place your foot heel first, roll it forward, transfer weight only after you confirm nothing is cracking under your shoe. Three steps, then stop and listen for five seconds. Three more. This is agonizingly slow. It works. At night, move even slower - your hearing becomes your primary sense.

Build this into reflex. Every time you walk to water, practice. Every time you approach a food source, practice. Stealth is not a skill you use sometimes. It is how you move through the world from now on.
