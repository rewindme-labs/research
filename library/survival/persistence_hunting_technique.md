# Persistence Hunting Technique
<!-- @field: description -->
Persistence hunting is the pursuit of prey over a distance of 15-35 km at a sustained trot until the animal dies of hyperthermia — overheating. The strategy exploits the fundamental physiological asymmetry between humans and most quadrupedal prey: humans dissipate heat through eccrine sweating across most of the body surface; most quadrupeds dissipate heat primarily through panting, which requires them to slow down or stop to breathe effectively. In hot midday conditions, a human who maintains a sustained trot while the prey is forced to gallop and then rest, gallop and rest, will outlast the prey's thermoregulatory capacity in 2-5 hours.

The technique requires three interconnected competencies: endurance running mechanics (the right gait, pace, and form), tracking (following the spoor accurately between sightings), and knowledge of the prey species' behavior, preferred resting terrain, and heat tolerance limits. No single competency alone is sufficient.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
Louis Liebenberg, a South African tracking expert, documented and filmed persistence hunts conducted by the Kalahari San (!Kung, Ju/'hoansi) between 2001 and 2007, published in *Current Anthropology* (2006) and *Journal of Human Evolution* (2008). His films show the full technique: initial sighting, flush, tracking at a trot, repeated flushes, and final capture of an exhausted kudu.

Daniel Lieberman and colleagues (2004, *Nature*) published the influential paper "Foot strike patterns and collision forces in habitually barefoot versus shod runners," but more relevant is the 2004 *Nature* paper "Endurance running and the evolution of Homo" (Bramble & Lieberman) which systematically documented the suite of anatomical adaptations in *Homo* that are specifically suited to endurance running: the nuchal ligament (stabilizes head during running), shortened forearms, long legs, enlarged gluteus maximus, and the Achilles tendon (an energy-returning spring). These adaptations are largely absent in other apes and appear in *Homo ergaster* (~1.8 Ma).

Atkinson et al. (2012) confirmed that the human aerobic capacity (VO₂ max relative to body mass) exceeds that of all commonly hunted ungulates, establishing the physiological basis for the technique.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Prerequisites:**

| Requirement | Details |
|-------------|---------|
| Time of day | Midday heat — 10:00-15:00. Human advantage maximized when temperatures exceed 35°C |
| Terrain | Open or semi-open. Dense bush reduces visibility of prey and favors their escape |
| Prey species | Medium to large ungulates: kudu, wildebeest, gemsbok, hartebeest. Smaller animals dissipate heat faster and are harder to run down |
| Tracker | At least one experienced tracker who can follow spoor at running pace |
| Hydration | Carry water. Without adequate hydration, the hunter overheats before the prey |

**Phase 1 — Initial flush:**

1. Locate prey at rest or grazing, within 500-1000m
2. Approach to visible range, then flush — the prey runs
3. Do not sprint. Maintain a steady trot, 7-10 km/h
4. Track the spoor at this pace — do not lose the trail

**Phase 2 — Repeated pursuit:**

1. Prey will run, then stop to pant — visible ahead or as a resting silhouette
2. Approach at your trot pace, do not sprint — keep the prey moving but do not exhaust yourself
3. The key is to prevent extended rest: each time the prey begins to recover thermal equilibrium, you arrive and flush it again
4. A persistence hunt involves 10-20 such flush-rest cycles over 2-4 hours

**Signs of heat exhaustion in prey:**

| Sign | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| Prey stops and looks back without fleeing | Approaching hyperthermia — reduced flight response |
| Prey's gait becomes uncoordinated (stumbling) | Core temperature critical |
| Prey lies down and cannot rise | Imminent capture |
| Prey stands with head down, sides heaving | Final stage — approach carefully but directly |

**Phase 3 — Final approach and kill:**

1. When the prey is clearly hyperthermically exhausted, approach slowly and directly
2. The exhausted animal may still have strength for a final burst — maintain alertness
3. Final kill requires a spear thrust or stone blow to a vulnerable point
4. Work quickly once the prey is down — the carcass is still hot and the site may attract other predators

## Materials
<!-- @field: materials -->

| Item | Purpose | Notes |
|------|---------|-------|
| Water (carried) | Hunter's hydration | 1-2 liters minimum for a 3-5 hour hunt |
| Spear or killing tool | Final dispatch | Must be carried the entire distance |
| Tracking knowledge | Following spoor at speed | Non-negotiable — without tracking, spoor is lost |
| Partner (optional but valuable) | One tracks, one maintains direction of travel | Increases efficiency significantly |
| Midday sun | Primary weapon | The heat itself exhausts the prey |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **High-value protein without ambush infrastructure:** Persistence hunting requires no trap construction, no projectile accuracy at distance, no complex group coordination. Two individuals with a spear and tracking knowledge can kill a kudu.
- **Midday-hour productivity:** Most forager activities avoid midday heat. Persistence hunting converts the most dangerous thermal hours into the most productive hunting opportunity.
- **Prey size accessible:** The technique works most effectively on medium-large ungulates (50-200 kg) that cannot be ambushed or chased down in short sprints — prey that would otherwise require large group coordination.
- **Caloric return:** A single kudu provides 50-80 kg of edible material — weeks of protein for a small group — from an investment of 3-5 hours and approximately 30-40 km of running.

## Day 0 — Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0 -->
It is 11 in the morning and the sun is already severe. A kudu is grazing 600 meters away. You have a spear and 1.5 liters of water.

Flush it. Walk toward it until it runs. Do not sprint. Set your pace: a steady trot, 8 km/h, follow the tracks.

The kudu will stop 400 meters ahead, turn, and look at you. Keep trotting. It runs again.

This happens 10 times over 2 hours. Each time, the kudu runs less far before stopping. Its sides are heaving. You are sweating. It is overheating.

At the 12th flush, it stops and does not run. It stands with its head down.

Walk directly toward it. Slowly. It may still bolt — be ready. But probably it cannot. Its legs are shaking.

You are hot and tired. It is dying from overheating.

The spear does the rest.

You just turned 4 hours of steady running into 70 kg of food. That is the best caloric trade in the history of the savanna.
