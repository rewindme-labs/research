# Persistence Hunting

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->
Most predators kill with speed, ambush, or weapons. Persistence hunting kills with patience and sweat. The technique exploits a physiological asymmetry between humans and quadrupedal prey: you cool your body through continuous evaporative sweating across 2-4 million eccrine glands. A kudu, wildebeest, or gemsbok cools itself by panting, and panting requires reducing speed or stopping entirely, because the respiratory cycle is mechanically locked to the galloping gait in quadrupeds. The animal cannot breathe deeply and run flat out at the same time.

The result: in ambient temperatures above 35 degrees C, a human maintaining a steady 8-10 km/h trot can keep moving while the prey is forced into a cycle of sprint-stop-pant-sprint-stop-pant. Each cycle, the animal's core temperature ratchets higher. After 2-5 hours and 15-35 km of pursuit, the prey collapses from hyperthermia. Its legs buckle. It cannot rise. The hunter walks up to it and kills it with a spear thrust or a rock.

This is not a hypothetical reconstruction. Louis Liebenberg filmed Kalahari San hunters running down kudu using exactly this method between 2001 and 2007. The kills were documented, measured, and published. The technique works. It requires three competencies that no single one can substitute for: endurance running mechanics, real-time tracking at speed, and knowledge of the specific prey species' behavior under thermal stress.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
The anatomical case for persistence hunting was formalized by Dennis Bramble and Daniel Lieberman in "Endurance running and the evolution of *Homo*" (2004, *Nature*). They catalogued a suite of adaptations in *Homo ergaster/erectus* (~1.8 Ma) that serve no function except stabilizing the body during sustained running: the nuchal ligament (dampens head oscillation), short forearms (reduce rotational momentum), a tall narrow waist (allows counter-rotation of thorax against pelvis), enlarged gluteus maximus (hip extension during running but not walking), long Achilles tendon (elastic energy return), and arched foot with short toes (stiff lever for push-off). Chimpanzees and australopithecines lack most of these features. They are not runners. *Homo ergaster* was.

Louis Liebenberg's fieldwork with the Kalahari San (!Xo and Ju/'hoansi groups) produced the first filmed and published persistence hunts in modern times (2006, *Current Anthropology*; 2008, *Journal of Human Evolution*). His documentation shows the complete sequence: initial sighting of a kudu herd at rest, selection of a single target individual, flush at a trot, tracking of the spoor at 8-10 km/h between sightings, repeated flushing of the target before it can fully recover, and final approach to the collapsed animal. Total pursuit distance: 25-35 km. Total time: 2-5 hours. Ambient temperature: above 40 degrees C.

Henry Bunn and colleagues (2010, *Journal of Human Evolution*) analyzed the faunal assemblages at FLK Zinj, Olduvai Gorge (~1.8 Ma), and found that the prey profile - dominated by prime-age adult wildebeest and alcelaphines rather than juveniles, elderly, or sick animals - is inconsistent with scavenging or ambush predation but consistent with pursuit-based hunting of healthy adults.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Prerequisites (do not attempt without all three):**

| Requirement | Why |
|-------------|-----|
| Ambient temperature above 35 degrees C | Below this threshold, the prey's thermoregulatory disadvantage narrows and the animal can outrun you indefinitely |
| Reliable water (carried or on route) | You will sweat 1-2 liters per hour for 3-5 hours. Dehydration kills the hunter before the prey |
| Tracking competence at trotting speed | The prey will move out of visual range repeatedly. If you lose the spoor, you have expended 2 hours of calories for nothing |

**Phase 1 - Selection and flush:**

1. Locate a herd at rest or grazing during late morning heat. Approach to within 500-800 meters
2. Select a single target. Larger animals (adult male kudu, wildebeest) overheat faster than juveniles or small-bodied species because heat production scales with mass while heat dissipation scales with surface area
3. Walk toward the target steadily. When it flees, note the direction and begin tracking at a steady trot. Do not sprint. Your pace should be 8-10 km/h - a pace you can maintain for hours

**Phase 2 - Repeated pursuit:**

1. The prey will gallop 400-800 meters and stop to pant. It is visible ahead or can be located by spoor
2. Continue trotting toward it. Do not speed up. The objective is to prevent extended rest. Each time the prey begins to recover its thermal equilibrium, you arrive and flush it again
3. Track the spoor between sightings. In sandy terrain, fresh tracks are sharp-edged, moist at the bottom, and show clear toe pad definition. Tracks older than 15 minutes have collapsed edges and dry interiors
4. A typical hunt involves 10-20 flush-rest cycles over 2-4 hours

**Signs of prey approaching collapse:**

| Behavior | Meaning |
|----------|---------|
| Prey runs shorter distances between stops (400 m becoming 200 m becoming 100 m) | Core temperature rising, locomotor capacity failing |
| Prey stops and faces you without fleeing | Flight response degrading. Hyperthermia approaching critical |
| Gait becomes uncoordinated, stumbling | Neuromuscular function compromised. Core temperature likely above 42 degrees C |
| Prey lies down and does not rise when approached | Terminal hyperthermia. Capture imminent |
| Excessive salivation, open-mouth breathing with head low | Thermoregulatory system maxed. Minutes from collapse |

**Phase 3 - Kill:**

1. Approach the collapsed or staggering animal directly and slowly. It may still have strength for one final burst. Stay alert
2. Kill with a spear thrust to the neck or chest, or a heavy stone blow to the base of the skull. Work quickly
3. Begin field butchering immediately. The carcass is already hot. Bacterial growth begins within 30 minutes in these temperatures
4. The kill site will attract predators. Process and carry away as much meat as possible in the first hour

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Large-animal protein without group coordination:** Persistence hunting can be conducted by a single experienced hunter or a pair. No ambush construction, no drive line of beaters, no projectile accuracy at distance. Two people with a spear, water, and tracking knowledge can kill a 120 kg kudu
- **Exploiting the thermal niche:** Most forager activities avoid midday heat. Persistence hunting converts the most dangerous thermal window into the most productive hunting opportunity. The hunter uses the heat as a weapon
- **Caloric return:** A single adult kudu yields 50-80 kg of edible material - muscle, organs, marrow. At 1,500-2,000 kcal per kg of mixed tissues, one kill provides 75,000-160,000 kcal. Enough protein for a group of 20 for a week. The investment: 3-5 hours of running and 3,000-5,000 kcal of personal energy expenditure
- **No technology beyond a sharp point required:** The technique predates projectile weapons, poison, traps, and nets. A fire-hardened wooden spear and a water container are sufficient. This makes it the oldest viable method for hunting prime-age adult large game

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->
It is 11:00. The sun is vertical. The air above the ground is shimmering. There is a herd of six wildebeest grazing 600 meters to the east. You have a fire-hardened spear and a hide bag with 2 liters of water. Your group has not eaten meat in four days.

Pick the largest bull. He weighs 200 kg and generates more metabolic heat per unit of surface area than any of the others. He will overheat first.

Walk toward the herd. Do not run. Walk with purpose, directly toward them. At 300 meters they will lift their heads. At 200 meters the herd will bolt. Note which direction the bull goes. Begin trotting after him. Your pace is steady: a speed at which you can breathe through your nose and speak short sentences. If you cannot speak, you are going too fast.

The bull gallops 600 meters and stops, sides heaving, head turned to watch you. He is panting. You are sweating. Keep trotting. When you reach where he was standing, he runs again. This time only 400 meters.

Drink water. Not a sip. 300 mL. Drink while trotting if you can, while walking if you cannot.

After the fifth flush, he runs 200 meters. His sides are pumping hard and fast. Saliva hangs from his mouth in long strings. He is not running from you anymore. He is running from his own body temperature. And losing.

After the eighth flush, he staggers. His back legs cross. He stops and does not run when you approach to within 50 meters.

Walk directly toward him. Slowly. He may have one final sprint. Be ready to jog after him for another 100 meters.

When he stands with his head low, legs trembling, foam dripping from his nostrils, he is finished. His core temperature is above 43 degrees. His brain is shutting down.

Close the last 10 meters. Plant the spear behind the shoulder, angled forward into the chest cavity, with your full body weight. Twist and withdraw.

He falls.

You have just converted 4 hours of steady running and 4,000 calories of personal expenditure into 100 kg of meat. That is the most efficient energy trade available to an unequipped hominin on the African savanna, and it has been available since 1.8 million years ago, the moment your ancestors developed the body to sustain it.

Drink the rest of your water. Butcher fast. The hyenas can smell blood from 3 km downwind.
