# Dry Season Water Sourcing
## Description
<!-- @field: description -->
In semi-arid savanna environments, surface water disappears for 4 to 8 months of the year. Seasonal rivers become dry sand channels; waterholes shrink to bare cracked mud. For early hominins operating in East Africa between 3.5 and 2 million years ago, the ability to locate water during the dry season was not an optional skill - it was the primary constraint on group survival and territorial range. Groups that could not find water within a day's travel of their sleeping site were forced to relocate or die.

Modern studies of Hadza, San, and Aboriginal Australian hunter-gatherers have documented a sophisticated body of knowledge for locating sub-surface and cryptic water sources in arid environments. This knowledge is learned over decades and transmitted socially. Physiologically, human water requirements in hot savanna conditions are 4 to 8 liters per day; beyond 36 hours without water, cognitive impairment begins, and death follows within 72-96 hours at high temperatures.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
Archaeological analysis of Oldowan and early Acheulean site distributions (2.5-1.5 Ma) consistently shows clustering along ancient drainage systems, dry riverbeds, and paleolake margins in East Africa - including Olduvai Gorge (Tanzania), the Turkana Basin (Kenya/Ethiopia), and the Omo Valley. This is not coincidental: early hominins were tracking sub-surface water accessible in sandy riverbeds and seeps from higher ground, even when no surface water was visible.

Geological studies of the African Pliocene indicate cyclical mega-droughts lasting decades to centuries, during which hominins would have faced extreme selection pressure for water-finding competence. Groups occupying territories with access to perennial springs, dry-season waterholes, or sandy riverbeds had significant survival advantages, explaining the clustering of archaeological material at specific geological features.

The San people of the Kalahari use a suite of techniques documented extensively by anthropologists including Lorna Marshall (1960s) and Richard Lee (1970s): excavating dry riverbeds at the outer curves of bends, following animal trails to hidden seeps, extracting water from tree roots and melons, and using hollow reeds to siphon water from excavated holes too small for a hand to reach.

## Water Location Methods
<!-- @field: procedure -->
Water-finding is systematic. Use these methods in order of energy expenditure, from least to most effort:

**Step 1 - Read the landscape (zero energy cost):**

| Feature | What it Signals | Distance to Water |
|---------|----------------|-------------------|
| Converging animal trails | Water source ahead | 0.5-5 km |
| Green vegetation strip in dry landscape | Underground water table near surface | On-site |
| Birds circling or flying in straight lines at dawn/dusk | Water source | 1-3 km (follow direction) |
| Rocky hillside base with darker staining | Seep or spring | On-site |
| Dry sandy riverbed outer bend | Water pooled under sand | 0-1 m below surface |
| Elephant digging in dry riverbed | Active water table at that point | Exact spot |

**Step 2 - Excavate dry riverbeds:**

1. Locate the outer curve of a dry sandy riverbed (water erodes and concentrates at bends)
2. Dig at the lowest point of the curve, where water last pooled
3. Dig in sand rather than clay - sand drains but also holds water in the pore spaces
4. Dig straight down, 30 cm wide, moving sand out efficiently
5. Stop every 20-30 cm and feel the sand - cool, darkened, or clumping sand signals proximity to water
6. Once water seeps into the hole, stop digging - allow it to pool rather than muddying it with continued digging
7. Use a hollow plant stem to sip from a small seep, or allow 20-30 minutes for a larger pool to form and settle

**Step 3 - Extract from plants (dry season emergency):**

| Plant Source | Method | Yield |
|-------------|--------|-------|
| *Raphionacme* water tuber | Excavate and squeeze | 50-200 ml per tuber |
| *Citrullus lanatus* (tsamma melon) | Crush flesh | 400-600 ml per melon |
| Fig tree roots (lateral roots near surface) | Cut root, hold upright, collect drip | 50-300 ml per meter |
| Morning dew on grass | Drag cloth or absorbent bark along grass at dawn | 200-500 ml per hour |
| *Boscia* tree hollow | Check hollow at base - often holds rainwater pool | Variable |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Dry season survival foundation:** Without water, all other survival strategies become irrelevant within 48-72 hours. Securing water is always the first priority on any day when no surface source is visible.
- **Range extension:** Groups that can excavate water from dry riverbeds can range into otherwise uninhabitable dry terrain, expanding territory and reducing competition at known waterholes.
- **Infant and juvenile hydration:** Young hominins are more vulnerable to dehydration than adults. Portable water from plant sources (melons, tubers) is critical for group members who cannot excavate their own water.
- **Predator avoidance:** Shared waterholes at night concentrate predators. Finding isolated water sources accessible only to the group reduces exposure to lions and leopards at peak feeding times.

## Day 0  -  Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->
You have no water. You have been walking since dawn. Your mouth is dry.

Stop moving. Face the landscape and read it before you expend more energy. Look for converging animal trails - two or more trails meeting at a point. Follow them. Look for a strip of greener vegetation cutting through dry ground. Walk toward it. Look for birds flying in a straight line at low altitude - they are going to water at dusk.

If you find a dry sandy riverbed, go to the outer curve of a bend. Dig there. Dig straight down in the sand, not clay. When the sand darkens and clumps, slow down. When water seeps in, stop. Wait 20 minutes. Use a hollow stem to drink from the hole if it is too small to cup your hands.

If you find no surface indicators, look for large melons or tuber plants with fleshy stems. Extract and squeeze. It is not enough for a day, but it is enough to keep moving.

You need water before food. Everything else can wait until tomorrow.
