# Primitive Wound Care & Hemostasis

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->
Primitive wound care covers a specific sequence of interventions applied immediately after injury to prevent death from three causes that kill in order: (1) blood loss within minutes, (2) infection within days, and (3) tissue necrosis within weeks. Without any manufactured tools or medicines, the human body can be kept alive through these stages using materials found in virtually any environment. The majority of fatal wounds in prehistory were not from the injury itself but from the failure to control secondary bleeding, keep the wound clean, and prevent the infection that followed exposure to soil bacteria.

Hemostasis (stopping blood flow) is the first priority. An adult human has approximately 5 liters of blood. Losing 1-1.5 liters (20-30%) causes hemorrhagic shock; losing 2+ liters is often fatal without intervention. Direct pressure is the single most effective hemostatic technique available without manufactured tools, and it requires nothing but hands and a cloth.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
Evidence of wound treatment predates writing by millions of years. Observations of chimpanzees pressing leaves onto wounds (documented by Jane Goodall at Gombe, Tanzania, 1960s) and applying insect secretions suggest the behavior is inherited from a common ancestor. Archaeological evidence of healed traumatic injuries in Neanderthal remains (Shanidar Cave, Iraq, c. 50,000 BCE) demonstrates that group wound care was practiced long before *Homo sapiens* appeared.

The Edwin Smith Papyrus (Egypt, c. 1600 BCE, copied from sources dating to 3000 BCE) is the world's oldest surgical text, describing 48 trauma cases with rational, non-magical treatments: suturing wounds that can be closed, applying fresh meat to fresh wounds for hemostasis (the contractile proteins in raw meat cause vasoconstriction), and using honey as an antibacterial dressing. Ancient Indian Ayurvedic texts (Sushruta Samhita, c. 600 BCE) describe over 120 surgical instruments and detailed wound irrigation protocols using water and wine.

During the American Civil War (1861-1865), before antiseptic technique was widely adopted, wound infection (gas gangrene, septicemia) killed more soldiers than combat itself. The Confederate army, cut off from quinine and carbolic acid, rediscovered plantain leaf (*Plantago major*) poultices and maggot debridement - field medicine reverting to pre-industrial primitive techniques out of necessity. Both worked.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Priority sequence: Stop Bleeding → Clean → Seal → Monitor**

**Step 1: Hemostasis (immediate - first 5 minutes)**

| Method | How | When |
|--------|-----|------|
| Direct pressure | Press firmly with the cleanest material available. Hold without releasing for a minimum of 10 minutes by the clock. If blood soaks through, add more material on top - do NOT remove the first layer (it holds the clot). | All wounds |
| Elevation | Raise the wounded limb above the level of the heart. Reduces blood pressure at the wound site by 20-30%. | Limb wounds |
| Pressure point | Press the artery supplying the area against the underlying bone: brachial (inner upper arm for arm wounds), femoral (groin crease for leg wounds). Use 3-4 fingers, not a thumb. | Severe limb bleeding only |
| Tourniquet (last resort) | A rope, strip of bark cloth, or braided cordage tied 5-8 cm above the wound (never on a joint) and tightened until bleeding stops completely. Mark the time. Maximum 2 hours before permanent nerve and tissue damage. | Life-threatening limb hemorrhage uncontrolled by pressure |

**Natural hemostatic agents (apply after initial pressure):**
- **Yarrow** (*Achillea millefolium*): Fresh crushed leaves packed directly into wound. Contains achillein, a proven hemostatic alkaloid. Used continuously from Trojan War tradition through World War I.
- **Spider web**: Applied as a dry dressing. Contains vitamin K and fibrin-like proteins that accelerate clotting. Confirmed effective in multiple clinical studies (2012, Journal of Biomedical Materials Research).
- **Plantain leaf** (*Plantago major*): Chewed or bruised and pressed onto wound. Anti-inflammatory, mild antibacterial, absorbs exudate.
- **Sphagnum moss**: Applied directly. Highly absorptive (holds 20x its weight in fluid), naturally acidic (pH 3.5-4.5), which inhibits bacterial growth. Used as wound dressing in WWI when cotton supplies ran out.
- **Powdered charcoal**: Applied to open wound. Adsorbs toxins and bacteria, promotes clotting. Does not heal but buys time.
- **Raw honey**: Packed into the wound after bleeding stops. Osmotic pressure kills bacteria. pH 3.2-4.5. Effective against MRSA in clinical trials. Bacteriostatic indefinitely.
- **Pine resin**: Warmed until liquid, applied as a sealant over a closed wound or as a barrier coating over moss or leaf dressings.

**Step 2: Cleaning (after bleeding controlled)**
1. If clean water is available: irrigate the wound under pressure. The pressure matters - pour from height, or use a hollow reed as a syringe. Volume matters more than antiseptic content for mechanical debridement.
2. Remove visible debris (dirt, wood splinters, stone fragments) with a pointed thorn, bone splinter, or fingernail. Contamination left in the wound guarantees infection.
3. Do NOT close a dirty, contaminated wound. Leave it open to drain. Closing a contaminated wound traps bacteria and creates an anaerobic environment ideal for gas gangrene (*Clostridium perfringens*).

**Step 3: Sealing clean wounds**
- A clean laceration with edges that can be held together without tension: apply pine resin, or use thin strips of plant fiber to bring edges together (butterfly closure). Animal sinew makes excellent suture material when boiled first.
- Pack the wound loosely with clean moss or plantain leaves. Change the dressing at least once daily.

**Step 4: Monitoring for infection**
The four signs of infection appear 24-72 hours post-injury: (1) increasing redness spreading outward from wound edges, (2) heat at the wound site, (3) swelling beyond the immediate wound, (4) pus (yellow/green fluid). A fifth sign - red streaking tracking up a limb toward the body core - means systemic sepsis is beginning. This is a life-threatening emergency.

**For early infection:** Apply honey directly into the wound. Keep the wound open and draining. Apply heat (warm water soaks) to draw infection to the surface.

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Laceration and puncture wounds:** Most common injuries in primitive environments (stone tools, thorns, falls). Direct pressure and plantain poultice covers 95% of cases.
- **Animal bites:** High contamination risk. Irrigate aggressively for 5+ minutes. Pack with honey. Do NOT close. Monitor daily.
- **Crush injuries:** Little external bleeding but massive internal bruising. Elevation and immobilization reduce swelling. Watch for compartment syndrome (extreme pain, skin tautness, loss of sensation distal to injury).
- **Burns:** Cool immediately with water for 15-20 minutes. Cover with honey or clean aloe vera gel. Do not break blisters. Burn victims are extremely vulnerable to systemic infection.
- **Infection control:** In a group, a festering wound becomes a community risk. Isolation of severely infected wounds prevents cross-contamination, particularly when flies are present.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->
You are bleeding. That is the only thing that matters right now.

**The first 10 minutes decide everything.** Take the cleanest material you have - your shirt, a piece of bark cloth, large flat leaves - and press it onto the wound with both hands and your entire body weight. Do not lift it to check. Do not ease the pressure. Do not do anything else until 10 minutes have passed. Set a mental count if you have no watch. Most wounds that kill from blood loss kill because the person kept lifting the dressing to look at the wound and interrupted clot formation.

If the wound is on a limb and blood is pumping out rhythmically with the heartbeat (arterial bleed), you have 3-5 minutes. Apply pressure and immediately get someone to apply a tourniquet 5-8 cm above the wound. Tie it tight enough that the bleeding stops completely. Remember the time. After 2 hours, the limb may be lost regardless, but the person will be alive.

**After bleeding is controlled:**

1. Find clean water and pour it over the wound continuously for 2-3 minutes. Mechanical flushing removes more bacteria than any antiseptic.

2. Look for yarrow - white flat-topped flower clusters on a ferny leaf stem. If you find it, crush a handful of the flowering tops and pack them into the wound. If you cannot find yarrow, look for plantain - the broad oval-leaved ground rosette that grows in almost every disturbed soil on Earth. Chew it briefly to release the juices and pack it against the wound.

3. If you have access to a beehive or stored honey, this is your best wound dressing. Pack the wound with honey, cover with a leaf or cloth, and change daily.

4. Leave the wound open. A clean wound that drains is survivable. A closed contaminated wound becomes a gas gangrene bomb within 24 hours.

5. Watch the wound every 12 hours. The moment you see redness spreading outward beyond the wound edge, or red lines tracking up a limb, you have hours to act. Pour boiling water over the wound (after letting it cool to hot-but-bearable), repack with honey, and reduce all other activity to conserve the immune response.

The human body can survive almost any wound that does not destroy a vital organ. Your job is to help it survive the first 72 hours. After that, it will usually do the rest itself.
