# River & Water Crossing Techniques

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->
River crossing is the set of techniques used to safely cross a body of moving water - ranging from a shallow stream to a substantial river - without bridges or boats. It is one of the most consistently fatal activities in wilderness survival: drowning accounts for a significant proportion of outdoor fatalities, and many occur in water crossings that seemed manageable. The danger comes not from water depth but from current force: water moving at 3 km/h exerts significant drag on a standing human; at 6-7 km/h, it can knock an adult off their feet; at 10+ km/h, it can carry a person regardless of swimming ability.

The decision to cross must precede the method. Many crossings that appear necessary are not, and many that appear safe are not. The first skill is correctly reading a river before committing to it.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
The oldest known constructed water crossings are stepping-stone fords in England (Dartmoor, c. 3000 BCE), but the underlying body of knowledge about reading rivers, locating fords, and constructing primitive flotation is immeasurably older. All early human population migrations depended on crossing major water barriers - the Bering land bridge crossing, the settlement of Australia (~65,000 BCE, requiring open-water crossing of at least 70 km at minimum glacial sea level), and the Polynesian exploration of the Pacific were all achieved with knowledge of water navigation that began at river crossings.

The Roman legions formalized river crossing doctrine in military manuals. Caesar describes the Rhine crossing (55 BCE) in *De Bello Gallico* with specific engineering detail: a timber trestle bridge built in 10 days across a 400-meter wide river under hostile conditions. The underlying knowledge of current speed, depth sounding, foundation stability, and anchoring was accumulated over centuries of campaign experience.

Indigenous groups in the Amazon and Congo developed bamboo and balsa log raft construction as standard river-crossing technology, documented by early explorers as immediately available to any experienced forest dweller within hours of needing a crossing.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Step 1: Find the right crossing point**

Never cross at the first point you reach. Walk the bank until you find:

| Feature | Indicates | Action |
|---------|-----------|--------|
| Wide, shallow, braided channel | Slower current, more traction | Preferred crossing zone |
| Gravel bar visible below surface | Firm footing | Safe to probe with stick |
| River bends: inside of the curve | Current is slower; sediment deposits | Cross at inside of any bend |
| River bends: outside of curve | Current is fastest; bank undercut | Avoid |
| Foam lines or standing waves | Turbulence below surface | Avoid |
| Diagonal ripples across surface | Shallow water over gravel bottom | Often fordable |
| Flat, dark, glassy surface | Deep water | Do not cross here |
| White water downstream of potential crossing | If you slip, you wash into rapids | Cross only if the below-water zone is clear |

**Step 2: Assess the current**

Throw a stick into the river and walk alongside it. If you can walk at a comfortable pace and keep up, the current is approximately 3-4 km/h and wading is potentially feasible for a fit adult. If the stick moves clearly faster than your walking pace, do not wade. Build a raft or find a better point.

**Step 3: Wading technique (for fordable crossings)**

1. Cut or find a sturdy staff 1.5-2m long (your height or taller). This is your third leg - your most important tool for a crossing.

2. Remove heavy boots and wrap feet in cloth if gravel is sharp; bare feet often grip better than boots on smooth rock. At minimum, loosen boot laces fully so boots can be kicked off if you are swept off your feet.

3. Unbuckle any pack or bag straps so you can shed the pack immediately if you fall. A waterlogged pack holds you underwater.

4. Enter the water at a slight downstream angle (45 degrees). Face upstream or slightly upstream-diagonal. Move the staff first, then your downstream foot, then your upstream foot. Never move two points of contact simultaneously.

5. If crossing with a group: form a line perpendicular to the current, each person gripping the shoulder or belt of the person in front. The strongest person faces upstream at the front. The line moves as a unit, sidesteps across the current. The combined weight and surface area is far more stable than individuals.

6. If swept off your feet: do NOT fight the current. Roll onto your back immediately, feet downstream, toes up. Use feet to push off rocks. Work diagonally toward the bank by swimming on your back using sidestroke with your arms. Do not stand until you are sure you are in water shallow enough that you will not be spun under.

**Step 4: Raft or flotation construction (for uncrossable rivers)**

Simple log raft (2-4 hours, one person):
1. Find 5-7 straight dry logs, minimum 15-20cm diameter, 3m length. Dry wood floats; green (fresh) wood is borderline.
2. Lay logs parallel. Lash two cross-poles perpendicular to the logs using cordage (vines, bark strips, twisted plant fiber), one at each end and one in the middle. Lash with multiple wraps and square knots.
3. Test by pushing the raft into the shallows and sitting on it. If it sinks below the waterline, add more logs.
4. Use a pole to push off the bottom in shallow water; in deep water, paddle with a flat piece of bark held as a blade.

Flotation aid (for fast construction):
- A sealed hollow gourd, sealed hollow bamboo bundle (both ends packed with bark), or an inflated sealed animal stomach each provides 5-10kg of buoyancy.
- Tie the flotation aid to your chest. This is not a life jacket substitute, but it flips a sinking person face-up.

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Movement between river banks:** Essential for exploring territory, finding food sources, and traveling in any riparian landscape.
- **Escaping predators:** Water crossings can break scent trails and provide barriers to pursuit by land predators not willing to enter fast water.
- **Accessing islands and delta systems:** Rich food resources (fish, waterfowl, eggs, aquatic plants) are often concentrated on river islands and in delta channels.
- **Group crossing with injured member:** Group wading formation allows crossing with someone who cannot wade independently. Raft construction is required for non-ambulatory individuals.

| Current speed detectable by observation | Stick comparison: walking pace = ~4 km/h |

| Raft buoyancy per dry log (pine, 15cm dia., 3m) | ~20-30 kg (varies with species and moisture) |
| Time to build a basic lashed log raft | 2-4 hours (one person, available materials) |
| Drowning time in cold water (<10°C) | Unconsciousness within 3-5 minutes (cold shock and incapacitation) |
| Downstream position advice | Feet first, on back; prevents head impact on submerged rocks |
| Inside of river bend | Current 30-50% slower than outside of same bend |
| Pack weight when waterlogged | Can increase 5-10x; lethal anchor if strapped on during swim |
| Survival flotation: body position | Back-floating, feet downstream, arms sculling laterally |

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->
You are standing at a river that you need to cross. Do not just wade in. You have 15 minutes to find a better crossing point, and it will save your life if you use them.

**Walk upstream.** Rivers slow at bends. On the inside of any curve, the current slows and deposits sediment. That is where you cross - not at the nearest point but at the next inside bend.

Look for diagonal ripples across the surface and a visible bottom through the water column. Throw a stick. If you can walk alongside it comfortably, the current is manageable. If it outruns you quickly, do not wade.

**What to do before you enter:**

1. Loosen every strap on your pack so you can shed it in one motion if you fall.
2. Cut a pole as tall as you or taller. This is not optional.
3. Remove your boots and tie them around your neck (or to the pack). Bare feet or socked feet feel the bottom better.
4. Enter at 45 degrees downstream. Face upstream or upstream-diagonal. Move staff first, then downstream foot, then upstream foot. One at a time. Always.

**If you fall:**
Roll immediately onto your back. Get your feet downstream and your toes up. Do not fight the current. Work laterally toward the bank with backstroke arm movements. Do not stand until you feel the bottom clearly. One person who drowns fighting a current and one person who survives the same current by floating - the difference is this decision, made in the first 3 seconds.

**If the river is not fordable today:** It may be tomorrow. Rivers fed by snowmelt or tidal systems drop significantly. Wait, particularly if you crossed the river somewhere recently and it was shallower. Rain upstream can double river volume in 6-12 hours. Check again in the morning.

**If you must cross now and cannot wade:** Five dry logs, two cross-poles, and any vine long enough to lash them together will carry two people. Spend 2-3 hours building it. This is not a delay - it is the difference between crossing and not crossing.
