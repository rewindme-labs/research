# Load Carrying Techniques
## Description
<!-- @field: description -->
The ability to carry loads while moving bipedally is one of the defining functional advantages of upright walking. Unlike quadrupeds, whose forelimbs are committed to locomotion, a bipedal hominin has two hands permanently free for carrying. This seemingly simple capability has profound consequences: it enables provisioning of group members who cannot forage for themselves (infants, injured individuals, the very old), transport of food from acquisition sites to safe processing locations, carrying of tools over long distances, and transport of water from source to camp.

Early hominins carried loads without manufactured containers. The techniques available are the ones that human bodies enable naturally: cradled loads, loads balanced on the head, loads wedged against the torso, infants carried on the body. Each technique has optimal load types and distances. Improvised containers - bark trays, large leaves folded into cones, animal stomach pouches - extend carrying capacity significantly.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
The biomechanical analysis of bipedalism's energy costs by David Carrier (1984, *Current Anthropology*) established that bipedal walking is energetically efficient at normal walking speeds - roughly equivalent to quadrupedal walking per unit distance despite the initial impression that balancing on two legs should be costly. When carrying load, bipedalism becomes even more advantageous: the human center of mass is positioned to allow load-bearing on the back and head with minimal additional energetic cost compared to unloaded walking. Quadrupeds must redistribute load across all four limbs and cannot efficiently carry dorsal loads while moving at speed.

Patricia Kramer and Adam Sylvester (2009, *Journal of Human Evolution*) analyzed the skeletal mechanics of load carrying in fossil hominins, concluding that *Australopithecus afarensis* was capable of carrying loads of 15-20% body weight for sustained distances - comparable to modern human porters at low load percentages.

Ethnographic study of the Ache of Paraguay (Hill and Hurtado, 1996) documented women carrying loads of 10-20 kg of gathered food, transported an average of 3-7 km per day during foraging. !Kung San women documented by Lee (1979) carried food loads of 7-15 kg routinely. Children as young as 5-6 began carrying small loads, developing the skill progressively.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Carrying technique by load type:**

| Load Type | Best Technique | Weight Limit | Distance |
|-----------|---------------|-------------|---------|
| Small stones/tools | Hand carry, fist grip | 1-2 kg | All distances |
| Sharp flakes | Wrapped in leaves, then carried | Any weight | All distances |
| Meat cuts | Cradled in one arm, or draped over shoulder | 5-10 kg | Short-medium |
| Tubers/plant food | Both arms cradled, or balanced on head | 10-20 kg | Medium-long |
| Water (in gourd/bladder) | Cradled, or carried balanced on head | 5-8 kg | Short-medium |
| Infant | Clinging to front or back, supported by one arm | 5-15 kg (growing) | All distances |
| Infant (very young) | Cradled against chest, one arm under | 3-5 kg | All distances |

**Head carrying (tumpline alternative):**

1. Create a flat pad from folded bark or grass bundle (distributes pressure on skull)
2. Place load symmetrically on top of skull, directly over the spine
3. Engage neck muscles and walk with slightly reduced step length
4. Keep load compact and below center of gravity (wide, flat better than tall, narrow)
5. Maximum sustainable load for untrained carrier: ~20-25% body weight
6. Advantages: frees both hands, distributes load through skeleton directly

**Back carrying (without a pack):**

1. Fold a large piece of hide, bark, or braided grass into a sheet
2. Lay load in the center of the sheet
3. Bring the four corners over the shoulders and across the chest, tying across the front
4. Load should ride high on the back - center of mass close to the body's center
5. A single carrying strap (vine, braided grass) looped across the forehead (tumpline) with the load hanging down the back is even more efficient for heavy loads

**Improvised containers:**

| Container | Method | Capacity | Best For |
|-----------|--------|----------|---------|
| Large leaf cone | Roll leaf into cone shape, pin with a thorn | 0.5-1 kg | Small, dry items |
| Bark tray | Peel large flat bark section, fold edges up | 2-5 kg | Larger plant foods |
| Animal stomach | Remove, clean, tie one end | 2-4 liters | Water, liquid foods |
| Woven grass basket | Braid dried grass, takes 1-2 hours | 5-15 kg | Long-term use |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Infant provisioning:** Young infants cannot forage and must be carried 100% of the time. A carrying technique that integrates the infant's weight against the body's center of mass with minimal energetic penalty is what makes extended juvenile dependency viable.
- **Food transport from site to camp:** Butchering a carcass at the kill site and carrying portions back to a secure camp location protects the food from competing scavengers and allows consumption at a lower-predation-risk location.
- **Water transport:** Carrying water from a source back to a dry camp location allows the group to extend its range beyond walking distance from water, dramatically expanding viable territory.
- **Tool transport:** Carrying quality stone material from outcrops to activity sites (see: *Stone Tool Caching*) is what enables strategic tool use separated from raw material source.

| Parameter | Value | Notes |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| Optimal carrying load (sustained travel) | 20-30% body weight | Above this, energy cost rises sharply |
| Head carrying energy efficiency | ~2% above unloaded | At loads up to 20% body weight |
| Back carrying energy efficiency | ~5-10% above unloaded | At loads up to 20% body weight |
| Ache women average daily food carry | 10-20 kg | Over 3-7 km foraging distance |

## Day 0  -  Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->
You need to get this food back to camp. It is 6 kilometers away. The pieces are too heavy to carry in your hands the whole way.

Find two large flat pieces of bark. Lay your food across them. Fold the edges up. Now you have trays. Carry one in each arm, balanced against your body.

If you have a long vine or braided grass strip, loop it across your forehead and arrange the load on your back, high and close to your spine. This takes the weight off your arms completely. Your hands are free. You can walk all day like this.

For sharp stone flakes: always wrap them in a leaf before carrying. Three seconds wrapping saves a deep cut on your palm.

If you are carrying an infant and a food load simultaneously, put the infant against your chest and the food load on your back. The infant's weight is at your center of gravity. The food is distributed behind.

The loads you fail to bring back are the calories you left on the ground. Learn to carry more and you eat better.
