# Animal Behaviour Knowledge

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

Animal behaviour knowledge is the understanding of how specific animal species move, feed, rest, mate, defend territory, and respond to threats. It answers critical survival questions: when does this animal drink, where does it sleep, what makes it charge, how fast can it run, can it climb, will it abandon a kill, and what does its alarm call mean for me? Every surviving hunter-gatherer culture possesses encyclopaedic knowledge of local fauna. San Bushmen trackers identify individual animals by personality and predict their behaviour hours in advance based on track analysis (Liebenberg, 1990). This knowledge transforms animals from unpredictable hazards into readable, predictable elements of the environment.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

Louis Liebenberg's field research with San Bushmen (Kalahari, 1990-2006) documented that expert trackers predict animal behaviour with 85-95% accuracy based solely on tracks and environmental context: "This kudu is heading for the pan because it is hot and this track shows it has been running." At Tai Forest (Ivory Coast), Boesch documented chimpanzee hunting strategies that exploit detailed knowledge of colobus monkey escape behaviour: chimps predict which tree a fleeing colobus will jump to and position blockers accordingly, requiring predictive mental modelling of another species' decisions. The Mbuti pygmies of the Congo (documented by Colin Turnbull, 1965) know the territorial ranges of individual duiker antelope and time their net hunts to intercept specific animals on predictable daily routes.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->

**Essential animal behaviour categories:**

| Behaviour | What to observe | Survival application |
|-----------|----------------|---------------------|
| Feeding time/pattern | When and where the animal eats | Ambush timing and location |
| Water schedule | When it drinks, which water source | Interception point for hunting |
| Sleep site/time | Where it rests, diurnal vs nocturnal | Avoidance (predators) or approach (prey) |
| Flight distance | How close before it runs | Stalking approach threshold |
| Charge trigger | What provokes attack | Avoidance protocols |
| Group structure | Solitary, pair, herd, troop | Assess risk and hunting strategy |
| Alarm signals | What sounds/postures indicate alertness | Know when you have been detected |
| Seasonal patterns | Migration, mating, denning | Long-term planning |

**Critical predator behaviour knowledge:**

| Predator | Active period | Attack style | Deterrent |
|----------|-------------|-------------|-----------|
| Lion | Dusk to dawn | Ambush from cover, short sprint | Group display, fire, noise |
| Leopard | Night, twilight | Stalk and pounce, drags kills to trees | Tree avoidance at night, fire |
| Hyena (spotted) | Night primarily | Pack pursuit, test weak individuals | Group cohesion, fire |
| Crocodile | Day and night | Ambush at water edge, death roll | Never kneel at water, use elevated bank |
| Snake (venomous) | Day (most species) | Defensive strike when surprised | Ground scanning, probe with stick |
| Wild dog | Day | Coordinated pack pursuit | Stay together, do not run |

**Building a species knowledge base:**

1. Observe every animal you encounter for as long as safely possible. What is it doing right now? Where is it going? What triggered its current behaviour?
2. Record patterns over days: "The impala herd drinks at the south pool between dawn and 2 hours after sunrise. They approach from the east. A sentinel stands on the termite mound."
3. Note reactions to your presence. At what distance did it notice you? At what distance did it flee? Did it flee uphill, downhill, into cover, or toward water?
4. Track seasonal changes. Where did the zebra herd go when the dry season started? When did the warthogs move to new burrows?
5. Share observations every evening. Pool group knowledge into a collective species database.

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Hunting success:** Knowing that warthogs return to the same burrow each evening allows pre-positioned ambush. Knowing that wildebeest herds circle back to water every 48 hours enables relay pursuit timing.
- **Predator avoidance:** Knowing lion hunting peaks at dusk shifts all water collection to midday. Knowing crocodiles strike at the water's edge means you never kneel to drink.
- **Interspecies exploitation:** Knowing that honeyguide birds lead humans to bee nests in exchange for wax (documented mutualism) converts a random event into a reliable food source.
- **Danger assessment:** Knowing that a hippo yawning is a threat display, not sleepiness, and that a mock-charging elephant with ears spread will stop but one with ears pinned will not, transforms ambiguous situations into actionable decisions.
- **Seasonal planning:** Animal migration patterns predict resource availability. When the herds move south, follow or shift to alternative food sources before the local game disappears.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

Every animal around you is broadcasting information. Learn to read it.

Start with the dangerous ones. What predators are in your area? Figure that out today. Look for tracks, scat, kill remains, claw marks on trees. Once you know what shares your territory, learn its pattern. Lions hunt at dusk and dawn. Leopards are nocturnal and solitary. Hyenas hunt in packs at night. Crocodiles never leave water's edge. Each predator's pattern tells you when and where to be careful.

Never approach water without scanning both banks and the water surface for 30 seconds. Crocodiles are invisible at the waterline. Never kneel at the water's edge. Fill containers from a standing position on an elevated bank. This single habit prevents the most common large-animal fatality in Africa.

For prey animals, observe their schedule. Spend one morning sitting on high ground watching a water source. Note which species arrives when, from which direction, and how long they stay. Within three days you will have a timetable that tells you exactly when to set an ambush.

Learn alarm calls. When a bird squawks and every antelope head snaps up, something triggered it. A raptor overhead, a snake, a predator approaching. Other animals have better senses than you. They are your early warning system. When the bush goes silent, something dangerous is moving through.

When an elephant faces you with ears spread wide, it is bluffing. Stand still, make yourself appear large, and it will turn away. When an elephant tucks its ears back tight against its head, it is going to charge through you. Move laterally immediately and get behind the largest obstacle available.

Share every observation. The person who saw the leopard tracks near the north water source this morning saves the person who goes there this afternoon. Animal behaviour knowledge is only useful when the whole group has it.
