# Wild Plant Identification & Foraging

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->
Wild plant foraging is the systematic identification, collection, and processing of undomesticated vegetation for food and medicine. Before agriculture, all calories not obtained through hunting or scavenging came from wild plants: roots, tubers, leaves, shoots, fruits, nuts, and seeds. While animals can run away, plants are stationary, making them the most reliable basal caloric and nutritional source for survival. However, plants protect themselves chemically - an estimated 5-10% of wild plants in any given biome are toxic, and some are deadly in doses as small as a single berry or a few grams of root. Foraging requires strict botanical identification or, in desperate situations, a codified testing methodology (the Universal Edibility Test) to separate edible calories from fatal neurotoxins, hepatotoxins, and gastrointestinal irritants. The skill is not glamorous, but in quantitative terms, gathering consistently outperforms hunting: among the !Kung San, women's plant gathering provides 60-80% of the group's total calories with a near-100% daily success rate, while men's hunting succeeds only 3-4% of the time.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
Foraging is the default human subsistence strategy, practiced by all hominins for over 3 million years until the Neolithic Revolution (~10,000 BCE). Dental microwear analysis of *Australopithecus africanus* teeth (c. 3.0 Ma) reveals patterns consistent with heavy consumption of hard seeds, underground tubers, and fibrous plant material. By the time of *Homo erectus* (~1.8 Ma), the human diet had diversified to include a wide range of plant foods processed through pounding and eventually cooking.

Ethnographic studies of recent hunter-gatherers, such as the San of the Kalahari and the Hadza of Tanzania, reveal that wild plants typically account for 50-80% of total caloric intake by weight, with women primarily acting as the gatherers and encyclopedic experts in botanical identification. Hadza women can identify and name over 300 plant species in their environment, including detailed knowledge of seasonal availability, processing requirements, and toxicity.

Even agrarian societies relied on "famine foods" - wild plants consumed during crop failures. During the Siege of Leningrad (1941-1944), the Soviet botanist Nikolai Vavilov's colleagues at the Institute of Plant Industry, despite starving to death themselves, maintained the world's largest seed bank while the city's population survived by foraging urban weeds: dandelions, nettles, linden leaves, and acorns. During the Siege of Sarajevo (1992-1996), urban residents similarly reverted to foraging vacant lots and parks. The loss of even one generation of foraging knowledge makes modern humans uniquely vulnerable: a modern person can starve to death surrounded by edible flora, or die from eating a root they mistook for wild carrot (when it was actually Water Hemlock).

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**The Universal Edibility Test (UET):**
If you cannot positively identify a plant, you MUST use the UET. This test isolates a single plant part (e.g., just the root, or just the leaf) and tests it over 24 hours. NEVER test more than one plant or plant part at a time. The entire process takes at least 24 hours to ensure slow-acting toxins are detected.

| Step | Time | Action | Pass/Fail |
|------|------|--------|-----------|
| 1. Separate | Hour 0 | Divide plant into root, stem, leaves, buds, flowers | Discard if: milky/colored sap, almond scent (cyanide), fine barbed hairs |
| 2. Contact | Hour 1 | Crush, rub on inner wrist/elbow, wait 15 min | FAIL = burning, itching, rash, swelling |
| 3. Lip | Hour 2 | Touch prepared portion to outer lip, wait 3 min | FAIL = burning, tingling, numbness |
| 4. Tongue | Hour 3 | Place pinch on tongue, hold 15 min, do not swallow | FAIL = extreme bitterness, soapy taste, burning |
| 5. Chew | Hour 4 | Chew pinch for 15 min, do not swallow, spit | FAIL = any adverse sensation |
| 6. Swallow | Hour 5 | Swallow single pinch, wait 8 hours | FAIL = nausea, cramps, vomiting (induce vomiting immediately) |
| 7. Volume | Hour 13 | Eat 1/4 cup of prepared portion, wait 8 hours | FAIL = any gastrointestinal distress |
| 8. Confirm | Hour 21+ | If no symptoms: this plant part, prepared this way, is safe | PASS |

**WARNING:** The UET tests ONE plant part prepared ONE way. Leaves may be safe boiled but toxic raw. Roots may be safe while flowers are lethal. Each combination requires a separate 24-hour test.

**Common Safe Families (Northern Hemisphere):**

| Family | Identification | Safety | Example Species |
|--------|---------------|--------|-----------------|
| Mustard (Brassicaceae) | 4 petals in cross, 6 stamens (4 tall, 2 short) | All safe, often peppery | Wild mustard, shepherd's purse, watercress |
| Mint (Lamiaceae) | Square stems, opposite leaves, aromatic | Most safe, good for teas | Wild mint, thyme, lemon balm |
| Mallow (Malvaceae) | 5 separate petals, fused stamens, slimy sap | Most safe, mucilaginous | Common mallow, hollyhock |
| Pine (Pinaceae) | Needles in bundles, cones | Cambium edible, needles for tea | Pine, spruce, fir (AVOID YEW: flat needles, lethal) |
| Composite (Asteraceae) | Flower heads with many tiny florets | Generally safe, some bitter | Dandelion, chicory, sunflower |

**DANGER - Families to AVOID Unless Expert:**

| Family | Why It's Deadly | Lethal Species |
|--------|----------------|----------------|
| Carrot/Parsley (Apiaceae) | Contains the most toxic plants in North America/Europe alongside edible lookalikes | Water Hemlock, Poison Hemlock, Giant Hogweed |
| Nightshade (Solanaceae) | Many species contain deadly tropane alkaloids | Deadly Nightshade, Jimsonweed, bitter potato berries |
| Buttercup (Ranunculaceae) | Contains protoanemonin - causes severe gut inflammation | All species mildly to severely toxic |

## Materials & Improvisation
<!-- @field: materials -->
- **Digging Stick:** A sharpened, fire-hardened hardwood stick (1-1.5m long) is essential for accessing high-calorie taproots and tubers (wild carrots, cattail rhizomes, Jerusalem artichoke). Hands alone cannot efficiently excavate roots 30-60 cm deep in compacted soil. This is likely the oldest tool in continuous human use.
- **Gathering Container:** Woven baskets, folded bark containers, a large leaf, or simply a shirt tied at the bottom to hold collected items safely. Without a container, foraging efficiency drops by 60-70% (you can only carry what fits in your hands).
- **Processing Tools:** Two flat/rounded stones (mortar and pestle) for grinding hard seeds, nuts (acorns), or tough roots to make them digestible (see: *Grinding Stone*).
- **Leaching Setup:** Some abundant foods (e.g., acorns) contain toxic tannins. They must be crushed to meal and rinsed repeatedly in running fresh water (place in a cloth in a stream) until the bitterness is completely gone before eating. Cold-water leaching takes 2-4 days; hot-water leaching (changing boiling water 3-4 times) takes 4-6 hours.
- **Fire:** Boiling or roasting wild plants serves three critical functions: (1) breaks down cellulose, making nutrients accessible to human digestion, (2) destroys many heat-labile toxins (alkaloids, glycosides), and (3) kills parasites from animal feces in the soil.

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
*   **Baseline Caloric Intake:** Starchy tubers and roots provide carbohydrates essential for survival energy. A single cattail stand can yield 10,000+ kcal per day of harvestable rhizomes. Nuts provide critical fats and proteins - a kilogram of acorn meal (after leaching) contains ~2,400 kcal.
*   **Nutritional Deficiency Prevention:** Scurvy (Vitamin C deficiency) is a major risk within 30-60 days on a strictly meat/grain diet. Foraged greens (dandelion, sorrel, watercress) or pine needle tea prevent it. Jacques Cartier's expedition (1536) was saved from scurvy by the Iroquois, who taught them to make tea from arborvitae (*Thuja occidentalis*) bark.
*   **Medicinal:** Many foraged plants overlap directly with field medicine: willow bark contains salicin (the precursor of aspirin) for pain relief, plantain leaves (*Plantago major*) reduce swelling and prevent infection in insect bites and wounds, and yarrow (*Achillea millefolium*) is a powerful styptic that stops bleeding on contact.
*   **Caloric Insurance:** Plant foraging provides a daily caloric floor that is independent of hunting success. Even when traps are empty and game is scarce, a knowledgeable forager is never at zero.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->
Before you hunt, before you trap, before you farm - you forage. Hunger sets in by Day 2. Starvation lethargy hits by Day 7, and with it comes impaired judgment that leads to fatal mistakes. The easiest, most reliable calories around you are plants, but grabbing the wrong one can kill you faster than starvation itself.

**First-Week Protocol:**

1. **Know the "Big Four" Killers:** Before you eat anything green, burn these into your memory. Water Hemlock (clusters of white flowers, chambered root that smells like raw parsnip - the most toxic plant in North America, one bite of the root causes grand mal seizures within 30 minutes). Poison Hemlock (tall, hairless stems with PURPLE BLOTCHES, "mousy" smell, killed Socrates). Deadly Nightshade (shiny black berries, bell-shaped purple flowers). Death Camas (grass-like leaves with white flower clusters). If you are NOT certain a plant is safe, do NOT eat it. Period.
2. **Target the "Safes":** Cattails (*Typha*) are the supermarket of the wild, found at the edge of any pond, lake, or slow stream globally. The young shoots are edible raw or cooked; the pollen (yellow dust from the flower heads) is flour; the rhizomes (underground horizontal stems) are packed with starch and can be eaten roasted or pounded into meal. A single dense cattail stand can feed you indefinitely. Dandelions are 100% edible: leaves (bitter but nutritious), flowers (sweet), and roots (roast as coffee substitute). If you see a pine tree, you have Vitamin C (steep needles in hot water for 10 minutes) and emergency calories (peel bark, scrape the thin white cambium layer beneath, eat raw or dry into flour).
3. **If You Don't Know, Run the UET:** Hunger makes people take stupid risks. Do not pop a random berry in your mouth just because it looks like a blueberry or tastes sweet. Many lethal plants taste sweet initially (Deadly Nightshade berries are reportedly pleasant). Run the Universal Edibility Test. It takes 24 hours. Yes, you will be hungry during those 24 hours, but you will not die of liver failure on Day 3.
4. **Boil Everything If Possible:** If you have fire, boil wild greens before eating. Boiling accomplishes three things simultaneously: it breaks down tough cellulose cell walls (making nutrients accessible), it destroys many heat-labile toxins (some alkaloids break down above 80C), and it kills parasites and bacteria from animal feces that contaminate ground-level plants.
5. **Acorns Are Survival Gold, But Require Processing:** If you have oak trees, you have a virtually unlimited food supply. But raw acorns contain tannic acid that causes severe kidney damage, nausea, and abdominal cramping. You must shell them, crush them into coarse meal (use a grinding stone), and leach them in water. The simplest method: wrap the meal in a cloth or stuff it into a woven basket and place it in a running stream for 2-4 days. Taste it daily - when the bitterness is completely gone, the tannins are leached. You now have a high-fat, high-carbohydrate flour (2,400 kcal/kg) that can be baked into flatbread or stirred into porridge. This single technique kept entire civilizations alive through winter.
