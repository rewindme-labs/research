# Territorial Awareness

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

Territorial awareness is the understanding of home-range boundaries, recognition of neighbouring groups, and management of inter-group encounters through avoidance, display, or confrontation. All social primates maintain territories: areas of habitual use defended against competitors. Chimpanzees at Ngogo (Uganda) patrol their territory borders in groups of 8-30 males, moving silently along boundary zones, listening for neighbouring group vocalisations, and attacking lone individuals from rival communities with lethal intent (Watts & Mitani, 2001). Early hominin groups almost certainly maintained territories centred on critical resources (water, tool stone sources, productive foraging zones) and managed inter-group relationships through a combination of avoidance, mutual display, and occasional conflict. Territorial knowledge is both spatial (where are the boundaries?) and social (who are the neighbours, and what is our relationship?).

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

John Mitani's 10-year study at Ngogo (Uganda, 2001-2010) documented chimpanzee lethal intergroup aggression resulting in territorial expansion: after systematically killing members of a neighbouring community over several years, the Ngogo community expanded its territory by 22%. This is the clearest non-human precedent for territorial warfare. At Laetoli (Tanzania, 3.66 Ma), the presence of multiple hominin trackways across the landscape suggests group movement patterns consistent with territorial ranging. In modern hunter-gatherer societies, the !Kung San maintain flexible territories averaging 250-500 km2 per band, with boundaries negotiated through inter-band kinship ties and seasonal reciprocal access agreements (Lee, 1979). The Yanomami of Amazonia maintain more rigid territorial boundaries defended through ritual combat and occasional lethal raiding.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->

**Establishing territorial knowledge:**

| Component | How to learn it | Time required |
|-----------|----------------|---------------|
| Home range extent | Walk the perimeter over several days | 3-7 days |
| Resource locations | Map water, food, stone, shelter within range | Ongoing |
| Boundary markers | Note natural features at range edges (rivers, ridges, open gaps) | 1-2 days |
| Neighbour detection | Listen for vocalisations, find tracks, observe fire smoke | Ongoing |
| Danger zones | Identify overlap areas where inter-group encounter is likely | 1-2 weeks |

**Daily territorial management:**

1. Listen at dawn. Sound carries furthest in still morning air. Vocalisations, chopping sounds, or fire smoke from unexpected directions indicate neighbouring group activity.
2. Check boundary zones every 2-3 days. Look for fresh human footprints, fire remains, discarded food, or tool-making debris from unknown sources.
3. When approaching boundary zones for foraging, travel in groups of 3+ minimum. Solo individuals at territory edges are maximally vulnerable.
4. Mark your presence. Leave conspicuous signs of occupation: maintained fire pits, tool-making debris, cleared paths. These signal active use to any group scouting your area.

**Inter-group encounter protocol:**

| Situation | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Distant detection (300+ m, no contact) | Observe silently. Note group size, direction, behaviour. Withdraw if outnumbered. |
| Mutual detection (50-300 m) | Group together. Stand tall. Display weapons visually. Do not approach or retreat. Assess intent. |
| Close encounter (under 50 m) | Vocal hailing. Open hands visible. Weapons present but not raised. Leader steps forward alone. |
| Hostile signals (weapons raised, charging) | Defensive formation. Women and children retreat. Adult males form line. Vocalize aggressively. Do not turn and run. |
| Peaceful signals (weapons down, hands open) | Reciprocate. Maintain distance. Exchange gestures. Consider offering a small food item as peace gesture. |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Resource protection:** The water source, the nut grove, the knapping stone outcrop - these are non-negotiable survival resources. Territorial awareness ensures your group maintains access through presence and deterrence.
- **Conflict avoidance:** Most inter-group encounters among foragers end without violence. But only if both groups detect each other early enough to choose avoidance. Surprise close encounters escalate rapidly.
- **Seasonal diplomacy:** Territorial boundaries among foragers are often negotiable. In lean seasons, reciprocal access agreements allow both groups to exploit each other's territory during different periods. This requires knowing who your neighbours are.
- **Expansion planning:** As group size grows, the current territory may become insufficient. Identifying adjacent areas with resources and assessing the strength of neighbouring groups informs expansion or migration decisions.
- **Safety buffer:** The boundary zone itself - even a 500 m strip of avoided overlap area - provides early warning distance against surprise attack.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

You are not alone out here. Other groups exist. They need the same water, the same food, the same shelter you do. Whether that relationship becomes cooperative or lethal depends on awareness.

Within your first week, walk the perimeter of the area you are using. Note where natural boundaries fall: a river, a ridge line, a stretch of open ground. These are your territorial edges. Know them.

Every morning, stop and listen for 60 seconds before doing anything. Sound carries in still morning air. If you hear voices, chopping, or smell smoke from a direction where your group is not, someone else is close. That is not a crisis. That is information.

Check your boundary areas every two to three days. Walk through and look for signs of human passage: footprints that are not yours, fire remains you did not build, freshly knapped stone debris from an unfamiliar technique. These signs tell you how close, how active, and how large your neighbours are.

Never go to boundary zones alone. Minimum three people, armed, moving quietly, observing before being observed. The group that sees first controls the encounter. The group that is surprised is at a disadvantage.

If you encounter another group, do not run. Do not charge. Stand together. Hold your weapons where they can be seen but not raised aggressively. Open your other hand, palm visible. Make eye contact with whoever appears to lead them. Let them see your group size and your readiness without seeing hostility. Most encounters between foraging groups end in mutual assessment and withdrawal. Let this be one of those.

Make your camp visible from a distance. Keep fire burning. Leave evidence of your occupation. A territory that looks actively used is a territory that neighbouring groups think twice about entering. An abandoned-looking camp invites exploration and encroachment.
