# Thermoregulation

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->
At 42 degrees Celsius core body temperature, your brain begins to cook. Proteins denature, cell membranes lose integrity, and the hypothalamus - the organ responsible for regulating your temperature - fails. Death follows within minutes. On the open African savanna at midday, ambient temperature regularly exceeds 40 degrees C in the shade and 55 degrees C at ground level in direct sun. A physically active hominin running, digging, or carrying a load in those conditions can reach lethal core temperature in 30-60 minutes without intervention.

The human thermoregulatory system is the most effective of any land mammal. You have 2-4 million eccrine sweat glands distributed across nearly your entire body surface, capable of producing 1-2 liters of sweat per hour. Evaporating that sweat removes approximately 580 calories of heat energy per liter. No other primate sweats at this rate. No quadrupedal predator on the savanna can match it. A running human dissipates heat through continuous full-body sweating while a running antelope, lion, or hyena must pant - and panting requires slowing down or stopping, because the respiratory cycle competes with the locomotive gait cycle. This asymmetry is the physiological basis of persistence hunting and the reason early *Homo* could exploit the midday thermal niche that every other large mammal avoids.

But the system has a hard constraint: water. Every liter of sweat is a liter of water lost. Without replacement, dehydration degrades sweating capacity within hours, and the advantage disappears. Thermoregulation in practice is the behavioral management of this tradeoff: scheduling activity around temperature, seeking shade during peak heat, monitoring hydration status, and recognizing the symptoms of heat illness before they become irreversible.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
Peter Wheeler's thermoregulatory hypothesis (1984, 1991, *Journal of Human Evolution*) proposed that bipedalism itself is partly an adaptation to heat management. His calculations showed that a bipedal hominin standing upright at solar noon exposes only the head and shoulders to direct overhead sun, while a quadruped of the same body mass presents its entire dorsal surface. The result: a bipedal hominin absorbs approximately 40% less solar radiation than a quadruped. Standing upright also raises the body above the superheated air layer at ground level (which can be 10-15 degrees C hotter than air at head height) and exposes more skin to wind for evaporative cooling.

Daniel Lieberman and Dennis Bramble's landmark paper "Endurance running and the evolution of *Homo*" (2004, *Nature*) documented the suite of thermoregulatory adaptations that appear in *Homo ergaster/erectus* around 1.8 Ma: reduced body hair over most of the surface (allowing unobstructed sweat evaporation), increased eccrine gland density, elongated limbs (increasing surface-area-to-mass ratio for heat dissipation), and a narrow trunk. These features have no explanation in terms of strength, climbing ability, or cold tolerance. They are heat-dissipation engineering.

Nina Jablonski and George Chaplin (2000, *Journal of Human Evolution*) connected the loss of body hair to the evolution of dark skin: once hair was reduced, the underlying skin required melanin protection against UV radiation. The package - hairless, dark-skinned, drenched in sweat - defines the thermal strategy of equatorial *Homo* and distinguishes it from every other primate lineage.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Daily activity schedule (tropical/subtropical):**

| Time | Thermal conditions | Recommended activity |
|------|-------------------|---------------------|
| 05:00-08:30 | Cool, temperature rising | Maximum-intensity work: hunting, long-distance travel, heavy digging |
| 08:30-10:30 | Warm, tolerable | Moderate work: foraging, food processing, tool repair |
| 10:30-15:00 | Peak heat, dangerous in direct sun | Rest in deep shade. Minimal exertion. Drink water |
| 15:00-17:30 | Cooling, still warm | Moderate work resumes |
| 17:30-19:00 | Cool | Camp tasks, evening travel, social activity |

Violating this schedule does not make you tough. It makes you dead. The lion sleeping under the acacia at noon is not lazy. It is competent.

**Finding and using shade:**

| Shade source | Temperature reduction | Notes |
|-------------|----------------------|-------|
| Dense tree canopy (acacia, fig, baobab) | 8-12 degrees C below open sun | Best option. Deep shade, breeze penetration |
| Rock overhang or cave mouth | 10-15 degrees C | Excellent. Stone stays cool through midday |
| Tall grass (standing, not lying in) | 3-5 degrees C | Marginal. Blocks direct sun but traps still air |
| Own shadow (standing) | Minimal | Better than nothing. Face away from sun |

Do not lie on sun-baked sand or bare rock. The ground surface in direct sun can exceed 65 degrees C and will conduct heat into your body. Lie on shaded rock, packed earth under trees, or green vegetation.

**Recognizing heat illness:**

| Stage | Symptoms | Action |
|-------|----------|--------|
| Heat stress (normal) | Heavy sweating, thirst, flushed skin | Drink. Continue monitoring |
| Heat exhaustion | Headache, dizziness, nausea, muscle cramps, heavy fatigue | Stop all activity. Move to shade immediately. Drink slowly. Wet skin |
| Heat stroke (emergency) | Confusion, slurred speech, hot dry skin (sweating stops), staggering | Wet entire body with any available water. Fan aggressively. This is a medical emergency with 20-70% mortality |

The critical transition is from heat exhaustion to heat stroke. When sweating stops on a hot day during exertion, the thermoregulatory system has failed. Core temperature is climbing uncontrolled. You have minutes, not hours.

**Water intake during activity:**

| Activity level | Minimum water per hour |
|---------------|----------------------|
| Resting in shade | 200-400 mL |
| Light foraging | 400-600 mL |
| Walking with load | 600-800 mL |
| Running or heavy labor | 800-1,200 mL |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Persistence hunting:** The entire strategy depends on exploiting the human thermoregulatory advantage. The hunter maintains a steady trot during peak heat while the prey overheats because it cannot pant and gallop simultaneously. Knowing your own thermal limits - when to push and when to wet your skin and drink - determines whether you kill a kudu or collapse next to it
- **Travel planning:** Groups that schedule movement for the first three hours after dawn and the last two before sunset can cover 20-30 km per day indefinitely. Groups that march through midday cover the same distance once, then lose a member to heat stroke
- **Camp site selection:** Camps sited near shade and water reduce baseline water consumption and resting energy expenditure. A camp under a large acacia with a stream within 200 meters is worth a 2 km detour over a camp in open grassland near a carcass
- **Protecting vulnerable members:** Infants and the elderly thermoregulate less efficiently than healthy adults. Active monitoring - ensuring shade access, water intake, and reduced midday activity for these individuals - is a group survival responsibility, not a luxury

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->
It is 10:30 in the morning. You have been walking since dawn with 5 kg of meat in a hide sling. The air temperature is 38 degrees. The sun is almost directly overhead. Your shirt is soaked. Your head hurts. You are thirsty in a way that makes your tongue feel thick.

Stop walking. Right now. Not in 20 minutes when you reach that tree line. Now.

Find shade. The nearest tree with a canopy wider than your body is 40 meters to your left. Walk to it. Sit down with your back against the trunk on the shaded side. Put the meat down. Spread your arms and legs - do not curl into a ball, that traps heat. Maximize the skin exposed to air.

Drink. Not a sip. 400 mL, steady. If you have more water, pour 200 mL over your head and neck. The evaporation from your scalp and the blood vessels in your neck cools the blood flowing to your brain faster than sweating alone.

You will stay here for four hours. This feels like waste. It is not waste. It is the reason you are alive at 15:00 to continue walking while the caracal that was stalking you at 10:00 is panting under a rock with its tongue out, unable to hunt until sunset.

At 15:00 the shadows lengthen and the air temperature drops below 35 degrees. Stand up. Drink again. Pick up the meat. Walk.

If at any point during the day you stop sweating while you are hot and exerting yourself, you are dying. Lie down in shade and pour every drop of water you have over your body. Do not save water for later. There is no later if your core temperature reaches 42 degrees.

You carry 4 liters of water for a full day in this climate. Not 2. Not 1. Four. If you do not have a container that holds 4 liters, that is your first priority before you travel anywhere in the heat.
