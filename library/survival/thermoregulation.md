# Thermoregulation
<!-- @field: description -->
The African savanna midday temperature regularly exceeds 38-42°C in the open. Without active behavioral thermoregulation, a physically active hominin can reach fatal core temperature (>42°C) within 30-60 minutes of sustained effort in full sun. The human body's thermoregulatory system — the most effective of any mammal — relies on sweating (evaporative cooling) as its primary mechanism, but this system fails rapidly when water intake does not match sweat loss.

Thermoregulation is the behavioral management of this physiological reality. It includes strategic scheduling of activity to cooler parts of the day, use of shade during the midday heat peak, wet-ground cooling, panting mitigation, monitoring body heat signals, and recognizing the early symptoms of heat exhaustion before they progress to heat stroke. Unlike most survival skills, thermoregulation failure is rapid — death or incapacitation can occur within hours without intervention.

Homo ergaster (~1.8 Ma) is the first hominin definitively associated with the full modern suite of thermoregulatory adaptations: reduced body hair over most of the body surface, increased density of eccrine (sweat) glands, darker skin as UV protection, and elongated limbs that increase surface area relative to body mass. These adaptations enabled sustained activity on the open savanna in conditions that forced most large mammals into shade.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
The thermoregulatory hypothesis for hominin evolution, formalized by Peter Wheeler (1984, 1991, *Journal of Human Evolution*) and expanded by Daniel Lieberman and colleagues, proposes that bipedalism itself is partly an adaptation to heat management: walking upright reduces the body surface area exposed to direct overhead sun (only the head and shoulders face the midday sun directly) and raises the body above the hot ground-level air layer.

Wheeler's calculations showed that a bipedal hominin in open savanna gains 40% less solar heat than a quadruped of equivalent mass. This, combined with enhanced sweating capacity, enables activity during the midday hours when quadrupedal carnivores are forced to rest — a competitive niche that early Homo exploited for persistence hunting.

Nina Jablonski and George Chaplin (2000, *Journal of Human Evolution*) documented the correlation between UV radiation latitude and melanin concentration in human skin, confirming that dark skin evolved as UV protection specifically in the context of reduced body hair — the two adaptations co-occurring in Homo ergaster.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Daily activity schedule (optimal):**

| Time | Condition | Activity |
|------|-----------|---------|
| 05:00-09:00 | Cool, rising temperature | Maximum activity — hunting, travel, heavy work |
| 09:00-11:00 | Warm, moderate | Moderate activity — foraging, processing |
| 11:00-15:00 | Peak heat | Rest in shade — minimal exertion |
| 15:00-18:00 | Cooling | Moderate activity resumes |
| 18:00-20:00 | Comfortable | Evening tasks, camp preparation |

**Shade selection:**

1. Deep shade is 8-12°C cooler than open sun in the same environment
2. Trees with dense canopy (acacia, fig) are best — avoid thin or leafless trees
3. Ground under shade retains moisture longer than open ground — cooler to sit on
4. North-facing rock overhangs stay cool through the midday hours
5. Do not lie on dry sand (retains heat) — lie on rock or packed earth in deep shade

**Monitoring heat symptoms:**

| Symptom | Severity | Action |
|---------|----------|--------|
| Heavy sweating, thirst | Normal exertion | Drink water, continue monitoring |
| Headache, dizziness | Early heat exhaustion | Stop exertion, find shade, drink |
| Nausea, muscle cramps | Heat exhaustion | Lie down in shade, wet skin, drink slowly |
| Confusion, stopping sweating | Heat stroke — emergency | Wet entire body with any available water, fan vigorously |

**Cooling techniques without water:**

1. Seek shade immediately upon first heat symptom
2. Spread arms and legs to maximize surface area
3. Wet any available cloth or bark with urine or any liquid if water unavailable (evaporation cools)
4. Fan with large leaves to accelerate evaporation from skin
5. Lie on cool rock or soil rather than sitting

**Water intake during activity:**

| Activity level | Minimum water per hour |
|----------------|----------------------|
| Rest in shade | 200-400 ml |
| Light foraging | 400-600 ml |
| Sustained walking | 600-800 ml |
| Running/heavy work | 800-1,200 ml |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Persistence hunting:** The persistence hunting strategy explicitly exploits the human thermoregulatory advantage — humans can sustain activity in midday heat that forces prey to overheat and stop running. Knowing when to hunt (midday) and how to avoid overheating while doing so is the core competence.
- **Travel planning:** Groups that schedule travel for morning and evening, resting midday, can cover far more total distance than groups that travel at random times and exhaust themselves in the heat.
- **Protecting group members:** Infants and elderly individuals have less efficient thermoregulation than healthy adults. Active monitoring and ensuring access to shade and water for vulnerable group members is a survival responsibility.
- **Camp siting:** Choosing camp locations with access to shade during midday hours reduces resting energy expenditure and water consumption significantly.

## Day 0 — Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0 -->
It is 11 in the morning. You have been moving since dawn. The sun is directly overhead and the air above the ground shimmers.

Stop.

Find shade — a large tree, a rock overhang, anything with solid, deep shadow. Lie down. Spread your limbs. Do not sit in a ball.

Drink. Not in gulps — steady, 200 ml every 20 minutes. If you feel fine, drink anyway.

You will not accomplish anything in the next 3 hours that is worth the cost of heatstroke. You will lose 2-3 hours of productivity. You will save your life.

The lion in the acacia tree across the clearing is doing the same thing you are doing. It is not laziness. It is competence.

When the light changes and the shadows lengthen — around 15:00 — rise and continue. The evening hours belong to you again.

Sleep is optional. Shade is not.
