# Snare Trapping
<!-- @field: description -->
A snare is a passive capture device that works while the trapper sleeps, forages, or performs other tasks — making it among the highest calorie-return-per-effort methods of animal protein acquisition available to a hunter-gatherer. A single individual can set 10-20 snares in 1-2 hours and then check them the following morning, potentially collecting 3-8 animals without any further active hunting. This passive food production model is a significant multiplier of individual foraging capacity.

Snares exploit the predictable movement patterns of animals — their runways, burrow entrances, watercourse crossing points, and feeding areas. Once you understand where animals move, a snare converts that knowledge into food automatically. The same snare can be reset and used repeatedly at a productive location for weeks.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
The oldest confirmed snare-type trap is a bone toggle from Sibudu Cave, South Africa (~77,000 ya), identified by Lyn Wadley and colleagues as a possible component of a spring snare mechanism. However, the use of simple loop snares made from plant fiber or rawhide almost certainly predates this significantly — the required materials (cordage) and animal knowledge are simpler than many other confirmed behaviors.

Ethnographic documentation of snare trapping is universal in foraging societies. The Hadza of Tanzania set grass-stem snares for guinea fowl and francolin in well-traveled grass corridors. The San of the Kalahari use rawhide loop snares for springhare and other rodents at burrow entrances. Aboriginal Australians set hair-and-plant-fiber snares at wallaby runways. The convergent invention of this technique across isolated cultures confirms that it follows directly from animal tracking knowledge and cordage production.

Experimental archaeology studies (Binneman et al., 1997) have demonstrated that simple loop snares made from twisted plant fiber can achieve catch rates of 15-40% per animal runway placement per night — sufficient to justify the effort of mass setting.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Step 1 - Location selection (determines success rate):**

| Location | Target Animal | Signs to Look For |
|----------|--------------|-------------------|
| Compressed runway through grass | Rabbit, rodents, mongoose | Droppings, flattened grass path, worn ground |
| Burrow entrance | Ground squirrel, mole rat | Fresh soil, track concentration |
| Game trail at narrow pass (between boulders/bushes) | Most species | Consistent tracks, hair on thorns |
| Low branch crossing a stream | Birds, small mammals | Regular crossing marks |
| Feeding area edge (fruiting tree base) | Birds, rodents, small primates | Droppings, feeding debris |

**Step 2 - Simple loop snare (no spring):**

1. Prepare a loop of cordage (twisted plant fiber or thin rawhide), 10-15 cm diameter for small rodents; 20-30 cm for rabbits and birds; 40-60 cm for larger animals
2. Form a fixed loop (the noose) using a bowline or simple sliding loop — the loop must slide shut under tension but not reopen
3. Suspend the loop 5-8 cm above the ground for small rodents (at head height for the target species)
4. Support the loop at the sides with two small forked sticks driven into the ground at each side, or tie to overhanging branch
5. The animal walks through the loop, the loop slides shut around the neck or body, the animal's struggles tighten the loop further
6. Anchor the far end of the snare cord firmly to a stake, tree root, or rock — the animal must not be able to drag the snare away

**Step 3 - Spring snare (increases capture rate and prevents escape):**

1. Select a springy sapling or branch 1-2 meters long at the trap location
2. Bend the sapling down and anchor it under tension using a simple toggle trigger:
   - Notch stick A vertically in the ground
   - Hook notch stick B over stick A horizontally
   - Tie the bent sapling to stick B
   - Tie the snare cord to stick B
3. When the animal enters the noose and pulls on the cord, the trigger releases, the sapling springs up, tightening the noose and lifting the animal
4. The spring prevents the animal from bracing against the ground to escape

**Checking and resetting:**

1. Check snares every 6-12 hours — animal in snare too long may be taken by scavengers or escape through extended effort
2. Collect animals immediately; reset spring mechanism before leaving
3. Remove scent from the snare by handling with dry bark or dry grass, not bare hands
4. Move snares that have had no catch after 3 consecutive days — the location is likely compromised

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Passive protein production:** 10-20 snares set before dark provide potential food collection the next morning with zero additional hunting effort — freeing daytime hours for foraging, toolmaking, or other tasks.
- **Species diversification:** Snares access species that are too fast, too alert, or too nocturnal for direct hunting — particularly fast rodents, wary birds, and nocturnal small carnivores.
- **Group provisioning during injury or illness:** When active hunting is impossible (injured group members, illness, extreme heat), maintained snare lines continue to produce food passively.
- **Knowledge multiplication:** Once a productive snare location is identified, the same location can be used season after season, each year's catch informing the next year's placement.

## Day 0 — Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0 -->
You have 2 hours before dark. You need food for tomorrow.

Find a game trail — a narrow path where the grass is pressed flat and there are droppings. The animals that use this path use it every night.

Cut 1.5 meters of cord from your supply. Form a loop 15 cm across. Tie the loop so it slides closed under tension. Push two small forked sticks into the ground on each side of the trail, 5 cm apart. Hang the loop between them, 5-6 cm above the ground. Tie the far end of your cord around the base of a nearby shrub.

Do this 8 more times in 8 different locations — different trails, burrow entrances, spots where animals have been eating.

Go to sleep.

In the morning, walk your snare line. If you set 10 snares with good placement, expect 2-4 animals. That is your protein for the day, caught while you slept.

Reset every snare. The same locations will produce again tomorrow.

Traps hunt when you cannot. Set them every evening.
