# Ambush Strategy

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

Ambush strategy is the positioning of hunters at locations where prey animals will predictably arrive, using concealment and patience to reduce the approach distance to within effective striking range before the animal detects the threat. It inverts the energy equation of pursuit hunting: instead of expending massive calories chasing prey across distance, the ambush hunter expends minimal energy waiting at a location the prey will visit voluntarily. Water holes, game trails, salt licks, fruiting trees, river crossings, and narrow terrain funnels are all predictable congregation points that reduce animal movement from random to deterministic. Ambush hunting requires detailed animal behaviour knowledge (when does this species visit this location?), terrain reading (where is the best concealment with attack access?), patience (remaining motionless for 1-4 hours), and explosive action precision when the moment arrives.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

Chimpanzees at Tai Forest (Ivory Coast) ambush colobus monkeys by positioning blockers in trees along predicted escape routes while drivers push prey toward them, achieving 78% success for coordinated ambushes versus 33% for solo attempts (Boesch & Boesch, 1989). At Olduvai Gorge (1.8 Ma), repeated concentrations of butchered animal remains at specific lakeside locations suggest persistent use of water-edge ambush sites. The Schoningen kill site (Germany, 300,000 BP) preserves 10 spears alongside 20+ horse carcasses at a lake margin, indicating that Homo heidelbergensis exploited predictable horse drinking behaviour for large-scale ambush kills. Among the San Bushmen, documented by Liebenberg (1990), ambush hunting at water holes during the dry season is the highest-success hunting strategy, achieving kill rates of 50-70% when animals are dependent on specific water sources.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->

**Identifying ambush sites:**

| Site type | Why animals come | Best concealment | Optimal timing |
|-----------|-----------------|------------------|----------------|
| Water hole | Drinking (1-2x daily) | Downwind bush/grass 10-20 m from water | Dawn, dusk |
| Game trail bottleneck | Habitual travel route | Beside trail in cover | Daily peak movement |
| Salt lick | Mineral nutrition | Elevated position overlooking | Varies, observe first |
| Fruiting tree | Seasonal food source | Concealed in adjacent trees or ground level | Morning feeding period |
| River crossing | Shallow ford, seasonal migration | Downstream concealment | Migration period |
| Terrain funnel | Narrow gap between rocks, dense brush | At the narrow point | When animals are driven or pass naturally |

**Setting up an ambush (water hole method):**

1. Scout the water hole for 2-3 days. Record which species visit, from which direction, at what time, and how long they stay. This is surveillance, not hunting.
2. Identify the primary approach trail. Animals use the same path repeatedly.
3. Select a concealment position 10-20 m from where the animal will lower its head to drink. Downwind (your scent blows away from the approach path). Behind natural cover (bush, rocks, termite mound).
4. Clear the kill zone of obstacles that could deflect a thrown weapon or impede a rush.
5. On hunt day, arrive 1-2 hours before expected animal arrival. Settle in. Do not move.
6. Remaining motionless is the critical discipline. No scratching, no position shifting, no whispering. Eyes on the approach trail.
7. When the animal lowers its head to drink, it is maximally vulnerable. Signal or execute.

**Ambush execution (2-3 hunters):**

| Role | Position | Action |
|------|----------|--------|
| Primary striker | Closest to kill zone, best concealment | First attack when animal is drinking/feeding |
| Blocker | On the most likely escape route | Cuts off retreat, secondary attack |
| Driver (optional) | Wide flanking position upwind | If ambush fails, drives animal back toward other hunters |

**Patience requirements:**

| Waiting time | What to expect |
|-------------|----------------|
| 30-60 min | Restlessness, urge to move. Resist. |
| 1-2 hours | Body stiffening, minor discomfort. Shift weight silently. |
| 2-3 hours | Boredom, attention drift. Re-focus every 5 minutes. |
| 3-4 hours | Optimal waiting limit. If nothing arrives, abort and try tomorrow. |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Energy efficiency:** An ambush hunter expends 100-200 kcal in a 3-hour wait. A persistence hunt expends 1,500-3,000 kcal over 15-35 km. The energy cost per successful kill is 5-10x lower for ambush hunting.
- **Weapon independence:** Ambush hunting at extreme close range (2-5 m) works with primitive weapons (fire-hardened sticks, heavy stones) that are ineffective at pursuit distances. No advanced projectile technology required.
- **Reliable protein:** Water hole ambush during dry season, when animals must visit specific water sources, achieves 50-70% success rates. This is the most reliable hunting method available without advanced weapons.
- **Low-risk hunting:** Close-range ambush of drinking animals minimises the dangerous phase of hunting. The animal is stationary, head down, and unaware. Pursuit of an alert, running animal risks injury from falls, kicks, and counter-charges.
- **Scalable group size:** Ambush works with 1 person (lowest success) to 6+ (highest success through blocking escape routes). It accommodates whatever group size is available.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

Stop chasing things. You are slower than everything you want to eat. Instead, let the food come to you.

Find the nearest water source that animals use. Spend one day watching it from a safe distance. Note exactly where animals approach from, what time they arrive, and which spot they drink at. Now you have a timetable and a map.

Tomorrow at dawn, arrive at that water source 1-2 hours before the animals do. Find a position 10-15 m from the drinking spot. Downwind of the approach trail. This is essential: if your scent reaches them before you see them, they will never arrive. Check wind direction with a wet finger. Position yourself so wind blows from them toward you, not the reverse.

Get behind cover. A bush, a rock, a termite mound, a depression in the ground. If the cover is thin, reinforce it by piling brush in front of you. You need to be invisible from the approach direction.

Now wait. Do not move. Do not talk. Do not scratch your nose. Everything you want to eat has vision optimised for detecting motion. A motionless human behind a bush at 15 m is invisible. A fidgeting human is spotted instantly.

When the animal arrives and lowers its head to drink, it cannot see you. Its eyes are looking at water and its ears are full of its own drinking sounds. This is the moment. Move slowly into throwing or thrusting position. If you have a partner positioned on the escape route, signal with an agreed gesture.

Strike fast and decisively. One committed attack at close range against an unaware animal has a 50-70% success rate. A hesitant attempt alerts the animal and you get nothing.

If it fails, do not chase. Mark where the animal runs. Come back tomorrow. It will return to the same water source because it has no alternative in the dry season. Patience kills more animals than speed. Every day you can afford to wait is a day closer to success.

Cover your skin with mud before the next attempt. Rub charcoal on your face and arms. These reduce your visual contrast and mask your scent. Small details compound into the difference between eating and starving.
