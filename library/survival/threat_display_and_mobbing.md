# Threat Display & Mobbing

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->
Threat Display and Mobbing are two distinct but interconnected non-lethal defense mechanisms developed by early primates to survive in environments dominated by apex predators (sabertooth cats, hyenas, leopards). A *Threat Display* is a psychological trick: maximizing one's perceived size, volume, and aggression to convince an attacker that a fight will be too costly, forcing them to retreat without actual physical combat. *Mobbing* is the cooperative, offensive version of this: a large group of weak individuals coordinates a massive, simultaneous Threat Display to actively harass and drive a predator away from a location, usually to steal a fresh kill (Power Scavenging) or protect an infant. These behaviors are universal across the animal kingdom, from crows harassing hawks to baboons confronting leopards, and represent the oldest known form of both self-defense and organized warfare.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
Before the invention of the wooden spear (~400,000 years ago) or controlled fire (~1.5 million years ago), early *Australopithecus* (3.5 million years ago) had absolutely no physical weapons capable of piercing the hide of a large African predator. They possessed no fangs, no claws, and were incredibly slow runners compared to four-legged cats. Standing barely 1.2 meters tall and weighing 30-40 kg, they were physically outclassed by every large carnivore on the savanna.

Yet, fossil records show they actively consumed bone marrow from large herbivores - animals they could never have killed themselves. Cut marks on bones from Dikika, Ethiopia (~3.4 Ma, though debated) and Bouri, Ethiopia (~2.5 Ma) demonstrate systematic butchering activity far predating the era of effective hunting weapons. To get this meat, our ancestors had to wait until a lion or sabertooth (*Smilodon*) killed an antelope, and then they had to *steal the carcass from the cat*. This is known as "Power Scavenging" or "Aggressive Scavenging," a term formalized by paleoanthropologist Lewis Binford in 1981.

The only way a troop of three-foot-tall hominids could force a lion to abandon its food without dying was to execute a perfectly synchronized mobbing display, using rocks, branches, and screaming to create an overwhelming sensory overload that broke the predator's nerve. Modern observations support this reconstruction: troops of baboons, which are smaller and weaker than lions individually, routinely mob and drive off leopards using coordinated screaming, charging, and rock-throwing. Biologist Robert Sapolsky observed baboon troops at Masai Mara regularly confronting and driving off leopards through sustained, aggressive mobbing lasting 15-30 minutes.

In modern human encounters with large predators, threat display remains effective. Montana Fish, Wildlife & Parks documented that over 85% of mountain lion encounters in which humans stood their ground, made noise, and appeared large resulted in the cat retreating. The success rate drops to near zero when humans run.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**The Threat Display (Individual Defense):**
If you accidentally stumble upon a predator (e.g., a wolf, a mountain lion, a bear) and flight is impossible, you must instantly switch to intimidation.

| Step | Action | Biological Signal |
|------|--------|-------------------|
| 1 | Freeze, face the animal | "I see you" - removes ambush advantage |
| 2 | Stand tall, spread arms/jacket wide | Maximizes perceived body size by 2-3x |
| 3 | Roar in the deepest voice possible | Low frequency = large lungs = large body |
| 4 | Maintain unbroken eye contact | Breaking gaze = submission signal across all mammals |
| 5 | Throw rocks at the face/body | Creates pain-risk calculation in predator's brain |
| 6 | Never turn your back, never run | Running triggers hardwired pursuit instinct |

1.  **Do Not Run:** Running triggers the predator's pursuit instinct, a neurological reflex as old as the predator-prey arms race itself (400+ million years). You will lose the race. A mountain lion can sprint at 50 km/h; you cannot. Stand your ground.
2.  **Maximize Size:** Spread your arms. Open your jacket. Stand on a rock or raised ground. Make your silhouette violently unnatural and massive. If two people are present, stand together - the combined silhouette reads as one enormous creature. Crows and other birds achieve this same effect by spreading their wings during mobbing.
3.  **Vocalize Aggressively:** Do not scream in a high-pitched panic (which sounds like dying prey to a carnivore). Yell in the deepest, loudest, most guttural roar you can produce. Low-frequency vocalizations physically travel farther through vegetation and signal larger lung capacity and body size. Barking or roaring signals you are a competing predator, not food. Silverback gorillas' chest-beat display at 10-20 Hz is audible over 1 km precisely because of its low frequency.
4.  **Maintain Eye Contact:** Stare directly into the animal's eyes. In the mammalian behavioral lexicon, breaking eye contact is a sign of submission. Every predator reads sustained, direct gaze as confidence. The animal is constantly calculating: "Can I take this thing without getting hurt?"
5.  **Projectiles:** If there are rocks near your feet, throw them at the animal's face, but do not bend over in a way that makes you look small or vulnerable. Pick up rocks without crouching - squat with your torso upright. Even a badly aimed rock creates a pain stimulus that shifts the predator's risk calculus. One rock hitting an eye or tooth can mean starvation for a solitary predator, and they know it.

**Mobbing (Cooperative Aggression):**
To drive a predator away from a food source (power scavenging) or defend a camp:

| Phase | Action | Minimum Force |
|-------|--------|---------------|
| Assembly | Gather 5+ individuals, arm with rocks and branches | Never fewer than 5:1 weight ratio to predator |
| Approach | Advance as a tight wall, shoulder to shoulder | No gaps, no stragglers |
| Barrage | Simultaneous screaming + thrown rocks + waved branches | Continuous, no pauses - 30+ seconds minimum |
| Maintain pressure | If predator retreats, advance slowly | Do not scatter or chase individually |
| Occupation | Move onto the resource/territory immediately | Establish presence before predator regroups |

1.  **Numbers:** Never attempt mobbing with fewer than five people. The strength is in the swarm. A solitary individual attempting to mob a predator off a kill will be killed. Five individuals represent a sensory overload that the predator cannot focus on single target.
2.  **Armament:** Every individual must carry projectiles (large rocks, ideally two per hand) or extensions of size (long, thick branches to wave, making each person appear 3 meters tall).
3.  **The Phalanx Approach:** Advance on the predator as a unified wall. Do not let anyone break ranks or step out alone. A predator will instantly isolate and attack a separated individual. The wall must advance at walking speed, not charging - running creates chaos and breaks formation.
4.  **Sensory Overload:** On a single signal (a shout, a raised arm), everyone screams simultaneously, waves branches violently, and throws rocks directly at the predator in a continuous barrage. The key word is *continuous* - a brief shout followed by silence reads as uncertainty. The noise must not stop for at least 30 seconds.
5.  **The Breaking Point:** Predators calculate risk instinctively. A predator knows that even a minor injury (a rock breaking a tooth, a stick scratching an eye) will lead to infection or impaired hunting ability, which eventually means starvation. The threshold is remarkably low: studies suggest most predators abandon a contested resource if they perceive greater than a 5% risk of any injury.

## Materials & Improvisation
<!-- @field: materials -->

| Material | Function | Why It Works |
|----------|----------|-------------|
| Large branches (leafy) | Wave to triple apparent body width | Visual size inflation |
| Rocks (fist-sized) | Thrown projectiles | Pain stimulus + injury risk |
| Sticks (2m+ length) | Jabbing deterrent | Maintains stand-off distance |
| Fire (if available) | Burning branch display | Exploits universal animal fire-terror |
| Metallic objects | Banging together = loud, unnatural noise | Novel sounds trigger flight response |
| Clothing (jacket, tarp) | Spread wide to increase silhouette | Visual size doubling |

*   **Branches:** You don't need a sharpened spear. A large, leafy branch waved aggressively makes you look 3 meters wide. Chimpanzees use this exact technique, dragging large branches during dominance displays.
*   **Stones:** The goal is not to kill the predator with a rock, but to create pain and confusion. Quantity of thrown rocks is more important than accuracy or size. A continuous barrage of 10 poorly aimed stones is more effective than one perfectly aimed boulder.
*   **Fire (Advanced):** Once fire is available, a burning branch replaces the leafy branch and guarantees immediate predatory retreat. There is no known large terrestrial predator that will approach a burning object held by a standing human. This is likely why Wonderwerk Cave in South Africa (~1.0 Ma) shows the earliest evidence of systematic fire use coinciding with dramatically increased occupation of predator-dense environments.

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
*   **Power Scavenging:** Securing high-calorie meat and marrow by stealing it from apex predators. This was likely the primary protein acquisition strategy for 1-2 million years before effective hunting weapons existed. A single stolen antelope carcass could feed 15-20 hominids for 2-3 days.
*   **Perimeter Defense:** Driving away solitary predators that wander too close to the camp's sleeping area, particularly at night. Coordinated shouting from multiple positions around a camp simulates being surrounded, causing most predators to flee immediately.
*   **Infant Protection:** The most violent and sustained mobbing displays observed in primate groups are triggered by threats to infants. Baboon mothers will attack leopards directly if their infant is seized, with the entire troop joining within seconds.
*   **Territory Establishment:** Consistent, successful mobbing of predators from a site causes the predator to associate that location with high risk, eventually avoiding it entirely. This effectively creates a predator-free zone around the camp through behavioral conditioning.
*   **Psychological Preparedness:** The practice of threat display builds group confidence and cohesion. A group that has successfully mobbed a predator once knows it can do it again, fundamentally altering the fear-based dynamic between prey and predator species.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->
It is Day 1. You are unarmed. You hear a low growl in the brush ahead of you, and a large mountain lion steps onto the path. You have no spear, no knife, and no fire. If you turn around and run, you will die with teeth in the back of your neck. Your brain is screaming at you to flee. Override it. That reflex was fine when your ancestors lived in trees. On open ground, it is a death sentence.

**The Protocol:**
1.  **Freeze, but do not submit:** Stop walking instantly. Keep your chest facing the animal. Lock eyes with it. Do not look away. Do not look at the ground. Do not look at your feet to search for a weapon. Your gaze is your first weapon. In the mammalian behavioral vocabulary, sustained eye contact means "I am not prey."
2.  **Inflate:** Open your jacket wide. Raise your arms above your head. If you have a backpack, hold it up high over your head with both hands. You must convince the cat that you are not a weak primate, but a massive, unknown shape it has never encountered. The cat's brain is processing your silhouette right now. Give it a silhouette that says "this thing is enormous."
3.  **The Voice of God:** Take a massive breath from your diaphragm and roar. Do not scream for help - screaming is high-pitched, and high-pitched sounds mean "small, panicking prey." Roar like you are furious it interrupted your day. Use the lowest, deepest gravel in your throat. Ancient, resonant, sustained bellowing. Your ribcage should vibrate.
4.  **Hold the Line:** The cat will likely pause. It is calculating. You are not acting like prey. Prey cries and runs. You are acting like a rival. It has never encountered a rival that stands on two legs and screams bass notes directly into its face. Its risk calculator is processing: "What IS this thing?"
5.  **The Slow Retreat:** While maintaining eye contact, keeping your arms wide, and continuing to roar aggressively, take one slow step backward. Then another. Never expose your back. Never crouch. Never turn sideways. You are 180 cm of vertical threat display, and you stay that way until you are out of sight.
6.  **The Bluff:** If the cat takes a step forward, stop retreating immediately. Stomp your foot on the ground as hard as you can, throw a rock if one is in your hand, raise your arms even higher, and scream louder. You are playing a game of biological poker with your life as the ante. You must convince the cat that it will lose an eye if it charges you. The math is on your side: a mountain lion with one damaged eye cannot hunt, and a cat that cannot hunt is dead within two weeks. The lion knows this. Eventually, the cost will exceed the benefit, and the cat will slink away. You just survived using nothing but psychology against 70 million years of feline evolution.
