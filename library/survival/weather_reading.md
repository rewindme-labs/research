# Weather Reading & Natural Prediction

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

Weather reading is the ability to predict near-term atmospheric conditions using direct observation of sky, wind, animal behaviour, plants, and physical sensations. Before instruments, before radio, before satellites, survival depended on recognizing the signs that precede storms, cold fronts, heat waves, and flash floods. A sudden temperature drop means a front is arriving. Towering cumulus clouds in the afternoon mean thunderstorms within 2-4 hours. Insects flying low means falling barometric pressure. These patterns are not folklore - they reflect measurable physics of air pressure, humidity, and thermal convection. A skilled observer can predict weather 6-12 hours ahead with 70-80% accuracy using only eyes, skin, and ears.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

Weather prediction is arguably the oldest applied science. Babylonian tablets from roughly 650 BC contain systematic cloud-to-weather correlations, but the practice is millions of years older. Theophrastus of Athens wrote "On Weather Signs" (c. 300 BC), cataloguing 80 indicators including wind direction, dew patterns, and animal behaviour - many remain valid today. Polynesian navigators (c. 1000 AD) read swell patterns, cloud formations over distant islands, and seabird flight to predict conditions across thousands of kilometres of open ocean. The Inuit developed snow-crystal reading to predict blizzard intensity. Aboriginal Australians tie seasonal patterns to astronomical observation, tracking the heliacal rising of specific stars to predict monsoon onset with accuracy within days.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->

**Cloud reading (most reliable indicator):**

| Cloud type | Appearance | Meaning | Time to weather |
|------------|-----------|---------|-----------------|
| Cirrus (high, wispy) | Thin white streaks, "mare's tails" | Fair now, front approaching | 24-48 hours to change |
| Cirrostratus (high haze) | Milky veil, halo around sun/moon | Warm front, rain coming | 12-24 hours |
| Cumulus (puffy) | White cotton balls, flat base | Fair weather if small | Stable |
| Towering cumulus | Tall, cauliflower-shaped | Thunderstorm developing | 1-3 hours |
| Cumulonimbus (anvil) | Dark, massive, anvil top | Violent storm imminent | 30-60 minutes |
| Stratus (low grey sheet) | Uniform grey blanket | Drizzle or light rain | Currently occurring |
| Nimbostratus | Dark, thick, featureless | Prolonged heavy rain | Currently occurring |

**Wind-based prediction:**

1. Face the wind. In the Northern Hemisphere, low pressure (storm) is to your left. In the Southern Hemisphere, it is to your right. (Buys Ballot's Law)
2. Backing wind (shifting counterclockwise, e.g. west to south to east) means deteriorating weather.
3. Veering wind (shifting clockwise, e.g. south to west to north) means improving weather.
4. Sudden calm after wind often precedes the most violent phase of a storm.

**Pressure indicators without a barometer:**

| Indicator | Rising pressure (fair) | Falling pressure (storm) |
|-----------|----------------------|------------------------|
| Smoke | Rises straight up | Curls downward, hangs low |
| Sounds | Distant sounds muffled | Distant sounds carry clearly |
| Smell | Normal | Earth, vegetation smells intensify |
| Insects | Fly normally, high | Fly low, swarm near ground |
| Swallows/bats | Fly high | Fly low (following insects) |
| Campfire | Burns clean, good draft | Smokes, poor draft |
| Dew | Heavy morning dew = fair day | No dew = rain likely |
| Clouds at sunset | Red/orange sunset = fair tomorrow | Grey/green sunset = storm |

**Storm proximity by thunder:**

1. See lightning flash. Count seconds until thunder.
2. Divide by 3 (or multiply by 340 m/s for exact distance).
3. Every 3 seconds = roughly 1 km distance.
4. If the count is shortening between flashes, the storm is approaching. Seek shelter.

**Seasonal and terrain awareness:**

- Mountains generate afternoon thunderstorms almost daily in warm seasons due to orographic lift.
- River valleys collect cold air at night (cold air drainage). Camp above the valley floor.
- Coastal areas: sea breeze (onshore) develops mid-morning as land heats. Land breeze (offshore) develops after sunset as land cools.
- Temperature dropping sharply while wind shifts to north/northwest: cold front arriving. Expect squalls, then clearing.

## Materials & Improvisation
<!-- @field: materials -->

| Tool | Construction | Purpose |
|------|-------------|---------|
| Smoke indicator | Any campfire | Observe smoke behaviour for pressure trends |
| Wind vane | Feather or leaf strip tied to a raised stick | Shows wind direction continuously |
| Dew check | Flat stone or leaf left out overnight | Presence/absence indicates humidity and pressure |
| Pine cone barometer | Hang dried pine cone from string | Scales open in dry/high pressure, close in humid/low pressure |
| Sound test | Strike two rocks together at camp | If distant echoes are unusually clear, pressure is dropping |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Storm preparation:** 6-12 hour warning allows time to reinforce shelter, secure materials, store firewood under cover, move camp from flood-prone areas.
- **Travel planning:** Avoid crossing rivers or exposed ridges when thunderstorms are building. Plan mountain crossings for early morning before convective clouds develop.
- **Water collection:** Predict rain to position bark troughs, wide leaves, or clay basins for collection ahead of time.
- **Fire management:** Predict dry spells to schedule charcoal production. Predict rain to avoid wasted effort on fire-starting.
- **Agricultural timing:** Recognize monsoon onset, frost indicators, and dry-season length for planting decisions.
- **Flood avoidance:** Read upstream cloud buildup over mountains to predict flash floods in valleys hours before water arrives.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

Look at the sky right now. What do you see? If the clouds are small, white, and puffy with flat bottoms, you have a fair day. Work, forage, explore. If they are tall, dark, and growing taller, you have 1-3 hours before heavy rain. Stop what you are doing and prepare shelter.

Light a fire and watch the smoke. If it climbs straight up and disperses high, pressure is stable - good weather holds. If it curls down, pools near the ground, or drifts in confused directions, pressure is falling. Rain or storm is approaching within 6-12 hours.

Check the wind. Face into it. In the Northern Hemisphere, stretch your left arm out - it points roughly toward the low pressure centre (the storm). If the wind is shifting counterclockwise over hours (west to south), conditions are worsening. If it shifts clockwise (south to west to north), the storm is passing and clearing follows.

At sunset, read the western sky. A clean red or orange sunset means dry air to the west - tomorrow should be fair. A grey, murky, or greenish sunset means moisture-laden air is approaching - expect rain.

Before sleeping, check for dew. Place a flat stone or large leaf in the open. If heavy dew forms by midnight, the next day will likely be clear (radiation cooling under clear skies). If no dew forms, clouds or rain are likely (cloud cover traps heat, prevents dew).

Build the habit of looking up every hour. Weather does not arrive without warning. It announces itself in cloud shape, wind shift, smoke behaviour, insect altitude, and the way sounds carry at dusk. Learn these and you will never be caught unprepared.
