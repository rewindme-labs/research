# Cooperative Hunting

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

Cooperative hunting is the coordinated pursuit, driving, trapping, or ambush of animals by multiple individuals acting in assigned or emergent roles. Unlike solitary hunting where one individual stalks one animal, cooperative hunting exploits group size to control prey movement, block escape routes, exhaust animals through relay pursuit, or drive herds into natural traps (cliffs, bogs, water). No individual early hominin could outrun, outfight, or overpower most savanna ungulates. Cooperative hunting solved this problem through strategy rather than individual capability. Chimpanzees at Tai Forest (Ivory Coast) hunt colobus monkeys cooperatively, with specific individuals consistently taking driver, blocker, chaser, and ambusher roles, achieving success rates of 78% in coordinated hunts versus 33% for solo attempts (Boesch & Boesch, 1989). Early Homo likely inherited and expanded this cooperative capacity.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

At Schoningen (Germany, 300,000 BP), the discovery of 10 wooden javelin-like spears among the remains of at least 20 horses at a lakeside site strongly implies organized group hunting, as driving a horse herd into a bog for slaughter requires coordinated positioning of multiple hunters (Thieme, 1997). The site at Ambrona (Spain, 400,000 BP) preserves elephant remains in association with stone tools and evidence of fire use, suggesting that Homo heidelbergensis drove large game using fire. At Olduvai Gorge (Tanzania, 1.8 Ma), concentrations of butchered wildebeest and zebra bones with stone tools at FLK North indicate repeated exploitation of the same killing ground, likely a natural bottleneck used for ambush. Among the Mbuti pygmies of the Congo, communal net hunting documents a living analogue: the entire band (30-60 people) participates, with beaters driving duiker antelope into nets held by other hunters, achieving daily protein yields that solo hunting cannot match (Turnbull, 1965).

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->

**Hunting strategies by group size:**

| Group size | Strategy | Best for | Success rate |
|------------|----------|----------|--------------|
| 2-3 | Ambush at water/trail | Solitary prey (deer, antelope) | 30-50% |
| 4-6 | Driving/flushing | Small herds into terrain traps | 40-65% |
| 6-10 | Relay pursuit | Large animals over long distance | 50-70% |
| 10+ | Surround and drive | Herds into natural traps (cliff, bog) | 60-80% |

**Ambush hunting (2-3 hunters):**

1. Identify a game trail or water source with regular animal traffic. Observe for 2-3 days to confirm timing patterns.
2. Select positions downwind, concealed by vegetation or terrain features, within 10-15 m of the kill zone.
3. Hunters remain motionless until prey enters the kill zone. Lead hunter signals with a predetermined gesture.
4. Attack simultaneously. One hunter targets the animal's movement (blocking escape), the other targets the animal directly.
5. If the first strike does not kill, pursue as a pair. An injured animal tires faster but remains dangerous.

**Drive hunting (4-6 hunters):**

1. Scout terrain for natural funnels: narrow valleys, cliff edges, river bends, dense thicket walls.
2. Position 2 hunters at the funnel's narrow end (the kill zone). These are blockers.
3. Remaining hunters form a wide arc 200-500 m from the kill zone, upwind of the prey.
4. Drivers advance slowly, making noise (shouting, banging rocks, waving arms) to push prey toward the funnel.
5. As prey enters the funnel, drivers close the arc, preventing retreat. Blockers attack when prey reaches the narrow point.

**Relay pursuit (persistence hunting, 4+ runners):**

1. Select a target animal from a herd. Track it at jogging pace (8-10 km/h). The animal sprints ahead but cannot maintain sprint speed.
2. First runner maintains visual contact and tracks at sustainable pace for 20-40 minutes.
3. When the first runner tires, the second runner takes over. The first rests and then rejoins at the end of the relay.
4. Continue rotation. The prey cannot cool down between sprint bursts because each new runner forces another burst.
5. After 2-4 hours and 15-25 km, the animal collapses from hyperthermia. Humans thermoregulate through sweating; most prey cannot.

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Large game access:** Animals weighing 100-500 kg (wildebeest, zebra, deer) provide 50,000-200,000 calories per kill. This feeds a group of 20 for 3-10 days and justifies the cooperative energy investment.
- **Protein reliability:** Solo hunting success rates average 15-25%. Cooperative hunting raises this to 40-80% depending on strategy and group coordination. Higher reliability means fewer hungry days.
- **Predator defence application:** The coordination skills used in group hunting transfer directly to group defence against predators. A band that can drive prey can also drive a lion away from a carcass.
- **Social bonding:** Shared hunts create intense cooperative bonds. Meat sharing after a successful hunt reinforces reciprocal relationships and political alliances within the group.
- **Territory control:** Groups capable of large-scale drives can exploit game resources that solitary hunters cannot, providing a competitive advantage in inter-group resource competition.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

You are slower than everything you want to eat. You are weaker than most of it. Alone, your hunting success rate is somewhere around 20%. With three other people and a plan, you can push that past 60%. Cooperation is not a nice idea. It is the only way you eat meat consistently.

Start with ambush. Find a water source where animals drink. Watch it for two days. Note what comes, when, and from which direction. Then position two people downwind, hidden in cover within throwing range of the water's edge. A third person stays further back as a blocker on the most likely escape path. When an animal is drinking, head down, it is vulnerable. Attack together. One throws or thrusts, the other cuts off retreat.

For larger groups, drive hunting is your most reliable strategy. Find a natural chokepoint: a narrow gap between rocks, a cliff edge, a river bend with steep banks, a dense thicket with only one opening. Put your two best fighters at the narrow end. Everyone else forms a wide arc on the far side, 200 to 500 metres away from the kill zone, upwind. Walk forward slowly. Make noise. Bang rocks together. Shout. Wave your arms. The animals will move away from you and toward the chokepoint. As they enter the narrow zone, your blockers attack. The drivers close in behind to prevent retreat.

If you have strong runners, persistence hunting works in open terrain during heat. Pick one animal from a herd. Chase it at jogging pace. It will sprint away but cannot sustain that speed. Follow its tracks. When you see it again, it will run again but slower. Rotate runners. After two to four hours, the animal collapses from overheating because it cannot sweat like you can. Walk up and finish it.

After every hunt, share the meat across the entire group, including those who stayed at camp. The nursing mother who tended the fire and watched the children made the hunt possible by freeing you to go. She eats. Every person who eats is a person who can work tomorrow. That is the logic. Never hoard.

Practice communication before the hunt. Agree on hand signals: stop, advance, go left, go right, attack now. Words carry on the wind and alert prey. Gestures do not. Two minutes of signal planning before you leave camp saves the entire hunt from falling apart when silence matters.
