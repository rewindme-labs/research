# Group Defense Against Carnivores
<!-- @field: description -->
The African savanna of the Pliocene and early Pleistocene was home to a predator guild more dangerous and diverse than any environment on Earth today. Early hominins shared their landscape with lions (*Panthera leo*), leopards (*Panthera pardus*), cheetahs (*Acinonyx jubatus*), African wild dogs (*Lycaon pictus*), spotted hyenas (*Crocuta crocuta*), the giant short-faced hyena (*Pachycrocuta brevirostris*, extinct), and saber-toothed cats including *Homotherium* and *Dinofelis*. Against this guild, a small-bodied, bipedal, relatively slow hominin was prey.

Group defense against carnivores is a suite of behavioral responses — not a single action — calibrated to the specific predator, the group's composition and size, the terrain, and whether the threat is imminent or preventable. The key insight is that most large carnivores, including lions, are risk-averse: they hunt because they need to eat, not because they want to fight. A coordinated group that raises the cost of attack above the predator's expected caloric return will usually deter the attack.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
Fossil evidence documents the predation pressure early hominins faced directly. The skull of the Taung Child (*Australopithecus africanus*, ~2.8 Ma) shows puncture wounds consistent with large eagle talons, identified by Lee Berger and Ron Clarke (1995). *Paranthropus robustus* specimens from Swartkrans show leopard canine punctures matching the spacing of the hominin skull vault. C.K. Brain's taphonomic analysis (*The Hunters or the Hunted?*, 1981) concluded that the Swartkrans cave accumulation represented primarily leopard kills carried to a tree above the cave entrance — hominins were a significant component of the leopard's diet.

Chimpanzee defense behaviors provide the closest behavioral analog. Jane Goodall documented at Gombe (1960s) that chimpanzees respond to leopard presence with loud display charging, branch breaking, and collective mobbing, typically driving the leopard away. The key variable was group size: lone or small-group chimpanzees fled; groups of 8+ individuals mobbed successfully.

Crucially, the emergence of controlled fire (~400,000-1 Ma, with earlier sporadic use) appears to have shifted the predator-hominin relationship dramatically. Modern studies of campfire effects on lion behavior show that lions avoid illuminated areas and that fire-maintained camps have orders-of-magnitude lower predation risk than fireless camps.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Decision framework — immediate threat:**

| Situation | Response |
|-----------|---------|
| Single predator, group 8+, daylight, open ground | **Mob** — advance together, throw, vocalize (see: *Predator Mobbing*) |
| Multiple predators or lions | **Retreat to trees** — do not mob packs or prides |
| Predator at night, no fire | **Remain in trees** — do not descend |
| Predator at night, fire present | **Feed the fire** — add wood, increase flame height, do not leave fire circle |
| Predator has seized a group member | **Immediate mob with maximum aggression** — charge with all available weapons |
| Predator approaching camp | **Stand, display, shout, throw** — before it reaches 20m |

**Camp defense at night:**

1. Place camp in the open, away from dense vegetation and water (leopard ambush terrain)
2. Sleep in a circular arrangement with fire at center, or at minimum one fire at the approach side
3. Keep a fuel supply (dry wood) within arm's reach of the sleeping area
4. One individual maintains light sleep — they respond to any sound from outside the group
5. If fire dims, add wood immediately — do not allow it to go to coals at night

**Active defense weapons:**

| Weapon | Range | Effectiveness | Preparation |
|--------|-------|--------------|-------------|
| Thrown rock (fist-sized) | 15-25 m | Moderate — causes pain, may injure | Available everywhere |
| Thrown stick (1-1.5 m) | 10-15 m | Low impact, high startling effect | Break from any shrub |
| Spear or fire-hardened point | 5-8 m (thrust), 15 m (thrown) | High if accurate | Requires preparation |
| Burning brand from fire | 2-5 m | Very high — fire fear in most mammals | Only if fire is lit |
| Loud coordinated vocalizations | — | High psychological deterrent | No preparation |

**Leopard-specific defense:**

Leopards are solitary ambush hunters. They select lone or isolated individuals and attack from cover at close range. Defense priorities:
1. Never allow any group member to be isolated, especially at night
2. When movement is necessary at night, move as a group with noise
3. Leopards avoid confrontation with alert groups — noise and activity are the primary deterrent

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Resource protection:** Beyond personal safety, defending carcasses and food caches from hyena clans and lion prides is an economic necessity. Groups that cannot hold resources against carnivore competition lose a significant fraction of their food acquisition to displacement.
- **Territorial exclusion:** Repeatedly defending a core area against predators — mobbing lions, maintaining fires at sleeping sites — gradually teaches local predators that this group's territory carries high cost, reducing long-term predation pressure.
- **Confidence in movement:** Groups with established defense protocols can move earlier in the morning, travel more boldly across open ground, and work near waterholes with greater security — expanding the effective range and productivity of foraging.

## Day 0 — Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0 -->
A lion is 50 meters away, watching your camp. There are 8 of you.

Do not run. Running converts you from a group into individual prey items that can be separated and chased.

Stand up together. All of you. Facing the lion. Make noise — all of you, at the same time. Pick up rocks.

If you have fire, add wood now. The flame height matters. A high fire is visible from 500 meters and tells every predator in the area that this camp is defended.

If the lion moves toward you, throw rocks. All of you throwing simultaneously is more effective than one person throwing accurately. The goal is to make the calculation unfavorable: attacking this group costs more than it returns.

If you have a spear or fire-hardened stake, the person with it stands at the front. Not because one person fights a lion — but because a pointed object directed at a predator's face changes the risk assessment.

Watch the lion's ears. When they flatten and it turns sideways, it is about to leave.

When it leaves, do not pursue. Let it go.
