# Footprint Tracking

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

Footprint tracking is the ability to read marks left on the ground by animals, humans, and environmental forces to extract information about species, direction, speed, recency, weight, gait, and intention. It is arguably the oldest scientific discipline: gathering observational data, forming hypotheses about the track-maker's behaviour, predicting future movement, and testing those predictions by following the trail. Louis Liebenberg (1990) argued that tracking represents the origin of scientific reasoning itself. Every surviving hunter-gatherer society practices tracking, and ethnographic evidence shows San Bushmen of the Kalahari identifying individual animals by their footprints with the same reliability that Western scientists identify fingerprints, achieving correct identification rates above 90% for known individuals (Liebenberg, 2013).

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

The Laetoli footprints (Tanzania, 3.66 Ma), discovered by Mary Leakey's team in 1978, preserve the tracks of two or three Australopithecus afarensis individuals walking through volcanic ash. These prints demonstrate upright bipedal gait with a modern foot structure, but more significantly, the preservation itself tells us about substrate reading: these hominins walked on damp ash that recorded their passage perfectly, the same type of substrate that preserves animal tracks today. At Ileret (Kenya, 1.5 Ma), preserved Homo erectus footprints described by Matthew Bennett (2009) show a modern stride pattern and group movement, with multiple individuals walking in the same direction. The San Bushmen tracking tradition, documented extensively by Liebenberg, distinguishes four levels of tracking skill: simple tracking (following clear prints), systematic tracking (interpreting partial signs), speculative tracking (predicting animal behaviour from minimal clues), and creative tracking (reconstructing events from scattered evidence).

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->

**Reading a single footprint:**

| Feature | What it tells you |
|---------|-------------------|
| Shape and toe arrangement | Species identification |
| Depth | Weight of animal, speed (deeper = heavier or running) |
| Sharpness of edges | Recency (sharp = fresh, crumbled = old) |
| Moisture in print | Very fresh (dew/rain not yet dried) |
| Debris in print | Old (leaves, dust, insect trails have accumulated) |
| Claw marks (carnivore) | Predator type and direction |
| Stride length | Speed and gait (walking, trotting, running, galloping) |
| Track width relative to body | Gait type (narrow = trotting, wide = walking or injured) |

**Aging a track (how old is it?):**

1. Press your own thumb next to the track with equal force. Compare edge definition. If your print is crisper, the track is older.
2. Check for spider webs or insect trails crossing the print. Takes 4-12 hours for spiders to cross a fresh depression.
3. Check shadow inside the print. In morning light, a fresh print casts a sharp shadow from its clean edges. Old prints have rounded edges and diffuse shadows.
4. After rain: tracks made before rain have splatter marks and softened edges. Tracks after rain are crisp on wet ground.
5. After wind: tracks older than the last wind event have windblown dust or sand partially filling them.

**Following a trail:**

1. Identify the track. Species, direction, likely destination (water, grazing, cover).
2. Stand at the last clear print. Look ahead along the direction of travel. Scan the ground 5-10 m ahead for the next disturbance.
3. If you lose the trail, stop. Go back to the last confirmed print. Look in an arc, checking for directional changes.
4. On hard ground where prints do not register: look for scuffed stones (displaced pebbles show lighter undersides), bent grass (stems slowly straighten over 2-8 hours), broken twigs at ankle height, and dung.
5. Tracking speed: on soft ground, a skilled tracker moves at 3-4 km/h. On hard ground, speed drops to 0.5-1 km/h.

**Common track identification (African savanna):**

| Animal | Track characteristics |
|--------|----------------------|
| Ungulate (antelope) | Two-toed cloven hoof, pointed forward, 4-8 cm |
| Large cat (leopard/lion) | Round pad, four toes, no claw marks (retractable) |
| Hyena | Four toes with claw marks, slightly asymmetric, 7-9 cm |
| Baboon | Hand-like, five digits, often with drag marks from long toes |
| Snake | Sinuous track or side-winding pattern, 2-5 cm wide |
| Human | Heel-ball-toe impression, 20-30 cm long |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Hunting:** Following game trails to intercept animals at water sources, feeding grounds, or resting sites. Tracking enables pursuit hunting where direct speed advantage is absent.
- **Predator awareness:** Identifying fresh predator tracks near camp triggers relocation or increased vigilance. A fresh lion print on the path to water changes the water collection plan.
- **Water finding:** Converging animal trails typically lead to water. Multiple species heading the same direction from different origins is a strong water indicator.
- **Group security:** Detecting human footprints from unfamiliar groups provides early warning of potential territorial conflict.
- **Seasonal migration:** Tracking herd movements across seasons builds the spatial knowledge required for long-range foraging planning.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

Every animal that walks through your territory leaves a message in the ground. Learn to read it. This is not optional, it is the difference between finding food and wandering aimlessly until your energy runs out.

Go to the nearest soft ground, a muddy patch near water, a sandy riverbank, a rain-dampened trail. Look down. You will see prints. Start with the obvious ones: large ungulate tracks look like two parallel teardrops, pointed forward. Cat prints are round with no claw marks. Dog and hyena prints show claw marks in front of the toe pads. Human prints are obvious.

Make your own reference print. Press your foot firmly into the mud next to an animal track. Now compare edge sharpness. If the animal print has crumbled, softened edges and yours is crisp, that track is old. If both edges look similar, the animal passed recently.

Now follow one trail. Stand at the clearest print. Look along the direction the toes point. Scan the ground ahead for the next disturbance. Walk to it. Repeat. If you lose the trail, stop moving. Go back to the last confirmed print and look in a wider arc. On hard ground, look for displaced pebbles with lighter undersides, bent grass, and broken twigs at ankle height.

Pay attention to what direction animal trails converge. If three different species are heading the same way from different starting points, they are heading to water. Follow them.

Check the ground around your camp every morning before you do anything else. Fresh predator prints within 50 metres mean something visited while you slept. That changes your day.

Cut a straight stick about as long as your arm. This is your tracking stick. When you find two consecutive prints from the same animal, lay the stick along them with one end at the heel of the first print and mark the stick at the toe of the second print. Now you know that animal's stride length. Use it to predict where the next print should be. This works even when some prints are invisible on hard ground.

Practice every day. Track everything that moves through your area. Within a week you will know which animals visit, when they come, which direction they travel, and where they are going.
