# Night Camp Safety & Sleep Security

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->
Night camp safety is the set of choices and behaviors that allow a human or group to sleep in a wild environment without being killed by predators, exposure, or environmental hazards before morning. It is the most urgent practical skill of Day 1 because sleep deprivation degrades decision-making within 24 hours and becomes life-threatening within 4-5 days. The average adult in a survival situation will attempt to stay awake indefinitely out of fear, accelerating their own physical and cognitive collapse.

The biological calculus is stark: without a constructed shelter, the primary threats during any given night in a temperate-to-tropical environment, ranked by frequency, are: (1) hypothermia from temperature drop and moisture, (2) opportunistic attack by medium predators (hyenas, leopards, wild dogs, crocodiles near water), (3) venomous arthropods and snakes seeking warmth, and (4) falls from elevated sleeping positions. The countermeasures for each are specific, learnable, and achievable on the first night with no tools.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
The oldest known hominid sleeping structure is a grass nest platform found at Sibudu Cave, South Africa, dating to approximately 77,000 years ago. The platform consisted of sedge and other grass species with aromatic insect-repellent properties, laid over a stone floor near the cave entrance. It represents intentional selection of bedding material for both insulation and arthropod control.

Earlier evidence comes from the behavioral repertoire of great apes. Chimpanzees and gorillas build fresh tree platforms (nests) each night, 10-25 meters above the ground, out of leafy branches. Analysis of 1,844 chimpanzee nests in Uganda (2011, American Journal of Primatology) found that 98.4% were built in trees, overwhelmingly in species with large straight trunks that are difficult to climb for leopards. This platform-building behavior is ancient, shared, and specifically anti-predator in function.

Medieval military field manuals (e.g., the Byzantine *Strategikon*, 6th century CE) prescribe specific camp layout rules: sentinels at fixed perimeter points in rotating shifts, fire maintenance through the night, no camp near dense vegetation that obscures approach, and picket lines of horses that react to disturbance before humans. These formalized protocols are elaborations of the same instincts observed in hunter-gatherer groups across the globe.

Among the !Kung San of the Kalahari (studied by Richard Lee, 1960s-70s), night camps are positioned to face the prevailing wind (predators approach downwind), fires are maintained through the night at the camp perimeter, and sleeping positions place the most vulnerable (children, ill) closest to the fire center. No formal sentry rotation exists, but light sleepers naturally wake to any unusual sound, a behavior documented as biologically preserved across all human populations.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Site selection - the non-negotiables:**

| Criterion | Why | Minimum |
|-----------|-----|---------|
| Distance from open water | Crocodiles, hippos, and predators hunting water-dependent prey | 200m from any lake or slow river |
| Elevation above flood line | Flash floods can rise 1m in minutes with no local rain | Sleep visibly above waterline |
| Wind direction | Sleep so that prevailing wind blows FROM your camp INTO the surrounding area (predators approach downwind of their prey) | Position camp with wind at your back |
| Clear sight lines | Predators use cover for approach | No dense brush within 30m of sleeping position |
| No dead trees overhead | Widow-makers (dead branches) fall with zero warning in any wind | Clear overhead space |
| Firm ground | Loose soil or leaf litter means you cannot hear approaching footsteps | Avoid deep leaf litter |

**Fire as the primary night defense:**

Fire is a more reliable predator deterrent than any other technique available without manufactured weapons. All large predators that pose danger to humans (lions, leopards, hyenas, bears) have deep ancestral fear of fire. The mechanism is primal: fire represents loss of territory and risk of death. A maintained fire at camp perimeter has been documented to completely suppress predator approach at tested campsites in the Serengeti and Kenya by the Wildlife Conservation Society.

- Build 2-3 fires arranged in a rough triangle around the sleeping area, not one central fire.
- A perimeter of fire forces any approaching predator to cross fire to reach the sleeping area. No large predator will do this voluntarily.
- Maintain fires with wood that burns slowly and produces coals: hardwoods (oak, mahogany, beech, eucalyptus). Softwoods burn fast and require constant attention.
- Each fire should be built as a top-down fire (largest wood at base, smaller on top, kindling at top lit last) for the longest unattended burn time.
- Gather 2x the wood you think you need. Fires require more than you expect through a full night.

**Elevated sleeping (without a shelter):**

When no shelter exists:
1. Find a large-trunked tree (60cm+ diameter) with low branches. Branches should be horizontal and at least 15cm diameter.
2. Climb to 4-6 meters minimum. Leopards can climb but are significantly disadvantaged above 5-6m for carrying prey. Lions and hyenas cannot climb.
3. Use cordage or a long strip of bark cloth to lash yourself loosely to the trunk or branch. You need to not fall during sleep - this is a real risk, particularly for children.
4. Do not sleep on a branch over water (crocodiles can grab from below if the branch is close to water surface).

**Arthropod security:**
- Shake out all clothing and footwear before putting on in the morning. Scorpions seek warmth inside shoes overnight.
- Do not sleep directly on the ground in tropical environments without a raised platform. Driver ants, scorpions, and snakes use ground-level warmth and scent.
- Aromatic plants placed around the sleeping area deter mosquitoes and some arthropods: eucalyptus, cedar, lemon grass, lavender, wormwood (*Artemisia absinthium*).
- Sleep with face up, not face-down in leaf litter (insects attracted to CO2 exhaled breath will find your face regardless; face-up lets you wake and respond).

**Watch rotation (in a group):**

| Group Size | Watch Protocol |
|------------|---------------|
| 2 people | 4 hours on, 4 hours off per person |
| 3-4 people | 3 hours on, 6-9 hours off |
| 5+ people | 2-hour watches with 1 person always awake; briefing at change |

The person on watch must stay seated upright at the fire. Lying down guarantees sleep within 20 minutes regardless of intent. Establish a pre-agreed alarm signal (specific call, whistle) before first watch begins.

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Night 1 alone:** Prioritize elevation and fire perimeter. Accept that sleep quality will be poor; goal is to survive to Day 2 with no injuries.
- **Night 1 in a group:** Establish watches immediately. The person who "doesn't feel tired" takes first watch. Establish fire perimeter. Designate a shelter-construction leader for Day 2.
- **Ongoing camp:** After Day 3-4, construct a raised sleeping platform (debris hut is warmer; elevated platform is safer from arthropods). The energy investment pays back in sleep quality immediately.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->
The sun is going down. You have 45 minutes of light left. Stop whatever else you are doing and do only this.

**In order:**

1. **Choose your site now, while you can see.** Find a large-trunked tree 200+ meters from any water. Clear the ground immediately around it of leaves and dry material (fire hazard). Look up: no dead branches. Look around: no dense brush within 30 meters. This is your camp.

2. **Gather wood. Twice as much as you think you need.** You need wood to last until dawn, 8-10 hours. Fill every arm's worth of carrying capacity. Prioritize dense, heavy logs - they burn all night. Light, dry sticks burn in minutes.

3. **Build three fires in a triangle**, each approximately 8-10 meters from your sleeping position and 8-10 meters from each other. Light them all before dark. Use the largest logs you have as the base of each fire. You are creating a perimeter, not a campfire.

4. **Select sleeping position.** If you are elevated in the tree (safest), use cordage to lash yourself loosely to the trunk. If on the ground, sleep inside the fire triangle, as close to the center as the heat allows. Place your back against the tree trunk - nothing can approach from directly behind you.

5. **If there are two of you:** One sleeps, one watches from the fire for 4-hour rotations. The watcher feeds the fires. The fires will tell you when something is approaching long before your ears will - predators circle fires, and their movement in the firelight is often visible 20-30 seconds before you can hear them.

6. **Before sleeping:** Shake out your shoes. Check inside them. Put them on, or place them inverted on a stick above the ground next to you. A scorpion sting at 3am is a bad way to start Day 2.

You will not sleep well on Day 1. Accept that. Your goal is not comfort; it is being alive at dawn, with your cognitive function intact enough to build something better tomorrow.
