# Waterhole Digging
<!-- @field: description -->
Digging a waterhole is the transition from passively finding water to actively creating a reliable water source. Rather than depending on natural surface pools that dry up, a group that can excavate a hole to the water table at a known location — a dry riverbed bend, a spring seep, a moist soil depression — converts a seasonal resource into a more permanent one. This is infrastructure investment: the initial effort of excavation is repaid by reliable water access for weeks to months.

Early evidence for intentional waterhole digging by hominins is difficult to distinguish archaeologically from natural features, but the behavior is documented in several modern animal species — most notably, African bush elephants dig waterholes in dry riverbeds during drought, and baboon troops use elephant-dug holes. Early hominins, with stone digging tools and group coordination, would have been capable of more deliberate and systematic excavation.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
Archaeological evidence for early hominin water management remains largely indirect — site distribution data showing occupation of areas without surface water during dry seasons suggests that groups could access sub-surface water through excavation or other means. The clustering of Oldowan sites along ancient drainage systems (Koobi Fora, Kenya; Olduvai Gorge, Tanzania; Turkana Basin) during inferred dry periods is consistent with groups maintaining access to excavated water sources in dry riverbeds.

Ethnographic documentation of hunter-gatherer waterhole digging is more direct. Richard Lee's fieldwork with the !Kung San of the Kalahari (1979) documented that San groups regularly excavate dry streambeds to access sub-surface water during drought, using sharpened digging sticks and cupped hands or hollow reeds for extraction. These excavations are sometimes maintained and returned to repeatedly across seasons.

The !Kung also describe "sip wells" — narrow excavations into damp sand too small for a hand, from which water is extracted by pushing a hollow reed to the base and drinking by mouth suction. This technique allows water access from holes that would not naturally pool enough for direct collection.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Site selection (critical — determines success or failure):**

| Feature | Signal | Priority |
|---------|--------|---------|
| Outer curve of dry sandy riverbed | Water last pooled here — most concentrated below surface | Highest |
| Green vegetation cluster in dry landscape | Water table near surface | High |
| Base of rocky hillside with dark soil staining | Seep or spring below | High |
| Center of dry clay pan | Water pooled here in wet season, but clay holds little | Low |
| Convergence of multiple drainage lines | Underground flow concentration | High |
| Spot where animals have been digging | Active water table confirmed | Highest |

**Excavation procedure:**

1. Clear the surface of the selected site, removing any dry surface crust
2. Begin digging with a digging stick (80-100 cm, fire-hardened tip) and hands, working in a circle 40-60 cm in diameter
3. In sandy substrate: push the stick in and work it in a circular motion to loosen sand, then scoop with cupped hands. Sand excavation is rapid.
4. In clay substrate: chip and lever with the stick tip. Clay is slow but holds water better once pooled.
5. As depth increases, have a second person remove excavated material from the hole
6. Every 20-30 cm, feel the deepest sand/soil — cool, darkened, clumping material signals approaching water table
7. At first appearance of water, stop digging. Allow the hole to stabilize and the water to accumulate.
8. Do not continue digging once water appears — this muddies the water and causes wall collapse.

**Extraction from narrow seep:**

1. Push a hollow plant stem (reed, bamboo segment) to the bottom of the pooled area
2. Seal your lips around the top and create suction — water will rise through the stem
3. Alternatively, use absorbent bark or a bundle of dry grass: push to the base, allow saturation, remove and squeeze into a container
4. Allow 20-30 minutes between extractions for the seep to refill

**Maintaining the waterhole:**

1. Cover the opening with flat stones or a bark slab to reduce evaporation and prevent animal fouling
2. Remove the cover only during extraction
3. Re-dig when walls collapse (after rain, or from animal disturbance)
4. A maintained hole will remain productive for weeks in the right substrate

| 2-3 people | Speed and efficiency | One digs, one removes spoil, one collects |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Dry season base camp enablement:** A camp that has a functioning excavated waterhole can remain in one location throughout the dry season rather than being forced to move to surface water. This stability reduces travel energy cost and predation exposure.
- **Territory extension:** Groups that can create water access in otherwise waterless areas can occupy territories other groups cannot use — reducing competitive pressure at known permanent waterholes.
- **Predictable daily water access:** Rather than risking the predator gauntlet at a communal waterhole every day, a group with its own excavated water source can drink in safety at its own camp.
- **Group construction knowledge:** Digging a waterhole is one of the earliest documented forms of group-coordinated environmental modification — a behavioral threshold with implications for shelter construction, trap construction, and eventually agriculture.

## Day 0 — Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0 -->
The surface water is gone. But you remember that this riverbed carried water two months ago.

Go to the outer curve of the bend — where the water was slowest and deepest. Find the lowest point. The sand here will hold water in the pore spaces, sometimes meters below the surface.

Start digging. Digging stick to loosen, hands to scoop. Work in a circle 50 cm across. Dig straight down, not outward.

At 40 cm, feel the sand. Is it cool? Does it clump? You are close.

At 60-80 cm, you will likely see dark, wet sand. Slow down. When water seeps in, stop. Do not dig more.

Wait 20 minutes. A small pool will form. Cut a hollow reed — push it to the bottom, drink through it. It is cool and clean.

When everyone has drunk, cover the hole with a flat stone. It will be waiting tomorrow.

You just made a well.
