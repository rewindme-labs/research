# Endurance Movement

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

Endurance movement is the uniquely hominin capacity for sustained bipedal locomotion over long distances across open terrain. Humans are the best endurance runners in the animal kingdom: capable of covering 20-40 km in a day at sustained pace, outlasting horses at distances beyond 35 km (as demonstrated annually at the Man vs. Horse Marathon in Llanwrtyd Wells, Wales), and pursuing prey to hyperthermic collapse through persistence hunting. This capacity derives from three integrated adaptations: bipedal gait (50% more energy-efficient than quadrupedal at walking speed), eccrine sweat glands (2-4 million across the body providing evaporative cooling), and elastic tendons in the legs (Achilles tendon stores and releases energy like a spring). Denis Bramble and Daniel Lieberman (2004) argued that endurance running specifically, not just walking, was a critical adaptation for scavenging and persistence hunting in open savanna environments.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

The Laetoli footprints (Tanzania, 3.66 Ma) confirm fully bipedal gait in Australopithecus afarensis, but the body proportions of Homo erectus (1.9 Ma) show the first truly modern long-distance locomotion anatomy: long legs, short arms, narrow waist, enlarged gluteus maximus, and a nuchal ligament in the neck for head stabilisation during running (Bramble & Lieberman, 2004). Homo erectus sites span 12,000+ km from Africa to Java, requiring sustained long-distance movement across diverse terrain over thousands of generations. Among the Tarahumara (Raramuri) of Mexico, documented by Dennis Bramble, individuals routinely run 80-160 km in single sessions during traditional ball games that persist for days. San Bushmen persistence hunts documented by Louis Liebenberg track kudu antelope for 25-35 km over 2-4 hours in midday heat until the animal collapses from hyperthermia.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->

**Sustainable pace management:**

| Activity | Speed | Duration sustainable | Caloric cost |
|----------|-------|---------------------|-------------|
| Walking (flat terrain) | 4-5 km/h | 8-12 hours | 200-300 kcal/hour |
| Fast walking (purpose) | 6-7 km/h | 4-6 hours | 300-400 kcal/hour |
| Jogging (pursuit) | 8-10 km/h | 1-3 hours | 500-700 kcal/hour |
| Running (sprint) | 15-25 km/h | 1-5 minutes | 800-1200 kcal/hour |

**Maximising daily travel distance:**

1. Walk at a steady 4-5 km/h. This is the most efficient speed for human bipedal gait, requiring the least energy per kilometre.
2. Rest for 10 minutes every 60-90 minutes. Studies show brief rests maintain pace over long distances better than pushing through.
3. Drink water at every rest stop. Dehydration reduces endurance by 30-50% before you feel significantly thirsty.
4. Eat small, frequent amounts rather than large meals. Digestion diverts blood from muscles and causes cramping.
5. Travel during coolest hours: dawn to 10 AM and 4 PM to dusk. Rest in shade during midday heat.
6. Walk on firm ground when possible. Sand, mud, and dense vegetation increase energy expenditure by 30-80%.

**Thermoregulation during sustained movement:**

1. Sweat is your cooling system. Do not try to prevent sweating. But replace fluids constantly: 250-500 ml per hour in moderate heat, up to 1 litre per hour in extreme heat.
2. Wet your head, neck, and wrists with water at every opportunity. These areas have high blood flow near the surface, and evaporative cooling here is most effective.
3. Carry water. The distance you can travel is limited by the water you carry. 2 litres enables roughly 4 hours of hot-weather walking.
4. Cover your head. A leaf hat, bark strip, or any shade-producing head covering reduces solar heat load by 40-60%.

**Foot care (critical for sustained movement):**

1. Check feet at every rest stop. Remove debris from between toes.
2. If shoes/wrappings are available, ensure no folds or stones create pressure points. Blisters begin with friction, not distance.
3. Toughen feet by walking barefoot on varied surfaces during the first weeks. Callus development takes 2-3 weeks.
4. In sandy terrain, wrap feet in leather or bark to prevent fine sand abrasion.
5. Clean and dry feet every evening. Damp feet develop fungal infections that can become disabling.

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Persistence hunting:** Running a prey animal to hyperthermic collapse over 15-35 km at jogging pace is the original human hunting strategy. No weapons required beyond endurance and tracking ability.
- **Territory exploitation:** A group that walks 8-15 km per day exploits 200+ km2 of territory. Larger effective range means more resource access and greater resilience to local shortgage.
- **Migration:** Seasonal movement between habitats (dry-season riverine to wet-season highland) requires multi-day travel of 50-300 km. Endurance capacity determines whether this is achievable.
- **Scavenging radius:** Reaching a vulture-spotted carcass 5 km away within an hour (fast walk) before hyenas arrive requires exactly the endurance capacity humans possess.
- **Emergency escape:** Moving the entire group 20-30 km in a day to escape fire, flood, hostile groups, or resource failure is possible because of endurance locomotion.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

You are built to walk. Not sprint, not sit, but sustain movement across distance for hours. This is the one physical ability where humans beat every other animal on the planet. Use it.

Walk at a pace where you can breathe through your nose and hold a conversation. That is your sustainable pace, roughly 4-5 km per hour. At this speed you can cover 30 km in a day with rest breaks and arrive with energy remaining.

Stop every 60-90 minutes. Sit for 10 minutes. Drink water. Check your feet. These short stops seem wasteful but they prevent the accumulated fatigue that forces long recovery stops later.

Do not push through midday heat unless you must. From 10 AM to 4 PM in hot climates, the caloric cost of thermoregulation nearly doubles your energy expenditure. Rest in shade during these hours and travel in the morning and late afternoon.

Water determines your range. Without water, you are limited to 3-4 hours of hot-weather walking before impairment. With 2 litres carried, you extend to 6-8 hours. Plan every long movement around water access: where will you refill along the way?

Cover your head. A improvised hat from large leaves or a bark strip draped over your head reduces solar heat absorption by half. Your head is 7% of your body surface but receives direct sun perpendicular to the rays. Shade it.

Your feet are your survival equipment. Inspect them at every stop. Remove any stone, thorn, or debris immediately. A blister today becomes an open wound tomorrow becomes an infection that stops you walking by day three. Prevention is absolute.

If you need to run, you can sustain a jogging pace of 8-10 km/h for 1-3 hours. This is persistence hunting speed. The animal you chase sprints at 40-60 km/h but cannot sustain it for more than 2-3 minutes. Then it overheats. You do not, because you sweat and it does not. Keep jogging. Swap runners in relay if you have partners. The animal collapses in 2-4 hours. This worked for two million years. It works today.
