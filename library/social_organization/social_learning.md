# Social Learning

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->
Individual learning is expensive. You touch the hot coal and learn not to touch hot coals. You eat the bitter root and learn that this species causes diarrhea. You survive both lessons, but only because neither was lethal. The forager who has to learn every plant, every predator behavior, every knapping technique, every water source through personal trial and error will die long before completing the curriculum. The knowledge base required to survive on the African savanna as a hominin is larger than any single lifetime of experimentation can generate.

Social learning is the mechanism that solves this problem. Instead of discovering fire-making, lithic knapping, tuber identification, and animal tracking independently, each generation inherits a behavioral library accumulated by all previous generations and adds to it. The child watches the mother knap for three years before touching a hammerstone. The adolescent follows the experienced tracker for hundreds of hours before leading a hunt. The knowledge flows from skilled model to naive learner through observation, imitation, guided practice, and correction - without requiring the learner to pay the survival cost of every mistake.

The critical distinction is between emulation (copying the result) and imitation (copying the exact action sequence). A chimpanzee watching another crack a nut can figure out the general idea and achieve the same outcome through its own method. A human child watching a knapper will copy the precise grip, the exact angle of the wrist, the specific striking location on the platform - the full motor sequence, not just the goal. This capacity for high-fidelity behavioral copying is what enables cumulative culture: each generation transmits the technique with enough precision that the next generation can refine it rather than reinvent it.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
Robert Boyd and Peter Richerson's *Culture and the Evolutionary Process* (1985) provided the mathematical framework proving that social learning is evolutionarily favored when three conditions hold: the environment varies across space or time, the cost of individual learning is high (testing a toxic plant may kill you), and reliable models with relevant experience are available. In hominin ecology, all three conditions are permanently met.

Michael Tomasello (*The Cultural Origins of Human Cognition*, 1999) identified shared intentionality - the capacity to understand that another individual has goals, knowledge, and a perspective that can be learned from - as the cognitive foundation of high-fidelity human imitation. Human infants demonstrate shared intentionality by 12-18 months: they follow an adult's gaze to the target, understand the adult's intent, and reproduce the demonstrated action even when a simpler alternative exists. Chimpanzees tested under identical conditions reliably take the shortcut. They copy results. Human children copy methods. The difference is small in any single episode. Over generational time, it produces the gap between Oldowan flakes and Apollo 11.

Kim Sterelny's *The Evolved Apprentice* (2012) extended this framework to propose that hominin ecology includes structured apprentice learning as a selected behavioral system - not an incidental byproduct. The increasing complexity of lithic technology from Oldowan to Acheulean to Levallois requires multi-year, structured exposure to skilled models. A skilled Acheulean handaxe cannot be produced by a naive knapper who has never seen one made, regardless of raw material quality or individual intelligence. The technique is too complex. It must be transmitted.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Learning modes, ordered by fidelity:**

| Mode | What happens | Example | Fidelity |
|------|-------------|---------|----------|
| Stimulus enhancement | Learner's attention is drawn to an object or location by the model's activity | Juvenile notices mother digging at a specific spot. Goes to that spot later | Low |
| Emulation | Learner copies the outcome but uses own method | Juvenile sees cracked nut, figures out own technique to crack nuts | Medium |
| Imitation | Learner copies the exact action sequence of the model | Juvenile reproduces the precise grip, angle, and strike pattern observed | High |
| Teaching | Model modifies own behavior to facilitate learner's acquisition | Adult slows movements, repeats steps, corrects learner's errors | Highest |

**Maximizing acquisition as a learner:**

1. Position yourself to observe the full sequence, not just the final product. Watch the hands, not the flake. Watch the angle of the wrist, the position of the core on the thigh, the preparation of the platform before each strike
2. Observe multiple complete repetitions before attempting the task. Motor sequences encode more reliably after 5-10 observations than after 1-2
3. Attempt the task immediately after observation, while the motor memory is still active. Delay degrades fidelity
4. When your attempt fails, present the failure to the model. Skilled models recognize errors visually and can correct with a gesture or a brief demonstration faster than with verbal explanation
5. Practice in the same physical context as the demonstration: same material, same body position, same tools. Context-dependent encoding is stronger than abstract encoding

**Structured skill transmission (model/teacher side):**

1. Slow the critical steps. Perform them at half speed during the initial demonstration. Speed conveys competence; slowness conveys teachability
2. Repeat the full sequence 3-5 times before allowing the learner to attempt. Repetition is not redundant - each observation encodes different aspects of the motor pattern
3. Decompose complex skills into sub-steps. Let the learner master platform preparation before attempting the full strike sequence
4. Allow errors. Correct after the error, not before. The learner who feels the hinge fracture under their own hand remembers the sensation longer than the learner who is warned about it in advance
5. Demonstrate in functional context. Knap where stones are actually used. Butcher at a real carcass. The environmental cues (smell, texture, urgency) bind to the motor memory

**Developmental windows:**

| Age range | Optimal learning domains |
|-----------|------------------------|
| 0-3 years | Vocalization, social norms, face reading, basic food identification |
| 4-8 years | Fine motor skills: fire tending, simple cordage, knapping basics, plant sorting |
| 8-14 years | Complex integrated skills: tracking, plant pharmacology, full knapping sequences, butchering |
| 14-18 years | Mastery under pressure: leading hunts, independent foraging, teaching younger learners |

## Materials
<!-- @field: materials -->

| Resource | Purpose | Notes |
|----------|---------|-------|
| Skilled model | Demonstration source | Must have genuine competence, not merely authority. Learners distinguish competent models from incompetent ones by age 4 |
| Practice materials (identical to production materials) | Learner attempts | Using inferior substitute materials produces inferior motor habits. Practice with the same stone, the same wood, the same fibers |
| Extended time in proximity | Repeated exposure | Skills like Acheulean knapping require an estimated 300-500 hours of observation combined with practice |
| Low-threat environment | Error tolerance | Learning is suppressed under acute survival stress. Predation pressure, starvation, and social conflict all degrade learning rate |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Cumulative technology:** The Acheulean handaxe (~1.76 Ma) is too complex to discover independently. It requires a specific reduction sequence, bifacial thinning, and platform management that take years to master even with a skilled teacher. Without social learning, this technology cannot persist across generations. Lose the last knapper, lose the technology
- **Hazard avoidance without personal cost:** Identifying which plants cause liver failure, which snakes are venomous, which terrain features indicate flash-flood risk, and which predator vocalizations demand immediate flight - these are acquired primarily through observation and instruction, not experimentation. The learner who tests toxic plants personally has a short career
- **Dietary range expansion:** The total catalog of edible resources known to any forager group - 200-400 plant species, dozens of animal species, seasonal timing for each, processing requirements to remove toxins - far exceeds what any individual could discover through personal trial. This knowledge base exists only because it is transmitted socially across generations
- **Group-level competitive advantage:** Groups with more efficient social learning systems accumulate more knowledge, exploit more resources, and adapt to new environments faster. When two groups compete for the same territory, the group with better cultural transmission wins - not because its members are individually smarter, but because they have access to a larger library

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->
You do not know how to knap. The woman sitting 3 meters from you does. She has been knapping every morning for 30 years. She does not speak your language. She does not need to.

Sit down beside her. Not across from her - beside her, on the same side, so you see her hands from the same angle she sees them. Watch her select the core. Watch how she turns it, looking for the platform. Watch her left hand position the core on her thigh. Watch the angle of her right wrist before the strike. Watch where the hammerstone contacts the platform - not on the edge, behind the edge.

Watch the flake come off. Watch her inspect it. Watch her rotate the core and find the next platform.

Do not ask to try yet. Watch five more cycles. Then ten more. Your brain is encoding the motor pattern. Every repetition strengthens it.

After 20 minutes, pick up a core and a hammerstone. Do not try to make a handaxe. Try to remove one flake. Copy what you saw: the grip, the angle, the strike location. Your first attempt will probably produce a step fracture - a flat, stubbed termination. That means your platform angle was too steep. Rotate the core and try again.

Show the failed flake to the woman. She will glance at it, take your core, rotate it to a better platform, and hand it back. That two-second interaction just taught you more about platform geometry than an hour of solo experimentation.

By the end of the morning you will have produced 3-4 usable flakes from 30 attempts. By the end of the week, 10 from 20. By the end of the month, you will be producing functional cutting edges on demand.

You did not discover knapping. You inherited it. The woman inherited it from someone who inherited it from someone who inherited it across 100,000 generations of knappers. You are now part of that chain.

Do not break it. When a child sits beside you next year, slow your hands down and let them watch.
