# Social Learning
<!-- @field: description -->
Social learning is the acquisition of information, skills, or behaviors from other individuals through observation, imitation, or direct teaching — rather than through independent trial and error. It is the mechanism by which culture accumulates: a group does not have to rediscover fire-making, lithic knapping technique, plant toxin identification, or animal behavior knowledge in each generation. Instead, each generation inherits a behavioral library built by all previous generations and adds to it.

The distinction between asocial learning (individual trial and error) and social learning (learning from others) has profound consequences for the rate of knowledge accumulation. A single individual working alone can discover, over a lifetime, perhaps a few new techniques or relationships. A group that pools discoveries through social learning accumulates knowledge at a rate proportional to group size — and once accumulated, that knowledge persists.

The human capacity for high-fidelity imitation — copying not just the outcome but the exact sequence of actions a model uses to achieve it — distinguishes human social learning from that of other great apes. Chimpanzees are capable of emulation (copying outcomes) but rarely achieve faithful action copying. This difference, subtle in infancy, compounds into an enormous cultural gap over generational time.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
The theoretical framework for social learning as an evolutionary force was developed by Luigi Luca Cavalli-Sforza and Marcus Feldman (*Cultural Transmission and Evolution*, 1981) and by Robert Boyd and Peter Richerson (*Culture and the Evolutionary Process*, 1985). Boyd and Richerson demonstrated mathematically that social learning is evolutionarily favored when environments are variable, when the cost of individual learning is high (death from testing a toxic plant), and when a reliable model with relevant experience is available.

Michael Tomasello (*The Cultural Origins of Human Cognition*, 1999) identified "shared intentionality" — the capacity to understand that another agent has intentions, goals, and knowledge that can be learned from — as the cognitive underpinning of high-fidelity human imitation. This shared intentionality is present in human infants by 12-18 months and is largely absent in other great apes, explaining the human-ape gap in cumulative cultural complexity.

Kim Sterelny (*The Evolved Apprentice*, 2012) extended this framework to propose that the hominin niche includes "apprentice learning" — structured multi-year exposure of juveniles to skilled adult models — as a behavioral system that coevolved with the increasing complexity of lithic technology and hunting technique.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Learning modes (from passive to active):**

| Mode | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| Observation | Watching a model perform without direct participation | Child watching mother knap a stone |
| Emulation | Copying the result without copying the exact method | Young hominid achieves similar outcome by own method |
| Imitation | Copying the exact action sequence of the model | Young hominid replicates each precise flaking stroke |
| Teaching | Model adjusts behavior to facilitate learner's acquisition | Adult slows movements, repeats steps, provides feedback |

**Maximizing social learning as a learner:**

1. Position yourself to watch the entire sequence, not just the result
2. Attend to hand position, force, angle — not just the final product
3. Attempt the task immediately after observation while the motor sequence is fresh
4. Seek feedback from the model — present your attempt, watch for correction
5. Repeat in the same environmental context: the tool, the material, the body position all matter

**Maximizing social learning as a model (teacher):**

1. Slow key steps — perform them at half speed during the initial demonstration
2. Repeat the sequence multiple times before the learner attempts it
3. Scaffold difficulty: let the learner practice easier sub-steps before the full sequence
4. Allow errors — correct after the error, not before (learners remember their own mistakes)
5. Demonstrate in real functional context, not abstract — knap where stones are actually used

**Critical period windows:**

| Age range | Optimal learning domain |
|-----------|------------------------|
| 0-3 years | Language, social norms, face reading |
| 4-8 years | Fine motor skills: fire-starting, cordage twisting, knapping basics |
| 8-14 years | Complex hunting strategy, plant identification, tool fabrication |
| 14-18 years | Full mastery integration: applying skills under real pressure |

| Resource | Purpose | Notes |

| Skilled model | Demonstration | Must have actual competence, not just authority |
| Practice materials | Learner attempts | Identical or similar to production materials |
| Time in proximity | Repeated exposure | Skills like knapping require hundreds of hours of observation + practice |
| Low-stakes environment | Error tolerance | Learning is suppressed under high predation or starvation stress |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Compound skill acquisition:** Skills like Acheulean hand axe production (refined by ~1.76 Ma) are too complex to discover independently in a single generation. They are learned from experienced knappers over years of observation and practice. Without social learning, this technology cannot persist.
- **Hazard avoidance:** Identifying which plants, animals, and terrain features are dangerous is learned primarily through observation of group members' reactions, stories of past events, and explicit guidance from experienced adults — not through independent experimentation (which may be lethal).
- **Range of diet:** Knowledge of which roots are edible in which season, how to process bitter tubers to reduce toxins, and how to locate seasonal water sources is accumulated group knowledge that must be transmitted socially. An individual discovering this from scratch would take a lifetime.
- **Cultural accumulation:** The gradual improvement in tool quality, shelter construction, and food processing strategies documented archaeologically over millions of years is possible only because social learning passes knowledge across generations, allowing each generation to build on what came before.

## Day 0 — Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0 -->
The child is 6 years old and watches you every morning as you knap.

You do not narrate. You do not explain. You simply knap, slowly enough that the sequence is visible: hold, strike, angle of blow, position of the core. The child is learning something you could not teach with words — the feel of the stone, the sound of a good flake, the sight of a platform that will work.

At 8 years, you let them hold the hammerstone.

At 10, their flakes are usable. At 14, their core management is correct. At 16, they can make a handaxe.

You have transmitted something that took 10,000 generations to develop. It took you 10 years to pass on.

The child does not know they are learning a skill refined by 1.7 million years of accumulated practice. They just know it is morning, and this is what you do, and they want to do it too.

That desire — to do what the skilled adult does — is the oldest learning mechanism there is.
