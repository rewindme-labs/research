# Alloparenting
<!-- @field: description -->
Alloparenting is the care of offspring by individuals other than the biological parents — grandparents, older siblings, aunts, uncles, and unrelated group members. In humans, alloparenting is unusually extensive compared to other primates: human children receive care from a far wider set of individuals than any other great ape, and this extended care network has profound consequences for reproduction, survival, and foraging capacity.

The core biological significance of alloparenting is that it enables human mothers to wean infants earlier and reproduce again sooner than would be possible if she were the sole caregiver. A chimpanzee female has an inter-birth interval of ~5-6 years; human forager women average ~3 years. This compression of the reproductive interval, made possible by the offloading of infant care to alloparents, roughly doubles the reproductive rate relative to our nearest relatives.

For the survival-focused group, alloparenting also means that foraging adults can range further, for longer, and return more food — because they are not constrained by carrying and monitoring a dependent infant every hour of every day.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
Sarah Blaffer Hrdy's synthesis in *Mothers and Others* (2009) provides the foundational anthropological treatment of alloparenting in human evolution. Hrdy argues that cooperative breeding — the extension of infant care beyond the mother-infant dyad — is the defining social behavioral innovation of the human lineage, driving cognitive evolution (infants who can read and engage caregiver intent survive better) as much as or more than any tool-use pressure.

The "Grandmother Hypothesis," formalized by Kristen Hawkes and colleagues (1997, *Current Anthropology*) proposes that the distinctive human pattern of post-reproductive longevity (menopause) evolved because grandmothers who provisioned grandchildren increased their inclusive fitness more effectively than continuing to reproduce directly. In the Hadza of Tanzania, Hawkes' field data showed that children's weight gain correlated with the amount of time grandmothers spent foraging.

Cross-cultural data compiled by Barry Hewlett and colleagues confirms that in all studied forager societies, infants are held, fed, and cared for by multiple individuals during any given day — the "village raises the child" pattern is universal, not a modern social invention.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Responsibilities by age and role:**

| Caregiver | Age | Primary contributions |
|-----------|-----|----------------------|
| Mother | Any | Primary nursing, baseline care |
| Father | Any | Provisioning, protection, occasional direct care |
| Grandmother | Post-reproductive | Supplementary food provisioning, direct infant care while mother forages |
| Older siblings (8+) | Juvenile | Holding, monitoring, playing with infant |
| Aunts/uncles | Adult | Emergency care, feeding supplementary foods |
| Unrelated adults | Any | Monitoring, brief holding, response to distress |

**Coordinating daily care:**

1. Before a foraging trip, ensure at least one capable alloparent is present at camp
2. Nursing infants (<12 months) require return to camp or carrying — cannot be left for full-day foraging trips
3. Older infants (12-24 months) can be left with a capable alloparent for a half-day
4. Children who can walk (24+ months) can accompany foragers on shorter trips
5. Alloparents who are not foraging should be the most knowledgeable about immediate threats (predators, weather) at the camp location

**Supplementary feeding by alloparents:**

1. Pre-masticated food (chewed by an adult, then fed to the infant) is the earliest form of supplementary feeding — documented in chimpanzees and virtually universal in forager societies
2. Soft cooked or pounded foods can be offered from ~6 months
3. Alloparents who forage explicitly for foods accessible to infants — soft berries, mashed tubers, fine plant material — increase infant growth rates significantly
4. The grandmother provisioning pattern involves particularly targeting dense, starchy, gathered foods (tubers, roots) that can be processed and fed to young children

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Compressed reproductive interval:** Mothers with active alloparent support reproduce significantly sooner after weaning than isolated mothers. Over a lifetime, this represents 1-3 additional surviving offspring — enormous inclusive fitness advantage.
- **Freed foraging capacity:** A mother with alloparent support can forage at full capacity for 4-6 hours per day rather than being constrained by the infant's needs. This directly increases the group's total caloric intake.
- **Infant survival:** Infants with multiple caregivers have lower mortality from predation, accident, and maternal illness. The redundancy of the care network means that a mother's temporary incapacitation does not automatically mean infant death.
- **Skill transmission:** Alloparenting creates prolonged contact between juveniles and infants — the juvenile alloparent learns caregiving skills while providing care, preparing them for their own eventual parenthood.

## Day 0 — Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0 -->
You are leaving for a full morning's foraging. The infant is 14 months old — cannot be carried all day, too slow to walk.

Your mother stays at camp. She is past reproductive age. She will carry the infant while you are gone, share her own food with it, keep it in the shade during the heat, respond to its distress.

You will be gone 5 hours. You will return with 8 kg of tubers.

If you had to carry the infant, you would have returned in 2 hours with 2 kg, exhausted.

This is not a modern arrangement. It is the oldest arrangement there is.

The entire structure — grandmother, older sibling, unrelated adult willing to hold a distressed infant — is the foundation on which human reproduction runs faster than any other great ape's.

Give the child to someone you trust. Go get the food.
