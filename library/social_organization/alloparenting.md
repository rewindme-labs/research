# Alloparenting

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->
A chimpanzee mother carries, feeds, and protects her infant alone for five to six years before she can reproduce again. A human forager mother, surrounded by her own mother, older children, sisters, and unrelated adults willing to hold a screaming infant for 20 minutes while she digs tubers, reproduces again in three years. That difference - two to three years shaved off each reproductive cycle - compounds across a lifetime into one, two, or three additional surviving offspring. Over generational time, it doubles the reproductive rate of the entire lineage.

Alloparenting is the term for care provided to offspring by individuals who are not the biological parents. In humans it is not occasional babysitting. It is a deep, species-defining behavioral system. Cross-cultural field data shows that in all documented forager societies, infants spend 40-60% of their waking hours being held, fed, or attended by someone other than their mother. The grandmother digs roots and pre-chews them for the toddler. The eight-year-old carries the infant on her hip while the mother forages at full capacity for six hours. The unrelated adult male responds to a crying infant because in a group of 25 people, every infant's survival matters to the group's survival.

The biological consequence is stark. Humans reproduce faster than any other great ape despite having the most dependent, slowest-developing offspring. This paradox resolves only when you account for the network of caregivers around each child. Remove the network and the system collapses: isolated mothers in any primate species reproduce at the rate their own body can sustain alone.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
Sarah Blaffer Hrdy's *Mothers and Others* (2009, Harvard University Press) is the foundational synthesis on human cooperative breeding. Hrdy argues that alloparenting is not a cultural convenience but the central behavioral innovation of the hominin lineage - more fundamental to human evolution than bipedalism, tool use, or language. Her core claim: the cognitive demands of reading caregiver intent, engaging multiple attachment figures, and negotiating social alliances as an infant drove the development of shared intentionality, the cognitive capacity that separates humans from all other apes.

The Grandmother Hypothesis, formalized by Kristen Hawkes, James O'Connell, and Nicholas Blurton Jones (1997, *Current Anthropology*), proposes that post-reproductive longevity in women (menopause) evolved because grandmothers who provisioned their daughters' children increased their own inclusive fitness more than they would by continuing to bear children. Hawkes' fieldwork with the Hadza of Tanzania produced the supporting data: children's nutritional status correlated more strongly with grandmother foraging effort than with any other variable, including the mother's own foraging output.

Barry Hewlett's cross-cultural studies of infant care among the Aka, Efe, and other Central African forager groups (1991, *Intimate Fathers*) documented the specific mechanics: Aka fathers hold their infants more than fathers in any other documented culture, averaging 22% of daylight hours in direct contact. Among the Efe, newborns are passed between an average of 14 different holders in their first days of life. This is not neglect. It is a network establishing multiple attachment bonds that serve as insurance against maternal illness, injury, or death.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->
**Caregiver roles by competence, not title:**

| Caregiver | Typical age | What they provide |
|-----------|------------|-------------------|
| Mother | Any | Nursing (exclusive protein/fat source until ~6 months). Primary night care |
| Grandmother (maternal) | Post-reproductive | Daytime provisioning. Processes and pre-chews food for weaned toddlers. Primary backup when mother forages |
| Older sibling | 8+ years | Carrying, monitoring, entertaining. Keeps infant occupied and safe within camp |
| Father | Any | Provisioning the mother. Direct holding and carrying (varies by culture: 5-22% of daylight hours) |
| Unrelated adult | Any | Emergency response to distress. Brief holding. Supervision during group activities |

**Organizing daily care around foraging:**

1. Before a foraging trip, confirm that at least one competent caregiver remains at camp. A competent caregiver can carry the infant, respond to distress, and identify immediate threats (predators, fire, water)
2. Nursing infants (under 6 months) cannot be separated from the mother for a full foraging day. The mother either carries the infant or returns to camp every 2-3 hours to nurse
3. Older infants (6-18 months) can remain with an alloparent for a half-day stretch if supplementary food is available. The grandmother or older sibling offers pre-chewed tuber, soft fruit, or bone broth
4. Toddlers who walk independently (18+ months) can accompany adult foragers on shorter trips. They forage inefficiently but learn plant identification and terrain through direct exposure
5. Juveniles (4-7 years) are old enough to be supervised by older children in groups. They do not require constant adult attention but need someone within earshot

**Supplementary feeding by alloparents:**

| Infant age | Food type | Method |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| 0-6 months | Breast milk only | Mother nurses exclusively |
| 6-12 months | Soft cooked tuber, mashed fruit, pre-chewed meat | Grandmother or mother pre-chews and transfers mouth-to-mouth or by hand |
| 12-24 months | Broader diet: root vegetables, soft-cooked meat, bone marrow, insects | Any competent alloparent can feed. The child can self-feed soft items |
| 24+ months | Full adult diet in small portions | Child eats with the group. Alloparent monitors choking, shares from own portion |

Pre-mastication (chewing food and transferring it directly to the infant's mouth) is documented in chimpanzees, all studied forager societies, and was standard practice in Western countries until the 19th century. It is not unhygienic in context - the adult's saliva begins starch digestion and the transferred food is warm, soft, and safe for an infant without molars.

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Compressed reproductive interval:** With reliable alloparent support, human forager women average 3-year inter-birth intervals compared to 5-6 years in chimpanzees. Over a 20-year reproductive span, this yields 5-7 surviving offspring instead of 3-4. The demographic impact is enormous
- **Full-capacity maternal foraging:** A mother with a grandmother or older sibling handling daytime infant care can forage for 4-6 hours at full physical output. Without support, she is limited to 1-2 hours of compromised foraging while carrying a dependent. The caloric difference feeds the entire group
- **Infant survival through redundancy:** If the mother is injured, sick, or killed, an infant with established bonds to multiple alloparents has a survival path. An infant bonded exclusively to its mother has none. In small forager bands where adult mortality from predation, infection, or childbirth runs 1-3% per year, this redundancy is not theoretical
- **Skill transfer to juvenile caregivers:** The 8-year-old who carries an infant for 3 hours daily is learning caregiving mechanics - how to hold, calm, feed, and monitor a small child - years before her own first pregnancy. She arrives at motherhood with thousands of hours of practice

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->
You need to dig tubers for the next five hours. The patch is 3 km from camp, across open ground, in direct sun. You cannot carry a 9 kg infant on your hip while digging with a stick in hard clay. You tried yesterday. You came back with 1.5 kg of food and a screaming child with heat rash. The group needs 8 kg from you today.

Your mother is at camp. She is 52 years old, past her last pregnancy, still strong enough to carry the child and walk the camp perimeter. She raised you and your four siblings. She knows when a cry means hunger, when it means pain, and when it means boredom. She has food she processed this morning - cooked yam, chewed soft.

Hand the child to her. The child will protest. Ignore the protest. Walk away. Do not look back for the first 200 meters, because if the child sees you hesitating it will scream louder and your mother will have a harder job. The child will settle within 10 minutes. It has done this before.

Go to the tuber patch. Dig at full intensity. Use both hands on the stick. Drive your body weight into the ground. You can do this because both your hands are free and your center of gravity is stable. You are not balancing a child on one hip while trying to lever a root out of clay with a single arm.

Come back at midday with 8 kg. The child is asleep on your mother's chest in the shade. Your mother has already fed it twice from her own prepared food. The child did not starve. The child did not overheat. The child did not get taken by a raptor or a snake, because your mother has been watching the ground and the sky for 52 years and she is better at it than you are.

This is not daycare. This is the reproductive strategy that outcompeted every other primate lineage on the continent. You are fertile again in 18 months instead of 36 because your body does not have to sustain a child alone. The next one will have the same network waiting for it.

Every infant is a group project. Treat it like one.
