# Group Vigilance and Sentinel Behavior
## Description
<!-- @field: description -->
Group vigilance is the emergent defense system that arises when multiple individuals in a social group divide the cognitive burden of scanning for predators. Rather than every individual simultaneously scanning and eating - an inefficient division of attention - groups develop informal or semi-formal sentinel arrangements where some individuals prioritize vigilance while others forage or rest. When a sentinel detects threat, specific alarm signals trigger coordinated responses from the entire group.

This behavior is documented across hundreds of species, from meerkats to baboons to chimpanzees, and represents one of the most fundamental advantages of group living. For early hominins without weapons or fire, group vigilance was the primary defense system against a savanna populated with specialized predators - lions, leopards, cheetahs, African wild dogs, and the extinct giant hyenas and saber-toothed cats of the Pliocene. Groups that organized vigilance effectively lost fewer members and raised more offspring.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
Fossil taphonomy (the study of how organisms become fossils) provides indirect evidence for predation pressure on early hominins. Lee Berger and Ron Clarke analyzed *Australopithecus africanus* cranial specimens from Sterkfontein and Swartkrans caves (South Africa, 2-3 Ma) in 1995 and identified puncture marks consistent with large eagle talons on two juvenile skulls - specifically, the Taung Child (*A. africanus*, ~2.8 Ma). This indicates that early hominins were prey not just for ground-level carnivores but also for aerial predators, requiring multi-directional vigilance.

Primate behavioral studies provide the clearest model for early hominin vigilance systems. Baboon troops (*Papio* spp.) in the Amboseli ecosystem (Kenya) have been documented by Jeanne Altmann and colleagues since 1971 in what has become the longest continuously running primate field study. Baboons show clear sentinel roles - adult males in particular spend significantly more time in upright scanning posture than foraging females with young. This division is not random: it is related to reproductive investment and predation risk.

Vervet monkeys (*Chlorocebus pygerythrus*) demonstrate the most sophisticated natural alarm system studied outside of humans: three distinct alarm calls for eagles (run to dense brush), leopards (run into trees), and snakes (stand up and look down), with receivers showing appropriate predator-specific responses even without seeing the predator. Early hominins almost certainly possessed comparable referential alarm signaling (see: *Vocal Alarm Calls*).

## Organization
<!-- @field: procedure -->
Effective group vigilance requires no explicit planning - it emerges from individual attention to group members' behavior and position. However, it functions best when understood explicitly:

**Core principles:**

1. Not everyone needs to watch simultaneously. In a group of 6-8, 1-2 individuals in elevated or exposed positions provide sufficient coverage if positioned correctly.
2. Sentinel positions should face different directions. Two sentinels both facing the same direction leave a 180-degree blind spot.
3. Sentinel individuals signal safety continuously through relaxed feeding behavior or social calls. Sudden silence is itself an alarm.
4. Alarm signals must be specific and immediately understood. Vague threat signals cause confusion during the critical seconds of response.

**Sentinel positioning:**

| Position | Coverage | Best For |
|----------|----------|---------|
| Elevated ground (termite mound, rock) | 270-360 degrees | Open savanna, daytime |
| Tree fork above group | 360 degrees aerial + ground | Mixed predator environment |
| Upwind of group | Detect predator scent before sight | Low visibility terrain |
| Perimeter while others drink | All approach vectors to water | Critical vulnerability point |

**Rotation:**

1. Sentinel shifts should be voluntary and not exceed 15-20 minutes - vigilance declines sharply with fatigue
2. A sentinel returning to the group signals transition by moving toward the center and beginning to forage
3. Another individual, seeing the sentinel relax, moves to an exposed position - this transition is automatic in well-coordinated groups
4. During high-risk periods (near water, at dawn/dusk), overlap two sentinels simultaneously

**Response protocol when alarm is given:**

| Alarm Type | Appropriate Response | Notes |
|------------|---------------------|-------|
| Ground predator (long-range) | Gather group, move to nearest tree | Sentinel climbs highest point |
| Ground predator (close, <30m) | Mob if group large enough, otherwise scatter and climb | See *Predator Mobbing* |
| Aerial predator | Crouch immediately, move to dense cover | Do not look up - move to shrubs |
| Unknown threat | Freeze, face direction sentinel indicated | Wait for clarification signal |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Feeding efficiency:** When sentinels monitor for predators, other group members can forage with more concentration and less interruption - increasing caloric intake by 15-30% compared to solitary foraging where individuals must split attention between food and predator detection.
- **Sleep security:** Rotating night vigilance allows the majority of the group to achieve deep sleep, critical for physical recovery, while maintaining predator detection.
- **Infant and juvenile protection:** Mothers with dependents are the group's least mobile members during predator response. Effective sentinel systems provide them early warning, maximizing the time available for protective action.
- **Waterhole access:** Water is the point of maximum vulnerability - all predators know waterholes, and all prey must use them. A sentinel system at the waterhole edge is essential for safe drinking.

## Day 0  -  Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->
Your group is eating. Everyone is looking down at the ground.

That is the moment predators choose to approach.

Pick the highest point near your group - a termite mound, a rock, the first branch of a tree. Stand there while the others eat. Face away from the group. Scan the grass in all directions, slowly. You are not looking for the full animal - you are looking for movement, for a shape that does not match the grass pattern, for a flicker of tail or ear.

When you see something, do not shout randomly. Give the specific alarm your group knows - the one for ground threat, the one for air threat. Keep it consistent. Your group's response in the next 3 seconds depends on which call you give.

After 15 minutes, move back into the group and begin eating. Someone else will take your position. If they do not, nudge them toward the high ground.

The group that watches together stays together.
