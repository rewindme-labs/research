# Division of Labour

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

Division of labour is the distribution of distinct tasks among group members based on skill, physical capacity, experience, and situational need. Instead of every individual performing every survival function independently, groups that divide work multiply their collective output. One person knaps tools while another scouts for game. A nursing mother processes tubers at camp while older juveniles gather firewood. The practice is observable in chimpanzee hunting parties at Ngogo (Uganda), where specific individuals consistently take the role of driver, blocker, or ambusher during colobus hunts (Watts and Mitani, 2002). In early hominin groups, division of labour allowed bands of 15-30 individuals to exploit a wider ecological range than any single forager could manage alone.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

The earliest indirect evidence for division of labour comes from Olduvai Gorge (Tanzania), where Mary Leakey's excavations (1971) revealed activity-specific zones within FLK Zinj (1.75 Ma): concentrations of knapping debris separated from butchery waste by several metres, suggesting different tasks performed by different individuals at the same site. At Koobi Fora (Kenya, 1.6 Ma), refitting studies by Toth (1985) showed that cores were prepared at one location and finished flakes transported to a butchering station 200 m away, implying coordinated task allocation. Among modern San Bushmen of the Kalahari, documented by Richard Lee (1979), women contribute 60-80% of caloric intake through gathering while men focus on hunting, a pattern likely extending back millions of years.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->

**Identifying natural role allocation:**

| Role | Best suited | Output |
|------|-------------|--------|
| Tool maker | Individuals with fine motor skill and knapping experience | Cutting flakes, scrapers, cores |
| Scout/tracker | Fast, observant individuals with terrain knowledge | Game locations, water sources, predator alerts |
| Gatherer | Anyone with plant knowledge, especially nursing mothers with infants | Tubers, fruits, insects, eggs |
| Camp processor | Injured, elderly, or those tending fire | Food processing, hide preparation, child supervision |
| Water carrier | Juveniles, fast runners | Water transport from source to camp |

**How to implement division of labour in a small group (4-8 people):**

1. Assess each person's physical condition and skills on the first day. Who can walk far? Who knows plants? Who has steady hands?
2. Assign primary and secondary roles. Every person covers at least two functions.
3. Rotate tasks weekly to prevent skill atrophy and single-point failure. If the only knapper breaks a hand, the group loses tool production entirely.
4. Debrief each evening. Who found what? What took too long? Adjust roles based on results.
5. Children above age 7 participate in gathering and processing tasks. This is training, not exploitation.

**Critical rules:**

1. Never send a single person on any task alone. Minimum team size for any excursion is two.
2. The camp must always have at least one fire-tender and one lookout.
3. Foraging parties return before dusk without exception.
4. The best forager teaches, not just produces. Knowledge distribution is a survival multiplier.

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Caloric efficiency:** A group of 6 with divided roles gathers 2-3x more food per person-hour than 6 independent foragers. The camp processor converts raw food into edible calories while gatherers continue working.
- **Risk reduction:** If one foraging party fails (no game found, no tubers in area), the other team's output still feeds the group. Diversified activity hedges against daily variance.
- **Tool availability:** A dedicated knapper produces more and better tools in 2 hours than a generalist produces in a full day. Specialisation improves quality and speed.
- **Childcare continuity:** Nursing mothers and injured members contribute through camp-based processing rather than becoming pure consumers. Every person produces.
- **Knowledge transfer:** Pairing experienced foragers with learners during dedicated gathering trips accelerates skill transmission across generations.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

You cannot do everything alone. Stop trying. If you are in a group, even a group of three, sit down right now and split the work.

Look at each person. Who moves fast? That person scouts and fetches water. Who has patience and steady hands? They make and maintain tools at camp. Who knows plants or has done any foraging? They lead the gathering party. Whoever is injured, exhausted, or caring for a child stays at camp, tends the fire, processes whatever food comes in, and watches for threats.

Never let a critical skill exist in only one person's head. If your best forager gets bitten by a snake tomorrow, the group starves unless someone else learned the routes, the plant identification, the digging technique. Teach everything you know while you work. Talk through what you are doing and why.

Every evening, gather and report. What did each team find? How long did it take? What worked? What was a waste of time? Adjust tomorrow's plan based on today's data. The group that reviews its performance daily outperforms the group that repeats the same pattern regardless of results.

Start this today, not tomorrow. Assign roles before the sun moves another handspan. The difference between organized survival and chaotic scrambling is measured in calories, and calories are measured in lives.
