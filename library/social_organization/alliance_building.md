# Alliance Building

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

Alliance building is the formation of strategic partnerships between specific individuals within and between groups for mutual benefit: joint defence, cooperative resource acquisition, political support in disputes, and shared access to mates or territory. Alliances differ from general group membership in being selective, reciprocal, and actively maintained through investment. Chimpanzees at Gombe and Mahale form long-term coalitions that determine dominance rank: an alpha male typically maintains his position through 2-3 key allies who support him in confrontations (de Waal, 1982). These alliances require social intelligence: remembering who helped you, who betrayed you, predicting who will support whom in a future conflict, and calibrating your investment accordingly. Robin Dunbar's "social brain hypothesis" (1998) proposes that primate brain size correlates directly with social group complexity, suggesting that alliance management was a primary driver of cognitive evolution.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

Frans de Waal's field observations at Arnhem Zoo (1975-1981) revealed chimpanzee political coalitions of remarkable sophistication: males formed and dissolved alliances strategically, with the alpha position changing hands three times as different coalition configurations formed. At Gombe (Tanzania), male chimpanzees who maintained the most reciprocal alliances (grooming given correlated with grooming received and support in conflicts) had higher reproductive success than solitary males (Gilby & Wrangham, 2008). Among the !Kung San (documented by Polly Wiessner, 2002), hxaro exchange partnerships create inter-band alliances: individuals maintain 10-20 exchange partners across bands spanning 200+ km, providing access to resources and refuge during local shortages.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->

**Building an alliance (within your group):**

| Step | Action | Purpose |
|------|--------|---------|
| 1. Identify | Assess who in the group has complementary skills or resources | Target the most strategically valuable partner |
| 2. Invest | Share food, assist with tasks, support in minor disputes | Demonstrate reliability and willingness to pay costs |
| 3. Reciprocate | When partner invests in you, return the favour promptly | Build trust through reliable exchange |
| 4. Maintain | Regular social interaction, grooming equivalent, check-ins | Prevent alliance erosion through neglect |
| 5. Test | Observe partner's behaviour under stress. Do they support you? | Verify alliance is genuine, not performative |
| 6. Defend | Support your ally publicly in disputes even when costly | Signal to the group that your alliance is real |

**Alliance management rules:**

1. Never maintain more alliances than you can service. 3-5 strong alliances are more valuable than 10 weak ones. Each alliance requires regular investment.
2. Track reciprocity. If you consistently give more than you receive from a specific partner, reduce investment. Asymmetric alliances are exploitation.
3. Alliances with high-ranking or highly skilled individuals provide greater returns but cost more to maintain. Alliances with lower-ranking individuals are cheaper but provide less leverage.
4. Public support matters more than private support. Standing with your ally visibly in a dispute signals to the entire group that attacking your ally means facing both of you.
5. Be cautious of alliance switching. An individual who betrays current allies to join a rising leader will eventually betray you too.

**Inter-group alliances:**

1. Establish contact through neutral encounters: shared water source, boundary meeting with no aggression.
2. Exchange small gifts: food items, tool stone, useful objects. Gift exchange creates obligation and signals peaceful intent.
3. Build toward reciprocal access: "You forage in our territory during the dry season, we forage in yours during the wet season."
4. Intermarriage (mate exchange between groups) creates the strongest inter-group alliances, as both groups now share genetic investment in children.

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Dominance & safety:** An individual with two strong allies is virtually immune to bullying by any single group member. Coalition strength, not individual strength, determines social position in primate societies.
- **Resource access:** Allies share access to food patches, water sources, and tool materials. Your ally's knowledge becomes your knowledge.
- **Conflict deterrence:** Groups with strong internal alliances present a united front that discourages inter-group aggression. A fractured group invites attack.
- **Crisis insurance:** When disaster strikes (injury, illness, loss of key individual), your allies are the ones who carry you, feed you, and protect your children. This is the social safety net.
- **Reproductive success:** In both human and chimpanzee societies, individuals with more reciprocal allies have higher reproductive success. Alliances are not a luxury; they are a survival necessity.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

You cannot survive alone. You also cannot survive by being generically nice to everyone equally. You need specific people who will stand next to you when things go wrong, and you need to earn that by standing next to them first.

Look at your group. Who has skills you lack? The tracker who reads ground signs you miss. The forager who identifies plants you cannot. The strong individual who can lift what you cannot. These are your strategic alliance targets. Not because you like them most but because the combination of your skills and theirs creates capability neither has alone.

Invest in these people. Share your best food find with them before sharing generally. Help them with their task before attending to yours. When someone argues with them over a sleeping spot or a food portion, stand physically next to your ally. Say nothing if you want. Your presence speaks.

Watch what comes back. A genuine ally reciprocates. They share back, they assist back, they stand with you. If you invest consistently and receive nothing, you are being exploited. Reduce investment and redirect to someone who reciprocates.

Three strong alliances are more valuable than a dozen casual friendships. Each alliance requires maintenance: shared meals, shared work, shared danger. You cannot maintain unlimited relationships at that depth. Choose carefully and invest deeply.

If you encounter another group and the encounter does not turn hostile, consider the long game. A gift of food or a useful stone creates an obligation. An obligation creates a relationship. A relationship creates the possibility of mutual access, shared information, and refuge when your territory fails. Every successful foraging society on Earth maintained inter-group alliances through exchange. Start building yours at the first opportunity.
