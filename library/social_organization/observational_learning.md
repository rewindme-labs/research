# Observational Learning

## Description
<!-- @field: description -->

Observational learning is the acquisition of skills and knowledge by watching others perform tasks. It is the foundational mechanism of cultural transmission: the process by which information passes between individuals and across generations without genetic encoding. A juvenile watches an adult crack a nut with a hammerstone and anvil, observes grip, force, angle, and stone selection, then attempts the same task with progressively improving accuracy over months or years. This is not mere mimicry. True observational learning involves understanding the goal of the action, identifying which components are critical, and adapting the technique to one's own body and situation. Andrew Whiten's research (2005) documented chimpanzee cultural variants across Africa: 39 behaviour patterns that differ between populations and are maintained through social learning, including tool types, grooming styles, and food processing methods. This is proto-culture, and it predates humans by millions of years.

## History
<!-- @field: history -->

At Bossou (Guinea), Matsuzawa's longitudinal study (1994-2020) tracked nut-cracking skill development in chimpanzees over decades. Juveniles begin attempting nut cracking at age 3, achieve basic competence by age 5, and reach adult proficiency by age 9, a 6-year learning curve acquired entirely through observation and practice. No adult systematically teaches the skill. At Tai Forest (Ivory Coast), Boesch and Boesch (1990) documented rare cases of chimpanzee mothers actively facilitating juvenile learning by leaving hammerstones in accessible positions, providing correctly oriented nuts, and in exceptional cases physically repositioning a juvenile's hand on the hammerstone. The Acheulean handaxe tradition (1.7 Ma to 200,000 BP) is the longest-lasting technology in human history, maintained across 1.5 million years and three continents solely through observational learning, since no written or verbal instruction existed.

## Procedure
<!-- @field: procedure -->

**How observational learning works (stages):**

| Stage | Learner action | Duration |
|-------|---------------|----------|
| 1. Attention | Watch the skilled performer. Focus on hands, materials, sequence | Minutes to hours |
| 2. Retention | Remember the sequence, the grip, the force, the materials used | Stored in procedural memory |
| 3. Reproduction | Attempt the task. First tries are clumsy and incomplete | Days to weeks |
| 4. Correction | Compare your result to the expert's. Identify differences | Ongoing |
| 5. Refinement | Repeat with adjustments. Accuracy increases over hundreds of attempts | Months to years |
| 6. Mastery | Performance matches expert level. You can now demonstrate to others | Variable by task |

**Maximising learning efficiency (for the learner):**

1. Position yourself where you can see the expert's hands, the material, and the result simultaneously. Sit across from them, not behind.
2. Watch complete cycles, not fragments. Seeing setup, execution, and result as one sequence builds procedural memory.
3. Count steps. "First she picks the stone, then she turns it, then she finds the flat spot, then she strikes." Numbering creates structure.
4. Attempt the task immediately after watching. Motor memory decays rapidly. Waiting a day costs 40-60% of retained technique.
5. When you fail, do not change everything at once. Change one variable (grip, angle, force) per attempt and note the result.
6. Watch other learners. Their mistakes reveal which steps are critical and which are forgiving.

**Facilitating learning (for the expert):**

1. Slow down. Perform each step at half your normal speed.
2. Exaggerate critical movements. The wrist snap that produces a good flake is invisible at full speed. Amplify it.
3. Pause between steps. Let the observer register one action before moving to the next.
4. Allow handling. Let the learner hold the tool, feel its weight, test the material.
5. Do not correct every error. Let the learner discover which mistakes produce which failures. Self-discovered corrections persist longer than imposed ones.

## Materials & Improvisation
<!-- @field: materials -->

| Resource | Learning function |
|----------|-------------------|
| Demonstration materials | Same tools/materials used by the expert for matching practice |
| Practice materials | Expendable raw materials for repetitive attempt-and-fail cycles |
| Observation position | Clear sightline to expert's hands and work surface |
| Time | Uninterrupted observation periods (30+ minutes) |
| Patience | Expert tolerance for slow progress; learner tolerance for repeated failure |
| Group setting | Multiple learners observe simultaneously, reducing expert teaching load |

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->

- **Skill multiplication:** One expert knapper teaching 4 observers through demonstration creates 4 new tool producers within weeks. This scales technology across the group without requiring individual invention.
- **Error avoidance:** A child who watches an adult test and reject a poisonous berry avoids the potentially lethal trial-and-error process. Social learning shortcircuits individual risk.
- **Innovation preservation:** When one individual discovers a superior technique (better knapping angle, productive foraging route, effective medicine), observational learning spreads it to the entire group and across generations.
- **Cross-age transfer:** Juveniles learn from adults, but adults also learn from peers who have recently solved novel problems. Lateral learning within a generation accelerates adaptation to changing conditions.
- **Cumulative culture:** Each generation learns the previous generation's full skill set and occasionally adds improvements. Over thousands of generations, this ratchet effect produces technology far beyond any individual's invention capacity.

## Day 0 - Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->

Watch everything everyone does. Right now your ignorance is your greatest liability and every person around you has knowledge you need.

When someone cracks a nut, stop what you are doing and watch their hands. How do they hold the hammerstone? Where exactly do they strike? How hard? Watch three complete cycles before you attempt it yourself. Then try immediately. Not tomorrow. Not after lunch. Right now, while your muscles remember what your eyes just saw.

When you fail, and you will fail, do not change your entire approach. Change one thing. Grip tighter. Angle differently. Strike harder. One variable at a time. When a change produces a better result, keep it and adjust the next variable. This is systematic debugging applied to stone age technology and it works.

If you are the expert, slow down when someone watches you. You have done this ten thousand times and your movements are invisible to a beginner. Make them visible. Lift the hammerstone high so the watcher sees the angle. Pause before striking so they see the target point. Let them hold your tools and feel the weight.

Do not hoard skills. The knapping technique you refuse to demonstrate is the technique that dies when you die. The foraging route you keep secret is the route nobody takes when you break your ankle. Every skill you teach to someone else is a skill that survives independent of your personal survival. In a group of six, any critical skill should exist in at least three heads.

Put children near working adults. Not as a chore assignment but as a learning opportunity. A 5-year-old watching nut cracking starts attempting it at 6 and is proficient by 10. That child becomes a productive food processor five years before they would have figured it out alone. Multiply that across every skill in your group and you have a compound advantage that grows every generation.
