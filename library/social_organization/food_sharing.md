# Food Sharing and Division
## Description
<!-- @field: description -->
Food sharing - the deliberate distribution of acquired food beyond the acquiring individual's immediate family to other group members - is one of the most distinctive behaviors separating hominins from other great apes. While chimpanzees sometimes share meat after a cooperative hunt, systematic sharing of plant foods, tubers, and other gathered items is essentially absent in non-human primates. Among early hominins, food sharing became a foundational social institution: it enabled provisioning of the young, injured, and elderly; created obligations and alliances that increased group cohesion; and allowed specialization of labor that improved overall group efficiency.

The evolutionary emergence of food sharing is linked to several concurrent developments: bipedalism (freeing hands to carry food), larger body size (requiring more food than any individual could easily acquire alone), longer juvenile dependency periods (requiring provisioning by adults other than the mother), and the increased hunting success of cooperative groups (creating surplus that exceeded one family's consumption).

## History
<!-- @field: history -->
Anthropologist Polly Wiessner's long-term study of !Kung San food sharing networks (1977-2010) documented that San individuals share food with an average of 7-9 households per week, spanning beyond immediate family to in-laws, friends, and reciprocal partners (*hxaro* network partners). She found that food sharing networks provided a measurable buffer against starvation during lean periods: households with wider sharing networks survived drought years at significantly higher rates than socially isolated families.

The paleoanthropological evidence for systematic food sharing appears in the archaeological record through site structure. Isaac Glynn's (1978) "central place foraging" model proposed that Oldowan sites like those at Koobi Fora represented central locations where hominins brought food items collected from dispersed locations for group consumption - essentially food sharing sites. The presence of both plant-processing evidence (grinding wear, seed remains) and animal bones at the same sites supports this interpretation.

Comparative primate research by Richard Wrangham showed that among chimpanzees, meat sharing follows specific social rules - high-ranking males receive preferential access, females trade meat for mating access - foreshadowing the rule-governed sharing systems seen in human hunter-gatherers. The transition from chimpanzee-style conditional sharing to systematic hunter-gatherer sharing is one of the key behavioral transitions in human evolution.

## Principles and Practice
<!-- @field: procedure -->
Food sharing in hunter-gatherer societies follows consistent principles that maximize group welfare while maintaining individual motivation to acquire food:

**Core rules documented across multiple cultures:**

| Rule | Rationale | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| Large game divided beyond immediate family | Individual cannot consume before spoilage; reciprocity builds alliances | Hadza elephant/giraffe divided camp-wide |
| Plant food gathered by individual, shared selectively | Lower acquisition cost, individual prerogative honored | San women share gathered food preferentially with children |
| Sick, injured, and nursing mothers provisioned | Dependency acknowledged; reciprocity expected when roles reverse | Universal in hunter-gatherer societies |
| Children eat before adults in scarcity | Developmental investment prioritized | Documented in !Kung, Hadza, and Inuit groups |
| Sharing creates debt, not gift | Receiver owes future sharing to giver | Explicit in San *hxaro* system |

**Practical sharing at a carcass site:**

1. Senior individual calls the group to the carcass site upon discovery
2. Primary carcass division: organs (liver, heart, lungs) consumed first - these spoil fastest
3. Secondary division: hind legs and back (most meat) divided among adult males who assisted in the kill or scavenging effort
4. Tertiary division: remaining portions distributed to females with young, then elderly, then other adults
5. Bones distributed to remaining group members for marrow extraction
6. No portion concealed from other group members at the primary division site

**Establishing sharing obligations:**

1. Share first and visibly - the act of sharing must be seen to create the social obligation
2. Share even when you have little - especially during observed scarcity, small acts of sharing build the most durable obligations
3. Track who shares and who does not - memory of past sharing is the enforcement mechanism
4. Reciprocate promptly when the situation is reversed - delay weakens the obligation network
5. Share across family lines - intra-family sharing is expected; inter-family sharing creates the alliances that buffer against bad luck

## Applications
<!-- @field: applications -->
- **Insurance against individual bad luck:** A hunter who fails for 2 weeks still eats because of past sharing. Without food sharing, individual foraging variance (some days nothing, some days surplus) would cause regular starvation episodes.
- **Extended juvenile dependency:** Human children require provisioning for 10-15 years - far longer than any other primate. This is only possible within a food-sharing network that distributes the cost of child-rearing across the group.
- **Division of labor efficiency:** When foragers know that their surplus will be shared with those who stayed in camp performing other tasks (childcare, tool manufacture, water collection), specialization becomes viable. This increases total group productivity.
- **Alliance building:** Regular food exchange creates and reinforces alliances between families and individuals. These alliances are activated during conflicts, migrations, and emergencies - making the food-sharing network simultaneously an alliance network.

## Day 0  -  Cookbook
<!-- @field: day0_cookbook -->
You came back to camp with more than you can eat tonight. Three other people came back with nothing.

Share it now, openly, where everyone can see.

Not because you will not be hungry tomorrow. You will be. But the person you share with today will remember. Two weeks from now when you come back empty-handed, they will share with you. That memory, across the group, across the months, is the system that keeps everyone alive.

The size of what you share matters less than the consistency. Share a little always rather than everything once.

When the group brings down something large - a deer, a large tuber patch, a honeycomb - call everyone. Divide it at the center of camp, in order: children first, nursing mothers, injured members, then everyone else equally. The rule matters more than any single division.

Groups that share together stay alive through winters and droughts that kill groups that do not.
